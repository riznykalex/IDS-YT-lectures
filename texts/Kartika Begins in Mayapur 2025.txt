"Kṛṣṇa dear devotees, welcome to Śrīdhām Māyāpur, one of the most sacred places in the universe. Śrīla Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura writes in *Mādhurya-kādambinī*: "The mercy of Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, who is sweetness personified, is a surging bank of clouds that brings life to the seeds of devotion planted in the field of the heart, extinguishing the fire of material desires and bestowing love upon all living beings."

Therein, he compares Mahāprabhu's mercy to a monsoon cloud and our hearts to dry soil. When that mercy falls, the barren ground of our hearts becomes fertile again. Bhakti sprouts, hope blossoms, life returns. Once, during Lord Caitanya's parikramā, dark clouds gathered above the devotees. The devotees looked up anxiously; they didn't have umbrellas in those days. But Mahāprabhu simply raised His lotus hands, smiled at the sky, and chanted loudly, "Hari bol, Hari bol, Hari bol." And the clouds, being conscious beings, became afraid and fled away.

The *Bhāgavatam* states that when we hear about Kṛṣṇa, He personally enters the heart and cleanses it of all impurity. So, the more we chant and hear here in Māyāpur, the more the dawn reveals itself. If we are inattentive, we will just see a Bengali village. If we are prayerful, we will see the spiritual world shining through.

Sometimes people think pilgrimage means visiting many temples, but the real pilgrimage is the journey of perception. When we begin to see everything as mercy here, then we have arrived. Śrīla Prabhupāda used to say, "Don't try to see Kṛṣṇa; act in such a way that Kṛṣṇa will want to see you." And when He wants to see us, He reveals Himself through every experience.

Let us offer our hearts to Śrī Rādhā-Mādhava, to Pañca-tattva, to Lord Nṛsiṁha, and to our beloved Śrīla Prabhupāda, who made it possible for all of us to be here. Śrī Māyāpur-kī jaya! Kārtika 2025, Gaurāṅga!""
