"# Discovering Mayapur - with Indradyumṇa Swami

Hari Kṛṣṇa, dear devotees. Welcome to this year’s Kārtika parikramā. Usually, we observe Kārtika each year in Vṛndāvana; however, this year we are spending the sacred month in Māyāpura. Śrīla Prabhupāda has taught us that Māyāpura and Vṛndāvana are non-different. This is also confirmed by Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura in his *Prārthanā*. Therein, he writes, *śrī-gauḍa-maṇḍala-bhūmi, yebā jāni’ cintāmaṇi, tāra haya vraja-bhūme vāsa*. One ought to know that Gauḍa-maṇḍala—Māyāpura—is equal to Vṛndāvana; there is no difference between the inhabitants of Vṛndāvana and those of Śrī Māyāpura. The first two weeks of Kārtika have been truly special. We traveled through sacred villages, visited the homes of Lord Caitanya’s intimate associates, had darśana of the worshipable deities, and met some of the wonderful souls residing in this holy land.

We began our month at Yogapīṭha, the sacred birthplace of the golden avatar, Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu. This holy site in Śrī Māyāpura marks the very spot where the Lord appeared to usher in the age of saṅkīrtana, the congregational chanting of the holy names. Just as Kṛṣṇa’s appearance in Mathurā transformed the world, Mahāprabhu’s descent at Yogapīṭha marked the beginning of a spiritual revolution meant to uplift all souls regardless of background or qualification.

In the heart of Navadvīpa lies Godrumadvīpa, the island of kīrtana, sanctified by the life and vision of Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, the pioneer of Lord Caitanya’s modern-day movement. Born in 1838 in Bengal as Kedarnath Datta, he was extraordinary from childhood: composing poetry at the age of two, studying the *Mahābhārata* and the *Rāmāyaṇa* by six, and inquiring about the absolute truth by the age of 10. He was brilliant, but his brilliance always turned towards the spiritual. In time, he became a magistrate and a respected scholar. But above all, he was a humble servant of Lord Caitanya Mahāprabhu. 

The world into which he appeared desperately needed such a pure soul. After the disappearance of Lord Caitanya and His followers, darkness had descended upon Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism. Many groups claimed allegiance to Mahāprabhu but engaged in illicit practices and false doctrines. Educated society turned away in disgust, thinking that devotion to Caitanya meant immorality and superstition. To restore the dignity of pure bhakti, the Lord sent Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura. He was gentle as a rose yet strong as thunder. When confronting hypocrisy—when the false mystic Bisha-sainya claimed to be an incarnation of Mahā-Viṣṇu—Bhaktivinoda personally investigated, exposed him, and had him punished, protecting the name of true religion even at great personal risk. 

He was a family man, and in 1874, his wife Bhagavati Devī gave birth to Bimala Prasad, who would later become Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura, the next link in the disciplic chain of mercy. Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura raised him from childhood in devotion, teaching him to worship the Śālagrāma-śilā of Kūrmadeva that he himself had discovered. Despite his governmental responsibilities, Bhaktivinoda was always absorbed in chanting and writing. He once said that the Lord’s name is the true religion of the sage. He quoted Mahāprabhu, "All the books should be bound up, and everyone should simply chant the holy names." His heart, however, longed to live in Vṛndāvana. One day, Lord Śiva appeared to him in a dream and said, "You desire to go to Vṛndāvana, but there is still much work to be done in Navadvīpa. What have you accomplished there?"

Shortly afterward, while standing on the roof of his house one night, Bhaktivinoda saw a golden light shining across the Gaṅgā. His son, Kamala Prasad, saw it too. Upon visiting, Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura discovered that this was the birthplace of Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, long forgotten by time. He fell to the ground in tears, understanding that Lord Śiva’s words had now been fulfilled. Soon after, Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī Mahārāja confirmed his discovery. Thus, through Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura’s vision, Yogapīṭha—the very spot where Caitanya appeared—was revealed once more to the world. 

Later, he established his home in Godruma and named it Svānanda-sukhada-kuñja, where he spent his evenings chanting, writing, and gazing upon the dhāma with tear-filled eyes. There he composed hundreds of songs, prayers, and essays, including *Śaraṇāgati*, *Jaiva-dharma*, and *Harināma-cintāmaṇi*, laying the foundation for the global Kṛṣṇa consciousness movement. In his old age, Bhaktivinoda took the dress of a paramahaṁsa-vaiṣṇava, devoting himself entirely to relishing the intimate pastimes of Rādhā and Govinda. He departed from this world on June 23rd, 1914, in Kolkata, his heart resting eternally in the service of the divine couple. Those who follow his path continue to feel his presence—the saint who revived the pure current of Lord Caitanya’s mercy.

In the sacred town of Kaṭvā, a moment unfolded that changed the course of the world. It was the moment when Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, then known as Nimāi Paṇḍita, accepted sannyāsa from Keśava Bhāratī Mahārāja. Why would the Supreme Personality of Godhead need to take sannyāsa? Why would the most beautiful youth of Navadvīpa, loved by all, renounce everything? In his early years, Nimāi Paṇḍita had opened a *ṭola*, a small school for Sanskrit study. His brilliance as a teacher was unmatched. Even the learned men of Navadvīpa were astonished by his command of logic and scripture. The very title "Paṇḍita" reflected his scholarship and his reputation. Yet at that time, some of the so-called educated men began to envy him. They criticized his teaching and mocked his divine insights. 

The Lord, who had descended to rescue the conditioned souls, thought very deeply: "I have come to save the fallen souls of this world. If they criticize Me, how will they ever be delivered?" To awaken respect and make His message acceptable to all, He resolved to accept the renounced order of life, sannyāsa. As Śrīla Prabhupāda explained many times, "In Vedic culture, when people see the saffron cloth of a sannyāsī, they naturally offer respect." So, the Lord decided that through His renunciation, He would conquer the world—not with weapons, but with His compassion. 

The ceremony took place here in Kaṭvā. When Nimāi’s hair was being shaved, it was a deeply emotional ceremony where the devotees cried out helplessly. They could not bear to see the beauty of the beloved Lord disappear beneath the marks of renunciation. Mother Śacī was not there, but her heart was breaking in Navadvīpa. Only Nityānanda Prabhu remained composed, understanding the divine purpose behind this unbearable scene. After taking sannyāsa, Nimāi received the name Śrī Kṛṣṇa Caitanya, the golden Lord whose compassion would spread across the world. He lifted His arms and began to chant the holy names, and the sound of that chanting has never stopped since. From that moment, the carefree scholar of Navadvīpa became the wandering mendicant of love. The Lord would melt the hearts in every town and village of the world, transferring the pain of separation into the ecstasy of Kṛṣṇa. Śrīla Prabhupāda often reminded us that Mahāprabhu accepted sannyāsa not to withdraw from the world, but to reach every soul within it. His renunciation was not detachment; it was the highest expression of mercy. And so, when one remembers Kaṭvā, one remembers the mercy of the Lord of Navadvīpa and the beginning of a mission that still flows to the hearts of His followers today.

In the land of Bengal lies Campāhaṭṭī, a place forever blessed by the presence of Śrī Jayadeva Gosvāmī, the saint whom devotees call God’s choicest poet. His songs were so beautiful that they captured the heart of the king of Bengal, Lakṣmaṇa Sena, who invited him to leave his humble village and reside in his quiet grove surrounded by fragrant campaka flowers. It was here that Jayadeva composed his immortal work, the *Gīta-govinda*, a tapestry of songs describing the most intimate and transcendental pastimes of Śrī Śrī Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. At the end of the work, he identified himself as the author: "He who appeared in the village of Kenduvilva just as the moon rises from the ocean has collected Śrī Kṛṣṇa’s expressions of lamentation. That Jayadeva is humbly narrating this song." He was not a poet seeking fame, but rather a devotee longing to glorify the Lord through art. 

Among all his stories, one shines brightest. While writing a verse, Jayadeva hesitated to describe Kṛṣṇa bowing to touch the feet of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. "How can the Supreme Lord bow down to His devotee?" he thought. He went to bathe in the Gaṅgā. When he returned, the verse had already been written in his own hand. He understood that Kṛṣṇa Himself had come and completed the line. Even the Lord wished the world to know that love alone rules over Him. Later, Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu would discuss the *Gīta-govinda* only with His closest companions, Svarūpa Dāmodara and Rāmānanda Rāya, relishing each verse and often weeping as He recited Jayadeva’s words. Śrīla Prabhupāda explained that although the *Gīta-govinda* is supremely sacred, it should be approached with purity and respect. He advised his followers to sing only its invocation, the *Daśāvatāra-stotra*, which glorifies the Lord’s ten incarnations. When Jayadeva completed his masterpiece, he closed with a beautiful prayer: "Whatever is delightful in the varieties of music, whatever is graceful in the fine strains of poetry, and whatever is exquisite in the sweet art of love, let the happy and wise persons learn that from the songs of Kavi Jayadeva."

Śāntipura is the sacred home of Śrī Advaita Ācārya, who cried out for the Lord to descend in this age of Kali. Here, together with Nityānanda Prabhu and Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, He planned the great inundation of saṅkīrtana that would wash the world in love of God. For us, this place holds a special significance. It was here that our Śrīla Prabhupāda often came before his journey to the West to beg for empowerment, to pray for strength to fulfill the impossible order of his spiritual master. 

During the 1940s and 50s, the old pujārī of this temple noticed a quiet Bengali gentleman who would sometimes visit on weekends. He wore a simple white cotton dhoti and a kurta and always came alone. He never asked for attention or honor. He would sit at the back of the temple chanting on his beads for hours—softly, deeply. Tears would fill his eyes, and his voice would sometimes break as he whispered the holy names: *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare / Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma, Rāma Rāma, Hare Hare*. The pujārī never disturbed him, but he remembered that face: peaceful, determined, and full of longing. 

Years passed. Then one evening in August 1965, the same pujārī looked up and saw a saffron-clad sannyāsī sitting quietly at the same place. He recognized him immediately. Again, the devotee was chanting, but this time with even deeper emotion. When the chanting ended, the sannyāsī offered full daṇḍavats, lying prostrate for a long time before the deities. Then he approached the pujārī, thanked him softly for his service, and turned to go. Before he left, the pujārī stopped him. "Who are you?" he asked. "I have seen you here for many years." 

The sannyāsī smiled and said, "My name is Abhay Charanāravinda Bhaktivedānta Swami. I am an unworthy disciple of His Divine Grace Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura Prabhupāda, my divine master. He has given me an impossible mission: to cross over the ocean and spread the teachings of Lord Caitanya in the Western countries. I do not know how this will be possible. That is why I have been coming here to the sacred house of Advaita Ācārya, where Caitanya Mahāprabhu, Nityānanda Prabhu, and Advaita Ācārya would gather to plan the saṅkīrtana movement. It was from here that they launched the flood of love of God that has swept across India. I have been praying that they may give me their mercy, that they may empower me and guide me. I only want to satisfy my spiritual master’s desire, but I feel so unqualified." As he spoke, tears again ran down his cheeks. Then he added quietly, "Tomorrow I am leaving for Kolkata to board a ship for America. I do not know what will happen there, but I am praying most earnestly here for help." He folded his hands and humbly asked the pujārī for his blessings. 

Years later, that same pujārī began to notice something extraordinary. Foreigners—white Vaiṣṇavas—were visiting the temple. They wore dhotis and saris. They chanted on Tulasī beads. They sang the holy names of Kṛṣṇa that the old Bengali devotee had once whispered in the back of the hall. One day, one of those devotees gave the pujārī a *Back to Godhead* magazine from America. As he turned its pages, his eyes stopped on a painting. Under the painting, it said: "Founder-Ācārya, His Divine Grace A.C. Bhaktivedānta Swami Prabhupāda, who brought Kṛṣṇa consciousness to the West." It was the same man, his quiet visitor now known across the world. At that moment, the pujārī realized that his old friend had indeed accomplished the impossible mission of his guru. Overwhelmed, the pujārī traveled to Māyāpura to tell the ISKCON devotees what he had seen. He wanted them to know that before crossing the ocean, Śrīla Prabhupāda had sat here in the small temple of Advaita Ācārya begging for mercy, and that his tears had watered the seed of a worldwide saṅkīrtana movement.

No pilgrimage to Māyāpura is complete without remembering the Śrī Nṛsiṁhapālī and Godrumadvīpa. Here lies the sacred lake where Lord Nṛsiṁhadeva washed His hands after killing Hiraṇyakaśipu. The self-manifested deity Nṛsiṁha, with the demon on His lap and Prahlāda at His feet, has been worshiped here since Satya-yuga. Pilgrims pray for courage and protection, knowing that the Lord will always shield those who keep faith in His name. Close to Nṛsiṁhapālī stands Harihara-kṣetra, where the Lord revealed a most unusual form—half Viṣṇu, half Śiva—known as Harihara. Here, Śrī Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura prayed: "O Śaṭhapāla Śiva, protector of the dhāma, grant me entrance into the sacred land of Navadvīpa." This place reminds us that Lord Śiva is *vaiṣṇavānāṁ yathā śambhuḥ*—the greatest of all Vaiṣṇavas, always meditating on Kṛṣṇa’s lotus feet. 

Across the Gaṅgā flows the mercy of Ambikā-kāla, home of Gaurīdāsa Paṇḍita, identified as Subala-sakhā, Kṛṣṇa’s intimate friend in Vraja. When Mahāprabhu prepared to take sannyāsa, Gaurīdāsa cried, "How will I live without You?" Out of compassion, the Lord manifested two sets of Gaur-Nitāi deities—one pair traveling with Him and one pair remaining here. He told His devotee, "Serve these deities as you serve us. We shall stay here eternally." Those golden forms still stand with raised arms, blessing all who remember them. 

Kṛṣṇa Nityānanda Prabhu married Jāhnavā and Jāhnavā-devī, joining renunciation and family life in the same divine service. We also visited two homes of Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, the great pioneer of the Kṛṣṇa consciousness movement. He stayed for many years at Sūrabhi-kuñja before moving further down the road to Svānanda-sukhada-kuñja. We spoke in both places on the life and glories of this great ācārya. Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura once said: "Oh for that day when the fortunate English, French, Russian, German, and American people will take up banners, mṛdaṅgas, and kartālas and raise kīrtana through their streets and towns. When will that day come?" 

Let the impression of the holy places we visited remain etched in our hearts and inspire us on our journey back home, back to Godhead. May the dust of these tīrthas, the association of devotees, and remembrance of Mahāprabhu’s pastimes continue to nourish our devotion. Māyāpura-kī jaya! Bhakti-śānti! And our own beloved Śrīla Prabhupāda-kī jaya! Haribol!""
