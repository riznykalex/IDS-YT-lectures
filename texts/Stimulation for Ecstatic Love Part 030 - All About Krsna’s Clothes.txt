""Everyone, a warm welcome back once again to our ever-ongoing series on the glories of our beloved Kṛṣṇa. We are continuing with our series on stimulation for ecstatic love, and this will be part 30. For part 30, we have chosen a very interesting topic: Kṛṣṇa’s clothes. It is a fact that Kṛṣṇa’s clothes can be a stimulus in developing ecstatic love for Him. In *Nectar of Devotion*, Chapter 41, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes: "Sometimes Kṛṣṇa’s different names, forms, paraphernalia and transcendental qualities provoke fraternal love. For instance, Kṛṣṇa’s nice dress, His strongly-built body, the auspicious symptoms on His body, His knowledge of different languages, His learned teachings in *Bhagavad-gītā*, His uncommon genius in all fields of endeavor, His exhibition of expert knowledge, His mercy, His chivalry, His behavior as a conjugal lover, His intelligence, His forgiveness, His attraction for all kinds of men, His opulence, and His happiness can all provoke fraternal love." So, one of those things was Kṛṣṇa’s dress.

Now, for today’s lecture, I had to do some extra research and I found a lot of material in *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, Chapters 5 and 6, the *Brahma-vaivarta Purāṇa*, the *Kṛṣṇa Janma-khaṇḍa* section, *Mahābhārata*, as well as a *śāstra* in the Vṛndāvana Research Institute in Vṛndāvana called *Śṛṅgāra-praṇālikā*—a beautiful name, *Śṛṅgāra-praṇālikā*—by a Puṣṭimārga Vaiṣṇava poet whose name was Yogi Gopeshwar. Let us begin by hearing a vision of Kṛṣṇa’s clothes as described in *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 1.12.8. There it says that while in the womb of His mother, He was wearing a garment of lightning yellow. Then, *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 2.2.9 says that other realized devotees see *kadambakiñjalka-piśaṅga-vāsanam*—Kṛṣṇa’s garments are yellowish like the saffron of a kadamba flower. And *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 4.30.26 says: "Dear Lord, the garment You have put on is yellowish in color like the saffron of a lotus flower, but it is not made of anything material."

*Kṛṣṇa Book* Chapter 21, which is entitled "The Gopis Attracted by the Flute," gives a very detailed description of how our Kṛṣṇa dresses in Vraja: "While discussing the transcendental vibration, the Gopis also remembered how Kṛṣṇa dressed and decorated with the peacock feather on His head, just like a dancing actor, and with blue flowers pushed over His ear. His garment glowed yellow gold, and He was garlanded by a Vaijayantī necklace. Dressed in such an attractive way, Kṛṣṇa filled up the holes of His flute with the nectar emanating from His lips." They remembered Him entering the forest of Vṛndāvana, which is always glorified by the footprints of Kṛṣṇa and His companions. That is how Kṛṣṇa dresses in Vraja.

It appears that He dresses very simply in Vṛndāvana. *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* also confirms this in 10.13.11. There it is said Kṛṣṇa eats only offerings of *yajña*, but to exhibit His childhood pastimes, He now sat with His flute tucked between His waist and His tight cloth on His right side, and with His horn, bugle, and cow-driving stick on His left. Kṛṣṇa Himself, of course, refers to His preferred simple life in Vṛndāvana in *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 10.24.24, one of my favorite verses; I’ve quoted it many times in this lecture series, speaking to His father Nanda Mahārāja: "My dear father, our home is not in the cities, towns, or villages. Being forest dwellers, we always live in the forest and on the hills."

Nevertheless, there are times in Vṛndāvana when Kṛṣṇa dresses more opulently. In *Gopāla-campū*, song 12, text 5, He says: "Very handsome and wonderfully dressed as a dancing actor, Kṛṣṇa was like a regal cloud, a regal cloud come to shower a rain of nectar." So, there is "regal," implying royalty. And in *Ananta-vṛndāvana-campū* Chapter 14, Kavikarṇapūra writes—it is very beautiful—"As the rising sun beautified the blue sky with a brilliant light, Kṛṣṇa got up and dressed gorgeously like a dramatic actor. The ocean of elegance appeared like a dramatic artist dressed in many beautiful, colorful garments." Now, Kavikarṇapūra describes that that was actually the day of Gopāṣṭamī, the day when little Kṛṣṇa began herding cows. On that day, mother Yaśodā decided to dress Kṛṣṇa up as what? Not a cowherd boy, but a prince in beautiful, colorful silken clothes with a big, beautiful turban and all types of jewels. As she was doing so, Kṛṣṇa kept saying, "Maiyā, this is not how cowherd boys are supposed to look." She replied, "Lālā, everyone is talking about you going out with the cows now, so it has become a grand celebration. Thus, I must dress you accordingly."

When she finished dressing Kṛṣṇa, she took Him outside where all His cowherd friends were waiting for Him. But as soon as the boys saw Him, they all started laughing because they had never seen Him dressed like that before—as a prince, royalty. Subala-sakhā said, "Look at Him, just look at Him!" And Raktaka said, "Yes, He’s dressed like a prince to take care of cows! He’s dressed like a prince to take care of cows!" Hearing this, the other cowherd boys started rolling on the ground, holding their sides as they continued laughing. The *ācāryas* say that Kṛṣṇa just stood there red-faced, much to the dismay of mother Yaśodā. Then, suddenly, all the cowherd boys hollered, and they started charging forward, and they tore off all of Kṛṣṇa’s princely clothes. Then they quickly dressed Him up again as a simple cowherd boy, and the only ornaments He had then were His peacock feather, His stick for controlling cows, a little bugle for calling the cows, and of course, Veṇu-vaṁśī, His flute. Vṛndāvana Kṛṣṇa! After the boys had dressed Kṛṣṇa as a *gopa*, they all stood back and, looking at their work, they said in unison—it is one of my favorite things—they all looked at Kṛṣṇa and they said in unison, "That’s our Kṛṣṇa! That’s our Kṛṣṇa, that’s our Kṛṣṇa, our cowherd boy!"

From mother Yaśodā’s efforts, it appears there are times that Kṛṣṇa dresses a little more, or a lot more, opulently than normal. *Gopāla-campū* also describes how Kṛṣṇa and His Gopīs would sometimes use bathing clothes, or there are descriptions of how they had night dresses, a little more opulent. That being the case, Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī writes in *Ujjvala-nīlamaṇi* 2.5 that Kṛṣṇa has a dressing expert in the spiritual world. Kṛṣṇa has a dressing expert who assists Him in the spiritual world; in other words, He has a person who is expert in dressing Him each and every day. He describes the technical name of this position in the spiritual world is *Vīta-sahāya*. *Vīta-sahāya* means this person is expert in clothes and dress codes. He is expert in clothes and dress codes, and he assists in dressing Kṛṣṇa. *Nectar of Devotion* Chapter 25, I found, states that sometimes Kṛṣṇa even likes to dress Himself nicely. There it is stated: "A person who is very fond of dressing himself is called *lalita*, or a meticulous dresser." This characteristic is found in Kṛṣṇa in two ways: sometimes He likes to decorate Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī with various marks, and sometimes when preparing to kill demons like Ariṣṭāsura, He takes care to arrange His belt very nicely. He’s a meticulous dresser.

We actually see Kṛṣṇa in fancy or meticulous dress daily throughout the world in our ISKCON temples, where we dress Lord Kṛṣṇa in opulent clothing and ornaments, not as a simple cowherd boy. The third verse of the *Gurvaṣṭakam* prayers says, "The spiritual master is always engaged in the temple worship of Śrī Śrī Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. He also engages his disciples in such worship. They dress the deities in beautiful clothes and ornaments, cleanse their temple, and perform other similar worship of the Lord. I offer my obeisances unto the lotus feet of such a spiritual master." We do this, of course, to dress Kṛṣṇa very opulently to gain a sense of awe and reverence for the Lord before we can actually appreciate His romantic, loving pastimes in Vraja. Just like we read the first nine cantos of *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* to understand who we are and who is God before going on to the 10th Canto, where Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes are more intimate in Vraja.

Śrīla [Gosvāmī] also describes the times actually in Vṛndāvana when Kṛṣṇa dresses more opulently. Therein it is described that His clothes come in a variety of colors, fabrics, shapes, and sizes. The Vrajavāsī poet Sūrdās describes Kṛṣṇa’s clothes at such times—when He is a little opulent—as incomprehensible even to the goddess of opulence. Now, we will move on. The main fabric Kṛṣṇa dresses in, that His clothes are made of, is described as a special kind of silk called *reśam*. *Reśam*—it is described as very shimmery and shiny. They compare it to the body of a fish. This silk is smooth like the feathers of a bird, pure like the water of the Ganges, and beautiful like a sheet of stars. It is described that hundreds of meters of *reśam* can be pulled through a middle-finger-sized gold ring. The silk Kṛṣṇa wears is very fine. Such *reśam* silk is so pure that it is not even necessary to wash it before any type of ceremonial use. According to the *Mitākṣarā* law—an old text that deals with the laws of inheritance—the mere exposure of *reśam* to air is sufficient to clean it. The law says this silk is literally washed by air. You just take it outside and let the air go through it; it is so pure it is considered washed. It is amazing.

Another type of cloth fabric that Kṛṣṇa wears is called *atlas*. This looks very much like silk; it is not silk, but it is thicker and heavier and even more shimmery and shinier than silk. *Śāstra* describes it as strong as a thick rod of iron, and oral tradition says that if 100 *mahābalīs*, meaning fighters, stand on one side and 100 other *mahābalīs* stand on the other side and play a tug-of-war with this *atlas* fabric, the fabric will not break. It won’t even tear. You have got 200 warriors pulling on it; it won’t even tear. Kṛṣṇa wears that—He probably needs it in the forest of Vṛndāvana with all this wrestling with His friends and going through the forest and taking care of His cows.

Another fabric that Kṛṣṇa wears is known as *jāl*. *Jāl* literally means net, or it can mean like a thin fabric that one can actually see through. I was reading that it is also known as *acchādani*, and women used to cover their face with it in Vedic times. I tried to find out how Kṛṣṇa wears it, but there was no detail; it just says Kṛṣṇa wears His cloth in many different ways.

Another type of cloth or fabric that Kṛṣṇa wears is known as *zarī*. This *zarī* is a golden- and silver-colored fabric which is woven actually by metallic threads made from 24-karat gold and silver. One poet describes it very poetically; he says this fabric is like the sun at noon during summer time, the full moon during winter nights, jewels hanging on the breasts of Mohinī-mūrti, and the ankle bells of the goddess of fortune, Lakṣmī. There are details about that particular fabric; it says Kṛṣṇa wears it on festival days.

As a note, there was a special fabric present at the time of Kṛṣṇa called *malmal*. I didn’t find any reference that Kṛṣṇa actually wore this fabric, but the *Mahābhārata* mentions *malmal* amongst gifts received by King Yudhiṣṭhira; he would receive gifts of this precious fabric. So, it was there during Kṛṣṇa’s pastime. I was curious, so I did a little bit more research on it, and I found out that *malmal* was made by weaving extremely fine cotton thread. One poet attempts to describe it by saying—it is a little esoteric—"The skin of the moon removed by the executioner star would not be so fine." It is written that a hundred yards of *malmal* fabric can pass through the eye of a needle. However, the point of the needle can hardly pierce it. It is also described as so light and transparent that it looks as if one who wears it has no dress on at all, but is simply smearing his body with pure water. Books from the 2nd century state that *malmal* fabric barely hid the modesty of those who wore it. The 7th-century Sanskrit writer Bāṇabhaṭṭa mentions in his works that the royal robes of the Emperor Harṣa were made from *malmal* fabrics. He writes that *malmal* fabric feels like it is woven by the wind. Again, there is no mention that Kṛṣṇa wore this fabric, but it was used by others during His time on Earth.

However, there is ample information on the colors of the fabrics that Kṛṣṇa wears. In the West, we see so many people wearing black, gray, brown, dark green, or purples. But life is very joyful in Vraja, so the Vrajavāsīs are very colorful. They wear clothes like we see now sometimes in Rajasthan; if you have been to Rajasthan, the ladies wear very beautiful, colorful clothes. It is described that according to the date, the occasion, the weather, and the *nakṣatra*—*nakṣatra* meaning the astrological details, the days of the week, the mood, etc.—fabrics were carefully chosen for Kṛṣṇa. They didn’t just go and purchase them; they purchased them on a particular auspicious day of the week. The mood of the seller was important, different astrological details were there, and everything was done very carefully. These fabrics were very, very colorful. And these colors were made using various natural pigments, natural dyes. I will describe the various pigments that were used in Kṛṣṇa’s clothes 5,000 years ago:

*   Blood-red-colored pigments (*kusumbī* color) were made from powdered rubies and beetroot.
*   Light-pink pigments (*gulābī* color) were made from roses.
*   Deep-red pigments (*sāraṅgī* color) were made from red vermilion powder.
*   Red pigments (*lāl* color) were made from flowers of *guruhāla* and *guñjā*.
*   Blue pigments (*nīla* color) were made from blue shells.
*   Yellow pigments (*pīlā* color) were made from turmeric rocks.
*   Saffron pigments (*keśarī* color) were made from the saffron paste that comes from Kashmir.
*   Deep-yellow pigments (*amarāsī* color) were made from the paste of mango fruits.
*   Light-yellow pigments (*cāndanī* color) were made from sandalwood paste.
*   Black pigments (*yām* color) were made from collyrium and various incense oils.
*   Light-black pigments (gray or *dhūmel* color) were made from the smoke of agar wood.
*   Green pigments (*harā* color) were made from the green leaves of poison-less trees.
*   Deep-green pigments (*gambhīra-hariyālā* color) were made from the paste of dark green leaves of poisonous plants.
*   Light-green pigments (*harit* color) were made from lime and the leaves from various kinds of plants.
*   Turquoise pigments (*pīrozī* color) were made from turquoise gem powder.

There are many, many other colors mentioned, but we don’t have much time. Different dyes were made by grinding stones from selected mountains and mixing them with different types of algae. These sciences are all lost, but this is our tradition.

At this point, we can discuss the different types of clothes that Kṛṣṇa wears. The Puṣṭimārga Vaiṣṇava poet Yogi Gopeshwar writes in his book *Śṛṅgāra-praṇālikā* that Kṛṣṇa’s undergarments are known as *ṭaṇiyā*, *kopīna*, *laṅgoṭā*, and *aṅgavastra*. Just to say those names, you feel purified. These were made from black threads that connect a triangular piece of white cloth on one side with a rectangular piece of cloth on the backside. These garments came in two different colors: white in the summer and red in the winter. The poet writes that there are various clothes that Kṛṣṇa wears below His stomach; they are called *jāmā* or pants, or *pāy-jāmā*, loose pants, or sometimes I read they are called *sūtana*. *Sūtana* underpants are specifically pants that are tied at the bottom. Then the poet describes Kṛṣṇa wears a *dhoti* tied around His waist, or a *mālākaccha*. *Mālākaccha* is a type of unstitched cloth which is worn like a *dhoti* above the knees while wrestling. Kṛṣṇa would use this particular unsewn garment when He would wrestle in the sands of Ramaṇa-reṭī with His friends.

A type of short pants that Kṛṣṇa wears during His *bāla-kelī*, during His water pastimes, are also known as *pārdānī*. He has pastimes in the waters with His friends and the Gopīs, so He wears short pants during His *jala-kelī* pastimes. Kṛṣṇa also sometimes wears a long skirt which is known as *kāñcana* or *kāñcukī*, a skirt with many frills of different vibrant colors, which He wears specifically during *Rāsa-līlā*, or He also wears that skirt at official meetings in His father Nanda Mahārāja’s palace.

It is a triangular, long piece of cloth which He ties around His waist while playing, and sometimes He wears a *mekhalā* or *nandanī*, which is a short piece of cloth which helps to tighten His already tight waist cloth. Then there are various types of clothes Kṛṣṇa wears below His neck. One is a shirt called *takuchīhā* and *jāpanī*, which is a sleeveless shirt. There is a piece of cloth—described something like a shawl with a border—known as *uparaṇā* that Kṛṣṇa wears. There is a similar kind of shawl called a *pāṭkā*. Kṛṣṇa wears this kind of shawl in three different styles:
1.  *Rājāśahī pāṭkā*, which is a gold or a gold-woven embroidered shawl which can be worn from the neck to the shoulders and hangs from the arms.
2.  *Rāsa-pāṭkā*, which is smaller in size, which Kṛṣṇa also wears during *rāsa* dance, specifically to avoid the movement of the *mālās* or necklaces around His neck.
3.  *Sauphī*, which is very small in size and which Kṛṣṇa ties around His neck during wrestling with the boys to wipe off perspiration from His face.

The poet describes a big coat that Kṛṣṇa sometimes wears known as a *vāgā*. There is another coat that goes beyond Kṛṣṇa’s waist known as a *cakra-dhāra-vāgā*. During summertime, Kṛṣṇa sometimes wears a *phaṭavī*, a sleeveless jacket filled with cotton, and a *sādharī*, a full-sleeved jacket embedded with stones. During the winter, He wears a *phāgo*—we discussed this last year; it is a large and long *bokomanda* filled with cotton and silk threads. Then there is a piece of cloth in a triangular shape that He uses to tie His hair, as well as an extremely long cloth which He uses to tie His hair up and make turbans. As a meticulous dresser, He sometimes does it Himself, or the servants do it, or mother Yaśodā does it.

Such clothes come in silk, cotton, *jāl*, and many other gold and silver *zarīs*. Then, of course, there is the black woolen *cādar*—we gave a lecture on that last year—that Kṛṣṇa uses, which is embroidered with gems and pearls, known as *kāmariyā*, and Kṛṣṇa uses that woolen *cādar* during His cowherd pastimes. Really interesting, Kṛṣṇa has *rumāla*; it is a handkerchief which comes in a variety of shapes like circles, triangles, octagons, and squares. One can often see the deities in Vṛndāvana’s main temples dressed in an assortment of these clothes, especially in winter. Not all of them, of course, but during winter, Kṛṣṇa doesn’t wear a *dhoti* and a sash; it is cold. So, these types of clothes you will see on Vṛndāvana deities in the wintertime.

During Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes 5,000 years ago, these types of fancy clothing were used by Kṛṣṇa only for special occasions. Generally, He walks barefoot around Vṛndāvana in a simple *dhoti* and a sash. These pastimes of royal dress are actually *kelī*. *Kelī* means fun pastimes. These royal dress pastimes are all *kelī* pastimes; they are not the norm, they are the exception. Variety is the spice of life, so Vṛndāvana is full of variety. I recently read about one of these fun *kelī* royal pastimes described in several *śāstras*, including the *Hasta-kalyā-līlā-parāvalī* (verses 97 to 99), the *Prēma-bhakti-rasa* Cowherd Chapter 9, and the *Kṛṣṇa-nīla-kaumudī*. In those scriptures, it is described that once upon a time, the cowherd boys rose from their after-dinner nap and quickly woke up Kṛṣṇa, saying, "Hari, just listen! Downstairs in the palace here at Nandagrām, there is a big commotion going on. Performers are waiting for Your arrival. There is going to be a royal assembly with lots of theater and dance. Let’s go quickly!"

Mother Yaśodā had her servants dress her son in very royal attire. The idea was that Kṛṣṇa would be sitting at the center of this royal assembly, so He would dress accordingly. Mother Yaśodā’s servants dressed Kṛṣṇa in elegant royal robes. They tucked a jeweled dagger in the sash around His waist, smeared sandalwood paste on His limbs, and placed a prince regent’s scepter in His hand. They tied Kṛṣṇa’s hair in a topknot and bound it with a red and golden string, and then they crowned all of that with the peacock feather. Finally, they placed a wreath woven out of tiny flowers on His head, and then a special class of servant boys adorned Kṛṣṇa with the following: flashing earrings, a precious pearl nose ring, a diamond choker, a jeweled locket, and assorted strings of giant pearls, all from the royal treasure chest.

By this time, Kṛṣṇa was getting restless to meet His friends and He wanted to join the fun below. Mother Yaśodā opened the door of this room and invited the cowherd boys to escort Kṛṣṇa to these royal festivities. Flanked by friends and servants, Kṛṣṇa held Balarāma’s hand as He entered this royal assembly. It is described as looking like the moon rising in the sky along with influential planets. Nanda Mahārāja, who was also dressed in royal clothes, was sitting on a large throne. Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma joined him, sitting on either side of him. Then Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma joined their palms and they very humbly offered respects to their elders. The throne itself was surrounded by silk carpets, upon which Kṛṣṇa’s friends took their seats in two groups. Subala, Arjuna, and their fans sat on the right side; Sudāmā, Śrīdāmā, Stoka-kṛṣṇa, and their friends sat on the left. Stout, very strong servants held lamps aloft while the *brāhmaṇas* recited auspicious invocations to inaugurate the evening’s royal entertainment. Row upon row of expert musicians played musical instruments and beat drums, and the townspeople, the Vrajavāsīs, quickly filled the royal court. But no matter how many guests arrived, mysteriously, there was still space to accommodate them all. By Kṛṣṇa’s mystic power there was always space to accommodate everyone who came for the fun pastime. Every guest from his or her particular vantage point could see Kṛṣṇa. With so many devotees gathered together in such a close space and with all of them lovingly chanting Kṛṣṇa’s names, so much bliss was generated that people wondered if entertainment was even necessary to enjoy the evening.

We should be the same. When we are in our temples or our *nāma-haṭṭas* or wherever we are with our friends and devotees chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa, we are perfectly happy. We don’t need any other type of entertainment. Hare Kṛṣṇa. In all seriousness, the residents of Vṛndāvana like to see Kṛṣṇa dressed in simple cowherd clothes. Fun pastimes are there, but they like to see Kṛṣṇa not as God, in awe and reverence; they like to see Kṛṣṇa as an innocent cowherd boy when they can relate to Him as a maid-servant, a friend, or in the mood of Him being their son. We can understand this from Kuntī, who met Kṛṣṇa dressed as a King. When the Vrajavāsīs went to meet Kṛṣṇa at Kurukṣetra and saw Kṛṣṇa as a king, she spoke these famous words, as found in *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*, *Madhya-līlā* Chapter 13, verses 129-131: "My dear Kṛṣṇa, here at Kurukṣetra, You are dressed like a royal prince accompanied by great warriors, but in Vṛndāvana, You appeared just like an ordinary cowherd boy, accompanied only by Your beautiful flute. Here there is not even a drop of the ocean of transcendental happiness that I enjoyed with You in Vṛndāvana. I therefore request You, please come back to Vṛndāvana and enjoy pastimes with me. If You do so, my ambition will be fulfilled." Hare Kṛṣṇa! Perfect way to end the class—words from the mouth of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī.
