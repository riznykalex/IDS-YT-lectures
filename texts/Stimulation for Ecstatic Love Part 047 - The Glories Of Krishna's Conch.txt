""# Stimulation for Ecstatic Love Part 047: The Glories of Kṛṣṇa's Conch

Hi Kṛṣṇa, everyone, welcome back once again to our ongoing series on the glories of our most beloved Śrī Viṣṇu, Kṛṣṇa. So today, we're continuing with our miniseries entitled "Stimulation for Ecstatic Love," and this will be part 47. In today's lecture, we will discuss the glories of Kṛṣṇa’s conch shell as a stimulus for awakening our love for Him. 

As we've mentioned several times, this miniseries is based on Chapter 26 of Śrīla Prabhupāda’s *Nectar of Devotion*. In the introduction to that chapter, Śrīla Prabhupāda specifically mentions Kṛṣṇa’s paraphernalia. He writes, "Some things which give impetus or stimulation to ecstatic love of Kṛṣṇa are His transcendental qualities, His uncommon activities, His smiling features, His apparel and garlands, His flute, His buffalo horn, His leg bells, His conch shell, His footprints, His places of pastimes such as Vṛndāvana, and His favorite plant, Tulasī-devī."

A couple of pages later, Prabhupāda writes, "Kṛṣṇa’s conch shell is known as Pāñcajanya." This Pāñcajanya is also mentioned in the *Bhagavad-gītā*. Kṛṣṇa sounded it before the Battle of Kurukṣetra. It is said that when Lord Kṛṣṇa blows on His transcendental conch shell, the wives of the demons become subject to miscarriages, and the wives of the demigods become blessed with all auspiciousness. In this way, the sound of Kṛṣṇa’s conch shell used to vibrate and circulate all over the world 5,000 years ago. 

So there, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes that Kṛṣṇa’s conch shell is mentioned in the *Bhagavad-gītā*. The exact verse, of course, is known by many devotees; it is Chapter 1, verse 15: "Lord Kṛṣṇa blew His conch shell, called Pāñcajanya; Arjuna blew his, the Devadatta; and Bhīma, the voracious eater and performer of mighty tasks, blew his terrific conch shell, called Pauṇḍra."

Now, it is interesting that ācāryas say that the Pāñcajanya conch is also a close associate of Kṛṣṇa’s lips, but it does not share the same degree of intimacy the flute enjoys with Kṛṣṇa’s lips. The ācāryas say it cannot even be counted in the same category as the flute. The conch enjoys the formal role of declaring Kṛṣṇa’s arrival or His challenge to an opposing enemy. These are considered transcendentally inferior to the flute’s intimate service of conveying messages to the Vraja-gopīs, especially Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. Nevertheless, this conch is very, very dear to the Lord, as we'll see in this lecture today. Due to its fame, sages chant the following mantra for worshiping the sacred conch of Kṛṣṇa. This mantra is found in the *Āgama-śāstras*, and I also found it in ISKCON’s Deity worship manual:

> *oṃ viśvāya namaḥ*

It’s very nice. "I offer my respects to the Pāñcajanya conch of Kṛṣṇa, which appeared from the ocean and which is worshiped by all the demigods." A beautiful mantra, and I’m sure a lot of ISKCON devotees know that mantra because it's meant to be used in Lord Kṛṣṇa’s worship when we're using the conch.

## The Origin of Pāñcajanya

In *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, Canto 10, Chapter 45, there is a very wonderful pastime wherein it’s described how Kṛṣṇa got this conch from the ocean. The pastime begins when Kṛṣṇa was studying with His brother Balarāma in the gurukula of Sāndīpani Muni in Ujjain. After the completion of His education, Kṛṣṇa wanted to offer Sāndīpani Muni some *guru-dakṣiṇā*. Sāndīpani Muni suggested Kṛṣṇa find his lost son. His son was lost, and after searching for a while, Lord Kṛṣṇa found out that his teacher's son was actually captured and abducted by a demon named Pañcajana. This particular demon lived in the sea, under the ocean, inside a conch shell. So, Kṛṣṇa soon entered into a fight with this demon and killed him, and thus He rescued the son of Sāndīpani Muni and He took the conch shell. 

In the purport of *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 10.45.45, it is stated that the conch shell the Lord took from Pañcajana, which is called Pāñcajanya, is the same one He sounded at the beginning of the *Bhagavad-gītā*. Another interesting fact is, according to the ācāryas, Pañcajana had become a demon in a way similar to that of Jaya and Vijaya. In other words, though appearing in the form of a demon, he was actually a great devotee of the Lord.

## Mercy to the Hellish Worlds

In *Gopāla-campū*, Song 2, text number 8, Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī states that after getting the conch from the ocean, Kṛṣṇa, accompanied by Lord Balarāma, actually went to Yamarāja’s abode and blew that conch very loudly. He writes specifically, "Accompanied by His elder brother Balarāma, Kṛṣṇa went to Yamarāja’s abode and suddenly sounded the conch, which was named Pāñcajanya." 

Jīva Gosvāmī describes that particular act of the Lord going to the hellish worlds where Yamarāja rules as an act of extreme mercy. He explains why in text 17. It's really amazing; I’d never read it before. Jīva Gosvāmī describes how, by the sound of the conch, the most sinful people in hell gave up their fear and became happy. The fires of hell became peaceful. On seeing Kṛṣṇa, all the swords became blunt. Having His *darśana*, all the torture machines fell apart upon seeing Kṛṣṇa. The blades in Asi-patra hell broke, the Raurava hell lost its cruelty, the hell called Raurava lost its fearful nature, and Kumbhīpāka hell stopped cooking anyone when Kṛṣṇa arrived. The hell in which sinners are thrown from a mountain gave up its terrible nature; the iron needles used in torture lost their power of stitching. The Vaitaraṇī River, hard to cross, became easy to cross when the Lord of the Universe arrived. Everyone became liberated because their sins had been destroyed. Seeing Kṛṣṇa, they attained the indestructible abode. Going on millions of airplanes, they looked at the lotus-eyed Lord and became freed of all sinful reactions. Oh Sage, when everyone saw the Lord of lords, the form of the Universe, hell became empty.

Wow. I was thinking, next time I hear the sound of the conch, I’m going to remember how purifying it actually is. That's the purpose of these lectures—to appreciate Vraja and everything connected to it.

## The Conch in Vṛndāvana Pastimes

After this, Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī concludes by saying that when Lord Kṛṣṇa returned their son, the guru and his wife became filled with happiness. Of course, Lord Kṛṣṇa’s conch is an eternal part of His paraphernalia, and thus by His mystic powers, we also hear of it earlier in Kṛṣṇa’s Vṛndāvana pastimes—like when He was a young boy—because rescuing the conch shell from the ocean happened actually after Kṛṣṇa had left Vṛndāvana; He was studying with Sāndīpani Muni in Ujjain. But by His mystic powers, because it's an eternal part of His paraphernalia, we also hear of it in His earlier pastimes in Vṛndāvana. 

For example, I was reading in *Ānanda-vṛndāvana-campū*, Chapter 15, by Kavi Karṇapūra; he describes how Kṛṣṇa was worshiped by Pāñcajanya, the conch, during Govardhana-līlā. He writes:

> "Govardhana, with the limbs of his own body, provided an elegant throne made of smooth stones and pearls for Kṛṣṇa. The demigod Varuṇa personally held a fine white umbrella over Kṛṣṇa’s head, which had a fringe of hanging pearls that appeared like falling raindrops. Vāyu, his arm trembling in ecstatic devotion, stood beside the Lord, fanning Him with a cāmara whisk. The full moon assumed the form of a mirror made of jewels. Pāñcajanya, the Lord’s conch shell, sanctified the atmosphere with loud sounds."

This is all happening at Govardhana Hill. The fulgent Sudarśana-cakra expanded as many lamps to illuminate all directions. Kṛṣṇa’s white lotus flower expanded into many white umbrellas to shield the Lord, and Kaumodakī, the Lord’s club, who is honored by His strength, stood like a jeweled pillar for the bathing ceremony.

## Nṛsiṃha and the Vraja-vāsīs

There are other instances in Vṛndāvana as well where the conch appeared in Kṛṣṇa’s līlās. For example, the following statement is found in the *Ratnākara*, written by Śrī Narahari Cakravartī: "Oh, Śrīvāsa, here Kṛṣṇa performed wonderful pastimes. The cowherd-śyāma played Garuḍa, and Lord Govinda, Kṛṣṇa, mounted his shoulders with a four-armed form. Garuḍa and Govinda looked very beautiful; therefore, this place is named Garuḍa-govinda." 

Now, if we analyze that verse, we can understand that the four-armed form mentioned there is Kṛṣṇa’s expansion, Lord Nṛsiṃha. Lord Nṛsiṃha holds four instruments or items in His hands: a lotus, a conch, a disc, and a club. All these four items are actually for the pleasure of His devotees. So in that pastime, Kṛṣṇa manifested the items of Nārāyaṇa, including the conch. As such, that conch is part of a playful pastime of Kṛṣṇa’s Vṛndāvana līlā, and as such, a stimulus for ecstatic love.

One might be surprised to hear that Nārāyaṇa—the Lord of Vaikuṇṭha—appears in Vraja-līlā. But according to our ācāryas, He is very much part of the Vrajavāsīs' lives; as part of social custom, He is the iṣṭa-deva of the Vrajavāsīs, the Lord of lords. Of course, Kṛṣṇa is God—we all know that—and He is the origin of Nārāyaṇa. But as we've explained many times, it’s because *aiśvarya-jñāna* or *yoga-māyā* causes the Vrajavāsīs to forget that Kṛṣṇa is God, so that they can love Kṛṣṇa in an intimate way as no one has ever been loved before—as a friend, as a child, or a lover. That's the special nature of Vṛndāvana. 

I was reading just this morning that on the suggestion of Garga Muni, when Kṛṣṇa appeared, Nanda Mahārāja installed a Deity of Lord Nṛsiṃha for the protection of Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma and his subjects. That Deity is still worshiped to this very day. Nanda Mahārāja also worshiped a Deity of Lord Varāha, whom he placed in the same temple as Lord Nṛsiṃha, and another small Deity of Lord Nārāyaṇa, which is actually no longer worshiped, having been disfigured. But next time you go to Vṛndāvana, you go out to Nanda-grāma, and you go into the palace, you can see these Deities. Lord Nṛsiṃha is there because He was the family Deity of Nanda and Yaśodā as per social custom.

There's even a sweet pastime where Kṛṣṇa Himself mentions Lord Nārāyaṇa as the Vrajavāsīs' worshipable Deity:

Once, while herding cows, the cowherd boys rested in a large circle with Kṛṣṇa in the middle in the shade of a big banyan tree. Their cows were resting peacefully after a long journey from Vṛndāvana village. The boys had been snacking along the way and were worried that whatever was left of their lunch packs would not be enough to satisfy their appetites later. One boy is quoted as saying, "My tiffin is as empty as my stomach." Another boy added, "I have barely enough to wet my appetite, why bother eating at all?" 

Kṛṣṇa said with a smile, "Friends, do not lament. We should always accept whatever we receive as Lord Nārāyaṇa’s mercy. If we do, we will never be disappointed by His grace. A morsel may satisfy any appetite, and without His grace, even a feast will not be enough." 

So pleased by Kṛṣṇa’s words of wisdom, the cowherd boys sat to eat. It’s described that they ate with renewed vigor, only to find that as much as they ate, their tiffins were never emptied. Kṛṣṇa is the master of all mystics. Again and again, it's described that the boys scooped food into their mouths only to see it reappear in their tiffins or lunch bags. When they finished, they still had as much in their tiffins as when they had started. Once again, they praised Him profusely, but Kṛṣṇa simply put His palms together and, turning His lotus eyes to the sky, said, "It’s all the mercy of Lord Nārāyaṇa." 

## The Rāsa Dance and the Power of Love

There is another very famous pastime of the Lord’s conch acting as a stimulus for ecstatic love in relationship to the ultimate Rāsa dance. During this particular Rāsa dance—while Kṛṣṇa and the gopīs were relishing each other's company—it’s all *līlā* that Kṛṣṇa detected some rivalry amongst the gopīs resulting in the neglect of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, even though this whole pastime of Rāsa-līlā, according to the Vrajavāsīs, is really for her pleasure. 

Krishna felt compelled to correct the gopīs' false pride, and mainly to show Rādhārāṇī’s special position amongst all the gopīs. At one point, Kṛṣṇa left the Rāsa dance, and as expected, all the gopīs were grief-stricken. In fact, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī was so overcome by emotion that she actually fainted. One of the girls suggested they go and find Kṛṣṇa and bring Him back because they felt His appearance would surely revive her. 

Meanwhile, Kṛṣṇa was hiding within a grove on the bank of a nearby lake—that lake was soon to be known as Nārāyaṇa-sarovara. Before long, the gopīs arrived and saw Kṛṣṇa silhouetted by the moonlight. They all cried out, "Look, there’s Kṛṣṇa, the son of Nanda Mahārāja!" But as soon as the gopīs started running towards Him, Kṛṣṇa froze in fear—because He had wanted Rādhārāṇī to come alone. So, to conceal Himself from all these gopīs, Kṛṣṇa instantly assumed the four-armed form of Nṛsiṃha. 

When the cowherd girls came into the meadow, they were astonished. Seated before them was Lord Nṛsiṃha in all His Vaikuṇṭha glory, *aiśvarya*. In His hands were the famous club, conch, disc, and lotus. Also around His neck was a Vaijayantī garland—reaching down to His feet. The gopīs whispered politely, "He’s not Kṛṣṇa; He’s the Supreme Personality of Godhead, Nārāyaṇa." As customary, they bowed down and prayed: "Oh Lord Nṛsiṃha, we offer our respectful obeisances unto You. Kindly be merciful to us; give us the company of the son of Mahārāja Nanda and thus vanquish our lamentation."

Actually, the glory of the gopīs' love for Kṛṣṇa was that even with the vision of the four-armed Nārāyaṇa, they couldn't be satisfied; instead, they begged for the boon of achieving Kṛṣṇa’s company. After offering obeisances, the gopīs departed. 

Meanwhile, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī’s personal maidservants had managed to revive her and encouraged her to find Kṛṣṇa. So it was that just after the first group had left, Lord Nārāyaṇa—Rādhārāṇī and her maidservants appeared in that same grove. To joke, Kṛṣṇa kept His four-armed form. When Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī happened upon Him, she was saddened to find Lord Nārāyaṇa, and she could barely mutter, "*oṃ namaḥ nārāyaṇāya*." However, seeing Lord Nārāyaṇa with His club, conch, disc, and lotus increased her feeling to see Kṛṣṇa even more. Thus, the conch, as well as Nārāyaṇa’s other regalia, acted as a stimulus for her ecstatic love.

The ācāryas say that like the other gopīs, seeing Lord Nṛsiṃha did not relieve Rādhārāṇī’s distress, but unlike the other gopīs, Rādhā’s love forced Kṛṣṇa to reveal Himself. They say this is the unmatched glory of Rādhārāṇī’s love. Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī describes this very beautifully in his *Ujjvala-nīlamaṇi*, verse 7: 

> "Prior to the Rāsa dance, Lord Kṛṣṇa hid Himself in a grove just to have fun. When the gopīs came—their eyes resembling those of deer—by His sharp intelligence, He exhibited His beautiful four-armed form to hide Himself. But when Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī came there, Kṛṣṇa could not maintain His four arms in her presence. This is the wonderful glory of her love."

## The Cowherd Boys and Transcendental Camouflage

There is a somewhat similar pastime: once, while playing near Dāna-ghāṭī, Kṛṣṇa hid from His friends. Finally, one group found a peacock feather on the ground and said, "Kṛṣṇa must have danced here!" As the boys hurried around, they closed in on Kṛṣṇa, who resorted to "transcendental camouflage." Beneath a small banyan tree, Kṛṣṇa manifested His form as four-armed Lord Nārāyaṇa, holding His symbols. 

When the boys spotted Kṛṣṇa’s bluish-black effulgence, they all raced to touch Him, but they stopped short. They were struck with wonder to see this towering form of four-armed Lord Nārāyaṇa, who was leaning casually upon His club while holding His conch to His lotus mouth, smiling at them fondly like a benevolent father. The boys spontaneously bowed down to Lord Nārāyaṇa, then sat there, dumbfounded. Touched by the love of His bewildered friends, Kṛṣṇa couldn't restrain Himself; He quickly resumed His original form as a cowherd boy, jumped up, and said, "Surprise!" and ran towards the grazing cows. 

## The Pāṇḍavas and the Conch

Finally, a pastime from *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu*: when the Pāṇḍavas were living in exile in Vṛndāvana, Duryodhana sent Durvāsā Muni to test them. The Pāṇḍavas were very anxious, thinking that Durvāsā Muni could easily bless or curse. Suddenly, they heard a loud noise reverberating in the sky. Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī says, "Just see, on hearing the reverberating sound of Kṛṣṇa’s conch shell—that was the sound of Kṛṣṇa’s conch, Pāñcajanya—the five Pāṇḍavas became like the five-faced one, Shiva." 

Rūpa Gosvāmī clarifies: "They became whitish in complexion like Shiva." He then goes very deep; he says this indicates the *sāttvika-bhāva*, the ecstatic bodily transformation named *vaivarṇya* (paleness of skin). He concludes they became so ecstatic on hearing Kṛṣṇa’s conch that the color of their skin faded, and then they knew nobody could harm them.

## Closing

In closing, I've heard it said by those who visited Vṛndāvana that the inhabitants of Vṛndāvana disdain the conch shell because when blown, it reminds them of Akrūra blowing the conch upon leaving Vṛndāvana with Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma to go to Mathurā. But in my research, I found a more authoritative perspective in *Gopāla-campū* 2.3.30. It concerns a letter that Nanda Mahārāja received from Kṛṣṇa, who was living in Dvārakā. 

Krishna writes, "Oh Father, I do not live in Indraprastha or Dvārakā, but rather in this letter. As I live in this letter, keep it in your pocket in place of My direct association like *vāṇī* instructions." Śrīla Jīva writes that just when the letter was being read, the sound of the Pāñcajanya arose in the direction towards the road to Mathurā. When that sweet sound arose, the Vrajavāsīs all desired to know about it and began shouting, "Victory, victory!" in astonishment. Then, in great bliss, they began running towards the sound. The women, children, and elders all ran with them, guessing that Kṛṣṇa was coming back. Hearing the sound of the conch, they started to become stunned. As they ran forward, Jīva says they saw Garuḍa on Kṛṣṇa’s chariot and could no longer move their feet. They remained standing stunned like trees when they saw the chariot. It ends with the joy of Kṛṣṇa coming back to Vraja.
