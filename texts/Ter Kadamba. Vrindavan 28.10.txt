"We have departed to a place called Ter Kadamba. Everything in this village reminds us that its development stopped approximately 5,000 years ago: the same narrow streets, sewerage that flows outside, and a very simple way of life for the people, combined with a rather elevated way of thinking, because they all live in the place where the mother of Harani appeared. Birds, dogs, and buffaloes—everything that is alive and motionless—lives next to each other.

Kabita Maharaja is peacefully communicating with the babaji, who has just given his blessing. Some people walk around these sacred places in slippers, others barefoot. There are many amazing things here, but unfortunately, our eyes, which are illuminated by our sledges, are not able to see those wonderful wonders that the residents went to see in Vraja-dhāma. Gurudeva does not close the camera. He often stops and films something—something unknown to us, hidden from our eyes—that attracts his attention. Here, for example, is this man carrying a bundle of brushwood, or some architectural patterns on buildings, or simply a peasant woman who came out to see us off with a kind look on our long journey. Well, here is an example of this babaji, who peacefully smokes his peace pipe. Gurudeva films everything that attracts attention in one way or another.

Spiritual teacher. For some, he is simply a spiritual teacher. For some, he is a spiritual father, but for others, he has become simply a physical father. We love our spiritual teachers as our father, but for this man, he became a real father. This is his son. 

For some time, our road will stretch across endless rice fields. Now, having crossed this bridge across the dry riverbed after the rainy season, we will wind our way, as if in a labyrinth, along the narrow dams that separate one rice field from another. The dam is literally 60 cm wide so that water from the reservoir can flow to another. And like equilibrists on a tightrope, we will balance between water, land, and dried rice in order to reach a place called Ter Kadamba. 

Such small villages are very different from large settlements, which are increasingly taking on the appearance of European villages. These small villages are full of clean water bodies, green lush grass, and clean air. It is a pleasure to walk here because your feet step into the soft, flour-like dust of Vṛndāvana. Everything here is amazing, from how these people live to what they use to heat their stoves and cook their food. These are dung cakes, and this is the house in which their hay or straw is stored. People and animals in these places live peacefully next to each other. The most striking thing that first catches your eye when you walk through the village is that there is no grunting, croaking, or honking, which is typical of our villages. It never occurs to people that they can kill animals or birds to use them as food. 

Someone was a little tired, someone had a little prick in their leg, but will this compare with the pleasure of walking on the dust of Vṛndāvana, soft as mother’s hands? Gurudeva takes photographs of the entire parimāṇa of Mahārāja and his son Gaurashakti. He often runs forward to do this. After a forty-minute journey through endless rice fields—tiring the eyes, sharp thorns cutting the hands, thorns pricking the feet—we approach a real paradise in the midst of this vast space. This place is called Ter Kadamba, and now we will dive into this paradise. As far as the eye can see, the column of devotees stretched out; there were 200 people, and the distance that the chains stretched, walking one after another, was about 2–3 km. So, hold your breath, we are diving in.

It is a small but very cozy kuñja. A kuñja is a plant that grows over you like a house. They provide cool shade, and at the foot of these plants, there is enough space for a person to sit down and rest. Here, in this small kuñja, is the place called Ter Kadamba. Here is the temple. The līlā that we will be told about today took place here.

Śrīla Prabhupāda had one close friend named Bābājī. He lived near the present Kṛṣṇa-Balarāma Mandira. If you go towards the parikrama-mārga, he lived in the place where Tamal Krishna Goswami later built his house. They were close friends of Śrīla Prabhupāda. Sometimes they could be seen sitting together, talking and laughing. The topics were all related to Kṛṣṇa consciousness.

Bābājī had a darśana of the mūrtis of Kṛṣṇa-Balarāma. He was a Kṛṣṇa-Balarāma bhakta. He was very kind to us, constantly inviting us to visit. We talked and joked, laughed. He told us different things about Kṛṣṇa consciousness and treated us to wonderful Kṛṣṇa-prasādam from Kṛṣṇa-Balarāma. I remember one day we were sitting with him, and he was describing the beauty of the Vṛndāvana forest, because it is an unusual forest. It consists of *kalpa-vṛkṣa* trees. You can place your head on the branches of this tree and ask it for devotional service. Your wish will be fulfilled.

A lot of poems are dedicated to the glorification of trees, bushes, and plants of Vṛndāvana because they create a wonderful atmosphere for the pastimes of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. Bābājī told us that when he first came to Vṛndāvana in the late twenties or early thirties, the whole of Vṛndāvana was covered with forests. He told us that if they walked for the Govardhana parikrama from the Kṛṣṇa-Balarāma Mandira to Govardhana itself, they might not see the sun at all because everything was covered with forests. There was a very dense crown on top of the forests. Now, if we had to walk under the scorching sun, it would be very difficult because there are practically no trees left. Unfortunately, all these trees are cut down and the mountains and rocks are blasted to make gravel for roads to make money. But if these people, instead of trying to make money this way, had preserved this amazing wonderland of Vṛndāvana in its original form, they could then preach the glories of Vṛndāvana throughout the world, and millions of tourists would come here for spiritual tourism. They wouldn’t have to worry about making money at all.

Bābājī also told us in the evening, when we were taking milk, about the garden—and he was putting his deities to bed—that in the late twenties, when he came here to Ramaṇa-reti, where the Kṛṣṇa-Balarāma Mandira is now, there were such jungles that there were even tigers there. Those tigers lived in the huts, and they would come and attack the huts of the milkmen. He also said that when the sadhus were sitting in these jungles with the tigers, sometimes they were attacked. What method did they come up with against the tigers? They placed large vats of hot, warm, sweet milk next to their huts, so that the tigers would come, drink this milk, and leave satisfied.

Vṛndāvana consists of twelve main forests, such as Vṛndāvana, Bāhula-vana, Mahā-vana, and so on. *Vana* translates as forest. Here we are in one of the original forests of Vṛndāvana, and from the name of this place it is clear what kind of trees grow here: *Tera Kadamba*. Kadamba trees grow here. This is one of the places where Kṛṣṇa plays with the calves and the cowherd boys. In different places of Vṛndāvana, Kṛṣṇa performs different pastimes with the devotees.

Vṛndāvana had its own places for Kṛṣṇa to play with his friends, the cowherd boys, to associate with his parents Nanda Mahārāja and Yaśodā, and to meet and dance with the gopīs. In a few minutes, Kṛṣṇa would come out of the forest and he would herd his cows. We see in *Kṛṣṇa Book* that as they left the forest, the cows would be jumping and the boys would be jumping. In a short time, we will continue our journey and go to Nandagrāma. As Mahārāja explained to us, Kṛṣṇa lived there, and every day he would leave Nandagrāma to graze the cows and calves together with his friends, the cowherd boys. They all played, jumped, and even the cows danced in ecstasy. They had fun, stealing bundles of money from each other and so on. All this happened here, on this road we were walking along. The boys played their games surrounded by cows, and in these games, neither Nanda Mahārāja nor Yaśodā participated, because they did not fit into the children's games. Boys have their own fun.

Once, Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura described how Mother Yaśodā wanted to follow Kṛṣṇa into the forest to help him herd the cows because she could not bear the separation that Mahārāja has so beautifully described. Balarāma began to dissuade Mother Yaśodā. He said, "Well, you better stay at home and prepare some kind of prasādam for our return; we will go graze the cows." But she continued to insist. Finally, Balarāma had to say sternly, "No, you will not go. This is only for boys."

A fortuitous scene when Kṛṣṇa left Vṛndāvana to go to the forest to save the cows, because it was very difficult, Mahārāja said, to endure separation from Kṛṣṇa. Every moment feels like at least twelve years. When Kṛṣṇa would go into the forest, the whole town of Vṛndāvana came out to see him off. The elderly gopas and gopīs, and small children who could not participate in these games, would walk to the very beginning of the forest. They looked for a long time at Kṛṣṇa going into the forest because they understood that they would not be able to see him all day. They felt a very intense separation all day long. But we feel—if we can feel—such separation even for a moment, this is how one can judge the level of one’s advancement in Kṛṣṇa consciousness: how much we miss Kṛṣṇa. 

It is very difficult, of course, for us to understand this, but Rūpa Gosvāmī gives a material analogy to make our understanding easier. Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura describes the material world as a dictionary through which we can understand what is happening in the spiritual world. In the dictionary, we can read the definition of a "horse." This is not the definition of a horse; it simply describes an animal with four legs that jumps, and so on. We get some vague idea of what a horse is. No material analogy can perfectly explain a transcendental phenomenon, but it helps. 

So Rūpa Gosvāmī describes what this *lobha* is. This is the greedy desire for Kṛṣṇa that the Vrajavāsīs have. How can he explain this to us? He gives an analogy: just as boys are attracted to girls and girls are attracted to boys, let me have the same strength, but in a transcendental way, for your lotus feet. Attraction to the opposite sex is the most powerful force in this world. And for us, for transcendentalists, this is a very big obstacle in the development of our love for Kṛṣṇa. Of course, material attachments and transcendental attachments are completely opposite to each other, but this analogy helps us understand that with the same force, multiplied by hundreds, the Vrajavāsīs are attached to Kṛṣṇa.
