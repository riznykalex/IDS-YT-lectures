For example, I need an example here, because this is a complex philosophical thing, and sometimes for these complex philosophical ideas, we need an analogy to understand it. One time, Madhumaṅgala, a brāhmaṇa boy—actually, the son of Sandīpani Muni—said to Kṛṣṇa... you know, he’s Kṛṣṇa’s funny brāhmaṇa friend, always telling jokes, you know? He said, "Kṛṣṇa, you’re a Vaiśya, you take care of cows, but I’m a brāhmaṇa. You should show some respect to me!" 

So Kṛṣṇa said, "Well, you see, Madhumaṅgala, more important than birth are one’s deeds, one’s activities. So you look at my activity, Madhumaṅgala; they are simply extraordinary. You must recognize I’m a very great personality." So, Madhumaṅgala laughed, and he said, "Kṛṣṇa, just see how conceited you’ve become!" This is Vraja-līlā. There’s... we don’t discuss this in public, but this is Kṛṣṇa becomes subservient to the love of his devotees. So Madhumaṅgala laughed, he said, "Kṛṣṇa, you’re becoming very conceited!" So Kṛṣṇa said, "No, Madhumaṅgala, it’s true! I have done so many great things. When I was 7 days old, Pūtanā came to kill me, but I killed her. Then I killed Tṛṇāvarta, and you saw how I subdued Kāliya serpent, and when I was 7 years old, I lifted Govardhana Hill. Madhumaṅgala, these are my super-excellent activities. You must recognize me from my activities." 

So in joking fun, Madhumaṅgala said, "Kṛṣṇa, as far as Pūtanā is concerned, it is said that the demigods protect children and old people, so we all know it was the demigods who actually killed Pūtanā, and now you want to take credit for that? Kṛṣṇa, as far as lifting Govardhana Hill, Nanda Mahārāja, Balarāma, all the cowherd men, they were propping up Govardhana Hill with their sticks! We all saw it, and you want to take credit? And as far as subduing Kāliya is concerned, you were finished! You were practically dead. Everyone saw your bluish complexion became even darker due to his poison, and you were trapped in his coils. Seeing you, Nanda Mahārāja and Yaśodā, they fainted. So seeing that, I, the brāhmaṇa, took pity on them and I chanted a mantra to subdue a serpent. There’s a mantra for subduing serpents. As a result, Kāliya became so frightened that he released you from his coils. Then you started jumping on his head and dancing as if you’d done a great job!" 

So Kṛṣṇa said, "Madhumaṅgala, I see. Please accept my humble obeisances!" This is Vraja-bhakti. So this special love in... well, servitorship, friendship, parenthood, conjugal love—this is the love of the Vrajavāsīs, and this is our goal. This is our *paramā*. And this mantra, *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Hare, Hare*, chanted properly, we can attain that supreme destiny. We have to discuss these things because sometimes we become a little familiar with the process. Therefore, Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura says from time to time a devotee has to go away to a secluded place without any other responsibilities for the time being, any other distraction, and simply focus on chanting *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa...* 

And as an example, Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, who is the great pioneer of this Kṛṣṇa conscious movement in the modern age, the last three years of his life he lived in samādhi. He stayed in a hut and he chanted day and night the Hare Kṛṣṇa mahā-mantra. But of course, our pioneering Kṛṣṇa consciousness in Western countries, there was a sense of urgency there, so he preached to his last dying breath, and those words are there in the Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam. We thank the Lord that he did that. His last breath gave us instructions, special mercy. 

### The Ten Offenses

So, Śrīla Prabhupāda stressed that proper chanting means to avoid the 10 offenses:
1. To blaspheme, criticize devotees who have dedicated their lives for preaching the glories of the Lord.
2. To consider the names of the demigods to be equal or superior to the name of Kṛṣṇa.
3. To disobey the instructions of the spiritual master.
4. To blaspheme Vedic literatures.
5. To interpret the holy names.
6. To consider the glories of chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa as imagination. No, imagination! It’s all true. 

One time, one of my god-sisters, she said to Śrīla Prabhupāda early in the movement, "Śrīla Prabhupāda, is it true that God Almighty is a cowherd boy? He’s blue of all colors, he wears a Vaijayantī garland that remains ever fresh, he plays a flute, he goes barefoot, he wrestles in the sand with his friends, and he dances with young girls in the autumn moonlight, and he killed monsters? Is this true?" So Prabhupāda closed his eyes and he thought, opened his eyes, he said, "My dear daughter, spiritual daughter, do you think I would cheat you?" *Hare Kṛṣṇa!* "Do you think I would cheat you?" No. Whatever Prabhupāda said, it’s as good as Vedas. We have the Vedic scriptures that Prabhupāda has mentioned several times—what comes from the mouth of the pure devotees is as good as the Vedas, even better in the sense that through the mouths of these pure devotees, the six Gosvāmīs, Viśvanātha Cakravartī, we’re getting a very intimate look into the spiritual world. 

And that look of the spiritual world is what attracts us away from the look of this material world. Cāṇakya Paṇḍita says you can only give up something if you get something better. So, bring Kṛṣṇa. If you’ve got time, Kṛṣṇa—read the Bhāgavatam, read the Gītā. Read what is the path back home. And by constant hearing, we lose our attachment to this material world.
7. To commit sin on the strength of chanting—another offense.
8. To consider Hare Kṛṣṇa to be like Vedic rituals.
9. To teach, or rather to explain—like we’re speaking today—intimate glories of the Lord to non-devotees.
10. And to remain attached to the material concept of life even after so many years of chanting. 

This is the greatest embarrassment: to maintain attraction for this material world when we’re constantly in touch with the all-attractive. Prabhupāda gave many definitions—"Elegance is truth spoken concisely"—he gave many definitions of the Absolute Truth, Bhagavān Śrī Kṛṣṇa. He said, "Kṛṣṇa means the all-attractive." So, this is not all-attractive; this is *duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam*—what we’re looking at in this world, temporary, full of suffering—yet we may be attached to it. What is this? "My dear Lord, I’m drowning in this fathomless whirlpool of material existence. Oh you, who give shelter to the shelterless, Rūpa says, 'Please just this one time, extend your hand to save me.'" So, what does that mean? The long arm of the law? Kṛṣṇa reaches out? No. He sends Guru, he sends his representative, and by serving him faithfully over many decades or many lifetimes as it may be, we’re blessed. 

What is our *sādhanā*? We’re struggling *sādhakas*, at least I am. How do we make progress? As Shakespeare says, "It droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven upon the place beneath." It’s all the mercy of Śrīla Prabhupāda. We’re simply his instruments. But Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī, he also gives an 11th offense. Those were 10 offenses, which interestingly enough he considers to be the most serious. And what is that? To become inattentive while chanting. Why? Because if you’re inattentive while chanting, you’ll naturally, or unnaturally, you’ll commit the other 10 offenses. If you’re not careful, by being inattentive, you may commit the other offenses. So to be attentive is so important in chanting *Hare Kṛṣṇa*. Focus, *ekāgra-manā*, focused on Kṛṣṇa. Thinking... no. In the beginning, we just hear the sound vibration, *Kṛṣṇa*. 

Prabhupāda said, in metaphor, he’s dancing on the tip of your tongue. He’s directly there in the form of his holy name. And in the *Bhāgavatam*, he said when you’re chanting *Hare Kṛṣṇa*, it includes all of the Lord’s pastimes. We’ve been lecturing for four years on the glories of Vṛndāvana in our Vṛndāvana lecture series—the glories of Vṛndāvana—so many hundreds of hours. It’s all there in the mantra, *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa...* Just have to hear the mantra. Sufficient, focused. Don’t let the mind wander. Arjuna, in the humble way, "My mind’s wandering." Kṛṣṇa said, "Bring it back and fix it on the self, on the Supreme Self, Kṛṣṇa." It’s so important. 

And in the 11th Canto, there’s a story of the Avadhūta brāhmaṇa who lived in the forest, and one day the king of the land, Mahārāja Yadu, was on an expedition and he passed by. So Mahārāja Yadu was so impressed with this Avadhūta brāhmaṇa and he began inquiring from him about spiritual life. And one of his questions related to focusing in one’s spiritual practices. Again, we know how the mind wanders, so the Avadhūta brāhmaṇa, this sādhu, recounted to Mahārāja Yadu what he learned—how to focus—from an arrow maker. How do you... you know, his guru is teaching him, "Focus your mind," so the guru gives a story of an arrow maker to teach him how to focus his mind. 

We know arrow makers in the old days, they would make arrows by hand. They would take long slivers of wood and they would spend hours sanding them down and carving them, and that wood had to be perfectly straight; otherwise the arrow would go off course, it wouldn’t pierce the heart of the enemy. So one day when this arrow maker he’s talking about was absorbed in his trade, he was sitting in his little shop, you know, making sure the arrows, the wood is perfectly straight and the head’s perfectly there, when suddenly the king of the land came through town. And this king was coming through the town with elephants and jugglers and singers and dancers all along the road. "Vivā, vivā!" Long live the king! Everyone’s—bugles were playing, drums were beating, and flowers were being strewn from the windows on the king. 

And afterwards, a friend came to the arrow maker’s little store and he asked, "How did you like that parade? Did you... did you see him?" The arrow maker said, "Who?" Friend said, "The king! Did you see him? The king was here, he came to town right past your store!" Arrow maker said, "I didn’t see him. I didn’t see anything. I only saw my arrows. I was working on them to make them perfectly straight." Focused. All that big commotion going around. So we know the nature of living in a big city like London, even Watford, there’s a big mess out there. As soon as you get out of Watford or the temple here, you’re driving, there’s traffic, there’s so much distraction. So like the arrow maker, we have to be oblivious to it when we’re chanting *Hare Kṛṣṇa*. 

We’re in the kingdom of God! We want to be in the kingdom of God. When you’re chanting *Hare Kṛṣṇa*, you’re in the kingdom of God, because Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura said the mantra is there, it’s not different than Kṛṣṇa, but it contains all of Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes. Just leave the world behind. Like Prabhupāda told my dear god-brother, Sat-svarūpa Mahārāja, when he gave him the *daṇḍa* at his *sannyāsa* initiation, "Sat-svarūpa Mahārāja, take this daṇḍa, never look back and think you’ve left anything valuable behind, and never envy the position of the materialistic sense-enjoyers." So we have to be like that when we’re chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa. There may be some... obviously there’s some attachments, because we’re not crying when we’re chanting, but we forget it all, this world, and we just chant, *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa...* 

It takes practice. Practice makes perfect. We’re practitioners, we’re *sādhakas*. Prabhupāda said one time, in the mood of humility to teach us, he said it took him 30 years to chant *japa* properly. Don’t misunderstand that! Another time, Prabhupāda said, "In my lifetime, I never knew what was sinful activity. I never forgot the lotus feet of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa." So like Mahāprabhu came in the mood of a devotee, Prabhupāda, to teach us—he said it took him 30 years because... why? Because it’s difficult to focus. I was reading an article that the average time of focus nowadays in America—for Americans—to focus, they can only focus for 7 seconds. There’s a study done and then the mind goes elsewhere. Why? Because the mind gets conditioned by these electronic devices that we have, from television to internet to iPhones. When you chant, drop it! Throw away that iPhone for two hours. If you keep it near you, that’s what Arjuna was complaining about. 

Iphones, people, the television, the news; it’s all so quick, the scenes are popping back and forth, people get bored, they change the channel many times. So in that sense, we are products of this age and therefore we have to practice being attentive, being devotees. We have to pray to Kṛṣṇa to help us. If we want to be successful, we don’t want to be guilty of the 11th offense. And then in due course of time, as Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī said, he feels it’s possible we can all go back home, back to Godhead, because we have Caitanya Mahāprabhu. He’s the incarnation for this age. Prabhupāda said when many instructions are given, the last instructions is the most important. So many incarnations have come, with all due respect, the Daśāvatāras, and they’ve given their instructions, they’ve reestablished dharma at the time of their appearance. But this is the age of Kali and this is the age when the most merciful incarnation appears. Mahāprabhu has one thing to say to us, quoting *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*: 

*Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Hare, Hare*. Stick to the straight and narrow. The word "disciple" comes from the word "discipline." Stick to the straight and narrow, chant, and we have every opportunity to go back home, back to Godhead. And if our spiritual masters see that we are sincere in that chanting, one day they will speak to us the confidential secret. The Vedas say that to a loyal disciple, one may speak the confidential secret. Therefore, oh most fortunate one, now please hear the glories of Goloka. All glories to Śrīla Prabhupāda. Thank you very much, Hare Kṛṣṇa.""
