"### Vṛndāvana Public Program

This is the sign of a monk in India. I gave up my gun for the *danda*—yeah, it's much more effective. Just after my tour in the military, I actually lost a lot of good friends. Half my high school football team joined; you know, we were very patriotic, we were hippies, and then we changed—we became—that’s a long story. But, you know, I was the left end, my best friend was the quarterback, my second-best friend was the right end, and like that, we were five. They kept me back at Camp Pendleton for some time for special operations training, underwater demolition, frogman. So my unit shipped out to Vietnam, and within one week they were ambushed by the Viet Cong and they were all killed, passed away. And that had a profound effect on my view of life.

So, as soon as I got out of the military, I began a spiritual search—a spiritual seeker, like I think many of you are also spiritual seekers. I read, I reread the Bible because I’m from a Roman Catholic family, so I read the Bible, and I read the Quran—was very helpful for me nowadays because I travel a lot in Islamic countries. I read the Torah, and I used to go to the synagogue and the priest would help me, would look at me—"not Jewish, are you?"—but I’m interested. I read the Lotus Sutras of Buddha and then I read *Bhagavad-gītā*. As Philip mentions in his writings, the *Gītā* is really the core, the summary of the Vedic teachings. They’re very vast, and we couldn’t actually read all of them in three lifetimes—maybe, yeah, maybe you’re going to do that. 

I was very touched by the philosophy; I guess you could say perfect questions, perfect answers, and all the things I was looking for. The answers to my questions about my friends were answered. So I decided to come to India, to the place where the *Gītā* was spoken, and I heard people live the *Gītā*. As a young man, I traveled here, and I hadn't made a firm decision which path I would follow. But I traveled the length and breadth of this great country. Actually, in the original scriptures, it’s called Bhārata. Nowadays we call it India. Bhārata means—well, in Sanskrit it can mean different things, but one meaning is "light," and this is the land of light. Not that it’s a tropical country, but light in the sense of knowledge, like it lights up the darkness of ignorance.

So, I traveled from the north to the south, in the east, in the west, and it was very interesting because I was investigating all these various paths and spiritual traditions. And the first thing I noted is that everybody accepted everybody else; people took their chosen path, but they were not disrespectful to other paths. And I thought, "That’s really unique; India is a really tolerant country." I experimented with everything; I was in the Himalayas doing yoga, and I was in the south doing various rituals. To make a long story short, I chose the path of *bhakti*, *bhakti-yoga*, as you know here. Many people have chosen that path of devotional service to Kṛṣṇa, the path of love and devotion. 

Therefore, through the ages—because this is 5,000 years ago—although this has remained a very simple village like Kṛṣṇa, when Kṛṣṇa came here, it’s a very popular destination for *ṛṣis*, *yogīs*, and saints and so forth. As a result, there are 5,000 to 6,000 temples in this village where they have the icon or they have the deity. You’ve seen the deity in the temples; deity worship is very prominent in India here. They have 5,000 temples, and in the last 40 years, I’m trying to visit them all, but some of them are little shrines inside people’s houses. I came to the understanding that everything in Vṛndāvana centers around Kṛṣṇa. All the people do is an act of devotion to the Lord; their family life, their work, their leisure, it’s all centered around Kṛṣṇa. So it’s a very wholesome community. 

One *ṛṣi* said: if you take rocks and throw them in a lake, the waves come out. But because people have thrown them in different places, they clash. But everyone throws the pebble in the same spot—it’s concentric, it just comes out, there’s no friction. So here, everyone’s interest is reviving their dormant relationship with the Supreme. They do it in many ways, as I said, but the main way—which I found really attractive, 'cause when I was young I liked to sing, dance, eat, and discuss philosophy—is what I found here. The spiritual practices here were much different than other places I’d been. I was never really that good at yoga; I just couldn’t get it right, couldn’t sit there. And the ritualistic performances in the south, it was tedious for my mind. But I came here, everyone’s singing and dancing, and I say, "What’s this? This is not spiritual life?" "Oh yes, this is it."

In this ancient book—it’s kind of like what you read after the *Bhagavad-gītā*—it’s actually described that in this age in which we live now, which we’re so proud of regarding our scientific advancement in technology, the *avatāra* of the Supreme comes in the form of a personality called Lord Caitanya. He’s actually Kṛṣṇa, but He comes kind of in a disguised form to spread what’s called the *yuga-dharma*. *Dharma* means duty; the ultimate duty really in life is to become self-realized, because in the end, that’s all you’ve got. You have to leave everything behind. But if you’re self-realized, at least next lifetime you start where you left off. If you make 54% advancement in your chosen path, then next lifetime you start out at 55%. So it’s a good investment.

So in every age, there is a technique, a process which is meant to be universally undertaken. And in this age, the scriptures describe it’s singing and dancing, transcendental sound vibrations. Like, we call them Holy Names, names of Kṛṣṇa. He’s all-powerful, so He’s present in the sound of His name. And He’s very pure, so if you associate with someone really pure, you also become pure. If you associate with a businessman, you can become like this; if you associate with an athlete, you start lifting weights. That’s what I mean by association. So if we can associate with the Supreme, although He’s very far away, in the form of sound, very quickly all the bad things in the heart go away—the lust, anger, greed—these obstacles in our path. And the real beauty of the soul becomes manifest, like a lotus flower; when the light’s there in the morning, the lotus flower begins to open.

This light, this practice of chanting, singing mantras, it’s really simple. You can do it in a shower—are you practicing yoga? Yes, in the shower, I’m chanting mantras, I’m singing. There are many mantras, many different mantras, but you know, we’re a little biased. There’s what we call the *Mahā-mantra*. *Mahā* means "great," *man* means "mind," *tra* means "to deliver." So there’s a mantra, the *Mahā-mantra*. The effects you get from all those other mantras are all included in one sound. And being American, I’m sure you know better than me: *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare / Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma, Rāma Rāma, Hare Hare*. Very, very powerful. 

Even outside our movement now, as I’m sure Philip will remark in his future writings, or maybe he’s already remarked, the chanting of *Hare Kṛṣṇa* is becoming very popular. You know, we started it, but it’s not ours. No one has a monopoly on the truth; the truth’s unlimited and you can learn from many seekers, many *yogīs*, many masters. This is actually the theme of your tour, where you meet many saintly personalities and you can learn a little something and become more spiritual. So the chanting has gone beyond our particular movement, which popularized it in the '60s, and I think that’s good because it is a universal process. 

We don’t have much time—we’re all busy working or we’re busy in our retirement. I’m really happy to see most of you are my age. Usually, you know, I do this often, the yoga groups come here, we want to give them the Vṛndāvana experience, and they’re all just kids. I’m trying to explain some philosophical things, and they’re falling asleep. You know, as you get older, these things are more meaningful to you. You only have so much time left and you realize that, "Okay, I’ve been a success in this world, but something’s missing. Something in my heart’s not fully satisfied, so let me do a spiritual search." So it’s really satisfying that—you know, I won’t ask you how old you are, especially the ladies—but we’re senior citizens. I went to America and I went to some theme park with some other people there; you know, it wasn’t really my interest, but they’re old friends. I got to the gate and the guy said, "Okay, you only have to pay $5 because you’re a senior citizen." I said, "Wait a minute, it says in your passport you’re 66." "Yeah, but you know."

There’s a good thing about old age; you know, all those things that we were so attached to, that were so important to us, all the dreams which didn’t manifest, kind of lose a little faith in the material existence and we look for something higher. So it’s good you’ve come in, because it’s like a treasure chest of spiritual paths, spiritual wisdom, spiritual personalities, and we wish you the best on your journey. You have a very good guide. I’ve been reading, and I now I’m blessed to have the *American Veda*. Philip, there’s a Russian Veda, you may not know. Yeah, there’s a Russian Veda. I did a program at the University of Yekaterinburg and one professor came up to me—*Nirvana* is the original culture coming from India, these scriptures are thousands of years old—he came and said, "The original culture comes from Russia." Really? He said, "Here’s our Russian Veda." Really? And I looked through it; there were many similarities. Of course, we would say that’s speculation, but we’ll leave it up to you to decide.

But yes, this is a very good book because it shows how powerful this country is in a spiritual way. As the West is meant to lead the world in science and technology—which is valuable, 'cause I won't get a hoarse voice because I have a microphone and many other things, I’m flying airplanes—India is meant to lead the world in spiritual enlightenment, spiritual wisdom. And the two can work together. Our spiritual master gave the analogy of the blind man and the crippled man; they’re both handicapped, but if they work together we can move forward. Like the blind man can put the crippled man on his shoulders, the crippled man can say, "Go left, go right." Like that, so they both make progress. Prabhupāda said like this; it’s a pretty bold statement. He said America’s like the blind man spiritually, in the sense that you know, not understanding some of the deep concepts we find in the Vedas, and India’s like the crippled man—it’s getting better, the economy—but you’ll see as you travel around. 

So if India and America work together to give this divine knowledge, this complete knowledge to the world, not only America can become a better place—as Philip has pointed out numerous times—the whole world can benefit from this culture. So today we want to give you the Vṛndāvana experience. I’ll try to summarize through your stay with us what I’ve experienced in the last 40 years. I don’t live here all the time; they won’t give me a visa. I tried to become an Indian citizen, the guy said, "Are you crazy? You want to give up an American passport for this?" I said, "But my heart’s here." He said, "Sorry." 

My field of activity is the Amazon jungle; I go there on the boats and we go through little rivulets and the jungle area, and we teach *bhakti-yoga* to the Amazon Indians. Believe me, that is an experience! You come around the turn, you see these little—I also teach in Russia. Since the Cold War, when I was a young man, I was going in in a three-piece suit with a wig and a mustache and an attaché case of bourbon. "Hey, you’re a phony!" "No, we don’t take intoxication." Because whenever they would check me, they would never think I was a religious person, 'cause in those days you got 20 years in Siberia. I had friends who were Lutheran and Christian Scientists and Catholics my age who were in this prison in Siberia. So you had to be really careful in how you did it. Nowadays, of course, we’re doing like this, but there’s maybe 20 or 30,000 people who have accepted this path of *bhakti-yoga*. I also go to China and I go to Mongolia. Mongolia, if any of you have time, we can sit and talk about Mongolia—what a culture! Going to Mongolia is like going—that culture is still very much like it was hundreds and hundreds of years ago. The people are wonderful, they’re Buddhists, and we shared our beliefs and each of us benefited. 

But four or five months of the year, now I come here. This is my retirement. Not playing bingo and dominoes and fishing. In my retirement, I’m using my internal energy as hard as I was working with my physical body when I was young, because it’s true, we only have so much time left and we want to achieve this goal as well: spiritual enlightenment. So the experience today will begin with the chanting, because that’s the main process here. To see people singing—and there, do they have these little bags? Yeah, where’s the bead bag? That bead bag, as you walk around Vṛndāvana, you won’t find this anywhere else in India; just here in Vṛndāvana, you’ll see people like this. And sometimes I’m in America walking like this, "Hey, what’s wrong with your hand?" "Meditation." This is a really good meditation because sometimes when you meditate, your mind wanders, but if the sound is going on, it’s much easier to focus. The mantra chanted out, it’s easier to keep the mind focused. So the same mantra, I’ve got it all. 

We have beads here for everybody, and different bags, and it’s up to you to choose your favorite color. We also have books at the end, and this is something really special. Our first class, kind of let your appetite, there’s a lot to experience. Everyone’s going to give a very beautiful chant. Singing—he knows him very well—he’s a devotee, my same master, he’s very learned, he wrote many books about life, etc. A little bit later this is Brother Har..., and his group. They’ve been together for years. Our good luck today is that he’s one of the best singers in our movement; he’s a beautiful singer. The main thing is not so much what activity we do, but the mood. I don’t want to embarrass him, but you’ll hear the *kīrtana* and our other, so we can move on. 

I think you know the mantra, right? You can repeat it: *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare / Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma, Rāma Rāma, Hare Hare*. At the temple we begin with the experience. It’s going to last about two hours, like a roller coaster ride, and finish, you’re going to go "Wow," at least I hope, right? So, this is a two-headed drum; this is *mṛdaṅga* drum, it’s typical to the eastern part of India and Bengal. This drum—we have *karatālas* which are symbols that keep time, and this is a harmonium. See, it’s a very popular—like a seat—it’s a very popular instrument in India. But interestingly enough, it doesn’t come from here; it’s a small chair. So when the French came here, they brought the harmonium, yeah, because it’s so cool and so mellow and so loving an instrument. That sing-along often happens, at least with the younger generation, as they dance. I’m looking at you, like I look at me, I have a hard time going up those, so I don’t feel you have to dance, but you can move, and if you want to dance, it’s called *līlā-nṛtya*. And then something really special we have—there’s different types of classic dance in this country. *Kathak*, we have the top *kathak* dancer in the country here today; that’s saying a lot. Very popular. And the amazing thing is, she’s not Indian, she’s Russian. Russians are very kind people, artist, music. She came here when she was four, she’s 23 now, studying with the master here.
