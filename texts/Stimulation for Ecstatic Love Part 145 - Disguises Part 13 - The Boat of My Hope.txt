### Stimulation for Ecstatic Love: Part 145 – Disguises (Part 13: The Boat of My Hope)

Hare Kṛṣṇa everyone, and welcome back once again to our ongoing series on the glories of our most beloved *nīm viṣṇu-pādāya kṛṣṇa-preṣṭhāya bhūtale śrīmate bhaktivedānta-svāmin iti nāmine namaste sārasvate deve gaura-vāṇī-pracāriṇe*. So, this morning I was reading *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, and I came across a purport by Śrīla Prabhupāda that nicely summarizes the purpose of our lectures about Vṛndāvana that have been going on, I think, close to five years now. How, in essence, we are trying to connect with Kṛṣṇa through *śravaṇam kīrtanam*—through hearing and chanting. Prabhupāda writes:

> "There are still many devotees of the Lord lingering there in Vṛndāvana in ecstasy in search of Kṛṣṇa and His childhood associates, the gopīs. It is not that such devotees meet Kṛṣṇa face-to-face in that attractive land. But a devotee’s eagerly searching after Kṛṣṇa is as good as seeing Him personally. How this is so cannot be explained. But it is factually realized by those who are pure devotees of the Lord. Philosophically, one can understand that Lord Kṛṣṇa and His remembrance are on the absolute plane and that very idea of searching for Him at Vṛndāvana in pure God consciousness gives more pleasure to the devotee than seeing Him face-to-face. Such devotees of the Lord see Him face-to-face at every moment."

Right, Kṛṣṇa. So, we are continuing with our series on Stimulation for Ecstatic Love, and this will be Part 145, in which we will continue our discussions on the art of disguises in Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa’s Vṛndāvana Vraja-līlā, their pastimes. Now, to begin with, we know—we all know—that although Kṛṣṇa is the Supreme Personality of Godhead, He relishes being subordinate to His devotees, that’s love—especially Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, the topmost devotee. Sometimes in her loving dealings with Kṛṣṇa, she manifests, as we’ve heard, transcendental anger (*māna*) towards Him and refuses to see Him. But Kṛṣṇa actually relishes this anger of Rādhārāṇī and becomes overwhelmed with ecstasy. But each time, He has to devise some method to appease Rādhārāṇī’s temper or anger, and this pastime is known universally as *māna-bhañjana-līlā*—the pastime of breaking Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī’s anger.

And we’ve shared with you a number of pastimes in that regard. We’re going to do it again because this pastime happens over and over again in Vṛndāvana. Regarding such pastimes, Śrīla Prabhupāda said in a lecture on *Nectar of Devotion* in Bombay, January 1973, referring to this mood of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī: "Kṛṣṇa wants to be controlled by the *gopīs*. He said Kṛṣṇa wants to be defeated by His friends. Kṛṣṇa wants to be refused Rādhārāṇī’s *darśana*. When Rādhārāṇī is angry, she refuses to allow Kṛṣṇa to see her. She has ordered the sakhīs, 'Don’t allow Kṛṣṇa to come here.' Then Kṛṣṇa flatters the sakhīs, saying, 'Kindly let me go.' But they reply, 'No sir, you cannot go. You cannot see her.'"

So, in this mood, the following *līlā* is adapted from a series of songs that have been passed down by the Giri family of Gada-giri in Orissa for hundreds of years. It was written by two medieval Gauḍīya poets, Śrī Canda and Ghanadās. And the pastime describes in a wonderful way in which Kṛṣṇa once broke Rādhārāṇī’s angry mood. In Orissa and Bengal, this song, I heard, is sung on festival occasions down to this very day. It’s still very popular after hundreds of years, and I really like it. It’s one of my favorite of all-time pastimes. And you’ll see, it’s quite long, so get ready, take your seats; it’s quite long, but thankfully so.

So, one day in Vṛndāvana, Kṛṣṇa was late for a rendezvous in the forest with Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. Distressed, Rādhārāṇī turned to Lalitā Sakhī and said—the way they expressed themselves in this past life is amazing—"Oh my dear Lalitā, where is the friend of my heart? Please show me His moonlike face. My dear Sakhī, where did Kṛṣṇa go? I have decorated this kuñja for Him. He invited me to Kuñja-vana, the forest grove. As such, my caste and prestige are now gone. Because of Śyāma, it is declared in all the villages of Vraja that Rādhā is unchaste. Tolerating infamy and reproach for the sake of Śyāma, my golden body is becoming crushed. Where is that holder of the flute? O Sakhī, has He given you any message or signal? Oh, I made and carefully folded a new outfit for Him out of unfading cloth. I strung together garlands of *campaka* and *nāgakeśara* flowers for Him. All of these things have become useless and withered, for He has not come. Struck by the five arrows of Cupid, the five airs of my life are going to leave. Oh my dear friend, what has that *vañcaka* Kṛṣṇa, who knows my innermost desire, not done to me? Who was that cruel girl in Vraja who was hiding that holder of the flute?"

Understanding the situation, beautiful Lalitā consoled Vinodinī Rādhā and went out to search for Mādhava. She looked for Vanamālī Kṛṣṇa in every gopī’s kuñja. At last, she came to Candrāvalī’s kuñja. There she found Vanamālī, Kṛṣṇa maddened after love for Candrāvalī. Upset, she took a *ketakī* flower leaf from the decoration in her hair, wrote a letter on that leaf, and threw it on the road where Śyāma would soon depart from the kuñja. Full of anger, Lalitā then came back to Rādhikā and said, "Oh my dear friend, what shall I tell you? Candrāvalī has bound up Muralīdhara, that *lampaṭa nāgara*, that womanizer." The poet Śrī Canda says, "O Kṛṣṇa, Lord Hari has rejected you."

The next morning, Kṛṣṇa woke up, and while on a stroll, He found that *ketakī* leaf letter. Wow. A *ketakī* leaf letter. He picked it up and immediately knew that it was Lalitā’s handwriting. His chest trembled. He thought, "Giving up the love of Kamalinī, lotus-like Rādhā, I am here with Candrāvalī." Breathing heavily, His mind disturbed, He thought, "How did they know I was here? Oh, what shall I do now?" The poet Śrī Canda says Kānu (Kṛṣṇa) became restless and apprehensive.

So then, Kṛṣṇa went to meet Śrī Rādhā. With folded hands, a cloth around His neck, and straw in His teeth, He came before her and began to plead. "Oh merciful Rādhī, please forgive my offenses." Humbly offering His obeisances again and again to her, He said, "Oh life of my heart, please show me your moonlike face. I am *dīna-jana*, a fallen person, but you are *dīna-bandhu*, the friend of the fallen. Please be merciful to me. Oh *karuṇā-sindhu*, oh ocean of mercy, please excuse this offender."

Hearing Kṛṣṇa’s humble prayer, Rādhārāṇī became even angrier. She was keeping her back to Kṛṣṇa, her moonlike face looking down on the ground as she began drawing lines in the earth with her fingernails. At that time, Lalitā held a mirror in front of Rādhikā’s face. Seeing Kṛṣṇa’s reflection, Rādhā became even angrier, and she began to chastise Lalitā. But when lotus-like Rādhā noticed that Kṛṣṇa was wearing a piece of Candrāvalī’s cloth, she said, "Kṛṣṇa, what kind of cloth are you wearing? There is *kājala* and vermilion on your lips. Go back to your dearmost Candrāvalī and try to console her." And seeing that His peacock feather was bent and the hair on His head completely disheveled, Rādhikā said, "Go present yourself to Candrāvalī. You have no shame. Even though the Yamunā River is nearby, you have not washed your face. Oh Vraja-bandhu, oh friend of the Vrajabāsīs, now your name 'Rādhā-bandhu' no longer applies. Before, you were *mugdha-nātha*, Lord of bewildered persons. Now you have become Candrāvalī-kānta, the Lord of Candrāvalī."

So Kṛṣṇa said, "Oh affectionate Rādhā, you are most forgiving. Please forgive me. Oh foremost of young girls, I’ve become bewildered and mistakenly touched a lily, thinking that it was a lotus." *O kṣamā-nidhi*, ocean of forgiveness, *kṣamā* please excuse me for all my offenses. Unable to bear your harsh, insulting words, I must now jump into the Yamunā River and end my life. I am like the body, and you are the life of that body. Without you, everything is empty." But Rādhā angrily responded, "Don’t touch me. Get out of here. I don’t want to see you. I don’t even want to see your shadow. Oh shameless one, go take a bath in the Yamunā, feed the brāhmaṇas, and become freed from sin."

All the sakhīs then spoke to Rādhikā on behalf of Kṛṣṇa. They said, "Oh crest jewel of young women, why are you so angry with Giridhārī? Not getting a taste of nectar, you are throwing it away. This will only bring you lamentation. You know how He danced on the heads of Kāliya and how He lifted Govardhana Hill. What kind of love do you have? Śukadeva Gosvāmī, Sanaka-kumāra, Jagat-kāmarāja, and all the sages of all the three worlds are meditating on His name. The same Kṛṣṇa is now standing before you with folded hands. Please don’t make Him more miserable and depressed. Show Him your face and relieve Śyāma of His sorrow."

But taking all of her girl companions to a secluded place, Rādhikā then expressed her anger to them. She said, "My dear sakhīs, henceforward, anyone who speaks to me about Kṛṣṇa or anyone who even utters His name will be considered my enemy. From today on, I will not look at any dark or deep-blue-colored things, even new clouds or dark dresses, etc." Hearing this, Lalitā said, "What? You are always thinking of that dark-colored boy and thus always carrying that contamination in your mind. How can you give up thinking of Him? The Yamunā’s water is dark. Will you stop going there to collect water?" Rādhikā replied, "I will no longer go to the Yamunā. I will no longer bind my dark hair or apply dark *kājala* to my eyes. I will not even look at a dark raincloud. Dark is my enemy. Dark is my enemy. Oh Viśākhā, that dark enemy has stung me."

Viśākhā said, "O Rādhikā, what kind of vow have you taken now? Don’t reject Him for whom you have already lost your caste and family. The cage for your pet parrots is dark. What will you do with that?" Rādhikā replied, "O sakhīs, break that cage and set the parrots free. Take this blue *marakata*-jeweled bangle off my hand and replace it with a golden one. Take this blue sarī and throw it in the Yamunā. Smear white sandalwood on all of these dark pictures or break them. Don’t put any dark-colored cloth on my bed. Make my kuñja white and don’t let that [darkness] shine into my kuñja."

A sakhī said to Rādhikā, "Oh my dear Kiśorī, please listen to me. Oh princess, how long will you banish all of these things? Your dark hair looks beautiful on your head. What will you do with it? There are dark circles under your eyes. What will you do with them? Please tell me." Hearing her words, Rādhikā became even more angry and replied, "I will not look at the mirror to see my dark hair. I will make my dark hair white by smearing sandalwood on it. I will not listen to the sound of the dark crow and cuckoo, and I will not even look at the dark night. O Viśākhā, my heart is burning from so much distress. Please don’t speak anymore *Kṛṣṇa-kathā*." Ever youthful, she broke the birdcage with her own hands and let the parrots out. Throwing away her *marakata*-jeweled bracelet, she replaced it with the golden one.

One gopī said, "Rādhikā, if Mohana plays His flute, how can you tolerate it? How can you stay steady? As soon as He plays His flute, all of your tolerance and resolution will fade away. What kind of promise are you making? As soon as you hear the sound of Mohana’s flute, you must run. He is like a magnet and you are like iron. You will certainly go mad for Him." But Rādhikā said, "My dear friend, listen to the painful story of what that thief Nīlamaṇi did to me yesterday. He for whom I have become a disgrace in Vraja—that same person yesterday night took rest at Candrāvalī’s kuñja. Yesterday on the bank of the Yamunā, Śyāma gave some indication to meet me at my kuñja, but He did not show up. I have been keeping His *guñjā* necklace as the sign of a signal, but Candrāvalī has usurped Him. What type of mantra did that woman apply to Śyāma that the flower bed I made for Him has become useless? I made a lamp of flowers for Him, but the boat of my hope drowned in the water. Last night I wasted the entire evening. Oh friend, don’t speak to me of the name Kṛṣṇa. Whoever speaks that name will now be my enemy. This is the vow of Rādhā. From today onwards, I give up all types of dark-colored things. I will not use dark eyeliner anymore in my eyes."

Another gopī spoke up, "Oh Rādhā, will you not cross the Yamunā to go to the market in Mathurā to sell yogurt? How is it possible for you to give up thinking of the dark form of He for whom, by not seeing for a single moment, you feel like millions of *yugas* have passed? How is it possible for you to give up thinking of He for whom you are carrying the reputation of being unchaste on your head? How can you stop going to the Yamunā to meet that dark gold? How can you stop going to the Yamunā to meet that dark gold?"

Śrī Rādhā replied, "I will not go to the Yamunā for water or bathing. Oh gopī, don’t quarrel with me. Previously, I did not know about that debauchee’s activities. My whole body is burning with poison from that dark womanizer. Uproot the *tamāla* tree that is near the kuñja. Drive that dark bee out. I don’t want to wear any dark bangles or hear the sound of any dark peacock. For Him I bore the burden of a scandalous reputation. I had to tolerate so much slander, indignity, and insults for Him. With great fear I go to take bath in the Yamunā, and Kānu always comes and harasses me on the way. What kind of mantra does He keep in His flute that steals my mind? Don’t allow the gardener to bring blue flowers to my home. My heart is breaking seeing the blue lily flower. Bring that golden *tup-buk* flower to me along with sandalwood. Smear it on me, and I will become white-colored."

Another gopī said, "O Rādhā, don’t be angry with Giridhārī. He is your real wealth. Oh my friend, unable to taste that nectar, that day will come when you will repent. He is controlling Śukadeva Gosvāmī, Sanaka-kumāra, and others. For seven days, Indra made it rain, but he was defeated by Kṛṣṇa. Now you are not speaking to Him, but the day will come when you will speak to Him first." Another Sakhī said, "Whatever happened, Rādhā, let it go. Now we have to go for *Sūrya-pūjā*. All right, let us go." That Śyāma came and requested you to forgive Him, but you would not even look at Him. You are angry with Him, but He has no fault." Ignoring the advice of all the sakhīs, Rādhā refused to give up her anger.

In the meantime, wandering around Vraja feeling unbearable separation from Rādhikā, Kṛṣṇa met with Vṛndā Devī. He humbly told her, "Oh my friend, I am drowning in an ocean of grief. Please save me. My heart is crying. How can I attain the lady of my life? I am ill-fated as the ocean of anger has come to me. Due to my fault, I have attained this situation. What shall I do now? Where will I go? In which way can I meet my *prāṇeśvarī*? Please show me some means out of this predicament. I am a great offender, but Kiśorī is very merciful. I am suffering in separation from Rādhikā. Please save my life and reunite us."

The goddess of the forest, Vṛndā, said to Śyāma, "The fire of Rādhikā’s soaking anger is increasing now more and more. Who can approach Rādhā and pour the ghee of your humility on the fire of her anger? She has closed her ears and refuses to hear anything. She even considers her hair the same as poison. She has stopped going to the Yamunā River for water. She will not even look at the dark night. She has banished all of her girl companions who had dark complexions. She threw her blue jeweled necklace away. She freed the parrots and broke their cage. She uprooted the dark *tamāla* tree that she was personally taking care of. Now she has smeared sandalwood all over the dark pictures and trembles with anger when she sees even the blue sky."

Crying, His heart troubled, Śyāma said, "Oh Vṛndā, how can I attain my lover? Please give me some intelligence whereby I can again have her *darśana*." Vṛndā Devī replied, "Oh Nīlamaṇi, listen. There is one way. You have to dress as a *nāpitī*, a female barber, and go to Rādhikā’s palace. There you should smear *kuṅkuma* on Rādhā’s feet, and thus you can have her *darśana*." Hearing Vṛndā’s words, immediately Kāla-cand agreed and requested Vṛndā to dress Him appropriately.

So Vṛndā dressed Śyāma as a lady barber. She made a braid in His hair and fixed a *ketakī* flower in it. She dressed Him in a colorful sarī. On His nose she placed a jeweled ornament; on His neck, a wonderful necklace; and also placed bracelets, armbands, a jeweled waist belt, and many other types of ornaments on Him. Finishing, Vṛndā Devī told Him, "Oh crest jewel of dancers, this is a very wonderful dress. You look very beautiful." Saying this, Vṛndā showed a mirror to Śyāma. Seeing His dress, Śyāma thanked Vṛndā Devī for her kind intelligence and help.

Then, taking a box of *kuṅkuma*, Śyāma hid at the entrance to Yāvat and waited for Rādhikā and her girl companions to come home. After some time, Rādhikā came accompanied by Lalitā and other sakhīs. Lalitā noticed a beautiful lady barber waiting to offer her service of smearing color on the ladies’ feet. Lalitā saw that this wonderful lady looked far more beautiful than even heavenly damsels, and that nectar was falling from her laughter. Lalitā inquired, "Oh beautiful lady, where do you stay? Are you living here in Vraja or somewhere else?"

The lady barber replied, "I stay at Mathurā. I am a *prema-kāṅgālinī*. I am a beggar of *prema*. I have come here to put some color on the feet of Kiśorī. Please show me where is the daughter of Vṛṣabhānu Mahārāja, who is famous as Rājeśvarī, the queen of the *rāsa* dance, Kamalinī, the lotus-like one, and Kiśorī-ratna, the jewel of all young girls." The poet Ghanadās says, "Please show me her feet." Lalitā said, "Oh lady, our Sakhī Śrīmatī is very sad. She is not interested in wearing any kind of ornament or decoration. Rādhikā is now uncontrollably angry at Kṛṣṇa. She has given up all kinds of decorations and says that her body is burning. Even if we smear cooling sandalwood paste and *kūri* musk on her body, it simply increases her pain twice over."

Hearing all this, the lady barber addressed Rādhārāṇī, "Oh Śrīmatī, please give up your sadness. Your moonlike face looks black; seeing that, my heart is breaking. Oh Rāsa-raṅginī, one who delights in pleasure pastimes, please give up your sulkiness. Oh Rādhā with beautiful hair, please be merciful to me. You are a resident of Yāvat. You are the giver of mercy. Oh Rāsa-raṅginī, don’t be angry in your mind."

Speaking these words of consolation to Rādhikā, Kṛṣṇa then opened His little box. Touching Rādhikā’s feet, Kṛṣṇa began cleaning her nails and then putting red-colored *kuṅkuma* on the bottom of her feet. With shaking hands, Kṛṣṇa disguised as a lady barber started writing "Śyāma" on Rādhikā’s foot. When Rādhikā saw this, she pulled her foot back and became very angry at that lady. With great indignation, Rādhikā said, "Who taught you to write this name? You are such a shameless lady. Are you not fearless? Get out from here." Saying this, Rādhikā drove her out of that village. That lady barber became very fearful and left that place chanting Rādhā’s name with tears in her eyes.

Crying and crying in separation from Rādhikā, Śyāma again came to Vṛndādevī. "Oh Vṛndādevī," He said, "please save me. Oh goddess of the forest, show me how I can reunite with that beautiful girl of Vraja. How can I meet with her? And how can her sulkiness be dissipated?" The fire of anger inside the heart of Rādhārāṇī is increasing more and more. Vṛndā Devī said to Śyāma, "Oh Nīlamaṇi, if you want to have *darśana* of Rādhā, you should accept the dress of Mālinī, a lady florist."

Kṛṣṇa said, "Oh Vṛndā, how can you be so bold as to again suggest that I disguise Myself and approach Rādhā? Following your instructions, I dressed as a lady barber and came near Rādhikā at Yāvat. The only result was that I had to run away. With great anger, Rādhikā pushed me with her hand and threw me out from there." Vṛndādevī replied, "Don’t think it’s all hopeless." The poet Ghanadās says, "Kṛṣṇa, you are Māyādhar, the Lord of illusion. Who understands your pastimes? All of this is a joke arranged by you."

The moonlike lady Vṛndā then decorated moonlike Kṛṣṇa as a lady florist. She ornamented His hair with many different kinds of flowers and placed a jeweled ornament known as a *sevantī* on the part in His hair. She then fixed an ornamental pearl on His nose and necklaces and *śaṅkha*-shaped earrings on His neck and ears. What can be more wonderful than the *sindūra* on His forehead, which appeared like the full moon shining above the blossoming lotus of His face? On His arms, she placed jeweled bracelets, and on His feet, jeweled ankle bells. The poet Ghanadās says also there is red *kuṅkuma* on His feet. Dressed in a white sarī, Kṛṣṇa’s beauty was so great that it could even divert the mind of a *sannyāsī*. Vṛndādevī brought a basket that was full of garlands made from *nīlotpala*, *campaka*, *punnāga*, *ketakī*, *mālatī*, *mādhavī*, *mālatī*, *kuṅda*, *bakula*, and many other kinds of flowers.

After adopting the dress of a Mālinī, Śyāma went to Yāvat. The master of illusion is now mad and bewildered in Rādhā-love. The master of illusion, Kṛṣṇa, is now mad and bewildered in Rādhā-love, thinking, "When will this sulkiness leave my beloved?" With a blissful mood, that Mālinī entered Rādhā’s home. Looking at the faces of Lalitā and the other sakhīs, she said, "Please help me." Seeing that beautiful Mālinī who possessed a most wonderful darkest complexion, Viśākhā asked her, "Oh dear one, where are you from?" The Mālinī replied, "Oh my friend, please listen. My birthplace is Mathurā. Due to fear of Kaṃsa, I now stay in Vraja. Where is your Rādhārāṇī? I am a Mālinī with fresh flower garlands. Please accept them quickly, for my time is running out and I have to return to my home soon."

Lalitā said, "Rādhā’s situation is very bad. Your blue lily flower garland will not be pleasing to her. Nor do we need flowers for anything else. We no longer have time for *Sūrya-pūjā*. Nor are we going to the Yamunā anymore for a bath, because the Rāhu planet of *māna* is attacking Rādhikā." Lalitā then indicated to that Mālinī that she should put a garland on Rādhikā. The Mālinī then placed garlands of *mallikā*, *campaka*, and *mālatī*—all white-colored flowers—on Rādhikā and became very happy.

The Mālinī then inquired, "Oh Rāsa-raṅginī Rādhā, what kind of dress are you wearing? I do not see any dark bangles, which would look very beautiful on your wrists, nor are there any blue *marakata*-jeweled ornaments on your arm. I don’t see any darkish gopīs in your house, nor do I see the famous black *tamāla* tree in your courtyard. Moreover, I’m surprised to find that all the dark-colored pictures in your house have been smeared with white sandalwood." The Mālinī then went to offer a blue lily flower garland. The Mālinī told her that it would help her to remember Kṛṣṇa. But as soon as Rādhā saw that garland, she became very angry and immediately threw the garland away. She said, "You are a low-class person. What are you saying? You are nonsense. Get out of here."

The poet Ghanadās says, with tears of shame in His eyes, Lord Śyāmasundara immediately left that place and went back to Vṛndā Devī’s cottage. Śyāma told Vṛndā that the *māna* sulky anger had still not left Śrī Rādhā. "Oh Vṛndā Devī," He said, "my heart is still crying. I will give up my life by now jumping in the Yamunā River." Vṛndā told Him, "Oh Śyāma, just be patient. Now you should dress as a *sannyāsī*. Then you should go to Rādhikā and beg the alms of her sulkiness. Beg for the alms of her sulkiness. When the jewel of *prema* becomes a beggar of *prema*, then Rādhā’s uncontrollable sulkiness must break." Kṛṣṇa said, "Without delay, quickly He arranged that *sannyāsī* dress for me, and I will go to that beautiful girl to beg alms." Vṛndā Devī replied, "No, it is not possible for me to give that dress to you. To obtain it, you have to go to Kailāśa and approach Lord Bholānāth, Bholānātha Śiva. Let us go there at once."

So in Kailāśa, Śyāma called out, "Oh Maheśa, where are you? Please give me your *darśana*. My heart is crying for Rādhikā." Hearing that voice, the Lord of Durgā stood up from His seat, and understanding that it was Kṛṣṇa, He became surprised. Kṛṣṇa said, "Oh holder of the trident, please dress me in your form. Give me ashes, matted hair, and *ḍamaru* drum. Oh Maheśa, only in this way will the *māna* of Rādhā go away." Hearing Śyāma’s words, five-headed Śiva pondered, and He said, "You are *Brahmānanda-nāyaka*, the hero of the entire universe. What can I say to you? You are the Lord of my illusion. You are playing a joke on me. Seeing you here today in your enchanting Mohana form, my *bhajana-kuṭīra* has become successful. How can I matt the hair on that head decorated with a peacock feather? How can the *tripuṇḍra* three-line *tilaka* look nice on that forehead decorated with two beautiful upright lines of *tilaka*? How can that ear decorated with jeweled earrings now wear a copper earring? How can I put a garland of bones on the same neck where Rādhārāṇī has placed a *vaijayantī* garland? How can that same shoulder that has been decorated with a golden cloth now carry a *bhikṣā-jhulī*, a cloth bag for begging?
