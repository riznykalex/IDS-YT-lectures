"# Commander-in-Chief of the Vaiṣṇavas - Kārtika Parikramā 2025

We are blessed to be here. Dear devotees, today we are blessed to be with one of our *parama gurus*, a wonderful, great Vaiṣṇava in our disciplic succession who comes directly from Ādi Guru, Śrī Kṛṣṇa Himself. We are speaking about the commander-in-chief. He appeared in this world in 1780. It is known that he lived to be 145 years old. Although he is not as famous as the *ācāryas* who have recently passed away, he still belongs to them and occupies a very important position in the chain of our disciplic succession. 

In fact, it is his name that we often repeat; we may not even realize it. We must, however, know about him, because it is part of the *maṅgalācaraṇa*. I offer my respectful obeisances to him for having discovered the place of appearance of the Lord, a fact which is acknowledged by all Vaiṣṇavas. Those of you who are more knowledgeable know that Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī is mainly known in our society as *kṣetra-sannyāsī*. I was very happy when our leaders asked me to talk about him today. In fact, in my devotional life—my "career," so to speak—I have visited his *samādhi mandir* many times, especially in the 1970s, praying for his mercy.

Today, I will use two main sources for this lecture. Perhaps not everyone knows about these books or autobiographies. I found a lot of information about Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī in the autobiography of Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura called *Svalikhita Jīvanī*. An autobiography is when the author himself describes his life. This great general, this wonderful devotee, was born in East Bengal, which is known as Bangladesh. In fact, many pastimes and stories related to Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī took place in the territory now known as Bangladesh. Because it is located in modern Bangladesh, there are certain difficulties for devotees to visit this place, and for us, this is a great loss.

His name was "Bābājī." The name itself implies that he was a *bābājī*. *Bābājī* means that a person has renounced the world and sits day and night chanting the holy names. This usually means repeating 300,000 names, which is 192 rounds. He is a charming example for us. We can set this bar for ourselves and also try to repeat 300,000 names in one day. He built himself a cottage in a solitary forest, planted a small *tulasī* garden near it, and repeated the holy names of the Lord 300,000 times a day. The holy name never left his lips, day or night. 

Usually, when we hear about our *ācāryas*, we hear that they were wonderful speakers and very powerful preachers. However, there is no information about him preaching publicly and actively. What place does he have in our *paramparā*? Its importance lies in the fact that he preached renunciation. He set an example of renunciation for us. It is impossible to convince people of the futility of material existence or of the positive alternative in Kṛṣṇa consciousness if a person is attached to this world. 

He gave the following example: one time a woman had a child who abused sweets and ate so many of them that she had to take him to the dentist. The mother was worried because she repeatedly had to take her child to the dentist due to his sugar consumption. During one visit, she requested the dentist, "Please tell my son to stop eating so many sweets." The dentist said, "Bring him back in a month, and then I will tell him." She was perplexed. She said, "Well, just tell him now." He said, "No, bring him back in a month." She followed the instruction. A month later, she brought her child, sat him in the chair, and said, "Now, tell him." The dentist said, "Young man, you need to stop eating so many sweets, because otherwise your teeth will rot." The boy agreed. The mother asked, "Why didn’t you tell him this a month ago?" The dentist replied, "Because a month ago, I was still eating sweets myself. My words would have had no force or benefit. Only if my own actions align will my words penetrate his heart." 

We can convince people of the transience of this world and give them the alternative of "Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare; Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma, Rāma Rāma, Hare Hare." Even if we are actively preaching on the street, saying, "This is the material world, and you have to get out from under its influence," it will have no effect if we ourselves are subject to that influence. We must follow a certain level of renunciation, and then we can offer them Kṛṣṇa consciousness. Then our words will have power.

This applies to any spiritual process. In the Bible, Luke 16:1 suggests that one cannot serve God while remaining attached to material things. So, the principle of renunciation is important in the life of every devotee. Of course, renunciation is a gradual process; we cannot simply imitate a *bābājī*. I read that during the *cāturmāsya* of these four months of the rainy season, in the first month he ate only four bananas in the evening. Imagine—he fasted all day, and in the evening, he took only four bananas. Could you do that? 

In the second month, he ate only guavas. In the third, he drank only whey. In the fourth, he ate only boiled banana flowers without salt. That was his renunciation. I remember in the seventies, when we were traveling and distributing books, one devotee was so inspired by *cāturmāsya* that he decided to follow in the footsteps of Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī. He began to fast, but the number of books he distributed sharply decreased. I turned to the GBC, and he told the devotee, "Your renunciation is that you need to feast every day [by distributing books]."

We also follow strict rules of renunciation: we do not engage in illicit relations, we do not take intoxicants, we do not engage in gambling. If you tell someone about this, they might say, "Are you a fool? How can you follow this?" I remember a story about a king who wanted to take a second wife. The queen's mother said, "Yes, you can, but her son must not inherit the kingdom. Only my son will inherit the throne." The prince said, "I renounce. I will not have children for the rest of my life, so you can go ahead and marry this girl." The demigods were so impressed by his vow of celibacy that they showered him with flowers from the sky, crying, "Bhīṣma! Bhīṣma!" Similarly, on the day of initiation, when you stand before the spiritual master and take your vows, the demigods observe your determination to follow a path of renunciation and may also honor you.

Śrīla Prabhupāda, as part of his morning walk, once saw a disciple walking barefoot in the cold. Prabhupāda asked, "Why are you without shoes?" The student, with a little pride, said, "Because I am practicing asceticism." Prabhupāda replied, "You don't need to do this. You have enough asceticism as it is. There is no need to increase it. Put on your shoes." Our *ācāryas* explain that true renunciation lies in the understanding that nothing belongs to me; everything belongs to Kṛṣṇa. Therefore, everything should be used in the service of Kṛṣṇa. 

We know that he first met Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura in 1880, as mentioned in Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura's journal, *The Harmonist*. They met for the second time in 1891 in Navadvīpa. It is mentioned that this happened on Ekādaśī. Today is Ekādaśī. These two great souls stayed together the whole day and night discussing Kṛṣṇa-kathā and chanting the Hare Kṛṣṇa *mahāmantra*. Do we do that when we get together with our Godbrothers and Godsisters? Or do we discuss politics and sports? We can take an example from them.

I read an interesting point that during this meeting, Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī advised Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura to preach the names of Gaurāṅga all over India and, if possible, all over the world. Isn't that what happens when we place someone in our heart? It becomes an inspiration to preach. We should appreciate the opportunity of associating with a pure devotee. Each moment can have a powerful effect. 

Later, Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura wrote about this second encounter. He said: "After spending the whole night together on Ekādaśī, the entire village gathered at 8:00 AM to go for *gaura-saṅkīrtana* through the streets. At the head of our group stood the venerable Śrīla Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī Mahārāja. I noticed that although he was over 100 years old, he danced like a lion, sometimes exclaiming, 'What divine names has Nitāi brought! He brought the market and sells it for the price of everyone's faith.'" 

When Bābājī sang, he cried and fell to the ground in ecstasy. I saw something I had never seen before; everyone present was overwhelmed by his mood. Their eyes filled with tears, their hair stood on end, and although they could hardly chant, they remained immersed in the *kīrtana*. There is a quote: "If one stays near a pure Vaiṣṇava for some time, one can get *bhakti*, the devotional energy emanating from his body, and then devotion will soon manifest in the heart." Once in London, Śrīla Prabhupāda was leading *kīrtana* in a small temple room. He suddenly stopped and asked the devotees, "Did you see them?" They asked, "Who?" He replied, "Ambarīṣa and Nārada Muni. They were dancing with you." When asked what they were doing, Prabhupāda said, "They laughed, looking at your attempts to chant the holy names in Kali-yuga." 

Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura says that this *bhakti* passes into us; it is impossible to buy it or otherwise acquire it. Once, a devotee said to Prabhupāda, "I just always want to be near your lotus feet." Prabhupāda replied, "Well, it's quite difficult because these lotus feet travel very quickly all over the world." Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī had a schedule: he lived six months a year in Vṛndāvana and six months in Navadvīpa. When he was in Navadvīpa, he sometimes went to Calcutta to visit Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura. They had such a close relationship that they exchanged personal items, even when it was painful to part with them.

Years ago, I visited the place in Calcutta where Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura lived. I had a mystical experience. I saw his great-great-granddaughter, who looked exactly like him. She was very happy to transfer this house to ISKCON, knowing it would be well cared for. In that house, I saw the very bookshelf and library that were in the paintings, as well as the chair he sat in and the bed he slept on. She handed me the *Bhagavad-gītā* he used to read; it was so dilapidated, literally falling apart. She showed me his *kṛṣṇa-śilā*, *govardhana-śilā*, and the deities of Kṛṣṇa and Jagannātha. When I finished the paperwork, I asked if his *kaṇṭhī-mālā* and *japa-mālā* were still there. An hour and a half later, she returned with a bag that looked 300 years old. She took out those beads on a copper plate. I touched them and chanted Hare Kṛṣṇa. I was allowed to stay in that room alone for half an hour, and I felt I was in Vaikuṇṭha.

When Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī was in Vraja, he lived at Suraj-kuṇḍa. He gave daily lectures on *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*. I heard that one night, Rādhārāṇī came to him in a dream and said, "In this kuṇḍa, there is a *govardhana-śilā* on which my jewelry and covers are imprinted. Take this *śilā* and worship it." Although the *śilā* weighed 100 kg, he lifted it easily, like a light flower. 

Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī always maintained good association. There is a story of a street sweeper who offered him food. People were shocked that a saint would accept food from a "low-born" person. Bābājī answered, "Gentlemen, before I descended from the spiritual world, Lord Kṛṣṇa ordered 88,000 saints to take birth in Vraja to serve the holy *dhāma*. The sweeper from whose hands I accepted the *roti* was one of those 88,000 saints. Shouldn't he be revered?" At that moment, all those respected people bowed down.

He also treated the street dogs in Vraja with respect, as he saw them as inhabitants of the holy *dhāma*. Once, he asked his servant, Bihārī, to read scriptures to him. Bihārī said, "I am illiterate, I cannot." Bābājī blessed him, and in a second, the servant was able to read the scriptures with love and devotion. Our *ācāryas* say that everything is possible if you receive the mercy of the Lord and His devotees.

Later, when young devotees came to study with him, they complained that he made them work in the garden instead of teaching them deep meditation. He replied, "Sirs, unless you purify your heart through the practice of devotional service, your attempts at deep study and meditation will only lead to dreams and sleep. Continue to work here in the garden until I deem you ready."

As he grew older, his body became very frail. His servant, Bihārī, would carry him in a basket on his head. This led to a famous story: when the party was looking for the birthplace of Lord Caitanya (the *Yoga-pīṭha*), there was much controversy. Bābājī was carried to a certain place, and he suddenly jumped four feet out of the basket, shouting, "This is the place where the Lord appeared!" Everyone unanimously accepted this as the truth.

His long life came to an end. His final instructions were: "Never forget that Gaura is more merciful and benevolent than Kṛṣṇa. Kṛṣṇa is the one who takes into account your misdeeds by dispensing justice." That is why in our tradition we chant the names of Gaura before we chant the names of Kṛṣṇa. I would like to conclude today with these words: The Vaiṣṇava general has gone to his eternal abode. Śrīla Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī Mahārāja has left this world in darkness. Our material eyes will no longer be able to see his ecstatic dancing during *kīrtana*, but he will continue to bestow his blessings upon us from his eternal abode. 

Hare Kṛṣṇa.""
