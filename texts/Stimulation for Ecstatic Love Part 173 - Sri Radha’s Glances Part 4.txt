Now, in Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī’s Padyāvalī, I was reading the other night, we find two very beautiful verses referring to the special nature of Rādhārāṇī’s glances. The first one I found was śloka 344, where Kṛṣṇa is addressing Uddhava referring to Rādhārāṇī’s eyes. He says—this is really nice—”Among the vraja-vāsīs, the gopīs are the best, and their best quality is their sweet words. Among the gopīs, there was one who is the best, and her best quality is her crooked, sidelong glance. And her best quality, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, is her crooked, sidelong glance.” Isn’t that wonderful?

Then śloka 198, wherein one of Rādhārāṇī’s friends says to her, “Rādhā, say something to the splendidly smiling, infallible Personality of Godhead who stands before you, filled with loving desires. Oh, fortunate girl, sprinkle Him with the flooding nectar of what? Your affectionate sidelong glance. Your affectionate sidelong glance.” Hare Kṛṣṇa.

And then Ujjvala-nīlamaṇi 749, we hear: “Once on the shore of the Yamunā, doe-eyed Rādhārāṇī tilted her neck in a particular way and glanced at Kṛṣṇa with dancing eyes, severely wounding His heart with the sharp tip of one of Cupid’s arrows.” I thought those are really beautiful verses describing the nature of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī’s kaṭākṣa, her glance.

So today, I would like to share a special pastime, actually, by the Vṛndāvana poet Gopāla Kavi. He was from the 16th century, in which we hear how Rādhārāṇī’s glances are the very life of Braja. That particular line appears in the pastime: “Rādhārāṇī’s glances are the very life of Braja.” Now, what’s interesting—it’s a very popular pastime, and similar versions I found, each with a slight variation, appear in Vidagdha-mādhava, Govinda-līlāmṛta, Kṛṣṇa-bhāvāmṛta, Ananda-vṛndāvana-campū, and Bhāvana-sāra-saṅgraha. All those books, I found similar versions of this pastime, but I found Gopāla Kavi’s narration a nice synthesis of them all, so I chose that version.

This līlā takes place during the early morning pastimes of the divine couple. At this point in eternal time, Rādhārāṇī was married to Abhimanyu and had moved from her childhood home in Varṣāṇā to her in-laws in Yāvaṭ. She was married, so she moved to her husband’s home in Yāvaṭ. And it’s interesting, she was followed and served there by her intimate friends like Lalitā and Viśākhā sakhīs. Now, her husband Abhimanyu, who by the way is described as just the shadow of Kṛṣṇa, was the son of Jaṭilā; and Jaṭilā, as we’ve discussed many times, was always suspicious that her new daughter-in-law, Śrī Rādhā, would sometimes sneak out and meet secretly with Kṛṣṇa. This was her obsession, which of course Rādhārāṇī would do with the help of her friends.

So, one time after a nighttime rasa dance in the forest of Vṛndāvana, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa returned to their respective homes just before sunrise. Rādhārāṇī, accompanied by Lalitā, Viśākhā, and her mañjarī servants, returned very quietly to Yāvaṭ. And entering the palace there without anyone noticing, Rādhārāṇī lay down to sleep. Now, the author describes that even while sleeping, she softly chanted Kṛṣṇa’s sweet holy names. Even while sleeping, Rādhārāṇī softly chants Kṛṣṇa’s sweet holy names.

Then just as dawn approached, the first rays of sunlight began to illuminate that palace that, by the way, was built by her father, Mahārāja Vṛṣabhānu. He obviously built a palace at Varṣāṇā—we all know that particular palace; we visit there often on Kartik Parikrama—but he built another one for his daughter when she moved to Yāvaṭ to live with her in-laws. Out of affection, he constructed an extraordinarily beautiful palace on the northern side of Jaṭilā’s residence at Yāvaṭ. Some detail there.

This palace was decorated with ornaments shaped like a flock of shining silver swans. It was decorated with ornaments, and the ornaments were shaped like a flock of shining silver swans if you looked very closely. But when peacocks saw those ornaments from a distance, they thought, “Oh, a rain cloud has appeared from a distance, oh, a rain cloud has appeared,” and they would immediately open their tails in great happiness. But when they came closer and saw the silver swans, which are natural—they’re natural enemies of the peacocks—they quickly closed their tails again.

So, soon after Śrī Rādhā had returned home and fallen asleep in the palace, her maternal grandmother—she has a grandmother, her maternal grandmother whose name is Mukhara—entered the palace. Seeing the doors open, she entered and called out, “Oh, respected Jaṭilā, I hope you are well. Please tell me, where is my granddaughter Rādhā?”

So Jaṭilā, she’s always in a bad mood, replied somewhat sharply, “She’s still sleeping in her room with her sakhīs.”

So Mukhara said, “Oh dear, it’s already dawn and she’s still sleeping; this is not very proper. I will go and wake her.”

So Jaṭilā replied, “Yes, please do, and tell her to get ready now to leave the house soon to go for Sūrya-pūjā, to worship the sun god.”

So Mukhara entered the chamber of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. While Rādhārāṇī was sleeping very deeply and hearing the sound of Mukhara’s ankle bells and the tapping of her walking stick, Viśākhā, who always sleeps nearby Śrī Rādhā, woke up. And rising from bed, she gently tried to wake up Rādhārāṇī, but Rādhārāṇī didn’t wake up; she was up all night rasa-dancing. So Viśākhā opened the curtains, letting the morning light enter the room, and so gradually Rādhārāṇī awoke. And seeing her grandmother, she smiled very sweetly. “Oh, grandma!”

So Mukhara said affectionately, “Oh, my innocent granddaughter, you are the very sun of my life, but it is not proper for a young girl to sleep so late in the morning. Please arise and prepare yourself to worship Sūrya-dev.”

But then, looking carefully—looking carefully—at Rādhārāṇī’s clothing, Mukhara suddenly became suspicious. She turned and she said to Lalitā, who actually never leaves Rādhārāṇī’s side, it’s always described like that, “Oh Lalitā, what is that yellow silk garment? Last night I saw Kṛṣṇa wearing exactly such a cloth, and now Rādhā is wearing it. What’s going on here? Kṛṣṇa’s in her chamber?”

So Viśākhā replied sharply, “Oh, old woman, have you become confused in your old age? What are you saying?” She’s defending Rādhā. “Can’t you see that the amber glass in the windows is reflecting the bright rays of the rising sun? And because of that reflection, Rādhārāṇī’s blue garment appears yellow-gold. How improper it is to think such things as you do!” So, of course, Paurṇamāsī has arranged all this. So hearing this, Mukhara felt embarrassed, and she quietly just left the room.

And just at that moment, Śyāmala-sakhī arrived. And seeing her, Rādhārāṇī said very warmly, “Oh, my dear Śyāmala, I was just thinking about you, and by destiny, you have appeared. If the tree of my desire to meet Kṛṣṇa bears fruit so quickly, then this morning will certainly be auspicious.” As soon as I can meet Kṛṣṇa, she’s thinking, “Everything will be auspicious.”

Śyāmala smiled and said, “My dear Rādhā, you look tired. It seems that you and Kṛṣṇa spent a very beautiful night together in the Vṛndāvana forest.”

Now, hearing this, Rādhārāṇī became a little shy. So she quickly changed the topic. She actually looked upwards towards the ceiling, where there was a painting of a dark rain cloud. Now, seeing that dark color reminded Rādhārāṇī of Kṛṣṇa’s beautiful complexion, and immediately she became overwhelmed with ecstatic love, and she fainted. Just seeing a dark rain cloud, a painted dark rain cloud. She looked upwards and saw this rain cloud and fainted. Then gradually regaining consciousness, she opened her eyes and said, “Oh, Śyāmala, when did you arrive?”

And understanding Rādhā’s mood, Śyāmala replied very playfully, “Just now, when you were reminded of your beloved. Just now, when you were reminded of your beloved.” Looking up.

Then Rādhārāṇī said, “Oh, Śyāmala, you are so cruel. For so long, my two eyes have been deprived of seeing that bumblebee-like Kṛṣṇa. Without His nectarean darśana, these eyes feel like poor orphans. Why do you not take me to Him? Why do you not take me to Him? It’s been so long since I’ve seen Him.” She’s thinking like that.

So Śyāmala laughed gently and said, “Hey, Rādhe, you met Kṛṣṇa just last night. Why do you speak like this? You met Him last night.”

And Rādhārāṇī’s reply is so sweet: “Oh, Śyāmala, you speak like an uneducated person. Don’t you know that after a flash of lightning, night appears even darker? Wow. After a flash of lightning, night appears even darker. In the same way, after seeing Kṛṣṇa even for a moment, the pain of separation from Him becomes twice as intense.” What an analogy.

So then Śyāmala looked deeply into Rādhārāṇī’s eyes and said, “Oh, Rādhe, your lotus eyelids are reddened with love for Kṛṣṇa. Your eyes reveal everything. They describe your pastimes with Kṛṣṇa last night—whispering, playful quarrels, embraces, sweet conversations, the rasa dance, and many other loving exchanges that cannot be described with mere words. They cannot be described with mere words. These eyes of yours, and especially your sidelong glances, are the very life of Braja. Your sidelong glances are the very life of Braja. Kṛṣṇa, the master of all arts, Kalānidhi, becomes defeated when trying to understand the limitless art of your glances. Your sidelong glances completely capture Him. When the gopīs see your sidelong glances, they feel their lives have become completely successful. Oh, Rādhe, your sidelong glances are like arrows released by the army of Cupid that strike Kṛṣṇa directly. And struck by those arrows, the poor son of Nanda can hardly even walk. All He can do is beg again and again for another one of your glances.”

She continued, “Oh, Rādhe, please do not delay. Come with me now to Nandagrāma. There waits a patient who has been struck by the arrows of your sidelong glances—the son of Nanda Himself, longing only to see you again.”

And the poet concludes, Gopāla Kavi sings, that the meeting of the glances of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa is a sight longed for by sages, devotees, and rasika lovers of divine pastimes. Hare Kṛṣṇa. Wasn’t that a wonderful pastime? Glances, glances, glances, Rādhā’s glances.

Although frightened at heart, Rādhārāṇī externally assumed an angry pose, became furious at Jaṭilā’s accusations, and together with Lalitā, in order to defend herself, fabricated a network of seemingly logical lies.
