"### Janmāṣṭamī Class 2025 – Whispers

Oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya. Oṃ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya. 

I am here with you today to celebrate the day of the appearance or birth of Lord Kṛṣṇa in this world. In our Vaiṣṇava calendar, we have many auspicious days, and each one of them is very auspicious. But we have a phrase that we often refer to: perfect, more perfect, and the most perfect. This is a very auspicious day. In his writings, Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura refers to this day as follows: he says that such holy days as Janmāṣṭamī and Rāḍhāṣṭamī are like the mother of all devotees. 

There is a lot that can be said about this festival of Janmāṣṭamī. In India, traditionally among Vaiṣṇavas, they devote the entire day to chanting and hearing the glories of Kṛṣṇa. We can go on and on talking about Kṛṣṇa. But today we also have another festival in Vṛndāvana awaiting us, and I am sure that Kṛṣṇa will be very pleased to see how we engage others in devotional service. I thought that today I would focus on a very small, but very important aspect, and the lecture will be based on it.

I had such a big list of līlās connected with Janmāṣṭamī; I just closed my eyes and pointed. And then such a līlā happened: the ceremony of naming Kṛṣṇa. This is a very important part; when you are born, mom and dad give you a name. Of course, in our culture, we usually don't know whether the baby will be a boy or a girl. We have names for girls in the list, names for boys in the list, and we are just waiting for the baby to come out and find out who it will be. This is a very important part, which is described in the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, in the Tenth Canto, the eighth chapter. A whole 20 verses are dedicated to this; therefore, this is a very important līlā. Usually, in the Vedic culture, the purohita or pujārī—who is also an astrologer—is called when a child is born, so that he can give him a name according to the astrological details at the time of his birth. Or in ISKCON, devotees usually write to me and ask what name we can give the child.

In Vedic culture, children who were born into the kṣatriya caste were given a name after 100 days. Kṣatriyas are warriors, and they have to wait 100 days from the moment of birth. Only then does the pujārī come and give them a name. And among the caste of farmers, they have to wait a whole year. In the Vaiṣṇava tradition, it is very interesting what Haribāla wrote. He says that the naming ceremony can be held on the tenth day from birth, on the 100th day from birth, or on the first year from birth. Sometimes devoted students write to me: "Gurudeva, we wrote to you a year ago asking you to give our child a name. A year has passed, and there is still no name." Gurudeva then replies: “Everything is fine, don’t worry. This is part of our Vedic tradition.” In the case of Kṛṣṇa, a very famous saint named Garga Muni came to give Kṛṣṇa a name. In fact, Vasudeva asked him to go to Gokula so that he could give a name to Yaśodānanda’s son.

If you remember, Kṛṣṇa appeared in Kaṃsa’s prison, and his father and mother were Devakī and Vasudeva. Because Kaṃsa was going to kill him, Vasudeva took Kṛṣṇa and carried him across the Yamunā to Vṛndāvana and told Nanda Mahārāja, "Here, please take care of Kṛṣṇa." Nanda Mahārāja and Yaśodā were very happy that they had such a beautiful little son, Kṛṣṇa. At that moment, Vasudeva was sitting in Mathurā thinking that now was the time to give a name to this child. Without the knowledge of Nanda and Yaśodā, he sent Garga Muni. When Garga Muni crossed the river and came to Kṛṣṇa’s parents, they were very surprised and said, "Oh, Garga Muni, are you here?"

There is no problem with this, because in Vedic culture the guest we do not expect is considered the most auspicious. When Garga Muni arrived, Nanda Mahārāja stood up, greeted him with folded palms, and offered him very wonderful prayers. This is such a part of our tradition. I found a very good prayer in this connection in the *Garga-saṃhitā*; verse forty-six is about accepting a seemingly holy person into one's home. If you are a sinner and holy people come to you, this is how you should receive the person. So, what do you get when a devotee knocks on your door in this way? You open the door, a devotee stands in front of you and says, "Kṛṣṇa." No one should think that this is some kind of concern. It is a great blessing if a holy person comes to your house uninvited. The *Garga-saṃhitā* says the following: 

"The saints' peaceful pilgrimage is meant to bring peace to householders. It is the saint, not the sun, who dispels the darkness in the hearts of men." 

What a wonderful poem. Moreover, the śāstras say that any seemingly unexpected guest should be welcomed, respected, and worshiped like God. *Atithi* is translated as "guest is equivalent to the Lord," because the Lord gives you a devotee—He gives you a *śrī-mān*. Garga Muni, not only did he not distribute books as we see now, but he was the most important priest of the Yadu dynasty. Although Nanda Mahārāja was very busy, he helped his wife. He still took the guest, sat him down, and washed Garga Muni’s feet. Nanda Mahārāja offered water to Garga Muni and took a cāmara and began to fan this guest, because it is said that this cāmara removes all the obstacles that he crossed on his way and his fatigue. He was a priest of the Yadu dynasty. When any person comes, we offer him dugdha (milk) or we feed him prasādam. We should respect every devotee in this way.

After Garga Muni had been received very warmly, he went on to perform the ceremony. Usually, parents should not give a name; the guru or the holy person who comes should give it. In those days, the priests, like pujārīs, were also astrologers who could look at the astrological chart of the newborn child and, according to his qualities, give him a name. The name comes from the priest who is the representative of Kṛṣṇa. In the same way, when we all joined ISKCON, Śrīla Prabhupāda was the representative of Kṛṣṇa, and he gave you the name. He gave you a name and a mantra: *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare, Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma, Rāma Rāma, Hare Hare*. This is the most important part because at the moment when you get initiation, the guru gives you a name, and then he initiates you into chanting the mantra. For a mantra to have power, it must be given by the guru. 

When we read the Holy Scriptures, we come across many different mantras. Of course, we do not remember them, but for special benefit, this mantra should be given by the guru. In this way, you can get all the blessings of this mantra. The mahā-mantra is the most merciful mantra; you can chant it and be cleansed of all your sins. Everyone can chant the mahā-mantra. Then *śuddha-nāma* happens, the heart is felt, and all our bad qualities seem to flow from our heart. In order to attain *prema*, which is the main stage in our *bhakti*, we have to chant this mantra. In order for it to have full power, it must be given by a guru. I was reading that Śacīnandana Swami gives a lot of emphasis on us becoming attached to the mantra we receive from our guru. He writes in his prayers, *Manaḥ-śikṣā* (Instruction to the Mind), Text One: 

"O dear brother, O mind, giving up all pride, develop unprecedented and unlimited attachment for Śrī Guru, for Śrī Vṛndāvana, for the abode of the cows, to the devoted inhabitants of Vṛndāvana, to all the Vaiṣṇavas on this planet, to the secret mantra given by the guru, to the holy names of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, and to the process of devotion of the young gopīs of Vraja. To hold your feet, I beg you with sweet words." 

This verse is endowed with a deep understanding of Kṛṣṇa consciousness. Today, we give special emphasis that we should develop attachment to the mantra given by your guru during initiation. He writes in *Haribālam*, addressing his guru, praying for mercy. We are not *adhikārīs*; we do not know how to pray correctly, so we can all learn this verse. Similarly, he prays from the heart to our guru. It is like transcendental poetry, like nectar. Translation: "O Gurudeva, lord of the three worlds, please save me, who am tormented by time and burned by the fire of repeated births and deaths. I surrender to you and take shelter of you." 

The mantra is very important, but we also get a name. Śrīla Prabhupāda once humorously said that even if at the time of death you can remember your name, then you can go back home, back to Godhead, because all these names are connected with Kṛṣṇa. The name that we get is very important, because it indicates that we are being accepted into a new family—the big family of the Lord. As a tradition, when a girl gets married, she takes the surname of her husband. Before my mother got married, her name was Mildred Edwards. After she got married, they started calling her Edwards-Tibet. My mother has now gone home to Godhead; during the time before she left her body, she was chanting the holy names. She even got to Kṛṣṇa before I did. We get a name, and at the end, we have the addition of *dāsa* or *dāsī*, which indicates that we are servants of this name, of a certain place or a certain person. 

When we get a name, what do we do first? We call our friends in Ukraine or we try to tell the whole world that our name is now, for example, Kṛtātmā Dāsa. I find, "I am the boss." But we must note that when the Muni came to give Kṛṣṇa a name, he wanted it to remain a secret. When we join ISKCON, we want everyone to know that we’ve become part of the family. But here, Garga Muni wants the fact that Kṛṣṇa was named to be a secret. Why? It's not a secret now; we chant the name Kṛṣṇa very loudly every day on the shores of the Baltic Sea. But if you remember, this is a whole big līlā associated with Kaṃsa, who had spies sent to look for Kṛṣṇa everywhere. The very first thing Garga Muni said was that we would hold this naming ceremony in the cowshed. He made sure that it was a very appropriate time when the cows and all the herders were in the fields grazing. 

He wanted this ceremony to be a secret. There is a famous rule of śāstra: *savidete mantra*, which means that when a secret reaches six ears, it is no longer a secret. When the secret reaches six ears, it means three personalities—each person has two ears. If the secret reaches two more people, then it will no longer be a secret. Just as we say *mantra* in Sanskrit, in English there is a proverb: "If three people know a secret, that secret can remain a secret if two of those people die." They were in the cowshed, and all the shepherds and the cows had gone into the fields. Everything was happening very quietly, literally in a whisper. 

This is a very important līlā. At that moment, Garga Muni gave a name to both Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma. The *Garga-saṃhitā* describes that at that moment, not only Kṛṣṇa was named, but Balarāma as well. All this happened in a whisper. Although he was invited by the gopas to give a name to Kṛṣṇa, first he gave the name to Balarāma, because Balarāma was a little older than Kṛṣṇa. In Vedic culture, the name is given first to the elder, then to the younger. Even with twins, the one who comes out of the mother's womb first is the elder. In Vedic culture, usually the elder is the child who comes out second, because the parents conceived him first, so he moves back and forth. 

Because Balarāma was the eldest, Garga Muni gave him a name first. Garga Muni said, "This son of Rohiṇī will be called Rāma because he will please his loved ones with his qualities. He will be known as Bala because of the greatness of his power, and Saṅkarṣaṇa because of his unification of the differences of the Yadu." He gave Balarāma three names: Rāma, Bala, and Saṅkarṣaṇa. I thought how cool that was. In the West, we only get one name. But I realized I also had three names when I was born. I have my name, then my father gave me a very strange last name, and then I have another last name, "Ivar." I asked my dad, "Why did you give me such a weird, stupid name? My friends say, 'Ivar'." Dad said that name was a great warrior, a Viking. I actually got very attached to my name, but then I met devotees. In 1971, I got a new name, and then a third one, *Mahārāja*. During the *sannyāsa* ceremony, they give such simple names, like *Giri-rāj*, but they gave me such an aristocratic name: Indradyumna Swami Mahārāja. It sounds very beautiful. I can even sign books and letters like that, but now I just write "Gurudeva," as I am simply your humble servant. 

Then he gave three names to the big child. He turned to the other boy, who was a little smaller. Balarāma was like a white cloud, and Kṛṣṇa was like a thundercloud—the color of his skin. He was so beautiful, very sweet. He gave him the name Kṛṣṇa. 

There is a certain verse when we hear the name of Kṛṣṇa coming from the mouth of Garga Muni. *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 10.8.13 says: 

"Your son Kṛṣṇa appears as an incarnation every millennium. In the past, he accepted three different colors: white, red, and yellow. And now he has appeared in black color. In another Dvāpara-yuga he appeared as Lord Rāmacandra in the color of a śuka (parrot). But all these incarnations are now assembled in Kṛṣṇa." 

We know that Kṛṣṇa has an infinite number of names according to his different infinite qualities and pastimes. Even with the most modern computer, you could not count the names of Kṛṣṇa. It is said that Lord Ananta-śeṣa speaks a new name of Kṛṣṇa every second from the very moment this world began, and he never repeated that name twice. 

But the name that Garga Muni gave during the naming ceremony where the cows live is considered to be the main name of Lord Kṛṣṇa. This is confirmed by our Prabhupāda. In a very important verse, he writes: "Kṛṣṇa is the original name of the Lord, and all other names are only part and parcel of that original name Kṛṣṇa." He suggests that the name Kṛṣṇa is more powerful than other names, and the result of chanting the name Kṛṣṇa surpasses the result of chanting all other names of the Lord. *Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare, Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma, Rāma Rāma, Hare Hare*. That is why we chant the mahā-mantra. We also chant the *Pañca-tattva* and *Caitanya* prayers so that we receive the mercy of Lord Caitanya, so that we can chant the mahā-mantra in the right frame of mind. Baladeva Vidyābhūṣaṇa in his *Viṣṇu-sahasra-nāma* commentary writes that of all the names of the Supreme Personality of Godhead, Kṛṣṇa is the most important. As confirmed by the Lord Himself, "My dear Arjuna, Kṛṣṇa is the most important of all My names." 

As neophyte devotees, our most important job in this movement is to chant the mahā-mantra. All other activities are auxiliary, to support our chanting. In the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava garden, many of them, when they leave this life, go to Sūrya-kuṇḍa, to Prema-sarovara, or to Varṣāṇā, and they chant 192 rounds. If you wanted to be a disciple of Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura, he had a requirement that devotees chant 64 rounds. Devotees had families, businesses, and jobs, but they all chanted 64 rounds. Once, he gave a very heavy definition that those who do not chant 64 rounds every day are animals. Śrīla Prabhupāda is our *ācārya*. An *ācārya*, according to time, place, and circumstance, can change the rules. Śrīla Prabhupāda said that you can chant 32 rounds. In 1965, the hippies and other people couldn't do it, so Śrīla Prabhupāda said, "Okay, then the minimum is to chant 16 rounds." Rest assured, because even according to our *ācārya* Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas, if we just chant, we can do everything else. Therefore, when you chant, just chant. Rūpa Gosvāmī writes: 

"May my respectful obeisances be given to the lotus feet of those wise entities who have learned the technique of liberation for the unfortunate creatures who are mired in the stinking mud of the ocean of material existence by simply hearing the two syllables of Kṛṣṇa's name. These devotees are overcome with bliss and their bodily hairs stand on end." 

Each one of us can do this. Therefore, when you chant, just chant. It is very simple, like that līlā of the naming ceremony of Kṛṣṇa thanks to Garga Muni. We can see that this līlā is fertilized with the philosophy of devotion as well as *rasa*. We could listen to this lecture again and again, because it is *śabda-brahman*, the transcendental sound. This is how we can always remember Kṛṣṇa, because the most important things in our philosophy are *śravaṇa* (hearing), *kīrtana* (chanting), and *smaraṇa* (remembering). We must always hear about Kṛṣṇa, and thanks to this we can progress. 

This is the way to welcome Kṛṣṇa into our hearts. Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura says in his *Kṛṣṇa-saṃhitā* commentary about how we invite Kṛṣṇa into our hearts: depending on the qualifications of the devotee, at a certain time Kṛṣṇa takes birth in the heart of a devotee. In the heart of another, he steals the clothes of the gopīs. He performs the *rāsa* dance in the heart of another; he kills Pūtanā or Kaṃsa in someone else's heart. He completes this in a very interesting way. When Kṛṣṇa has a relationship with Kubjā in someone else's heart, similarly, if you love someone, that person is constantly in your heart and you think of him in your mind. If we constantly hear about Kṛṣṇa, then this love will awaken in our heart. When the moment comes when we have to leave this world, if Kṛṣṇa is in your heart, you go straight to the spiritual world. You just pass through all these planets that you meet on the way. 

We have quoted a lot of Sanskrit verses today. But in connection with the fact that you go where your heart takes you, I would like to quote the Holy Bible, as yesterday was the day that Mary, the mother of Jesus, ascended back to heaven. It says in the Bible, Matthew 6:21: "For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also." 

Today we had Kṛṣṇa-kathā. Then some of you who do not fast can eat prasādam. That’s okay; those who are preachers and need energy can eat. Usually, when we conclude our speech, we recall our opening remarks. As we celebrate this festival of Janmāṣṭamī, we deepen our faith that Kṛṣṇa is real and the place where He lives is also real. In my public lectures, I always conclude that there is a positive alternative to this world: the spiritual world. That is the goal of human life—back home, back to Godhead. I always say that you cannot see it, you cannot touch it, and you cannot taste it. And how do you know that the spiritual world exists? Sages always answer that it is because people descend from that abode, like our sublime spiritual teacher, Śrīla Prabhupāda. 

You must be able to convince people that there is a spiritual world, that Kṛṣṇa is God, that Kṛṣṇa is a little cowherd boy who plays with other cowherd boys in the sands of Ramaṇa Reti, that He dances with the gopīs, that He plays the flute. We are going there. Do you want to come with us? In the 1970s, when we preached on the streets of San Francisco, we often inserted the mahā-mantra and sang, "We are going home, back to Godhead," and we asked people if they wanted to come with us. They might have grimaced, but in fact, we invite everyone to come with us. 

I will conclude the lecture with a very beautiful poem. I will read it in English:

*If there was a world where you could live forever,*
*If there was a world of endless pleasure,*
*If each word was a song,*
*Would you come along?*
*If there was such a world,*
*If there was a world with flowing streams of nectar,*
*If there was a world where people dance forever with hearts of bliss,*
*Who could resist it?*
*If there was such a world, we can’t deny the nature,*
*About the sky, you never have to die,*
*If there was a world, you could give your love,*
*If there was that to fill up your heart,*
*To souls tied in bonds of love,*
*I know you’d go, I know you’d go there.*
*If there was a world, we can’t deny the nature that is deep within our soul,*
*That longing for a better life where love is not a word but something real.*
*Have you heard about that world beyond the sky?*
*You never have to die.*

*Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa...*""
