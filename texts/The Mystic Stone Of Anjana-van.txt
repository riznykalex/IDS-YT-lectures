"# The Mystic Stone of Anjana-vana

Dear devotees, a warm welcome back to Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma. In our classes this past year, we have been basing a lot of our content on the forests of Vṛndāvana. It is sure and certain that Kṛṣṇa prefers the rural setting of Vṛndāvana to the big cities. The pastoral scene is the playground of God, and the dust of Vṛndāvana—Kṛṣṇa actually confirms that in *Bhāgavatam* 10.24.24, the verse we were quoting some months ago: "My dear father, our home is not in the cities or towns or villages. Being forest dwellers, we always live in the forest and on the hills."

I would like to begin today by setting the scene with two very beautiful verses by Śrīla Prabodhānanda Sarasvatī from his *Vṛndāvana-mahimāmṛta*, Chapter 6:

> "In the abode of Vṛndāvana, please worship Śrī Śrī Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, whose jokes and laughter are as splendid as millions of rising moons. The movements of whose eyebrows create millions of wonderful, transcendental comedians, and whose words are like millions of flowing streams of the cooling sweetness of transcendental bliss. All glories to the fair and dark divine couple who, somewhere in Vṛndāvana, play dice, somewhere playfully hide from each other, somewhere steal each other’s property, somewhere embrace, imitating trees and vines, somewhere ride together on a swing, somewhere speak wonderful riddles, somewhere imitate the movements of birds, deer, and other forest creatures, and somewhere amuse themselves with jugglery and special magic tricks."

Taking a hint from Śrīla Prabhupāda, today I would like to take you on a transcendental, magical mystery tour of Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma to a most amazing forest called Añjana-vana. Añjana-vana is one of the sub-forests of Vṛndāvana. A very famous and nectarine pastime took place there 5,000 years ago—of course, the pastimes are eternal. Remember that there are 12 main forests in Vṛndāvana and 132 sub-forests, and then you have sub-sub-forests and sub-sub-sub-forests *ad infinitum*. But this Añjana-vana is so important, so significant, that our beloved Nārāyaṇa Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī—I will be quoting him again profusely—describes it in his famous *Vraja-bhakti-vilāsa*. In fact, he goes so far as to give a *praṇāma-mantra* for Añjana-vana, which we found in Chapter 11, verse 17:

> "O forest named Añjana-vana, obeisances to you, who is the delightful sporting place of demigods and Gandharvas, and who is the personification of variegated joys."

Nārāyaṇa Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī continues: "One should offer four obeisances while chanting that mantra. He who does so obtains all desires and remains ever-youthful." That is interesting—remains ever-youthful. How do you remain ever-youthful in a world of birth and death? You cannot, but if you chant these mantras and you practice Kṛṣṇa consciousness, especially if you chant the *mahā-mantra*, you will go back home, back to Godhead, where there is no birth, disease, old age, and death.

Then, in order to get the full benefit of visiting this particular forest, he writes: "Perform three *prāṇāyāmas* while reciting the following mantra as you enter into Añjana-vana. Remember all this. He writes on the fourth day of the bright fortnight in the month of Bhādra, a person having gone to Añjana-vana attains the happiness of getting a spouse and variegated happiness."

As we have mentioned a number of times, there is a presiding deity in each and every forest in Vṛndāvana. In the future, Kṛṣṇa willing, when we visit these various forests on our *parikramā*, we should know and remember who the presiding deity of each and every one of these forests is. We should research that and find out why. Well, just like when you visit someone’s home or house, you should know or be aware of who the owner of the house is; it is common sense. Visiting Vṛndāvana’s forests, it is more than common sense to know the presiding deity, because without the blessings of these presiding deities, we will neither see nor hear the actual nature of those transcendental forests.

Nārāyaṇa Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī reveals who is the presiding deity of Añjana-vana. Yes, again in *Bhakti-vilāsa*, he writes the presiding deity of Añjana-vana is Lord Puṇḍarīka. Then, out of his endless compassion, he gives us a *praṇāma-mantra* for the presiding deity of Añjana-vana. This is a day of *praṇāma-mantras*, I guess you could say. He gives it with the *bīja-mantras*. Special mantras have the *bījas*, which is like the essence of the mantra; it has to be there in order to get the full benefit. Like when we chant Gāyatrī, *oṁ bhūr...* the *oṁ* is there first; it is the *bīja*. So this is the *praṇāma-mantra* for the presiding deity of Añjana-vana with the *bījas*:

> "I surrender myself unto Lord Puṇḍarīka, the presiding deity of Añjana-vana."

Now finally—bear with me—he reveals that in this mystic forest, we are going to be telling some amazing pastimes. There is a mysterious lake, a *kuṇḍa*, where Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī and her girlfriends secretly come to bathe from time to time. We know that Rādhārāṇī bathes in Rādhā-kuṇḍa, especially at 12 noon, but there are many transcendental reservoirs of divine water throughout Vṛndāvana, and the inhabitants bathe in all these lakes and streams and so forth. So this is one of her favorite places as well, and it is called Kiśorī-kuṇḍa. Of course, there are a number of Kiśorī-kuṇḍas around Vṛndāvana, but this is one of the more prominent ones. To properly honor Kiśorī-kuṇḍa in this particular forest, the Gosvāmī composed a *praṇāma-mantra* as well. Remember, *praṇāma-mantras* give us the essence to move the *bhāva* of this particular person or place. For this lake, he writes as follows:

> "Kṛṣṇa, obeisances to you, O king of all *tīrthas*. Obeisances to you, who are a delightful place for the bathing of Kiśorī Rādhā. Obeisances to you, who is filled with waters of brilliant reddish and yellow colors. Obeisances to you, who arranges various sports for Kṛṣṇa."

After revealing this mantra, he says: "While performing *ācamana*, dip 13 times in Kiśorī-kuṇḍa while chanting the following mantra: *Yaśodānanda, obeisances to Kṛṣṇa, the pleasure-giver of Yaśodā. Constant obeisances to Śrī Priya Rādhikā. Obeisances to you, O Kṛṣṇa, having a Kiśorī-rūpa. Obeisances to Vallabhā Rādhikā.*" (That is *Bhakti-vilāsa* 11.18).

So we have set the scene. Now, in Añjana-vana, there is a village called Añjanoka—the village of Indulekhā-sakhī. Añjanoka is where she resides. Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī has, as you know, eight very close maidservants who are her age or a little older; they are called *sakhīs*. Those of you from Māyāpura will know better than me; every morning you go to *maṅgala-ārati*, and there is Rādhā-Mādhava, and there are the eight *aṣṭa-sakhīs* on the altar: Lalitā, Viśākhā, Campakalatā, Vidyā, Citrā, Indulekhā, Raṅgadevī, and Sudevī. Now, these eight *aṣṭa-sakhīs* display the topmost love for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, but they are especially inclined towards Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, and they always prefer to serve under her and please her in every way possible. For example, I was reading that whenever the divine couple have a lover's quarrel—transcendental lover's quarrel—these eight maidservants of Rādhārāṇī always take her side in the quarrel, always, without exception.

Indulekhā is described as the sixth *aṣṭa-sakhī*, and I found her *praṇāma-mantra*. This is something quite amazing. I found her *praṇāma-mantra* in the *Sāra-candrikā*—it is a rather obscure *śāstra*, but it is there, it is authorized, in the second chapter, 23rd verse. It is quite an elaborate Sanskrit *śloka*, and I am not at all an expert in Sanskrit, so I will just read the very beautiful translation of her *praṇāma-mantra*:

> "O Śrī Rādhikā, I glorify your friend Indulekhā-sakhī, who regularly celebrates a festival of dance, whose complexion is brighter than *haritāla*, whose clothes are of the complexion of the reddish pomegranate flower, and who wins over the brilliance of the rays of the moon by her effulgence."

Indulekhā is three days younger than Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. She has a tan complexion, and she wears clothes the color of a pomegranate flower, as we heard in her *praṇāma-mantra*, which is bright red in color. She has a very fiery temperament and is contrary by nature. *Śāstra* lists some of her amazing skills, of which we will mention a few. She is learned in the science and mantras of *Nāga-śāstra*—you can guess what that means; it describes different methods for controlling snakes. She is also learned in *Sāmudrika-śāstra*, which describes the science of palmistry, you know, reading the lines in someone's hand. It is like astrology; this is palmistry. When I read that, I thought, "That is unusual." Then I remembered that Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura was also expert in palmistry. I read in *The Harmonist* one time that sometimes his disciples would approach him and put their hands out, "Gururāja, can you read my palm?" So Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī would take their palms, look at them very carefully, and then he would say to each person, "All bad. Just chant Hare Kṛṣṇa." He would read the palm and say, "All bad. Just chant Hare Kṛṣṇa." Anyway, Indulekhā is also expert at stringing different types of necklaces and garlands. She is expert at decorating teeth with red substances, she is expert in the science of gems, and she is also very expert at weaving cloths. She has a very intimate service, which she cherishes probably the most of all her services, and that is carrying messages back and forth to the divine couple. As such, the *ācāryas* say she is fully aware of the confidential secrets of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa.

Now, her closest friends are Tungavidyā and Paṅgudikā, and together the three of them provide ornaments and exquisite garments for Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. And then something really interesting: all three of them stand guard together over the divine couple's treasury. Now, like the other *sakhīs*, Indulekhā has service outside her own village, Añjanoka, in the forest of Añjana-vana, which, by the way, is just next to Varṣāṇā. Her village—actually, Rādhārāṇī's home is Varṣāṇā, it is a big hill—and the *aṣṭa-sakhīs* all have their eight homes around Varṣāṇā. So, of course, the *sakhīs* move all over Vṛndāvana and have various pastimes. One of the prominent places of Indulekhā's pastimes is at Rādhā-kuṇḍa. Each of the eight *aṣṭa-sakhīs* have *kuñjas*, which means small forest groves, surrounding Rādhā-kuṇḍa. Now, I found in *Govinda-līlāmṛta*, Chapter 7, verses 81 to 84, a very beautiful, detailed description of Indulekhā's *kuñja*, which I would like to recite:

> "In the Agni-koṇa (southeast) direction of Rādhā-kuṇḍa is a *kuñja* named Pūrṇendu, which means a full moon, which bestows happiness upon Indulekhā." *Govinda-līlāmṛta* says, "The exteriors of this *kuñja* are decorated with *sphaṭika-maṇi*, which means very clear crystals, and *candrakānta-maṇi*, which means natural moonstones." *Govinda-līlāmṛta* continues, "All the trees, creepers, bumblebees, and birds around this *kuñja* are pristine white in color, and the birds are so indistinguishable from each other that only by hearing them can one make out the differences between them. They all look the same, everything is the same color, they are like white, and the only way you can distinguish between the individual birds is when they sing; they have a different warble, a different sing-song." So, on full moon nights, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa often go there wearing pure white clothes—everything is white—and they have their loving pastimes together in that *kuñja*, and there, Indulekhā serves them.

Now, back to Indulekhā’s village of Añjanoka in the forest of Añjana-vana. It is very, very interesting how this village became known as Añjanoka. The description is given in *Bhakti-ratnākara* by Śrīla Narahari Cakravartī Ṭhākura: how one time Rādhārāṇī was being dressed in beautiful blue clothes by her very close girlfriends, the *sakhīs*. So why blue? Because Rādhārāṇī, more often than not, favors blue saris or blue clothes, blue *cādaras*, because in this way she feels as if she is in Kṛṣṇa’s constant embrace. The *śāstras* say she feels that she is in Kṛṣṇa’s constant embrace because Kṛṣṇa is bluish-black like a monsoon cloud. She also likes to be decorated with blue sapphire jewelry and blue lotus garlands, obviously. 

On this particular occasion, as her girlfriends were dressing her, they dressed her in the cloth and then—it is mentioned—they put cool sandalwood paste on her limbs, and they lightly sprayed her hair with the scent of *aguru* and *ud*. Towards the end of the dressing, they put a dot of musk on her chin. You sometimes see that—our *pūjārīs* in ISKCON, when they dress Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, they put a dot there; it is either painted or they put a dot of musk. They were just about to apply black ointment, *kājala*, to her eyelids—ladies use this ointment like mascara, I guess you could say, to enhance their beauty as well as to protect their eyes. 

The *gopīs* were just about to apply this *kājala* on Rādhārāṇī’s eyes when, all of a sudden, everyone heard the sound of Kṛṣṇa’s flute floating through the air of Vṛndāvana. You have seen pictures or paintings sometimes, right? Kṛṣṇa is playing his flute, and the artist draws these waves as it is going outward through the forest. We all know what effect Kṛṣṇa’s flute has on the *gopīs* because it is imbued with Kṛṣṇa’s love for the *gopīs*; this is his way of calling them, actually, and with that flute, he controls them. For this reason, the *gopīs* sometimes consider the flute to be their enemy because they cannot resist it; Kṛṣṇa controls them, and also the flute has a very unique position—it is always very close to Kṛṣṇa’s lips—so they feel the flute is their opposition. 

As soon as the sound of Kṛṣṇa’s flute entered into Rādhārāṇī’s ears, she became overwhelmed with a desire to be with him, to meet with him, and unable to control herself, she jumped up and ran out of the *kuñja* to meet Kṛṣṇa. She ran out of that forest, and it all happened so fast that her girlfriends were not able to put the final touch, the finishing touch of the *kājala*, to her eyes. Anyway, as soon as she met up with Kṛṣṇa, he happily had her sit in a very beautiful seat—a seat of flowers arranged by, of course, Vṛndā-devī, who facilitates all this, every detail, actually. But when Kṛṣṇa looked at Rādhā, he noticed that she was very nicely dressed and very nicely decorated, but it is described he saw that her eyes were devoid of any *kājala*. 

So, when he inquired from the *sakhīs* what the reason for this was, they just said, "Well, your flute is the culprit, Kṛṣṇa, because it dragged her away, and we could not finish." At that point, Kṛṣṇa smiled, and taking advantage of the opportunity, he placed his finger on a large stone which was within arm's reach. He rubbed that stone slightly with his index finger, and he produced the most beautiful dark *kājala* that the *gopīs* had ever seen. I mean, this was just a stone there, of course, nothing is just ordinary in Vṛndāvana, but this stone produced that black ointment, that black *kājala*, the most beautiful *kājala* the *gopīs* had ever seen. And there was great affection.

He personally applied that *kājala* on the eyes of his dearmost Rādhārāṇī, and *Bhakti-ratnākara* says it so beautifully: "Kṛṣṇa, being absorbed in transcendental mellows, *rasa*, applied *añjana* (kājala) to Śrī Rādhikā’s eyes with the greatest of pleasure." So in Sanskrit, the word for *kājala* is *añjana*. From that day forward, that rock became famous as *Añjana-śilā*. *Śilā* means a stone—like we have Govardhana-śilās and we have Śālagrāma-śilās—so here is an *Añjana-śilā*, a *śilā* of *kājala*. Therefore, the forest where we are describing these pastimes today, Añjana-vana—*vana* means forest, the forest of *kājala*—and in Indulekhā’s village, because that is where her home is, the village became famous as Añjanoka. Isn’t that wonderful to hear these histories? This is real history. 

After applying the *kājala*, Kṛṣṇa held a mirror in front of Rādhārāṇī so that she could see her face, her eyes with the *kājala*. The *ācāryas* say that while Rādhārāṇī enjoyed seeing her eyes that had been anointed with *kājala* by Kṛṣṇa—that is what made it special, why she enjoyed looking at that—Kṛṣṇa enjoyed seeing Rādhārāṇī’s beautiful face reflected in the mirror. So sweet, so sweet, *madhura, madhura*. 

At that place where Kṛṣṇa decorated Śrī Rādhā with the *kājala*, there is a throne; there is still a throne there. We took our *parikramā* party there one time, and there is also, in that little region, a beautiful temple with deities of Śrī Śrī Rādhā-Añjana-bihārī. *Bihārī* means "the enjoyer of Rādhā," be-decked with the *kājala*. Śrī Śrī Rādhā-Añjanī are exquisitely beautiful deities, especially Rādhā’s eyes. Nowadays it is within the actual village of Añjanoka. Please go there. When you are there on your *parikramā*, you can come with us and take *darśana* of Rādhā-Añjana-bihārī. While there, that stone is very prominent, it is right there in the courtyard. Go over there; there are other boulders there, but if you rub your hands there, nothing happens. But go to that particular stone, that boulder, and rub your finger just like this, and mystically, there is this black substance on your finger, very much exactly like *kājala*, actually. That is the mystery—run your finger on the *Añjana-śilā* and put that transcendental *kājala* on your eyes. Boys and girls, it doesn’t matter, because who knows, maybe with that mystic *kājala* you will see Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa just before your eyes. Maybe, because it is something very transcendental: *premāñjana-cchurita-bhakti-vilocanena*. When the eyes are covered with the salve of love of God, one can see Kṛṣṇa as he is. So I am sure that *kājala* will help. *Hari Kṛṣṇa*.

Now, we will move on to another pastime in a village nearby in the forest of Nandigrāma. There was a place where Kṛṣṇa liked to wrestle with his friends; we often hear that cowherd boys are wrestling. So one day, Rādhārāṇī and Kṛṣṇa were visiting that particular setting, and they were sitting and enjoying each other’s association very near to that wrestling arena, when Rādhārāṇī said, "My dear Kṛṣṇa, what is that large square drawn in the sand over there?" And Kṛṣṇa smiled and answered very proudly, "My dear Rādhārāṇī, that is a wrestling arena where me and my cowherd friends dress as wrestlers and engage each other in hand-to-hand combat." So Rādhārāṇī was intrigued, and raising her eyebrows, she asked Kṛṣṇa, "Do you sometimes win?" Kṛṣṇa was surprised by her question—maybe a little bit hurt—so he replied, "Devī, no one knows how to wrestle like I do. I always win, except for the times I pretend to lose to your brother. He is quite a poor sport, a poor loser." So Rādhārāṇī smiled and very calmly replied, "Kṛṣṇa, my brother Śrīdāmā tells me just the opposite. He says that he always wins when you wrestle together and that you never take it very well." So Kṛṣṇa shot back, "Of course he would say that! Of course he would say that!" 

Just then, Lalitā took a step forward and said, "Kṛṣṇa, why don't you show us your wrestler's dress and the moves you use to defeat your opponents?" So shaking his head, Kṛṣṇa replied, "That is impossible. Wrestlers dress only for a live match; we don't model, even if it is for royalty." Lalitā said, "What if someone were to challenge you to a match? Would you accept?" Sakhī, Kṛṣṇa boasted, "I am the son of a king; how can I refuse a challenge to fight?" Then Lalitā said, "Then you should accept my challenge, Kṛṣṇa. I challenge you to fight with my friend Rādhā. She is the goddess of victory, *Jaya-śrī*, and we *gopīs* shall join in the fight as well." That is nicely described. On hearing Lalitā’s words, it made Kṛṣṇa nearly faint in ecstasy. He thought—he pinched himself—"Is this a dream? No, I am awake. My pious life spent protecting cows has now yielded this sweet request from the *gopīs* to wrestle with them. It is incredible." 

But he paused, and finally said, "But how can I, a prince, wrestle with weak, untrained women, especially a very beautiful, delicate princess like Śrī Rādhā? Nope, it would not be right." But then Viśākhā spoke up, "But how can you, O noble one, refuse a challenge? If the people of Vraja hear of it, your father will hide his head in shame." So nodding his head, Kṛṣṇa replied, "I see, I see. King Cupid, the enemy of pure *brahmacārīs* like myself, has sent you ladies to test me. So be it then, so be it. Never let it be said that the son of Nanda ran away from a challenge." Then, while he encircled—he was walking around Rādhā, who by the way covered her smiling face—Kṛṣṇa said to her, "Rādhā, get ready to wrestle!" 

So, giggling and chatting, the *gopīs* ushered Rādhā into a nearby grove, where she and those *gopīs* changed into wrestling clothes. At the same time, Kṛṣṇa selected his most colorful wrestling cloth from a forest storehouse. What is a forest storehouse? Well, we mentioned that many months ago; one of Vṛndā-devī’s services in facilitating these pastimes is that she has different storehouses in different parts of Vṛndāvana’s forest with particular items that can be utilized in the pastimes that take place in those particular places. Each forest, there is a different mood, there is a different pastime, there are different devotees, so she sets up these storehouses; whatever they need for the pastime, just open the door and go in and take it, like, you know, something like *kalpa-vṛkṣa* trees—you can ask a *kalpa-vṛkṣa* tree for an orange, for an apple, for a spoon, for a plate, for whatever. So wonderful, Vṛndāvana! 

Kṛṣṇa took his colorful wrestling cloth from a nearby forest storehouse, and then shortly thereafter, the *gopīs* emerged from the grove they were in just as Kṛṣṇa walked out of the storehouse. When Kṛṣṇa saw Rādhārāṇī and the *gopīs*, he was amazed at their wrestling attire and their hair, which is described was tied neatly in topknots above their heads. Kṛṣṇa was dressed in a golden cloth knotted in the front and the back, and his legs were exposed below the knee, and he wore a bright orange belt around his waist, and his only ornaments were his blue wristbands. The *gopīs* had tied their lower cloths in the manner of *kṣatriyas*, and they also wore bright cloth belts. 

To begin the wrestling match, Kṛṣṇa invited the *gopīs* into the wrestling arena—it gets really blissful now—and he smeared *ghee* on the ground in worship of the Earth. Then he positioned the girls on one side while he stood on the other, and he gave a little speech: "My dear girls, before we begin, let me describe the rules that our wrestling school follows. The rules of our wrestling school: we cowherd boys concentrate on moves that cause great pain to our opponents. Hanumān is our hero, and so we follow the Hanumān-tī school, let us describe Hanumān-tī school delineated," he said, "in the *Mālā-purāṇa*." "In killing demons," Kṛṣṇa goes on, "I have been known to use knee strikes to the chest and punches to the head, along with hair-pulling, girls, and strangleholds." So hearing this, the *gopīs*’ eyes got very wide, and it is described that mild-mannered Citrā-devī leaned over to Lalitā-sakhī and whispered, "What kind of game have you gotten us into? What kind of game have you gotten us into, Lalitā?" 

So Kṛṣṇa continued, "My wrestling style," he said, "is similar to that used by the Vānara king Bālī in his defeat of Rāvaṇa." So at this point, Tuṅgavidyā whispered to the girls, "Now he is comparing us to Rāvaṇa! Oh dear, now he is comparing us to Rāvaṇa! Oh dear!" So Kṛṣṇa continued, "However, because you are all of the weaker sex, we will not engage in the combat wrestling I do with my friends, which I have described. Instead, we will have pastime wrestling, which you can find in the *Mālā-krīḍā*, like *gopī-yuddha*, something like what pastime wrestling," Kṛṣṇa said, "yes, which is recommended for play and amusement." So the *gopīs* sighed—you know, some made a sigh of relief—and then okay, they nodded in agreement, and then Kṛṣṇa joined his palms and directed the *gopīs* to offer prayers to the patron deity of his wrestling school, Śrī Hanumān. He commanded, "You, being women, it is only fair that you fight me all at once rather than one at a time," and he kind of looked to the side and smiled. So he declared, "Enough talk, let the fight begin!" 

Kṛṣṇa stepped into the wrestling arena, he slapped his palms, reached down to rub some earth beneath his hands, bent his knees, and he yelled, "Jai Hanumān! Jai Hanumān! Jai Hanumān!" Then, well, this Kṛṣṇa’s intensity, you could say, actually startled the *gopīs*, so each girl tried to push another girl forward—"You go, you go"—and finally Rādhā was in front with Lalitā and Viśākhā on her sides, and all the other girls were now behind them. So this, how would you say, this *gopī* army slowly started advancing from their side of the arena towards Kṛṣṇa, and as the combat began, Kṛṣṇa wrestled with all the *gopīs* at once when suddenly he slipped out of their grasp and he confronted Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī alone, right in the center of the arena. At that point, the other *gopīs* encircled the divine couple, encouraging Rādhā with cheers of "Victory! Victory! Victory! Victory to you, Śrī Rādhā! Get him! Get him down on the ground!" And as the wrestling match continued, Rādhārāṇī had almost pinned Kṛṣṇa’s shoulders to the ground—I remember in high school I used to wrestle, and you know, the whole point was, if you wanted to win the match, your opponent had to be on his back, and if you could pin both shoulders, it wasn't easy, to the ground, you won the match—so Rādhārāṇī had almost pinned Kṛṣṇa’s shoulders to the ground, which, you know, caused the *gopīs* to cheer even louder. But then Kṛṣṇa escaped, and suddenly, without warning, he reappeared and caught each and every *gopī* simultaneously from behind! He expanded himself, just like we heard the other day when he was with the cowherd boys taking *prasādam* in the forest, he would expand himself so each cowherd boy was thinking Kṛṣṇa is taking *prasādam* with me; he often does that. So Raghunātha dāsa Gosvāmī has wonderfully summed up this transcendental pastime in his *Vraja-vilāsa-stava*, text 93. He says—it is quite a long Sanskrit verse, but he says—"I sing the glory of Nandigrāma, where charming Śrī Rādhā, being eager to wrestle with Kṛṣṇa, dressed herself and her *sakhīs* in wrestling costumes for the pleasure of her beloved Madana. Today, I would like to finish with a beautiful verse about the *kājala* pastime in Añjanoka, the village of Indulekhā-sakhī. It was composed by my scholarly friend, Hari Pārṣada Prabhu from the GBC Śāstra Committee. He lives in Mumbai. The other day, a devotee politely questioned me on the phone, "Mahārāja, you are often quoting new *ślokas* respectfully written by Hari Pārṣada Prabhu, but they are nice, but wouldn't it be more appropriate to quote ancient scriptures, *ślokas* from ancient scriptures or from our *ācāryas*?" So I said, "Yes, that is certainly appropriate," but I replied also, and I said that there was once a devotee named Jayadeva—not our Jayadeva Gosvāmī, this Jayadeva lived in the year 1300 A.D.—and he wrote a book called *Candrāloka*, which essentially is a manual on Sanskrit poetry. But when he started writing *Candrāloka*, many people objected. They said, "Everything else has already been written by previous *ācāryas*. Why do you write a new manual? Is it to prove them all wrong? Everything has already been written by previous *ācāryas*; why write a new manual? Is it to prove them all wrong?" So it is a known fact here in India that every new generation that writes their realizations based on *śāstra* often faces doubt from the older generations—"What is the need for this?" 

So, understanding this, at that particular point in history, Jayadeva wrote in the beginning of his *Candrāloka* book, he wrote: "Readers should kindly not doubt that the opinions of previous great *ācāryas* of poetry will be shown as inferior by this new creation of mine. In fact, their opinions will be highlighted and glorified even more, just as beautiful, doe-eyed ladies appear even more beautiful when a useless black substance named *añjana* is applied to their eyes." That is so beautiful! That is *Candrāloka*, first chapter, verse number five. The idea is that the writings of the previous *ācāryas* are like the eyes of beautiful ladies, and any present-day writer's realizations may be as useless as dark *añjana* (kājala), but when this dark *kājala*—these writings—are applied on the beautiful eyes of the writings of the previous *ācāryas*, their writings become even more beautiful. *Hari Kṛṣṇa*. 

So, we thank Hari Pārṣada Prabhu for his beautiful *ślokas*; they bring out the beauty of the previous *ācārya*'s writings, like *kājala* on the eyes. *Hari Kṛṣṇa*. So I would like to finish with the verse he wrote for this class—it only took him 20 minutes; I asked him because I knew about this Jayadeva and I wanted him to write something, and then I could explain why I am having him contribute in this way on a regular basis. > *Sadā-vasti-kṛṣṇa yāti kṛṣṇatvam.* "The quality of the presence of Kṛṣṇa is eternally existing in the eye of Śrī Rādhikā, yet Kṛṣṇa himself is again bestowing Kṛṣṇa—'Kṛṣṇaness'—blackness into that eye. What could be a greater surprise than this?"
