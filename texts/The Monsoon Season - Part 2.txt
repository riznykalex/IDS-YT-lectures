"# The Monsoon Season - Part 2

Hare Kṛṣṇa, dear devotees. A warm welcome back to our ongoing series on the glories of Śrī Vṛndāvana. We are continuing with our series on the seasons of Vṛndāvana. Hare Kṛṣṇa. So yes, we are continuing with our description of the monsoon season, and this will be part two. What better way to begin describing it than to quote Śrīla Prabhupāda? Vedic etiquette is such that if a disciple hears spiritual truth from the mouth of any devotee, big or small, he must approach his spiritual master and hear the same truths from his mouth in order to realize such knowledge. Narahari Sarakāra writes in the *Kṛṣṇa-bhajanāmṛtam*, and I quote him on hearing teachings from the mouths of other great personalities, and even on understanding these teachings, one should bring them to the guru. And he continues—again, I quote—these teachings should be heard again from one's guru; one should never neglect one's guru. All right, Kṛṣṇa. And Śrīla Prabhupāda also writes in *Guru-vandanā*—we are familiar with this quote—"My only wish is to have my consciousness purified by the words emanating from my spiritual master’s lotus mouth."

So, to begin our second class on the very beautiful monsoon season, I will share with you some things that Śrīla Prabhupāda has said about this rainy season. In his *Kṛṣṇa* book, in chapter 20, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes:

"There are very strong winds blowing all over Vṛndāvana in the rainy season, carrying clouds from one place to another to distribute life-giving water to needy living entities." He says that water is needed after the summer season, and clouds are just like a rich man who, in times of need, distributes his money even to the point of exhausting his whole treasury. In the same way, clouds exhaust themselves by distributing water all over Vṛndāvana in the rainy season. And Prabhupāda goes on: "During the rainy season in the evening, there are glowworms visible at the tops of trees, and they glitter just like lights, but the luminaries in the sky, like the stars and the moon, they are not visible during the rainy season. Small ponds, lakes, and rivulets become filled with water; otherwise, the rest of the year they remain dry. During the rainy season, all living entities in the land, in the sky, and in the water become very much refreshed, exactly like one who engages in the transcendental loving service of the Lord."

Then Śrīla Prabhupāda shares with us: "We have practical experience of this with our students in the International Society for Kṛṣṇa Consciousness. Before becoming students, they were dirty-looking, although they had naturally beautiful personal features, but due to having no information of Kṛṣṇa consciousness, they appeared very dirty and wretched." He uses the word "wretched." "Since they have taken to Kṛṣṇa consciousness, their health has improved, and by following the regulated principles—the rules and regulations—their bodily luster has increased. When they are dressed with saffron-colored cloth, with *tilaka* on their foreheads and beads in their hands and on their necks, they look exactly as if they have come directly from Vaikuṇṭha." Very nice analogy by Śrīla Prabhupāda, how the rains make Vṛndāvana come to life after the hot season of summer; so, in the same way, this Kṛṣṇa consciousness movement is giving life, real life, to the members of this movement. And thank you, Śrīla Prabhupāda, for that.

So, in our research at the Vṛndāvana Research Institute, I came across a really beautiful monsoon season pastime as told by the famous Vaiṣṇava poet Paramānanda Dāsa. The people of Vraja, because it is a famous pastime, call this pastime *Bañjārā-līlā*, or the pastime of a messenger; *bañjārā* means a messenger. So one time, on a cloudy monsoon day, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī was sitting in the late afternoon on her balcony in Yāvaṭ, with all her sakhīs around her. And suddenly, she saw a messenger with a big bag on her shoulder crossing through the village of Yāvaṭ, where she lived with her in-laws and her so-called husband, Abhimanyu. And seeing this messenger walking to the village of Yāvaṭ, she started crying, and concerned, Lalitā said to Śrī Rādhā, "Oh rosebud in the garden of sakhīs, why are there tears in your eyes?" And Śrī Rādhā replied, "Lalitā, I am missing my maternal home. No invitation has come for me from Barṣāṇā for the Teej festival." 

Now, Teej is the third bright day of the monsoon season in which all the ladies in Vṛndāvana go to their maternal home for swinging on a swing. It is a three-day festival; the girls go to their home where their original family members are, and they stay for three days, so we are going to swing. This is the Teej festival. So Rādhārāṇī continued, "My mother and father in Barṣāṇā, they must have forgotten me and my marriage to Abhimanyu here in Yāvaṭ, and my brother Śrīdāmā, who is always busy with royal services, seems to have forgotten me as well." Then Viśākhā spoke up, saying, "Dearest Rādhe, please don't speak like that; your mother and father love you more than their own lives." So Śrī Rādhā said, "Then, dearest Viśākhā, let us send a message to them. Please quickly call that messenger over there who is passing through Yāvaṭ; I am sure he is from Barṣāṇā, and he can take a message to Barṣāṇā."

So Viśākhā was astonished, and she asked Śrī Rādhā, "How can you know that the messenger is from Barṣāṇā?" So Śrī Rādhā replied, "Because of the deep love I have for my village Barṣāṇā, if any Barṣāṇā-vāsī crosses by me, my senses come alive, even if I am sleeping." Wow. So quickly, the sakhīs sent their mañjarīs to that bañjārā, to that messenger, and called him over to see Śrī Rādhā. So the messenger came, and he offered his respects to Śrī Rādhā, saying, "Oh princess of Barṣāṇā, how can I serve you?" So Śrī Rādhā said, "My dear bañjārā messenger, do you know my father Vṛṣabhānu and his wife Kīrtidā? And do you know my dear most brother Śrīdāmā?" The messenger replied, "Dearest Rādhā, the glories of your family are known even in the heavenly planets, and it is true, who is the unfortunate person on this planet who doesn't know your glorious family? And I am from Barṣāṇā myself, so surely I know your illustrious family."

But Śrī Rādhā, she started crying again, and she said to the messenger, "Oh messenger, then please go to Barṣāṇā and tell my mother that I miss her so much, but it appears she has forgotten me. So quickly, then go to my father and tell him day and night his daughter cries in separation from him, and tell my brother that I miss his long talks with me. And then ask all of them, have they forgotten about me?" The messenger smiled at Śrī Rādhā and said, "Rādhā, do not cry. I will run and deliver your messages to your family. Then surely they will come and take you back to Barṣāṇā for the festival that is coming up."

The messenger took off, and when he arrived at Barṣāṇā, he very quickly went to see Mother Kīrtidā, and after paying his respects, he said, "Oh Mother of the most beautiful daughter, Śrī Rādhā has sent a message for you. The Teej festival is just around the corner, and she has yet to receive an invitation to her maternal home here in Barṣāṇā for the swing festival. She fears that you may have forgotten her and she will be condemned to stay in the prison house of her in-laws." We discussed that many classes ago. So hearing this, Mother Kīrtidā Sundarī became very emotional, and gathering many sweets and gifts, she sent Śrīdāmā in a chariot back to Yāvaṭ to bring Śrī Rādhā and her girlfriends to Barṣāṇā.

Now, that evening, upon seeing Śrīdāmā arriving in Yāvaṭ, Śrī Rādhā exclaimed to her girlfriends, "Oh sakhīs, just see, my brother Śrīdāmā is here!" And as soon as Śrīdāmā descended the chariot, she ran forward and embraced her brother, and she asked him, "My dearest brother, how are Mother and Father at home? How are my uncles and aunties? And how is my dear most calf?" *Tuṅgī* is another name for her calf; we generally know it as Tuṅgī, but perhaps this is a nickname. But it is stated there in this beautiful description, "How about my dear most calf, Śrī Mohinī?" So Śrīdāmā replied, "Oh Rādhā, everyone is in bliss, but they miss you so much." Then Śrī Rādhā prepared different dishes by *pūjā* for her brother, and after feeding him, she took permission from Jaṭilā, Kuṭilā, and her husband Abhimanyu, who were a little suspicious; she took permission to leave, and then she and her sakhīs got up onto a bullock cart driven by Sudāmā and left for Barṣāṇā for the three-day Teej festival of swinging.

So hearing the news that Śrī Rādhā was coming for the Teej festival, the whole of Barṣāṇā was there to receive her, to greet her when she arrived. And upon arriving, Śrī Rādhā jumped off the chariot and ran and embraced her mother, Kīrtidā Sundarī, and afterwards she met with her father, King Vṛṣabhānu, who gave her many precious ornaments to wear for the swing festival. So the next morning, upon waking up on the first day of the swing festival, Śrī Rādhā asked her mother, "Maya, where will the festival take place today?" And Mother Kīrtidā Sundarī replied, "My dearest Śrī Rādhā, it will take place on the banks of Prema-sarovara." Many times we have taken our parikrama party there. "It will take place on the banks of Prema-sarovara," she said, "and your brother Śrīdāmā has installed the long swing for you."

So hearing this news, Śrī Rādhā and her girlfriends ran towards Prema-sarovara for swinging. And at that point, Kṛṣṇa, who against all odds is always looking for opportunities to be with Śrī Rādhā, heard she was going to Prema-sarovara, which means the Lake of Love. And then he revealed his mind to his cowherd friend Madhumaṅgala. When he heard she was going there, he said, "Madhu, my beloved Śrī Rādhā is going to swing at the Teej festival, which is being celebrated at Prema-sarovara. Now you are the most intelligent of the brāhmaṇa community, can you think of a way that I can spend some precious time with her there?" 

So, happy at being recognized as the most intelligent amongst the intelligent class, Madhumaṅgala, he thought carefully for a moment and suddenly he came up with an idea—a very interesting idea. He said, "Yes, my dear friend Kṛṣṇa, one of my cowherd friends lives in Barṣāṇā and just got married yesterday. Now it is a custom here in Vraja that the mother-in-law of the new bride takes the new bride to meet Śrī Rādhā, so that Rādhārāṇī can give the new bride her blessings. It is a tradition here in Vraja. So, I have a plan," Madhumaṅgala concluded. "Just follow me."

So then Madhumaṅgala took Kṛṣṇa to meet this newly married couple. But when the mother-in-law of the bride heard that Madhumaṅgala and Kṛṣṇa were coming to meet them, she told her new daughter-in-law, "Don't you dare look at this rascal Kṛṣṇa, who steals the hearts of all the young girls of Vraja!" But when Madhumaṅgala and Kṛṣṇa arrived, this new bride saw Kṛṣṇa out of the corner of her *dupaṭṭā*—the veil the girls wear on their heads—she looked out the corner and she saw Kṛṣṇa, and she became astonished at Kṛṣṇa's beauty and grace, and fainted to the point that no one could revive her for the time being. 

So Madhumaṅgala whispered into Kṛṣṇa's ear, and taking that opportunity, Kṛṣṇa quickly left and, disguising himself as that new bride who had fainted, went to the girl's mother-in-law and said in a very sweet, feminine voice, "Maya, I am ready now to go to Prema-sarovara to get the blessings of Śrī Rādhā for my marriage; that's where she is, with the swing festival. So let's go and get her blessings, that's our tradition." It was Kṛṣṇa saying that in a sweet voice. So now, not recognizing Kṛṣṇa in disguise as her daughter-in-law, the mother and Kṛṣṇa, disguised as the daughter-in-law, left to meet Śrī Rādhā at Prema-sarovara where the swing festival was beginning.

Now, as they proceeded, the mother-in-law instructed her new daughter-in-law, who was Kṛṣṇa, "Now, when we get there, just look at Śrī Rādhā and touch her lotus feet, but don't speak too much, for she is the princess of Barṣāṇā." Now, when the mother-in-law and her daughter-in-law arrived at Prema-sarovara, they saw that Śrī Rādhā and all her friends were wearing green-colored sarīs and red-colored blouses. Why? Because it was the monsoon season, and the Vṛndāvana forest was just blooming with all these beautiful red and green flowers, so they dressed like that. 

So coming up to Śrī Rādhā, this mother-in-law said, "Dear, beautiful Śrī Rādhā, please accept my prayers and respects. This beautiful young lady married my son yesterday, and I am bringing her to you for your blessings so that their marriage will be successful." Then Kṛṣṇa—disguised Kṛṣṇa—approached Śrī Rādhā and touched her feet, and the feet of all her gopī friends, one after another. And after that, Śrī Rādhā said to the daughter-in-law, "Dear one, I give you my blessings. Now please uncover your face; I want to see your beauty and give you some gifts," which was part of the tradition—Rādhārāṇī also gives gifts at that time. But disguised Kṛṣṇa hesitated to remove his *dupaṭṭā*, so Śrī Rādhā said, "That's all right, dear one, I can understand you are shy." Then, turning to the mother-in-law, Rādhā said, "Dear lady, you can go home now, and I will take your daughter-in-law to swing with me."

And then, "I will safely send her back with my friends before midnight." So with the blessings of Śrī Rādhā, the mother-in-law happily went back home, and disguised Kṛṣṇa started walking with Śrī Rādhā to the swing. And as they walked along, Śrī Rādhā began asking this daughter-in-law many questions, but the daughter-in-law kept replying—just, she didn't really reply much. Rādhārāṇī was asking so many questions about her life, and the daughter-in-law was, she wasn't giving any direct answers. And Śrī Rādhā thought, because she is a very chaste and shy girl, this is why she doesn't speak much. 

So finally, they arrived at the place for swinging, and Śrī Rādhā actually invited this daughter-in-law to sit close to her on the same swing. And with the help of Rādhā's sakhīs, the swing started; the swing was being pushed by those sakhīs. And as they pushed it more and more, uncharacteristically, it started going higher and higher and higher—the *pūrṇamāsī* is involved here—the swings that are going higher and higher. And as it did so, this disguised Kṛṣṇa, in a feminine voice, said, "Oh Śrī Rādhā, I'm so scared, please, you hold on to me tightly! Oh Śrī Rādhā, I'm so scared, please, you hold on to me tightly!" 

Now, hearing this, all the sakhīs below were shocked to see how this new gopī dared to give instructions to her highness—we know the queen of Vṛndāvana, Śrī Rādhā—to hold her. It wasn't the proper etiquette to give instructions to Rādhā. So Lalitā, it is so sweet, Lalitā looked up and called out to this new gopī, "Listen, young girl, Śrī Rādhā kindly took you on her swing, and now you think you can give her instructions? Do not misuse her kindness, young girl!" But then suddenly, this new gopī—Kṛṣṇa in disguise—just lunged and embraced Śrī Rādhā, and seeing that, all the sakhīs immediately stopped the swing from swinging and, running forward, grabbed this new gopī and, chastising her, pulled her off the swing. 

But lo and behold, as they did so, a famous flute fell out from this new gopī's clothes. A famous flute fell out from this gopī's clothes, and all the sakhīs and mañjarīs of Śrī Rādhā stood there in astonishment, realizing that this new gopī, this daughter-in-law who had come to Śrī Rādhā for blessings, was actually Kṛṣṇa. And as Kṛṣṇa very happily uncovered his face, Śrī Rādhā became very angry because of Kṛṣṇa's deception, and in a huff and a puff, she left to sit under a nearby kadamba tree in *māna*—in anger. 

Now, at this point, one of our ācāryas points out an interesting fact. It is really interesting. He writes, usually Kṛṣṇa assumes a disguise to pacify Śrī Rādhā when she is in *māna*, so he can get closer to her; he assumes many times a female form or whatever to come closer to Rādhā and appease her *māna*. But in this pastime, the ācārya points out his disguise causes her to go into *māna*. In other words, his disguise causes her to become angry—a subtle point, but very relishable. So anyway, seeing Śrī Rādhā so angry, Kṛṣṇa tried to appease her but to no avail. So then he asked the sakhīs, "Help me console her," but Śrī Rādhā was so upset with Kṛṣṇa's deception she wouldn't listen to anyone. 

Even Lalitā tried, but Śrī Rādhā said to her, "Lalitā, Kṛṣṇa always does these things. You remember last monsoon, when I was swinging on the swing, he came silently from behind me and bound my *dupaṭṭā* to a thorn, tearing it." So then Lalitā, like all gopīs—you know, wanting to somehow bring Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa together—this is what the gopīs, the sakhīs and the gopīs, really want; they want Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa to be together because they are always lamenting in separation. So one of the main services of the sakhīs and the gopīs is to bring Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa together. So in this mood, Lalitā said something extraordinarily beautiful to Kṛṣṇa. It is probably my most favorite part of this pastime. She said to Kṛṣṇa:

"As a river takes shelter of two banks, as a flower takes shelter of two leaves, as a child takes shelter of two parents, and as rain takes shelter of two dark clouds, in the same way, Kṛṣṇa, you must take shelter of Śrī Rādhā's two lotus feet by joining your two palms together, and while shedding tears, apologize to her from your two lotus eyes."

Please allow me to recite that again. She said to Kṛṣṇa:
"As a river takes shelter of two banks;
As a flower takes shelter of two leaves;
As a child takes shelter of two parents;
As rain takes shelter of two dark clouds;
In the same way, Kṛṣṇa, you must take shelter of Śrī Rādhā's two lotus feet by joining your two palms together, and while shedding tears, apologize to her from your two lotus eyes."

So Kṛṣṇa joined his palms together and, with his head bowed, walked over to Śrī Rādhā and fell at her lotus feet, begging for forgiveness. Now, the ācāryas say that at this point, Śrī Rādhā's heart just melted to see the Lord of the three worlds—they say who is her lover—ask for forgiveness. So she gave up her *māna*, as she always does, and while embracing Kṛṣṇa, she said to him, "Kṛṣṇa, let us make this season successful and swing together now on that kadamba tree, where the cuckoos sing the songs of our meetings, where peacocks dance looking at your hair, thinking it is a dark cloud, and where bumblebees hum around us, making an umbrella above our heads."

"Where the cuckoos sing the songs of our meetings, where peacocks dance looking at your hair thinking it is dark clouds, and where bumblebees hum around us, making an umbrella above our heads." True poetry. Then the Divine Couple sat on the swing, and all of Śrī Rādhā's girlfriends took turns pushing the swing. Now, it is said that the swing swung so high that Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa's lotus feet touched the crescent moon high in the sky. And at that point, Śrī Rādhā herself became afraid and held Kṛṣṇa tightly, which became the source of all her happiness and the happiness of all of her girlfriends as well, her sakhīs and mañjarīs of Kṛṣṇa.
