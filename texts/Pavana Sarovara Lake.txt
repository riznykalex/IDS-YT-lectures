"# Glories of Pāvana Sarovara

Hare Kṛṣṇa, dear devotees. Welcome back to our ongoing series on the glories of our beloved Śrī Vṛndāvana. 

*Nama oṁ viṣṇu-pādāya kṛṣṇa-preṣṭhāya bhū-tale, śrīmate bhaktivedānta-svāmin iti nāmine.*

As you know, we concluded our discussions on Yāvaṭ, so we will move on to our next destination in Braja. In that mood, Śrīla Prabodhananda Sarasvatī has a very beautiful verse in his *Vṛndāvana-mahimāmṛta* (1.2). He writes: "I am not strong enough to go to the far shore of the great nectar ocean of Vṛndāvana’s glories; who can go there? However, because I love Vṛndāvana, I will now dip into that ocean. I pray that this endeavor may become successful and bring an auspicious result." We feel the same way as we go on with our discussion today.

Not far from Yāvaṭ—actually within walking distance—is Nandagrāma, where Kṛṣṇa, as we know, spent much of His youth. We spoke at length about Nandagrāma sometime last year in our Vṛndāvana lecture series, but we only briefly touched on a very special holy territory there—one of my favorites, actually—and that is the sacred lake of Pāvana Sarovara. Today, I would like to discuss Pāvana Sarovara more in depth, but first, I think it would be good to give a brief summary of what we covered about Pāvana Sarovara last year to refresh our memories.

We can begin with a very beautiful verse glorifying Pāvana Sarovara by one of our favorites, Śrīla Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura, from his book called *Stavamṛta-laharī*. It is verse number seven from the song entitled *Nandīśvara-aṣṭakam*, and he writes: "May Nandīśvara Hill, which has as its base a lake named Pāvana that purifies and liberates all it touches with the purifying breeze from its shore, delight me."

### The History of Pāvana Sarovara

When Nanda Mahārāja established his residence in Nandagrāma after leaving Gokula—because in Gokula so many demons were coming, and Kaṁsa was sending a demon every day, as we know, to kill Kṛṣṇa—Nanda Bābā decided to move everyone more into Vṛndāvana proper. He established his residence at Nandagrāma on top of the hill, and while doing so, he excavated a very large lake at the bottom of Nandīśvara Hill. That lake became famous as Nanda Sarovara. He and his family, as well as many other Vrajavāsīs, would bathe daily in the waters of that divine lake.

Actually, I was reading that he and his family would bathe on one side of the lake, and King Vṛṣabhānu, the father of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, and his family would bathe on the other side. During that bathing, very often Rādhā would swim underwater to the middle of the lake and have what the ācāryas call their underwater pastimes, where no one could see.

One day in the Nandagrāma palace, little Kṛṣṇa saw Mother Yaśodā making some unusual types of prasādam—not the usual that she would prepare for Him to go into the forest with His friends, like rice balls or *saktu*. She was cooking something different, so Kṛṣṇa asked her, "Maiyā, what kind of funny things are you preparing today?" 

She replied, "Lālā, tomorrow your father is going on pilgrimage, so I am making some fried things that will last for several days." In the scriptures, I was reading it says that she was making little crackers called *maṭhī*. *Maṭhī* is made of fried dal and fried noodles; they become very crispy in the ghee. 

Kṛṣṇa said, "Maiyā, and where is Father going?" 

Mother Yaśodā replied, "Lālā, I am busy cooking; go and ask your father yourself."

Kṛṣṇa went to find His father and asked, "Father, where are you going tomorrow?" 

Nanda Mahārāja smiled and said, "My dear son, I am going to Prayāga to take a bath where the Gaṅgā, the Yamunā, and the Sarasvatī join together. There is that famous *saṅgama* there; when we go to Kumbha-melā, we bathe in that *saṅgama*."

Kṛṣṇa said, "Father, Vṛndāvana is all-auspicious; there is no need to go anywhere else." That is so true, but Nanda Mahārāja didn't take the words of his young child seriously. He said, "Enough talk, enough talk. Now my dear son, I have to attend to my royal duties."

The next morning, Nanda Mahārāja got up early to take his daily bath in Nanda Sarovara before beginning his journey to Prayāga. However, when he arrived at the bottom of the hill, he saw a very effulgent personality—a rather large personality—rolling in the dust of Vṛndāvana, who then got up and, with great awe and reverence, entered into Nanda Sarovara to bathe. Nanda Mahārāja had never seen this distinguished personality in Nandagrāma, so he called out, "Who are you?"

That effulgent personality replied, "Dearest Nanda Mahārāja, I am the King of all holy rivers, Prayāgarāja." 

Nanda Mahārāja said, "And why have you come here today, Mahārāja?" 

Prayāgarāja replied, "I have come here to bathe in Nanda Sarovara because, all year long, people bathe in my waters in Prayāga, leaving their sinful reactions behind. I have come here to roll in the dust of Braja and bathe in this sacred lake to become *pāvana*—purified."

Nanda Mahārāja was stunned, and even more so when he glanced at the other side of the lake and saw many beautiful ladies bathing there. But they were not the usual women who bathed daily in the lake, for these ladies were adorned in beautiful silk sarīs made of gold and silver threads, and they were wearing gold bangles, priceless earrings, and necklaces. The scriptures describe their necklaces as shining like the sun. 

Nanda Mahārāja went around to that other side of the lake and approached these ladies. Very respectfully, he said, "Excuse me, ladies, who are you?" 

One said, "O Mahārāja, I am Gaṅgā." Another said, "Mahārāja, I am Sarasvatī." Another said, "I am Yamunā." Another said, "I am Godāvarī." Like that, the rest of the ladies responded with names of various other holy rivers like Kuru-jāṅgala, Kāverī, Narmadā, Brahmaputra, Mahānanda, etc. 

Nanda Mahārāja asked, "And why have you all come here today?" 

They replied, "All year long, people bathe in our waters and leave us with their sins. We have come here to roll in the dust of Braja and bathe in this lake to become *pāvana*, to become pure."

After seeing and hearing all of this, Nanda Mahārāja, with great reverence, took his own bath in the lake and afterward went back up the hill to Nandagrāma. By this time, young Kṛṣṇa was awake. Seeing his father approaching, he came before him and asked, "Oh Father, when are you leaving for Prayāga?" 

Nanda Mahārāja said, "Lālā, I decided not to go." 

Kṛṣṇa, with a little smile on his face, said, "Oh, and why not, Father?" 

Nanda Mahārāja replied, "Lālā, today all the holy waters personified came here to Vṛndāvana to take a bath in Nanda Sarovara to become *pāvana*—purified. So why should I take the trouble to go anywhere else when they have come here to Braja to bathe?"

From that day forth, Nanda Sarovara became known as Pāvana Sarovara—*pāvana* meaning "purifier." You can just imagine how purifying it is to bathe there. I have had the good fortune on several occasions to go and take a bath in that auspicious lake, and it makes a lot more sense when you know all these transcendental līlās connected to Pāvana Sarovara.

### Sanātana Gosvāmī’s Bhajan-kuṭīra

When pilgrims arrive at Nandagrāma, the rule—the *vidhi*—is that they first take a bath in Pāvana Sarovara before visiting Nandagrāma itself. When Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu visited Vṛndāvana, He also personally bathed in Pāvana Sarovara. 

It was also, as I was reading, one of Śrīla Sanātana Gosvāmī’s favorite places to stay in Vṛndāvana. We know that the Gosvāmīs were wandering throughout Vṛndāvana because one of their assignments from Caitanya Mahāprabhu was to discover the lost places of Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes—the *līlā-sthānas*. They would go some distance and stay the night under a tree, but in their wanderings, they became attached to certain places. Sanātana Gosvāmī was very attached to this Pāvana Sarovara. On the southeastern side of Pāvana Sarovara is a secluded forest in which the Vrajavāsīs at that time constructed a *bhajan-kuṭīra* for Sanātana Gosvāmī.

When Sanātana Gosvāmī first came to Pāvana Sarovara, his presence went unnoticed by the locals because Vaiṣṇavas are very humble. He was so absorbed in loving devotion to Kṛṣṇa that he stayed there and did his *bhajan* without making any real effort to maintain himself. It is written that out of concern for Sanātana Gosvāmī, one day Kṛṣṇa appeared before him at Pāvana Sarovara in the guise of a local cowherd boy carrying a pot of milk. Kṛṣṇa said to him, "Bābā, while tending my cows, I saw you here with nothing to eat, so I brought you some milk. Please drink it, and later I will come back for the pot." 

The *śāstra* says the boy’s speech and beauty enchanted the mind of Sanātana Gosvāmī, and the bliss he felt as he drank the milk convinced him that the cowherd boy who had come and given him the milk was none other than the Supreme Personality of Godhead Himself. While thinking he had acted improperly by not recognizing Kṛṣṇa, a voice came from the sky and told him not to worry, it was okay. The same voice then directed the local villagers to begin providing Swamiji with shelter. Within days, the villagers built him a simple, suitable *bhajan-kuṭīra*. Whenever he was in that area of Nandagrāma—because he was wandering throughout Braja—that is where he would stay. When his brother, Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī, stayed at nearby Tāra-kadamba, they would regularly visit each other. 

Presently, I have seen that near the *bhajan-kuṭīra* of Sanātana Gosvāmī is the *samādhi-mandira* of Akiñcana Kṛṣṇadāsa Bābājī, who was a very close friend and a very dear godbrother of Śrīla Prabhupāda, known for his dedication to chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa. Remember, in the early seventies, when we would come to Vṛndāvana, sometimes we would have the opportunity to associate with Akiñcana Kṛṣṇadāsa Bābājī. He was just a personification of kīrtana. He was elderly, but he would play that drum and sing. 

### Pastimes of the Divine Couple

Five thousand years ago, Pāvana Sarovara was one of Kṛṣṇa’s favorite playgrounds. The ācāryas say that He could be found there almost every day. It is written that in the mornings, before leaving Nandagrāma to go to places like Govardhana or Kāmyavana, Kṛṣṇa and the cowherd boys would first water their cows at Pāvana Sarovara and then drink the refreshing water themselves—liquid nectar. In the afternoon, when Kṛṣṇa would return to Nandagrāma, He would first stop at Pāvana Sarovara to water the cows before returning them to the barns. 

In Kṛṣṇa’s time, Pāvana Sarovara was a place where the Vrajavāsīs gathered on summer evenings. This was the social life of the Vrajavāsīs; in the summertime, they would gather on the shores of Pāvana Sarovara to relax, share stories about Kṛṣṇa, and relish the company of Nanda Mahārāja. 

During these warm summer evenings, Kṛṣṇa would often make small talk with Rādhārāṇī’s husband, Abhimanyu, who the ācāryas say only thought of ways to increase the size of his herd and was therefore oblivious to Kṛṣṇa’s escapades with Rādhā. 

Anytime Kṛṣṇa was milking cows in Nandagrāma, or Tāra-kadamba—because that was also a place where cows were kept—Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, on the pretext of fetching water, would come from either Varṣāṇā or Yāvaṭ to a lakeside watchtower at Pāvana Sarovara. She would climb up into that watchtower and spy on her beloved from afar. King Vṛṣabhānu originally constructed this watchtower on the northern side of the lake so that Rādhārāṇī and her friends would have a place to rest when visiting Pāvana Sarovara. It also served as a storage place for Rādhārāṇī’s cooking provisions. In the summer months, Kṛṣṇa’s breakfast would be cooked, served, and eaten on the banks of Pāvana Sarovara. The ācāryas say the rustic atmosphere there added a sense of freedom to the breakfast. 

### Other Nearby Lakes

A little ways from Pāvana Sarovara is a smaller lake known as Līlā-kuṇḍa. This lake is situated in a forest of banyan trees facing Nandagrāma, and there, each day, the cowherd boys would enjoy drinking buttermilk. Mother Yaśodā would make butter by churning yogurt, and then she would feed her son and His friends the leftover buttermilk, sweetened or salty depending on the time of the year. 

A little further away is another lake where the Gopīs would arrange for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa to meet. This lake is called Sahasī-kuṇḍa, also known as Sārasī-kuṇḍa. The name *sahasī* derives from the word "determined," and the ācāryas say it reflects the mood of the young Gopīs in bringing Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa together in a place so close to Kṛṣṇa’s home. 

One day, the Gopīs managed to bring Rādhārāṇī to Sārasī-kuṇḍa, where Kṛṣṇa was waiting for her. They sat Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa on a beautifully decorated swing and were having *saṅkīrtana*. The Gopīs were loudly singing the glories of the Divine Couple. Mother Yaśodā, who was in the kitchen at Nandagrāma, could hear the kīrtana, and as a result, she blissfully sang along. 

On another occasion, Mother Yaśodā went in search of Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma and found them at this nearby Sārasī-kuṇḍa. The boys were so absorbed in play that they didn't see Mother Yaśodā arrive. When they finally did see her, she called out, "Oh my sons, you are the swans (*sāra*) of my life, one black and the other white." Overhearing Mother Yaśodā, all the cowherd boys decided to call the lake Sārasī-kuṇḍa (Swan Lake). Both names have been used ever since.

### Closing Prayer

We can finish today with a very beautiful prayer by Śrīla Raghunātha Dāsa Gosvāmī from his *Vraja-vilāsa-stava*, the perfect way to end:

> "On the pretext of going to fetch clear water, the lotus-eyed Gopīs again and again meet Lord Kṛṣṇa with great happiness and love at Pāvana Sarovara lake, which is filled with many kadamba trees and the pleasant humming sound of swarms of bees. May that Pāvana Sarovara lake protect us all."

Hare Kṛṣṇa. I hope that has inspired you to come to Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma and visit that transcendental abode. We will continue to wander through the twelve forests and share with you all the unlimited glories of that special place. Thank you, keep well, and I will be back in a couple of days.""
