"### Three Pots of Nectar: Śrīla Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī

Hi, Kṛṣṇa, everyone. Once again, a warm welcome back to our continued lectures on the glories of Vṛndāvana and all the amazing Vaiṣṇavācāryas who spent time there. Viṣṇupādāya! So, today I would like to speak on another Vaiṣṇavācārya whose immense contributions to our Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava tradition have very much defined our Kṛṣṇa consciousness movement—our International Society for Krishna Consciousness—as we see it today and also as it will progress into the future. I feel very honored today to speak about this particular person, Śrīla Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī.

Before doing so, I’d like to acknowledge the sources of my information today so that everyone is assured of its authenticity, as well as to give credit where credit is due for those who researched the matter well before me. First and foremost, I want to acknowledge that a lot of what I will be speaking about today I got from the very śāstra that Śrīla Kṛṣṇadāsa, or one of them, brilliantly composed and wherein he gives some details of his personal life. Those who have read it will know what I am talking about; it’s the great epic, *Śrī Caitanya-caritāmṛta*.

Secondly, I researched a number of topics in the transcendental magazine, *The Harmonist*, which was written regularly in Bengali by Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura under the name *Sajjana-toṣaṇī* in 1879 in Bengal. After Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura passed away, his son, Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī, launched an English version of *Sajjana-toṣaṇī* called *The Harmonist*. It was a magazine which he ran—I think weekly—until he passed away in 1936. *The Harmonist* included articles by Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura as well as those that Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura had—his original articles—and Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī had those Bengali articles translated into English. So, anyway, much of what I will be sharing today will be from what I learned from those articles of Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura. Also, much credit goes to our Vaiṣṇava scholar, Hari Pārṣada Prabhu, from our GBC Śāstric Advisory Committee. His lectures and his discourses on Śrīla Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī were a big help for me in preparing this lecture.

#### Identity in the Spiritual World

Our custom these last two months has been to introduce an ācārya by mentioning their identity and their service in the spiritual world, specifically Goloka Vṛndāvana. In the *Gaura-gaṇoddeśa-dīpikā* by Kavi Karṇapūra, text 167, we learned that, "The Vraja-gopī Guṇamālā-devī appeared as Rāghava Paṇḍita’s sister, Damayantī-devī, and Ratna-rekhā-gopī appeared as Kṛṣṇadāsa—meaning Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī—and Kalāvatī-gopī appeared as Kṛṣṇānanda." So, therein we learned that Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja is Ratna-rekhā-gopī in Vraja-līlā, although a number of scholars write that he is Kaṣṭurī-mañjarī in Kṛṣṇa-līlā. We find this often; they say he is Kaṣṭurī-mañjarī. In my readings, I found out that Kaṣṭurī-mañjarī is 13 years old, she has the complexion—the color—of gold, and she dresses in clothes the color of white crystal, and she very blissfully resides in a part of Raṅgadevī’s grove called Kuṣṭha-kuñja.

So that’s Kavirāja’s position. That’s a name devotees used to address him, Kavirāja Gosvāmī. That’s his identity in Goloka Vṛndāvana. Unfortunately, there is not a lot of information about the life of Śrīla Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī, at least as much as we would all like, and that is true, of course, of many of the ācāryas that we’ve been discussing. But the notion of history in both the Vaiṣṇava and Vedic traditions focuses less on the linear flow, like from one event to another, but more on the defining moments and achievements of great personalities.

We do know that Kavirāja Gosvāmī was born—or we prefer to say appeared—approximately in the year 1507. This is about 10 years after Lord Caitanya’s departure from this world. He appeared in a village called Jhamatpur, which is near Kaṭwā. Kaṭwā was famous as the place that Lord Caitanya took sannyāsa. His father’s name was Śrī Bhāgīratha and his mother’s name was Sunandā. Now, his father, Śrī Bhāgīratha, was a vaidya; a vaidya means a doctor, a physician. And in those days, vaidyas were also known as kavirājas. That’s why his father is referred to as Śrī Bhāgīratha.

The word *kavi* in Sanskrit also means poet, and that is the prestigious title that his son, Kṛṣṇadāsa, acquired. Kṛṣṇadāsa later became Kavirāja—king of the poets—Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī. And we’ll be hearing a lot about how he became king of the poets. Well, to begin with, it’s said that Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī gave us "three pots of nectar." What are these three pots of nectar that he left us? There are three literatures which we know were definitely composed by him—a great gift to all of us. And these three literatures are:
1. *Govinda-līlāmṛta*
2. A commentary on *Kṛṣṇa-karṇāmṛta* named *Sāraṅga-raṅgadā*
3. His magnum opus, *Śrī Caitanya-caritāmṛta*

*Śrī Caitanya-caritāmṛta* is considered the primary literature for Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas on the life and teachings of Caitanya Mahāprabhu. He once declared it his favorite scripture; it was his favorite śāstra. But *Śrī Caitanya-caritāmṛta* was not the first literature or biography about Lord Caitanya Mahāprabhu to be written. In fact, there were several biographies about Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu prior to the *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*. In my research, I found out that the very first biography to be written about Mahāprabhu was Murāri Gupta’s *Kṛṣṇa-caitanya-carita*, which he wrote in or around 1514 A.D. in Sanskrit. Then came the great genius in our Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sampradāya, Śrī Kavi Karṇapūra, who composed two literatures which describe the life of Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu: the *Caitanya-carita-mahākāvya*, which he finished in the year 1543, and a drama called *Caitanya-candrodaya-nāṭakam*, and that he finished in 1553 A.D. Then the illustrious Vṛndāvana dāsa Ṭhākura—I just love to say the names of these devotees—Vṛndāvana dāsa Ṭhākura composed *Śrī Caitanya-bhāgavata*, a favorite of many devotees in our movement, in 1574. And only then came Śrīla Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī, which he finished in 1615 A.D.

#### Writing the Caitanya-caritāmṛta

Now, the question may arise as to why there was a need for Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī to compile another book, another śāstra, about Lord Caitanya when there were already three very authorized and well-read biographies. Well, there’s a reason, and Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja answers that question himself in *Caitanya-caritāmṛta, Ādi-līlā*, Chapter 8, text 71. He says: "In Vṛndāvana there were many other great devotees, all of whom desired to hear the later pastimes of Lord Caitanya." In other words, Murāri Gupta and Vṛndāvana dāsa Ṭhākura and Kavi Karṇapūra had all written about the early pastimes of Lord Caitanya in great detail. But the devotees living in Vṛndāvana at the time of Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī, they wanted more nectar—we always want more nectar—they wanted to hear about the later pastimes of Mahāprabhu, specifically the pastimes that took place in Jagannātha Purī, where the Lord’s associates like Rāmānanda Rāya, Svarūpa Dāmodara Gosvāmī, Sārvabhauma Bhaṭṭācārya, and Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī had so many dealings with those devotees. And these Vṛndāvana devotees, they wanted to hear that nectar.

These Vrajabāsīs were confident that Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī could write such a book about the later pastimes of Mahāprabhu. Why? Because he’d already amazed everyone with his *Govinda-līlāmṛta*. He’d already written that book by this time. *Govinda-līlāmṛta*—I was reading that as an impetus for the Vaiṣṇavas’ meditations. Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī had summarized Kṛṣṇa’s daily eightfold pastimes in a short poem of 11 verses, and that was entitled *Aṣṭa-kālīya-līlā-smaraṇa-maṅgala-stotram*. Kavirāja Gosvāmī presented that, and the Vaiṣṇava community felt that these 11 gem-like verses should be elaborated on. Therefore, Raghunātha dāsa Gosvāmī personally requested Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja to take up that work, to elaborate on those 11 verses, so that he did with *Govinda-līlāmṛta*.

With the completion of *Govinda-līlāmṛta*, Kavirāja Gosvāmī’s literary skills and devotional achievements were fully recognized by Vṛndāvana’s Vaiṣṇavas, who then, after he completed that, requested him to take up another task: to write about the later-day pastimes of Caitanya Mahāprabhu, and that became *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*. As those of you who have studied *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* will know, Kavirāja was initially a little hesitant. He was very humble and he didn't feel qualified to write about the glorious pastimes of Caitanya Mahāprabhu. So what he did—what any self-realized devotee would do—he asked the Lord for help. How did he do that? Well, he was in Vṛndāvana at that time, so he went to the temple of Madana-mohana, the temple established by Śrīla Sanātana Gosvāmī, and he prayed to Madana-mohana for authorization and empowerment to write about Caitanya Mahāprabhu.

As we know, an amazing thing happened. He was standing there in front of the deity Madana-mohana, he was praying, and as he was praying, a garland fell off Madana-mohana’s neck. The pūjārī, who I believe was doing ārati at the time, picked up the garland and he gave it to Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja, and he said, "It’s very interesting, this garland has been given to you by Madana-mohana." In our Gauḍīya culture, this is called *ājñā-mālā*. *Ājñā-mālā* means, quote, "a garland which carries the order of the Lord." I was so pleased to discover that. *Ājñā* means order, *mālā* means garland, so *ājñā-mālā* means a garland which carries the order of the Lord.

He acknowledges this at the end of *Caitanya-caritāmṛta, Antya-līlā*, Chapter 20, verse 99. He says: "The Śrī Madana-mohana deity of Vṛndāvana has given the order that is making me write. Although this should not be disclosed, I disclose it because I am unable to remain silent." Well, thank you. He shared with us that intimacy of acknowledging that the order and the mercy came from Madana-mohana.

#### The Language of the Magnum Opus

Now, it’s very interesting that Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī wrote *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* in Bengali because he was very expert in Sanskrit. He’d written the epic *Govinda-līlāmṛta* in Sanskrit. Why did he write *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* in Bengali? Well, of course, he wrote it for the masses of people who obviously don’t know Sanskrit. Again, I mentioned in another lecture that Prabhupāda said one time, "To master Sanskrit takes 30 years." So he wrote for the general mass of people. Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī once said, "The day will come when people will learn Bengali just so they can read and relish *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*."

He was also very expert in Sanskrit. Hari Pārṣada Prabhu gave me a wonderful example—a Sanskrit verse from *Govinda-līlāmṛta*, Chapter 23, verse number 9—to show what a poet he was, how expert he was in utilizing the Sanskrit language. In this particular verse, Kṛṣṇadāsa Gosvāmī is describing the dance moves of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, and in the Sanskrit description of her moves, we also hear her dancing. The poets say this is extraordinary. Kṛṣṇadāsa is Kavirāja, king of the poets, in both Sanskrit and Bengali. It was actually Jīva Gosvāmī, as per his custom of giving titles of honor to qualified devotees, who gave Kṛṣṇadāsa the title Kavirāja.

#### Leaving Home for Vṛndāvana

Regarding his early life, Śrī Bhāgīratha was a physician. Unfortunately, he passed away when Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja was, I think, six years old, and by that time, I believe the mother had passed away as well. So Kṛṣṇadāsa and his dear brother, Śyāmadāsa, were brought up by some family relatives. But it’s mentioned that because of his father’s passing away when he was so young, Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja developed an intense sense of renunciation in his heart which eventually led him to leave home in search of the absolute truth. But as many of us know, there was a specific incident which acted as a catalyst that propelled him to leave home and go to Vṛndāvana.

He had a very strong difference of opinion with his brother, Śyāmadāsa. Śyāmadāsa had great respect for Lord Caitanya Mahāprabhu but, for whatever reason, unfortunately, had little or no respect for Lord Nityānanda Prabhu—a grave mistake. So one day, Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja scolded his brother for not having faith in Nityānanda Prabhu, and that night, Lord Nityānanda Prabhu appeared to him in a dream and ordered him to go to Vṛndāvana. He said: "Do not be afraid. Go to Vraja, for there you will attain all things." 

When Kavirāja Gosvāmī woke up the next day, he immediately gave all his possessions away and happily left for Vṛndāvana. It’s mentioned in *Prema-vilāsa* that when he reached Vṛndāvana, he immediately took shelter of Śrīla Raghunātha dāsa Gosvāmī. The spiritual master is the revealer of the Dhāma. As soon as Kavirāja Gosvāmī got to Vṛndāvana, he searched out Raghunātha dāsa Gosvāmī, accepted him as guru, and eventually took initiation from him. After taking initiation, he mainly lived at Rādhā-kuṇḍa doing bhajan and his sevā, writing *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*. 

#### Philosophical Depth and Legacy

One reason why *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* is so popular is that it is interspersed with perfect siddhāntas, or philosophical conclusions, as given to us by the Gosvāmīs of Vṛndāvana. Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī had the chance to hear these truths and philosophies from five of the six Gosvāmīs in Vṛndāvana. All the other biographies of Caitanya Mahāprabhu were mainly līlās, or pastimes—which, of course, were wonderful—but Kavirāja Gosvāmī made *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* a biography which had philosophy in it.

Some scholars say that Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī departed this world in 1617, which would mean that he left at the age of 87. There are two samādhis of Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī: one is at Rādhā-kuṇḍa and the other is at the Rādhā-Dāmodara temple in Vṛndāvana. The samādhi at Rādhā-kuṇḍa is a *vapu-samādhi*, meaning this is where his form is; the samādhi at the Rādhā-Dāmodara temple is a *puṣpa-samādhi*, which means some of his artifacts are kept there.

I would like to conclude with some very beautiful words of Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī from one of the *Harmonist* magazines in the 1930s:

> "If it were to happen that all the books in the world were destroyed, leaving only the Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam, then the people of this world would still be able to achieve the ultimate goal of life. Even if the Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam were lost, leaving only the Caitanya-caritāmṛta, there would be no loss to humanity, for that which has not been revealed in the Bhāgavata is found in the Caitanya-caritāmṛta. The supreme absolute truth is Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, the combined form of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. The Caitanya-caritāmṛta is His sound incarnation."

Let us help these ācāryas by sharing our good fortune with anyone and everyone we meet, thus giving them the greatest pleasure. Thank you so much.""
