"Hare Kṛṣṇa, dear devotees. Today, we are sitting in a very sacred place, the home of Lord Caitanya’s uncle, Candraśekhara Ācārya. Many of the youthful pastimes took place right here. Śacīmātā often sent Nimāi to this courtyard, and in these very lanes, the waves of saṅkīrtana first began to rise. Later, Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura called this spot "Vṛndāvana within Māyāpura." Here, he installed Rādhā-kuṇḍa, Śyāma-kuṇḍa, and Giri Govardhana, saying, "Mahāprabhu has revealed His Vraja so that the world may taste its fragrance." 

It is our good fortune that we are also sitting next to the samādhi-mandira of Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī. Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta’s praṇāma-mantras describe his whole life: *gauravāṇī-pracāriṇe*—spreading the message of Gaurāṅga. From childhood, he displayed extraordinary intelligence. At six, he memorized volumes of Sanskrit. At 12, he was debating scholars. As a young man, he mastered astronomy and mathematics. The King of Tripura appointed him as court historian and offered him a pension for life. He could have been famous, wealthy, and honored; yet, he walked away from it all to assist his father, Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, in reviving the lost current of Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavism. The external world called it renunciation; he called it obedience.

At that time, the spiritual landscape of Bengal was in ruins. British missionaries mocked the faith. Impersonalism was fashionable. Caste pride had strangled the saṅkīrtana movement. Professional reciters and cheap imitators had stolen the attention of the people. Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura prayed, "Oh Lord, send me a ray of Viṣṇu to restore Your path." That ray was Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī. He received dīkṣā from Śrī Gaura-kiśora dāsa Bābājī, a saint who owned nothing and feared no one. Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī later said, "What I learned from my Gurudeva surpassed all my academic education." 

But in 1914, both his father and his spiritual master departed within months of each other. It was a crushing separation. Left alone, he sought guidance in their instructions: "Preach, revive Navadvīpa-parikramā, print and distribute literatures, establish the Nāma-haṭṭa. Never retreat into solitary bhajana. Make Māyāpura shine brighter day by day."

Living here in this very spot, he prayed for direction. One morning, a page of *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* blew against his hut. On that page, Sanātana Gosvāmī receives four instructions from the Lord: write books, establish holy places, worship the Lord, and teach bhakti-rasa. Bhaktisiddhānta took it as a direct order. Soon after, he had a vision of his father, of Gaura-kiśora dāsa Bābājī and Jagannātha dāsa Bābājī, of the Pañca-tattva, and the Six Gosvāmīs. They all said, "Distribute Gauravāṇī with full strength. No obstacle can stop you. We are with you." With that blessing, he vowed to chant 1 billion holy names—around 192 rounds daily—for nine years. During those years, he still worked, wrote, and trained devotees. When asked where he lived, he would smile, "In Vṛndāvana." In Vṛndāvana, because for him, this place where we are now was Vṛndāvana. 

After fulfilling that vow in 1918, he took tridaṇḍi-sannyāsa before a photo of his guru the day before Gaura-pūrṇimā. Some criticized him: "How can one take sannyāsa before a picture?" He replied, "If the Lord can appear in a picture to accept our worship, surely my guru can appear to give me this vow." He knew that to protect the mission, he would need an order respected by society. That single act of courage opened the door for the preaching explosion that followed.

He founded the Gauḍīya Maṭha, initiated disciples by quality not caste, and organized vast Navadvīpa-parikramās with thousands of pilgrims, elephants, and hundreds of mṛdaṅgas. He used printing presses as his temples, newspapers as his pulpits, and scholars as his audience. Within two decades, more than a hundred centers carried the flag of Rūpānuga-bhakti. To the sentimental, he appeared strict. To the thoughtful, he was mercy itself. When he debated scholars, his words were sharp, but his heart was soft. In private, he prayed, "May I never desire anything but the highest good for my worst enemies." This combination of thunder and compassion reflected Lord Nṛsiṃhadeva’s own mood—cutting falsehood, protecting devotion. He repeatedly warned his disciples, "An institution is only a vehicle. If you forget the purpose, the vehicle becomes a prison." Yet he also said, "Without organization, how will you spread the name?" His genius was balanced: principle without rigidity, structure without pride. 

Toward the end of his manifest pastimes, he gave his famous instruction: "Knock on every door. Carry the current of bhakti-vinoda to the whole world. The West must receive Mahāprabhu’s mercy. One of my disciples will cross the ocean." And that disciple, of course, was His Divine Grace A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupāda.

Today, every temple, every chanting party, every devotee across the globe is fruit from that seed sown right here. So when we sit in this courtyard, we’re not tourists; we are heirs of a revolution. From here, the current of pure devotion began to move again after centuries of stagnation. From here, books, kīrtanas, and fearless preachers flowed out like sacred rivers. And from here, by the power of his vow and his compassion, the door to the entire world opened. Let us take a moment to remember that our own service, whatever it may be, stands on the foundation he laid. If we can keep even a fraction of his determination and purity, the current will continue to flow through us.

Now, as the conch sounds and the lamps are offered, let us offer our gratitude to Śrī Śrī Rādhā-Mādhava, Lord Gaurāṅga, Lord Nityānanda, to Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, and Śrīla Gaura-kiśora dāsa Bābājī, and to our param-guru, Śrīla Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura, whose vision made this moment possible. Let us thank our own beloved Śrīla Prabhupāda who carried that mission across the vast ocean. Gaurāṅga! Hari! Hari! Hari! Bol!""
