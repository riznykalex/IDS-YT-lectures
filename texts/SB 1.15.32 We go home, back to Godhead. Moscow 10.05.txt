"### SB 1.15.32: We Go Home, Back to Godhead

I remember singing, "Full of grace, Vāsudeva, on grace," singing to the highway, maybe a good questionnaire... We read the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, Canto 1, Chapter 15, Text 32. Regarding the Yadu dynasty, mother, and all souls, and regarding *ātma-vicāra* (self-inquiry), regarding Bhagavān Himself, and the classic mathematics of the soul—my lecture, and also on April 1st—thinking about the *Bhagavad-gītā*, the way of the manifestation of His appearance and departure. The *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* itself, the end of the dynasty, and also about His abode. Lord Kṛṣṇa drew attention to whether His brother is one, but he did not give; he is a lonely thinker, King Yudhiṣṭhira.

To convey His trace, the manifestation of His pastimes, we decided to sit down. Translation: "Learned that Lord Kṛṣṇa returned to His abode and, realizing that the earthly manifestation of the Yadu dynasty had come to an end, Mahārāja Yudhiṣṭhira decided to return home, back to God." 

Morality—one should draw attention to the instruction of the *Bhagavad-gītā* after God left the earth, His abode in the spiritual heaven. God and those who are eternally immersed in transcendental loving service to Him remain eternally young, for there is no old age, no disease, and no death. And since there is no death, there is no birth. Therefore, Mahārāja Yudhiṣṭhira also began to think about returning to God. God appears on Earth or any other mortal planet along with His companions who live with Him eternally, and members of the families of the devotees who helped in the pastimes are also His eternal companions, as well as Mahārāja Yudhiṣṭhira with his brothers, mothers, and so on.

This verse describes the great determination of one of the greatest devotees in history, Mahārāja Yudhiṣṭhira, to return home, back to God. Such intensity! And we should be inspired by his example of our determination to return home to God. Everything that pure devotees do should be an example for us. Because their birth is unusual, everything they do during their life is unusual, and the departure of such a devotee from this world is also unusual. The place where a pure devotee goes after leaving the body is also not ordinary; everything related to a pure devotee goes beyond common understanding.

For example, Śrīla Prabhupāda was born immediately after the appearance of Kṛṣṇa, and it is completely unusual that we can celebrate the day of the appearance of Śrīla Prabhupāda immediately after the day of the appearance of Kṛṣṇa. His family, his father, grandfather, and great-grandfather—all were pure devotees; this is also a very rare phenomenon. Śrīla Prabhupāda’s life was completely unusual because he told us, "There was not a single moment in my entire life when I forgot the lotus feet of Lord Kṛṣṇa." He also told us, "In my entire life, I never committed a single sin." And when the time came for Śrīla Prabhupāda to leave this world, he did not mourn his imminent departure; he happily took this opportunity to leave the body and returned to Śrī Vṛndāvana.

Those who live in Vṛndāvana say that birth in Vṛndāvana guarantees a return to the Godhead. Life in Vṛndāvana guarantees a return to Goloka. Death in Vṛndāvana definitely ensures a return to Vaikuṇṭha. You teach us how to practice Kṛṣṇa consciousness, and we must follow the example of great devotees. Spiritual science is not as complicated as material science. We just need to follow the example that a pure devotee shows with his birth, his life, and his leaving of the body. This is exactly what Kṛṣṇa says in the *Bhagavad-gītā* (3.21): *yad yad ācarati śreṣṭhas tat tad evetaro janaḥ*—"Whatever action is performed by a great man, common men follow in his footsteps. And whatever standards he sets by exemplary acts, all the world pursues."

Does Kṛṣṇa mean, by pronouncing this verse, so-called great personalities in sports, in the entertainment industry, or in politics? No. He says, in fact, great personalities like Yudhiṣṭhira, the pure devotees. Therefore, we should study the biographies of the greats. Sometimes, traveling around the world, out of curiosity, I ask people, "What is your favorite book?" 

I once lived with a family in London. I asked the father of the family, "What is your favorite book?" He said, "My favorite book is the *Bhagavad-gītā*." His wife smiled and said, "I like it more than him." Their teenage daughter, who was studying to be a chemist, said, "I like it more." Another 12-year-old son was sitting on the couch reading something new, and he said, "I like it more." 

Therefore, our tradition has many saints in the past. We have saints in the present. I am sure that in the future we will also have many holy personalities. Sanātana Gosvāmī says that just like bell metal can be turned into gold through a chemical process, an ordinary person can become a Vaiṣṇava through training and preparation.

Once, Śrīla Prabhupāda was sitting with his students in Māyāpura, and they were repeating japa. Suddenly, one of the students said, "I want to be like you." Everyone looked at him with condemnation, as if they wanted to say, "You can't say such things; you can't strive to become like a spiritual teacher." But Prabhupāda took it differently. He said what he said was perfectly acceptable because, for a student, a spiritual teacher is an ideal example of a devotee. Therefore, a student strives to become like a spiritual teacher.

Yes, a spiritual teacher is sometimes called an *ācārya*. An *ācārya* is one who teaches by example. And the student is the one who follows. Therefore, Prabhupāda was happy. He patted him on the shoulder and said, "I bless you that you are achieving that." In this verse, we emphasize how much we must strive to achieve the highest goal, *param gatim*, to return back to God. Yudhiṣṭhira showed an example; after Kṛṣṇa left this world, after a very short time, a few years later, he also returned home to God because he was not interested in being here without Kṛṣṇa.

One of my exalted spiritual brothers, Govinda Mahārāja, said that there exist two categories of pure devotees in this world. The first category comes from the spiritual world to feel compassion for conditioned souls and comes independently to help them, just as, for example, Jesus Christ came. But another category, those who leave the spiritual world of Vaikuṇṭha, comes here to Earth. These are those whom the Supreme Lord Kṛṣṇa Himself asked to come and preach Kṛṣṇa consciousness. An example of the first category is Jesus; an example of the second category is our Śrīla Prabhupāda.

One fine day, when Śrīla Prabhupāda was in Vṛndāvana, Kṛṣṇa turned to him and said, "I want you to preach Kṛṣṇa consciousness in the material world." Prabhupāda did not answer, "Oh Lord, I don't really like asceticism; the material world is very ascetic." But Kṛṣṇa replied, "Don't worry, just write books about the science of Kṛṣṇa consciousness and return to the spiritual world. I will organize everything else."

When Prabhupāda was to visit South Africa, it was necessary to get vaccinated against yellow fever. He flew to Nairobi, Kenya. After he finished his sermon in Africa, he returned to India, and at the border, immigration officers asked him for his yellow fever vaccination certificate. He said it was with the secretary, but the secretary said, "I left it in the office in Kenya." The immigration officer said, "Well, we apologize, we cannot let you in the country in this case; you must stay in the quarantine room for 15 days so that we can be absolutely sure that you do not have yellow fever."

Śrīla Prabhupāda, the *ācārya* of the International Society for Kṛṣṇa Consciousness, was forced to cancel his entire sermon and all his trips and spend 15 days in quarantine, and all he could do was chant the Hare Kṛṣṇa mantra. On the 15th day, he said to his secretary, looking out the window, "I didn't want to come to this terrible place." The servant said, "I apologize very much for my mistake." Prabhupāda meant, "I didn't intend to come to this terrible place—I mean, in general, the entire material world. I didn't want to come here. Kṛṣṇa told me, 'Go, just write books, I'll organize everything myself, the rest.' And I left everything."

But in order to set an example for us, he talked about how he treated the material world. He said, "This is a terrible place." We treat it as if, "Well, of course, the place is terrible, but still there are pools and parks and lawns, and the sun is not bad, the light is good." But Kṛṣṇa-dāsa Kavirāja says that everything you see in this world is bad; so the concepts of "good" and "bad" are not applicable to the material world. Is everything really so strict? Maybe there's something good? No, everything is bad. 

It's very important for us to understand that our perception is imperfect, and therefore we must perceive everything through the way the *guru*, *sādhu*, and *śāstra* see it. Only after that can we develop the determination to return home, back to God. 

The material world is often described as a distorted reflection of the spiritual—an inverted reflection. Like the reflection of apple trees on the riverbank. You see reflected apples and they seem very tasty to you. Some animal can approach the water and try to eat an apple, but instead of an apple, it takes a mouthful of water. In the same way, we try to enjoy the material world, but there are no pleasures here. The spiritual world is eternal, full of knowledge and bliss. Unlike the spiritual world, the material world is not eternal; it is filled with ignorance and suffering. Who wants to receive eternal life, full of knowledge and bliss? And who wants to temporarily live in a world full of ignorance, stupidity, suffering, tears, disappointment, and depression? We are going home!

Sometimes the material world is compared to an imitation of the spiritual. Just as in China they make counterfeits of branded goods, in the material world, everyone is deceived by counterfeits. 

In this world, we can see some emotions, some love, some relationships, but all this is only a reflection of true spiritual relationships. Therefore, material relationships never satisfy you. The spiritual world exists—a place where life is eternally full of knowledge. Its inhabitants come from there, and they come to us either out of compassion or at the request of the Lord, and they tell us about the process of returning there. 

Different traditions are given at different times, but the essence of their message is absolutely true. Kṛṣṇa came five thousand years ago. Jesus Christ appeared two thousand years ago. There were also others, and their very lives prove that they came from a special world because they were special individuals. Their very existence is proof. 

Even if you've never been to America, when you see Americans, you understand that these people are not quite Russian because they don't really speak Russian. You can understand that America exists because Americans come from this country. Looking at the devotees, you can see that Vaikuṇṭha exists.""
