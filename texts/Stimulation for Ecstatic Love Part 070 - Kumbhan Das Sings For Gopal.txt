### Stimulation for Ecstatic Love Part 070: Kumbhan Dās Sings For Gopāl

Hare Kṛṣṇa, everyone. Welcome back once again to our ever-ongoing series on the glories of our beloved Śrī Śrī Kṛṣṇa. All glories to Śrīla Prabhupāda. We are continuing with our miniseries on "Stimulation for Ecstatic Love," and this will be part 70. As you may remember, we have been discussing various Vaiṣṇava poets who inspire our devotion for the Lord, and last week we spoke about the illustrious Sūradās. While doing so, you may remember we mentioned another poet who was a close friend of Sūradās, whose name was Kumbhan Dās. We mentioned at that time that he is famous as "another gem amongst Vraja-bhāṣā poets." 

Actually, both Sūradās and Kumbhan Dās were part of the famous eight Aṣṭa-chhāpa poets in the Vallabhācārya-sampradāya. They are very well known in the Vallabha-sampradāya, particularly these eight. Vallabhācārya was present in Vṛndāvana when Caitanya Mahāprabhu visited there in the year 1515. Devotees who are well-versed in the *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* know that there are some differences of opinion between Lord Caitanya and Vallabhācārya that exist between their followers even down to the present day. But nevertheless, Lord Caitanya and our Śrīla Prabhupāda maintained great respect for Vallabhācārya. 

In *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*, Madhya-līlā chapter 19, verses 62 and 63, Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja Gosvāmī writes about how Lord Caitanya once received Vallabhācārya. At that time, Śrī Vallabhācārya was staying in Āḍail, and when he heard that Caitanya Mahāprabhu had arrived, he went to His place to see Him. 

> *tāṅre daṇḍavat kailā prabhu kṛpā-ālingane*
> *ghana-dṛṣṭi-kṛpā-hāsye kaila kuśala-praśne*

Vallabhācārya offered Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu his obeisances, and the Lord embraced him. After that, they discussed topics about Kṛṣṇa for some time. Now, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes in the purport to verse 62:

"Vallabhācārya was a great learned scholar of Vaiṣṇava-viśiṣṭādvaita. In the beginning, he was very much devoted to Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, but since he thought he could not receive proper response from Him, he later joined the Viṣṇu Svāmī sect and became the ācārya of that sect. His sect is celebrated as the Vallabhācārya-sampradāya. This sampradāya has had great influence in Vṛndāvana, near Gokula, and in Bombay."

Śrīla Prabhupāda goes on to give a long, detailed description therein of Vallabhācārya's life, concluding with: "Vallabhācārya had two sons, Gopīnātha and Viṭṭhalanātha, and in his old age he accepted the renounced order of life. In 1530 he passed away from the material world at Vārāṇasī. His book known as *Ṣoḍaśa-granthā* and his commentaries on *Vedānta-sūtra* (*Aṇubhāṣya*) and *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* (*Subodhinī*) are very famous. He wrote many other books besides."

So again, there are some outstanding differences between the Gauḍīyas and the Vallabhas, but Śrīla Prabhupāda has instructed us to maintain respect on this issue. For example, on November 25th, 1976, Prabhupāda wrote a letter to my dear Godbrother, Girirāja Svāmī, who was then Girirāja Dāsa. He wrote:

"My dear Girirāja, please accept my blessings. I am in due receipt of your letter. We can't rectify what is written in *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*. We have no bad feeling or disrespect for Vallabhācārya. We consider ourselves the most obedient servants of Vallabhācārya. The exchange between Lord Caitanya and Vallabhācārya was on friendly terms. We learn from *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* that in the beginning Śrī Vallabhācārya criticized Śrīdhara Svāmī, and then Caitanya Mahāprabhu criticized Vallabhācārya. Such kind of friendly criticism you will always find amongst learned scholars, but that does not mean any ill feeling with one another. Such things will go on in this world, but that does not mean we have got any disrespect for any Vaiṣṇava-ācārya. Hoping this meets you in good health. Your ever-well-wisher, A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami."

It is quite clear. Śrīla Prabhupāda also writes in *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 1.1.1:

"Within the past 500 years, many erudite scholars and ācāryas like Jīva Gosvāmī, Sanātana Gosvāmī, Viśvanātha Cakravartī, Vallabhācārya, and many other distinguished scholars, even after the time of Lord Caitanya, made elaborate commentaries on the Bhāgavatam, and the serious student would do well to attempt to go through them to better relish the transcendental messages."

So, following Śrīla Prabhupāda's lead, we may also respect, admire, and benefit from the illustrious poetry of one of Vallabhācārya's disciples, Kumbhan Dās, as we will do today.

Now, this great devotee, Śrī Kumbhan Dās, was born on the 11th day in the month of Caitra (March-April) in the year 1499 in a village called Jamunāvaṭa, near Govardhana. It's interesting: his parents were farmers, and when his parents passed away, he married and took over the responsibility of the farm. From the beginning of his life, I was reading, I could see he was a very serious devotee. It's written that he ate only grains from his own field, he used a bowl of water for a mirror, and he shunned everything that was not directly related to the Lord. 

One day, when he learned that a great person, an ācārya named Vallabhācārya, had come to the nearby village of Aṃṭhaura at Govardhana Hill, he and his wife went to meet Vallabhācārya. During the meeting, Kumbhan Dās's wife said to Ācārya, "Oh Mahārāja, you are very exalted and a great Vaiṣṇava, please bless me with children." 

So, Vallabhācārya smiled and said, "By the grace of Govardhana Gopāla, you will have seven boys." Hearing this, Kumbhan Dās looked at his wife with some disappointment and said, "Dear one, what have you asked of the guru? Children! You could have asked him for the Lord himself, for he is capable of giving Him to you." 

Wow. So Kumbhan Dās's wife said shyly, "I asked for what I wanted. Now, you ask for your Lord." So Kumbhan Dās very humbly joined his hands and prayed, "Dear Master, please bless me with the service of Gopāla. Please bless me with the service of Gopāla." 

Now, he was referring to the deity discovered by Mādhavendra Purī on Govardhana Hill. Śrī Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja writes in *Madhya-līlā* 4.1:

"I offer my respectful obeisances unto Mādhavendra Purī, who was given a pot of sweet rice stolen by Gopāla, celebrated thereafter as Kṣīra-corā-Gopīnātha. Being pleased by Mādhavendra Purī’s love, Śrī Gopāla, the deity at Govardhana, appeared to the public vision."

So there we hear about Gopāla, and in the purport, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes: "Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura says that this Gopāla deity was originally installed by Vajranābha, the great-grandson of Kṛṣṇa. Mādhavendra Purī rediscovered Gopāla and established Him on top of Govardhana Hill. This Gopāla deity is now situated at Nāthadvāra and is under the management of the descendants of Vallabhācārya. And as most of us know, He's called Śrī Nāthajī, but originally He was Gopāla."

So at that point, after hearing the request of Kumbhan Dās to worship Gopāla, Ācārya smiled and said, "Kumbhan Dās, go now to the Jamunā River and take a bath. When you come back, I will initiate you into the service of Gopāla." 

So Kumbhan Dās left to bathe in the Jamunā, and when he returned, Vallabhācārya initiated him in his ceremony right next to Govardhana Hill. And at the initiation, he said, "Kumbhan Dās, from this day forward, because you write such beautiful poetry and you're such a good singer, you should sing for Gopāla the eight-fold daily pastimes of the Lord, and eat only after having His darśana." Got direct instruction from his spiritual master: "From this day forward, you should sing for Gopāla the eight-fold daily pastimes of the Lord, and eat only after having His darśana." 

So it's interesting to note here that by Vallabhācārya's blessing, Kumbhan Dās and his wife did eventually have seven children—all sons. However, I was reading that Kumbhan Dās, although a very responsible householder, was completely devoted to Gopāla, and thus he was, you could say, internally detached from family life. Once, he was asked, "Kumbhan Dās, how many sons do you have?" He replied, "One and a half. One is Catur-bhujadāsa, and the half is my son Kṛṣṇadāsa. Catur-bhuja stays near Gopāla, worships Him, does sevā, and experiences the joy of devotional service. And my son Kṛṣṇadāsa, he takes care of cows and comes daily to have darśana of Gopāla, but he has yet to taste the deep nectar of pure devotion. My other sons are of no use to Gopāla in any way, so their existence is equal to non-existence." Pretty strong! 

Unfortunately, one day, when his son Kṛṣṇadāsa had taken the family cows to the forest for grazing, suddenly a tiger attacked the cows, and when Kṛṣṇadāsa ran forward to protect them, the tiger attacked him, and he died. On hearing this incident, instead of expressing deep grief, Kumbhan Dās said, "Though I am missing my dear son today, I am extremely proud that he gave up his life protecting Gopāla's cows."

So, Kumbhan Dās took the instruction of his spiritual master, Vallabhācārya, to heart, and every day he would come to sing bhajan for Gopāla in His temple. Bhajan that he wrote—you could say poems that he wrote—he would sing for Gopāla every day. Actually, in his lifetime, Vallabhācārya requested each of these eight famous Aṣṭa-chhāpa poets in his line to sing daily for Gopāla. And so, it was arranged that each would sing a different rāga or melody in different tālas or rhythms at specific times of the day, depicting a particular līlā and its mood at that time of the day. 

The poet Paramānanda Dās would sing for the Maṅgala Āratī. Nandadāsa (we're mentioning these poets here) would sing for the śṛṅgāra ceremony. Govinda Svāmī would sing for the ḍālā ceremony. Kumbhan Dās would sing for the Rāja-bhoga Āratī. Sūradāsa would sing for the utthāna ceremony. Catur-bhuja would sing at a time when bhoga was being offered, and Chitta Svāmī would sing melodies for the sandhyā Āratī. Hare Kṛṣṇa, oh, there's one more, yes—Kṛṣṇadāsa, he would sing for the śayana ceremony. So, there's the eight.

So many devotees appreciated, in particular, the poetry and singing of Kumbhan Dās. Historically, one of them was the Rājā Mān Singh of Amber, that great Kṛṣṇa-conscious devotee, general in the army of Emperor Akbar. We spoke extensively of him; you may remember in a previous lecture, maybe two years ago. So once, Rājā Mān Singh was returning from his conquests somewhere in India and heading home to Āgrā, when he thought to himself, "It has been many days that I have not visited Mathurā or Vṛndāvana." He's a great general, but he's a Kṛṣṇa-bhakta. So after the battles, he's coming back to Āgrā and he's thinking, "For many days I have not visited Mathurā-Vṛndāvana; I should again visit those sacred places." 

So, he's reading, it was summertime, and thinking like this, he reached Mathurā. Then after bathing and resting, he went for darśana of the famous Keśava-deva deity. Keśava-deva deity was still being worshiped there. And from there, he headed for Vṛndāvana. 

Now, when the Gosvāmīs of Vṛndāvana heard that Mān Singh was coming for darśana of the Vṛndāvana deities, they decided to dress their respective deities very beautifully. Many of these mahantas or these Gosvāmīs in charge of various temples, they thought, "Oh, you know, this great general is coming, he's a Kṛṣṇa-bhakta, very important person, let's decorate our deities or dress them very wonderfully." 

So now, when Mān Singh arrived in Vṛndāvana proper, he visited I think four or five temples, and then he left to visit the temples at Govardhana Hill. He reached the village of Govardhana just before noon and he camped next to Mānasa-gaṅgā Lake, and there he had darśana, of course, of Harideva. Afterwards, someone informed him about the worship of Gopāla on Govardhana Hill. So curious, Mān Singh left, and upon reaching the village of Gopālapura, he inquired, "What was the time for darśana of this deity?" He had not heard about it before, Gopāla up on the hill. And he was informed that the utthāpana darśana was already over and the next darśana would be soon at the time of offering bhoga. 

So hearing this, Rājā Mān Singh, he started climbing up Govardhana Hill, and it was a very hot day, and he'd been on the move for some time, and he was very tired, and it's described he was suffering from the heat. However, as soon as he reached Gopāla's temple, at that very moment the temple doors opened, and upon entering the temple, Rājā Mān Singh prostrated himself. This big country-soldier, it fell flat on the ground. And rising up and seeing Gopāla for the very first time, he said, "Gopāla is certainly Kṛṣṇa Himself, the moon of Vraja. I have heard this in the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, but now I am seeing it with my own eyes. And here in front of Gopāla, kīrtana is being sung so sweetly with much devotion. This is a blessed day for me." 

It was recorded by his scribes, in fact: the singer was Kumbhan Dās. He was singing at his scheduled time. And as Rājā Mān Singh continued to listen to Kumbhan Dās's singing, he was greatly moved. Historians write that Kumbhan Dās was singing: *rūpa nā dekhaṅ, naīṁ kachu aṅga-aṅga prati nava-nava-mān-a-nī*. "My eyes cannot rest, and my eyelids cannot close, seeing the radiance of my Lord. Each limb of Śrī Gopāla is such that my heart is fully satisfied simply seeing through these eyes." 

So, Rājā Mān Singh, he stood mesmerized, gazing upon Gopāla and relishing the singing of Kumbhan Dās. After some time, he left and he went back to the camp he set up at Mānasa-gaṅgā. And when he arrived there, he inquired as to who that devotee was whose poetic singing had melted his heart. And when the local people informed him it was Kumbhan Dās, they told Rājā Mān Singh that he lived in the village of Pārolī, situated on the parikrama-mārga around Govardhana. 

So the next morning, Rājā Mān Singh rose there at Mānasa-gaṅgā, and after bathing, he went on Govardhana-parikramā—the great country going on Govardhana-parikramā—and when he reached Pārolī, he saw Kumbhan Dās, who was seated with his niece, having just finished his morning oblations, and he was fully absorbed in thoughts of his beloved Lord Gopāla. Approaching Kumbhan Dās very respectfully, Rājā Mān Singh came and he bowed down to Kumbhan Dās, and he sat next to him. 

Now, seeing that Kumbhan Dās was completely absorbed in meditating on his beloved Gopāla, Kumbhan Dās's niece mentioned to him that the great Rājā King Mān Singh was present, sitting next to him. To that, Kumbhan Dās said, "What can I do? Can you please go and converse with the Rājā elsewhere? I don't want to be distracted from my morning meditation on my Lord Gopāla. In fact, because I was in a rush today, I have not properly applied my tilaka. My dear niece, can you run and bring a bowl of water so I can properly apply that tilaka mark on my forehead?" Remember, he was very renounced; he didn't have a mirror; he just put tilaka on with the reflection of the bowl of water. So his niece brought a bowl filled with water, and looking at his reflection in the bowl, Kumbhan Dās started to apply tilaka. 

The Rājā was sitting right next to him. Seeing this, Rājā Mān Singh was surprised, and he offered Kumbhan Dās his own bejeweled small mirror, which he used himself, like when dressing in the mornings. He said to Kumbhan Dās, "This is a jeweled mirror, please, you can keep it." 

So Kumbhan Dās said to Rājā Mān Singh, "Sir, ours is a simple straw-roofed home. Who knows, someone might come in and kill us for this mirror." Then Rājā Mān Singh, he placed a bag of gold coins in front of Kumbhan Dās as an offering, to which Kumbhan Dās said, "Sir, I do not need money. I have a small piece of farmland which yields enough for our needs." 

Then Rājā Mān Singh said, "But may I give a deed assigning a village to you?" 

So Kumbhan Dās said, "Sir, I am not willing to accept anything from you. I have everything I need." At this, Rājā Mān Singh said, "Bābā, please allow me to give you at least something. Command me." 

So Kumbhan Dās said, "Will you really do what I ask you to do?" 

And Rājā Mān Singh, he folded his hands and he said, "Yes, I will do whatever you ask me to do." 

So Kumbhan Dās said, "I request you to never offer me anything again, for I have everything I need for Gopāla-sevā, and I have the great service of singing for Him each and every day." 

So impressed, Rājā Mān Singh said, "Bābā, I have traveled far and wide and I have seen many so-called saints whose devotion is mixed with name, fame, and money, but here sitting next to me is one who is truly a devotee of the Lord." So saying this, Rājā Mān Singh, he prostrated himself before Kumbhan Dās, and then he left. Hare Kṛṣṇa. 

So naturally, I think we can conclude today with several verses from Kumbhan Dās's poetry. They are from his *Śrīmān-adaśā-gānī*:

"Śrī Rādhikā, the embodiment of the nectar of pure love, is fully immersed in bliss and a playful mood. The daughter of Śrī Vṛbhānu is like a golden vine wrapped around a dark Tamāla tree. She is always engaged in the company of youthful Kṛṣṇa, who upholds Govardhana Hill. Kumbhan Dās says, when they are together, love prevails in their play." 

"Śrī Kṛṣṇa, who is the remover of all sorrow, is resting in Śrī Rādhā's abode. Lalitā and the other gopīs are fanning them and creating a cool breeze. As Śrī Rādhā hears His voice, the fire of separation that has been burning in her heart is cooled. The poet Kumbhan Dās says, Lalitā addresses Him with the name Rādha-ramaṇa—He who enjoys pastimes with Śrī Rādhā."

Hare Kṛṣṇa. Some of his poetry. So next week, we'll continue with part two of the glories and the poetry of the great Vaiṣṇava saint and poet, Kumbhan Dās. There's much more to be told about him. So thank you for listening. Jay Śrīla Prabhupāda, see you next Friday.
