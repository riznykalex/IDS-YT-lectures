Today we'll begin a new and exciting topic, and that is Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī's tears—her tears.

A teardrop in Sanskrit is called *aśru*. The word literally means *aśnute vyāpnoti netram*—“that which fills and covers the eyes.” In other words, tears naturally rise and overflow from the eyes.

Now, in devotional service, in Kṛṣṇa consciousness, such tears are not ordinary, for they are one of the eight *sāttvika-vikāras*. *Sāttvika-vikāras* means the natural bodily signs that appear when the heart is deeply moved by spiritual emotion.

Bharata Muni kindly lists these eight symptoms of ecstatic love in his famous *Nāṭya-śāstra*. And, interestingly enough, the same exact verse has been quoted by Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī in his *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu* (2.3.16):

*stambha-sveda-romāñca-svara-bheda-vepathuḥ*
*vaivarṇyam aśru-pralaya ity aṣṭau sāttvikāḥ smṛtāḥ*

There are eight natural physical reactions that arise when a person experiences deep spiritual emotion, and they are as follows:

Becoming stunned or still, perspiring, the hair standing on end, the voice choking or breaking, trembling, the skin becoming pale, and today's subject—the tears flowing from the eyes, tears flowing from the eyes.

Now, these eight symptoms of ecstasy can appear in a pure devotee while experiencing the nine principal moods of devotional service. What is the source of these ecstatic symptoms? The nine principal moods in Kṛṣṇa consciousness. We call these moods *rasas*, rasas, and they are as follows:

Śṛṅgāra, which means love;
Hāsya, which means joy;
Karuṇā means compassion;
Raudra means anger;
Vīra means heroism;
Bhayānaka means fear;
Bībhatsa means aversion;
Adbhuta means wonder;
And Śānta means peace.

So these different moods, these different rasas, have different symptoms that arise. But there is only one symptom that remains constant in all these moods, all these rasas, and that is tears—tears.

Thus, you could say tears reign supreme over all other expressions.

Now, in *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu* 2.3.53, our beloved Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī goes into even more detail about tears in devotional service, or Kṛṣṇa consciousness. He writes:

“When water flows from the eyes, whether due to joy, anger, or sorrow, it is called aśru.”

Again, tears.

“Tears that come from joy are cool, whereas tears that come from anger are warm.”

And he continues:

“In all cases, certain physical signs may be seen. The eyes may move restlessly, they may become red, and one may rub the eyes.”

This is the detail of the science of love.

Now, Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī further explains in his commentary on this exact verse of Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī that along with tears there may also be other natural bodily responses. For example, the nose may begin to run.

Wow.

It might be important to note here that while we often hear there is no real sorrow in the eternal abode of the Lord, we know there are many instances where the residents of Vṛndāvana are crying.

So how is that?

Well, one of the ācāryas in the Nimbārka-sampradāya, whose name was Madhvācārya, a well-known poet in Vṛndāvana, makes it quite clear. He wrote:

“The tears shed in the Lord's abode are not caused by any of the threefold miseries—adhyātmika, adhibhautika, and adhidaivika. Māyā has no role there. Rather, these tears are the pure nectarean expressions of transcendental love for Kṛṣṇa, arising from ecstatic devotion.”

And in the *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*, many of you know it is said:

*Kṛṣṇa sūrya-sama; māyā haya andhakāra*
*yāhāṅ kṛṣṇa, tāhāṅ nāhi māyāra adhikāra*

“Kṛṣṇa is like the brilliant sun, and māyā is like darkness. Where there is Kṛṣṇa, there is no influence of māyā.”

Hare Kṛṣṇa.

So like this we can understand that the tears of the Vrajavāsīs, and especially those of Śrī Rādhā, because she is the best devotee and Kṛṣṇa's dearest lover, are not born from suffering but out of pure unalloyed love—born of pure unalloyed love.

Now, the Vrajavāsī poet Nāgarīdāsa wrote very beautifully:

“The tears of Śrī Rādhā are the precious jewels of Gokula. They are preserved within the mother-shell hearts of the gopīs. The reward of love itself appears as her teardrops. Pearls and gems are insignificant in comparison—insignificant in comparison. Her tears are the beads adorning Kṛṣṇa's neck.”

Wow.

So let's take some time now to look at some specific examples of Rādhārāṇī's pastimes of crying tears—some instances where she cries and why she cries.

In Rūpa Gosvāmī's play *Vidagdha-mādhava* (5.32), we find a very beautiful scene.

After Kṛṣṇa tries to pacify Śrī Rādhā, her *māna*, her loving anger, is described as softening. But instead of becoming peaceful, she starts to cry.

At that time her beloved Lalitā-sakhī gently rebukes her and says:

“Rādhā, if you choose to love Kṛṣṇa, the son of Nanda, then your tears will never stop. I have warned you again and again not to become so attached, but you don't listen. Now tell me, what is the use of your crying?”

Transcendental.

And in *Gīta-govinda* 11.32, by the famous Jayadeva Gosvāmī, we find an example of her tears of joy—tears of joy.

Therein, upon seeing Kṛṣṇa in a secluded grove, Rādhārāṇī became overwhelmed with happiness. Her eyes became so restless, as if they were stretching beyond their limits just to see Him more. And from that intense joy, streams of tears began to flow from her eyes like drops of perspiration pouring out.

Jayadeva Gosvāmī.

Now, here's an important note about tears of joy.

Jīva Gosvāmī explains something very interesting in his commentary to *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu* 2.3.54. He writes that sometimes too many tears—even tears of joy—can become an impediment.

Why?

He writes:

“Because if the eyes are filled with tears, one cannot clearly see Kṛṣṇa.”

And he has a little note there. He notes that Rukmiṇī in Dvārakā criticized flowing tears of joy, calling them “creators of obstacles,” creators of obstacles.

So poetic.

Now, in another verse in *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu*, attributed to Śrī Bilvamaṅgala Ṭhākura, Kṛṣṇa speaks to Rādhārāṇī about her tears that arise from loving anger.

He writes:

*Rādhe padāt kena koṣmāt aśmasu vācaḥ paruṣatā?*
*Oho kathaṁ te kuchayoḥ patanti karuṇā-kaṇāḥ?*

While sporting in the kuñja, Śrī Rādhā became angry and resentful. Śrī Kṛṣṇa could not determine the reason for it, and He asked:

“O beloved, why are you angry and speaking harshly to Me? And why are these streams of tears, shining like a necklace of pearls, falling upon your chest?”

Wow. These are all meditations.

And Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī describes Rādhārāṇī's crying tears out of distress—or separation. Her tears out of distress, or it's also described as separation.

In his *Padyāvalī*, verse 349, it is written:

“When Kṛṣṇa left for Mathurā, Śrī Rādhā began to cry intensely, overwhelmed by the agony of separation. Seeing her condition, her friend Viśākhā tried to console her:

‘O Rādhā, whose thighs are as graceful as a baby elephant, do not stain your moonlike face with tears mixed with the mascara from your eyes. Śrī Hari, the ocean of mercy, will surely bestow His grace upon you once again.’”

Wow.

And an unknown poet expresses these tears of Rādhārāṇī in the mood of separation most beautifully as follows:

*Yādava yāti Govindo Mathurātaḥ punaḥ sakhi,*
*Rādhe yānaṁ yānaṁ tvan nāma viparyayaḥ.*

“Alas, O dear friend, Govinda has left us to go to Mathurā. Now, if Govinda goes further beyond Mathurā to another place, then in Rādhā's eyes we shall see the opposite of Rādhā.”

And he clarifies this.

The opposite of “Rādhā” is “Dhārā.” You simply switch it.

*Dhārā* meaning a stream of tears.

Isn't that beautiful?

A stream of tears.

The opposite of Rādhā is Dhārā—a stream of tears.

So in this way, so many examples are given in the scriptures to show how Śrī Rādhā expresses herself through tears: tears from attachment, tears of joy, tears of anger, tears of jealousy, and tears of separation—all transcendental.
