But first, I would like to share with you something extraordinary that I found in my research. I was actually in shock—I mean, blissful shock. It’s in the Śrī Nārada Pañcarātra, of course, by Kṛṣṇa-dvaipāyana Vyāsa. It’s from the second rātra, chapter five, and the book I was reading was translated by my godbrother, Bhūmipati Dāsa. Therein is a beautiful dialogue between Lord Śiva and Nārada Muni where we find what? Śrī Rādhā-kavaca, the divine mantra of protection granted by Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. I never knew it was there! It’s so nice.

Therein, Nārada Muni is speaking to Lord Śiva. It begins: Śrī Nārada uvāca: sarvaṁ śrutaṁ jagannātha yad yan mānasi vāñchitam, adhunā śrotum icchāmi rādhikā-kavacaṁ param. Śrī Nārada Muni said, “Oh Lord of the universe, I have heard from you whatever I wanted to hear. Now I wish to hear about the Śrī Rādhā-kavaca.”

Lord Śiva replied, “Oh best of the brāhmaṇas, hear with attention as I reveal to you the most coveted, rare, auspicious, and supremely blissful Rādhā-kavaca. Śrī Kṛṣṇa has revealed this kavaca to me at Śataśṛṅga Mountain in the transcendental abode of Goloka, in the beautiful Rāsa-līlā arena surrounded by the gopīs in the forest of Vṛndāvana. He did so at the request of Śrī Rādhā. Simply by receiving this kavaca, a person becomes relieved of sinful reactions and cured by killing 100,000 brāhmaṇas and indeed all sinful reactions accumulated for millions of births. Oh foremost of the brāhmaṇas, the sage presiding over this kavaca is Nārāyaṇa Ṛṣi, and it is meant for achieving devotional service at the lotus feet of Kṛṣṇa.”

So, of course, I didn’t quote all the Sanskrit, but a little bit to begin. And for proof, here is the mantra. I am only going to quote some of the Sanskrit, but here is the mantra: Pātu rāseśvarī rādhā svapne jāgaraṇe ca mām, jale sthale cāntarikṣe sevite ca layāśaye. “May Rādhā, the queen of the Rāsa-līlā, protect me during sleep as well as during wakefulness, and may she who serves the Lord as He lies on the ocean protect me on the land, in the water, and in the sky. May that fully accomplished and dearest one protect me from the east. May the controller of the southeast protect me from that direction. May the reliever of distress protect me from the south, and may those who have been delivered from hell protect me from the southwest. May she who enchants the Lord, who wears a garland of forest flowers around His neck, protect me from the west, and may she who is worshipped by Vāyu protect me from the northwest. May she who is worshipped by Lord Kūrma protect me from the north, and may the supreme controller who resides on the Śataśṛṅga Mountain protect me from the northeast.”

Hari Kṛṣṇa! It continues: “May she who enjoys in the land of Vṛndāvana and who wanders about in its forest protect me in the forest, and may the Queen of Vraja protect me at all times and in all places. May that goddess whom the Supreme Lord Śrī Kṛṣṇa worships first by chanting the six-syllable mantra protect a most pathetic soul like me.”

Hari Kṛṣṇa! The Sanskrit is quite long; you can refer to that scripture. That book is available at Rasa Bihari Lal’s bookshop in Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma. I didn’t know there was a Rādhā-kavaca. Hari Kṛṣṇa!

Now, regarding… we are getting back to Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī’s eyes. In Vedic astrology, the planet Venus, known as Śukra, is said to be the giver of beautiful, radiant, deep, and captivating eyes. And when Śukra is powerfully situated in one’s horoscope, harmonized with both the sun and the moon, then one’s eyes become extraordinarily attractive. Like deep monsoon clouds, yet gentle in expression, it’s described. Their darkness is saturated with sweetness and rasa, and their brilliance is softened by compassion and grace. Now, as rare as those eyes may be in this world, they are nothing compared to Śrī Rādhārāṇī, the original Goddess of Fortune.

And the poet Navala Mādhurī reveals this in a beautiful pastime. He writes: “One day, Śrī Rādhā was feeling intense separation from Kṛṣṇa. So, she called her gopī friend Tārāvalī for a consultation. Because amongst the intimate gopīs of Śrī Rādhā, Tārāvalī is expert in Jyotiṣa-śāstra, astrology, including palmistry—she’s good at palmistry—and an expert at the subtle science of omens. So Rādhā said: ‘My dear Tārāvalī, you are the knower of all signs and secrets. Please look at the stars in the sky. Do they announce the coming of my beloved? Examine my palm carefully—do you see the lines of reunion with my Śyāma? Observe the forest around us—do the birds and breezes carry auspicious omens of His return? Leave aside everything, my dear friend. Look into my eyes. Can you see there the threefold bending form of Kṛṣṇa?’”

She said, “Look into my eyes. Can you see there the threefold bending form of Kṛṣṇa?”

So the gopī Tārāvalī replied: “Oh my most dear friend, the constellations above, the markings upon your palm, and the omens whispering through the forest all attempt to answer your question. But your eyes themselves reveal the truth, for within your vision you forever behold the Lord of your heart.”

Isn’t that sweet? Now, another famous Vrajavāsī poet, his name was Mangala Dāsa—he was a medieval poet—he describes that not only does Śrī Rādhā’s beloved Kṛṣṇa reside always in her vision, in her eyes, but so do all the divine goddesses as well. It’s interesting; in a very poetic way, he writes: “The eyes of Śrī Rādhā are as sacred as a temple. Goddess Sarasvatī resides in the pure whiteness of her eyes. Goddess Lakṣmī dwells in the reddish corners at their edges. Goddess Pārvatī abides in the deep darkness of her pupils. Goddess Gāyatrī flows within her tears of divine emotion. Goddess Śacī rests gracefully upon her eyelashes. Goddess Svāhā burns gently within the warmth of her glance. Goddess Aditi shines in the bliss of her vision. Goddess Caṇḍī manifests her fierce and protective expression. Goddess Sandhyā appears in the soft blinking of her eyes, and Lord Keśava, Kṛṣṇa, the master of all goddesses, fully resides within her divine sight.”

Thus confirming so much nectar just about her eyes alone. Well, speaking about nectar, amṛta, I found a beautiful piece spoken by Lalitā-sakhī in glorification of her beloved Rādhā. It’s mentioned—well, not mentioned, it’s stated in the Kṛṣṇa-stuti of Balabhadra. So Lalitā-sakhī, Rādhārāṇī’s dearest, she says: “Oh Rādhē, Śrī Kṛṣṇa is pure concentrated bliss, like sweetness solidified into golden jaggery. But when He beholds you, that sweetness melts into flowing honey and spreads its fragrance throughout all of Vṛndāvana. Your eyes are like cups filled with nectar, holding within them the beauty of Kṛṣṇa. And whoever gazes into your eyes feels as if they are drinking that beauty directly, tasting the sweetness of Kṛṣṇa Himself from the overflowing nectar of your divine glance.” Many thanks to that poet. Hundreds of years ago he wrote, and now it’s broadcast around the world.

Now, I would like to share another pastime—a couple of pastimes today. It has an interesting title: “I can see a peacock in my vision.” It’s written by one of my favorite, often-quoted poets, Lalit Kishor Vrajavāsī. So, he writes in a poetic way: One time, Jaṭilā, who we know is Rādhārāṇī’s mother-in-law, became suspicious of Śrī Rādhā’s frequent outings and determined to prevent any secret meetings with Kṛṣṇa. She confined Rādhārāṇī to her quarters for two long days. That’s a long time to be separated from Kṛṣṇa for the Vrajavāsīs, for Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. And on the last day, Lalitā-sakhī came to see Śrī Rādhā. But the moment she entered the room, her heart trembled seeing Śrī Rādhā’s condition. With tears in her eyes, Lalitā spoke: “Oh my dear friend, these two eyes feel like barren land. They are dry, hard, and desperately longing for the heavy rain of Śyāma’s darśana. Without seeing Him, I cannot find peace anywhere. The whole world feels tasteless and empty. My eyes have lost their light. Life feels aimless. Each day passes without purpose, without the blissful vision of my Śyāma. My heart is sinking in an ocean of fog, of doubts, and anxiety. Tell me, Lalitā, does Śyāma feel the same? Does He think of me as I think of Him?”

So, at that point, Lalitā-sakhī embraced Śrī Rādhā, and she spoke very softly as well—two confidantes speaking to each other. She also spoke softly: “Oh Rādhā, these thoughts are born from separation. Your mind has become conquered by the cruel king known as vipralambha, meaning separation. He makes you weep like an orphan. But as soon as the prince of union”—I love that—”as soon as the prince of union appears, all this sorrow will vanish. Now be peaceful. Chant the holy names of your beloved. They are the only solace for this pain of separation.”

Wow! So, when Śrī Rādhā finally drifted into a light sleep, Lalitā quietly slipped away and went to meet Mother Pourṇamāsī, where she explained everything to her. Now, a plan was needed to reunite Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. But they knew that Jaṭilā was, as she often does, guarding the palace constantly. They discussed that taking Rādhā outside would be risky, and bringing Kṛṣṇa inside would be dangerous. So, they came up with a brilliant idea and put a plan into action.

So, the following day, Lalitā approached Kuṭilā—who’s Kuṭilā? Kuṭilā is Rādhārāṇī’s sister-in-law, who, like Jaṭilā, is always guarding Śrī Rādhā—and said, “Kuṭilā, Śrī Rādhā has been unwell and has been sitting indoors for days. May I take her to the rooftop to feed the birds and breathe some fresh air? Perhaps she will regain her strength and later return to her household duties.” So Kuṭilā agreed to the proposal, and Lalitā happily took Śrī Rādhā up to the roof. Now, it’s described it was a beautiful day; the sun shone brightly and clouds drifted across the sky. And with her eyes filled with tears, Śrī Rādhā began feeding some of the birds that had landed there. And suddenly, the atmosphere around her seemed to absorb her mood, because clouds gathered, thunder rolled, and lightning flashed. And from every direction, peacocks began running towards the palace rooftop, running along and up to the palace rooftop, eager to dance in the coming rain.

Now, at that moment, a soft breeze blew from the direction of Nandagrāma, where Kṛṣṇa lives. Now, to Śrī Rādhā, after days of being indoors, that breeze felt soothing like a whisper of hope—like a whisper of hope. And suddenly, Lalitā noticed Rādhā forming her hands like, like—if you could say—like binoculars, so we can see easier. And she said to her: “Rādhā, what are you looking at so intently?”

And Rādhā replied: “Lalitā, just to see where the breeze is coming from.” Looking towards Nandagrāma, “Just to see where the breeze is coming from. But oh Lalitā, now I see something very beautiful, something very colorful coming toward me. It’s a peacock. Lalitā, I can see a peacock in my vision.” The title of the poem: “I can see a peacock in my vision.”

Now, an unexplainable joy suddenly arose within Śrī Rādhā’s heart. And gathering some grains in her hand, she got ready to feed this peacock. But then she saw a flock of peacocks approaching. And among them, there was this one whose beauty surpassed all the rest. His feathers shimmered with unusual brilliance. His movements were not like that of an ordinary bird. There was a familiar sweetness to this peacock. So, approaching Śrī Rādhā, this peacock came close, and he bowed very gracefully and began eating grains from her hand. But as he lifted up his face, their eyes met—the peacock and Rādhā’s eyes met. And in that glance, something stirred within Śrī Rādhā’s heart. And after a few moments, a realization dawned upon her.

She said: “This is no ordinary peacock. Rather, this is Śyāma, Kṛṣṇa Himself. It’s my Mayūra-vihārī. Mayūra-vihārī, meaning the playful Lord of Vraja, disguised in feathered splendor.” And with trembling hands, she gently loosened the subtle bindings of His costume, revealing a dark and enchanting form beneath. And as she embraced Him, her lover, the long agony of separation dissolved in an instant. Her eyes, once dry like a desert cracked by heat, now overflowed with life and radiance, shining like a full moon reflected in fresh monsoon rain. The long-awaited monsoon had finally arrived.

So, Lalit Kishor concludes: “That evening, it was not only rain that fell upon the rooftops, but a shower of bliss. A monsoon of divine joy had poured down from above. May the eyes of Śrī Rādhā remain forever adorned with the beautiful vision of Śyāma, Kṛṣṇa.” Wow!

It’s short, but in essence, it says everything about Kṛṣṇa consciousness: “Prabhupāda, I emphatically say to you: ‘Oh brothers, you will obtain your good fortune from the Supreme Lord Kṛṣṇa only when Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī becomes pleased with you.’”
