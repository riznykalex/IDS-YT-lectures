"Today we read from the Fourth Canto of the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, Chapter 20, Verse 31: *oṁ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya*.

### Translation
"My Lord, due to the influence of Your illusory energy, all living entities in this material world have forgotten their present constitutional position. In their ignorance, they always crave material happiness in the form of social status, friendship, and love. So, please do not ask me for any material benefits from You. But as a father who does not expect requests from his son does everything for his benefit, please grant me what You think will be best for me."

### Commentary
It is the duty of a son to depend on his father and not ask anything from him. A good son believes that his father knows what will be best for him. Thus, a pure devotee does not ask anything from the Lord in the form of material benefit, nor does he ask for spiritual benefit; he is completely surrendered at the lotus feet of the Lord, and the Lord takes care of him, as stated in the *Bhagavad-gītā* (18.66). The father knows the needs of the son and satisfies them, just as the Supreme Lord knows the needs of the living entity and fully supplies them. This *pūrṇa-dāma* is the introduction to the above.

The problem is that due to forgetfulness, the living entity creates artificial problems and becomes entangled by material activities. As a result of these activities, there is no end—life after life, we can observe different types of living entities, each entangled in their material activities. Our duty is simply to surrender to the Supreme Personality of Godhead and let Him do what He thinks is best.

This speaks of the very strong influence of the illusory material energy. Under its influence, the living entity forgets their constitutional position—that they are the servant of God. Of course, the material energy is also a personality and a servant of the Lord. It is not such a thankless task to keep the living entity in forgetfulness. The question arises: why does the Lord ask His servant, the material energy, to make the living entity forgetful? This does not look like God, and we always say that the Lord cares about our spiritual well-being. Why then does He ask Māyā to keep us all the time in illusion?

He says, "I very quickly release My devotee from the ocean of birth and death." So it sounds very strange that the Lord asks Māyā-devī all the time to keep the living entity in illusion. But we must understand that our desire for God is not forced. As we have discussed many times in lectures, we have a very small amount of independence. This means that we have a choice. This is the nature of love: we can choose for ourselves whom to love and whom not to love. Love does not mean you have to love me; love means you love me if you want to. And therefore, the Lord cannot tell us that we *have* to love Him. That would not be love. Love is always a spontaneous choice. 

If there was no choice in the spiritual world, there would be no real love there. Only a very, very small number of living beings made the choice to forget Kṛṣṇa. Imagine taking foam from all the sand in the world. How many beaches exist? If you imagine all the beaches in the world—in Russia, in Crimea, in California, in Australia—only Brazil has 8,000 kilometers of beach coastline. If you imagine how many souls exist in the world, it is impossible to even count. A computer cannot imagine such a number. The number of souls who chose to be here in the material world is a very tiny part compared to the vast majority of living beings who remain there. We are actually all guests here in a foreign country. 

That moment when we made this main, fatal mistake is described in the scriptures. It is amazing how scientifically the scriptures describe that very moment when, like a bubble of foam falling from the total amount of sand, we left the spiritual world. It is like a good photographer who knows when to take a picture. I never studied at a photography school; I never even held a camera in my hands until someone gave me one, but somehow I get good pictures. I click and click. The best pictures aren't those where someone stands frozen and smiling; they are the ones where everything is spontaneously in motion. All art—in the theater, in painting—is about movement. You just need to catch that moment, for example, when someone looks at you, fire bursts out of their eyes, their hair is blowing in the wind, and in another second, it is gone.

A good photographer takes many pictures, like on my Facebook—I took 10,000, and I had to set aside 1,000. There is a function on my Canon camera that allows you to take a series of pictures. Sometimes when something special happens, I turn on this function, and then I look and see that I caught the moment. That is exactly what we do with our classes; we look for the moment when we left the spiritual world. Who could have taken a picture of us at that moment? No one, but someone wrote a poem about it. As soon as someone begins to feel a hostile attitude towards Kṛṣṇa and a thirst for sensual pleasures, they fall. 

It is not like a court case that can last 25 years, as happens here in India. Sometimes court cases last for many, many years, and both sides bribe judges, and 25 years pass, and nothing has been decided. You can live until your death and the case doesn't even end. That is not how it is in the spiritual world. Love means Kṛṣṇa, and sensual pleasure means "me" and "for myself." There is no place for the feeling of selfish pleasure in the spiritual world. 

In the Vedic *śāstras*, it is said that one must judge someone by their actions. What are the actions that led us to leave the spiritual world? The enlightened soul who wrote *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* says in this scripture that a living being is attracted by the material illusory energy since time immemorial. Therefore, the illusory energy, Māyā, gives them all kinds of suffering. This is the result. Did we make the right decision? No, we made the wrong decision, and all because we turned our backs on Kṛṣṇa. Turning our face to Kṛṣṇa is equivalent to turning our face to the sun—it is golden and shining. But turning our backs to the sun, we see our own dark shadow. Although the sun continues to exist, it is behind us. Therefore, turning our backs to the mercy of Kṛṣṇa, what do we see? We see the shadow of matter. 

This is not good. When I was young, I really liked living in California because it is always sunny. Each state has its own slogan, and the main characteristic of California is that it is called the "Sunny State." We loved to be in the sun all the time. But in the next life, we could be born as dolphins. Someone once asked Śrīla Prabhupāda, "What about those who surf? They all look so happy." Prabhupāda said, "They are unhappy; there is a play on words there—they 'suffer' on this earth." 

We grew up in California, and we didn't like sitting at home with windows sealed against the snow and frost. When I first arrived in London, there was thick snow, and a reporter asked, "How do you like London?" When I arrived in Moscow and went out to the airport, I looked around and said, "God, what am I doing here?" But Prabhupāda once said—we really appreciate this quote—that preaching in the snows of Russia is sweeter than the sweetest mango. Turning away from the radiance of Kṛṣṇa, we find the world in front of us dark, hostile, and merciless. To add salt to the wound, we think that it is all great—we take alcohol, drugs, engage in illicit sex, participate in politics and other nonsense—and we only begin to realize what we turned away from when God or His devotee intervenes.

One night, I was praying for mercy, and suddenly an insight descended upon me. This says something about what it means to meet a pure devotee; it is a miracle. The first time I met a devotee, I had never met one before. He was the same American guy like me—same age, same build, same education—but when I looked at him, it seemed to me that this man was not from this world. When he danced, it seemed his feet did not even touch the ground. He was so happy, shining, filled with this energy of love. When I looked at myself, I was the complete opposite. When he finished his *kīrtana*, he turned to me and my ex-wife and said, "Come with me; I will tell you about my spiritual master." That was my first experience meeting devotees. When we were walking back, my wife asked me what I thought about him, and I said, "I want to be like him." She said, "That would be great." She didn't know then that he was a *sannyāsī*.

This is mercy. We are not looking for a devotee ourselves; Kṛṣṇa sends us a devotee to save us on His behalf. Prabhupāda writes in his comments that just like a father does not wait until his son asks for help but does everything necessary for the good of his son, the Lord gives the devotee everything necessary for his good. He descends to the material world and acts in such a way as to attract our attention. We all want to enjoy, and He gives us the highest pleasure. We want to sing; He teaches us what song to sing. We want to dance; He teaches us. He does it in His various forms for different people at different times. 

The greatest show, the most attractive show, is the movement of the Lord. The Lord dances with His closest servants, and this is depicted in the *śāstras*. The Lord dances, and at first, He exists alone, but then He spreads His energy of pleasure, Rādhārāṇī appears, and then the Lord again unites into one whole. The recitation of Mahāprabhu’s pastimes—the Lord is joined by His closest companions from Vṛndāvana, and the *rāsa* dance takes place. In Caitanya-līlā, all these closest companions of the Lord come, and they dance in the movement of *saṅkīrtana*. When the Lord dances with His closest associates, who are so enlightened and filled with love and compassion, only people with the most stony hearts cannot help but be attracted. 

Sometimes, when we have beautiful *kīrtanas* at programs and festivals, I watch people. I see how they want to dance with us, but they just can't get up. Once, we held a festival in Brazil, in a city next to New Gokula. When we held the *kīrtana*, everyone present stood up and started dancing with us—old and young, and even the little ones. I told someone next to me, "This is the benefit that we receive during chanting in Kali-yuga." In one life, you can receive much more benefit than in a thousand years of life spent meditating in other *yugas*, or performing fire sacrifices, or worshiping deities. 

But what if someone does not want to keep up? Then, in this case, the Lord has a secret weapon. If people do not want to keep up, you can simply invite them to a restaurant; this is a very powerful weapon. The Lord comes with His weapon, the *gadā* (club), and in Kali-yuga, it is simply *prasāda*. Even before Mahāprabhu appeared, it was planned that *prasāda* would be a very powerful weapon. This is well described by Nārada Muni. 

Many, many years ago, I read about how people in Kali-yuga would suffer greatly. Nārada Muni felt great compassion—a very distinctive and important characteristic of a Vaiṣṇava. He cares about the liberation of others more than his own. He went to Vaikuṇṭha, where Kṛṣṇa was busy with His pastimes, and Kṛṣṇa said, "Well, please come in. There must be something on your mind." Nārada Muni bowed down, offered his obeisances, and when he sat down, he began to cry. Kṛṣṇa began to stroke and soothe him: "My dear, why are you crying? You are a great soul; there is everything for spiritual life." Nārada Muni raised his eyes with tears and said, "Lord, what will happen to the poor souls in Kali-yuga? Is there no hope for them?" 

Kṛṣṇa finally said, "Okay, enough. Just for your sake, Nārada, I will liberate all people. I will take a golden form; I will chant the holy name." Then He said, "Nārada, now go to all the demigods and tell them to get ready to participate in the *saṅkīrtana* movement." Eventually, Kṛṣṇa came down to Earth. Nārada Muni also wanted to take part in this movement because he had acquired great spiritual power by controlling his mind and senses and tasting the *prasāda* offered to the Lord. 

Once, Nārada wanted to get the *prasāda* when the Lord was eating. He looked in, and like this, before He turned around, I turned away—I looked out like this three or four times. Then the Lord looked at me and said, "So, can I have a little mercy from Yours?" He gave me some, and when I began to show the eight signs of ecstatic love, the Lord said, "What happened to you? Look at yourself; you are in ecstasy!" I said, "You left nothing for me; You forgot to leave for Your brother!" The Lord said, "You know, there was no time to think at all; they gave me, I quickly threw it in my mouth and ate everything." 

And so on. The king of dances—at the end of creation, the Lord begins to dance, and the whole world plunges into flames. Thanks to the dance of Śiva at the moment of destruction, the universe becomes unmanifest. When the Lord began to dance, all the planetary systems trembled, and the Earth left its orbit and began to fall into the ocean. Then Bhūmi (the Earth personified) approached Śiva. She could not approach the Lord because He was dancing. She said, "What is happening? Your husband is dancing as if during the dissolution of the universe." Yes, He was dancing in ecstasy. She spoke very sternly to her husband: "Why are you dancing like that? The time has not come to destroy the universe." 

It was just the mercy of the Lord. She said, "And You left it for me? I am Your wife, the better half. You left something for me?" He said, "Oh, I know what happened so quickly. There is a tiny bit left." She was angry, but she decided to call upon the Lord so that He would give her His *prasāda*. She said, "The difference between you and me is that I will share this *prasāda* with all the souls in Kali-yuga." She performed austerities, and in the end, the Lord revealed His mercy and said, "I will give you My mercy in the form of *prasāda* because I know that you will share it with all the fallen souls in Kali-yuga." 

It is interesting that this is Durgā personified, the energy that keeps us all here in illusion. But in the depths of her heart, she has a very strong desire and is very compassionate to fallen souls; she wants their liberation. Although her service is to keep all living souls here in illusion, as a servant of the Lord, she participates in this mission to distribute *prasāda* to all fallen souls. We should consider ourselves very fortunate. We all owe it to Nārada Muni, who learned about the suffering and went to the Lord. 

We have said this before in our lectures: on our Polish tour, Nārada Muni is such a holy protector. As you know, in Catholicism, there are holy protectors for specific things; Christopher is responsible for the journey. So, here is our holy protector on the Polish tour. We must follow in his footsteps, for he is also very dear, and Vṛndāvana is in his heart. 

In conclusion, as the essence of the whole philosophy of the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, we read this verse: "I wander all over the earth meditating on Her who is the moon shining on the stage of the *rāsa* dance, who lights all the lamps in the palace of Śyāma, who is the main treasure, the main jewel in the necklace of Vraja." I found this verse today. I thought, "Yes, exactly this verse will now be my motto." As a *sannyāsī*, I also travel all the time all over the earth. I also want to follow in Her footsteps—She who is like the moon shining in the arena of the *rāsa* dance, who lights all the lamps in the courtyard, who is the main treasure in the necklace of Vraja.

This is where our lectures end this time in Vṛndāvana. Unfortunately, the day after tomorrow, I am leaving Vṛndāvana. I am going to Europe, then to South Africa for a month to help them with *ratha-yātrā*, then to Ukraine, then to Russia for Vyāsa-pūjā, then to America for Vyāsa-pūjā. Thank you very much, everyone, for coming these last few days and participating in our programs. For those who are still staying here, my spiritual brother will arrive somewhere around the 28th or 29th. I don't want to say he will conduct the program, otherwise he might be upset, but for you, there might also be a chance to participate in his programs. At least I can't imagine that he won't conduct his *kīrtana* and *Bhāgavatam* here, since this is his life and soul. Keep in touch, and maybe then you will be able to get to his program, and by his grace, you can also get to see transcendental places.""
