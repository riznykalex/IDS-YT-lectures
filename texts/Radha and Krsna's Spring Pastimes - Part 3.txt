Namo namaste sārasvate deve gauravāṇī pracāriṇe.* So, we are continuing with our discussions on springtime in Vṛndāvana, and today I would like to touch on the *rāsa-līlā* pastime that is unique to spring in Vṛndāvana. We did discuss the spring *rāsa* about a year and a half ago, but I would like to analyze it in more detail in the first part of this lecture and then discuss *kuñja-bhaṅga-līlā*, the pastimes of the Gopīs sweeping the *kuñjas*—the groves of Vṛndāvana—each morning after the *rāsa-līlā* dance. This *kuñja-bhaṅga-līlā* is actually one of the most interesting pastimes that I’ve ever read about, and I’ll share it with you later.

So, as we discussed a few weeks ago in our series on the *rāsa* dance itself, there’s not only one *rāsa* dance, but there’s a number of different *rāsa* dances in different places and in different seasons in Śrī Vṛndāvana. Actually, there’s four types of *rāsa-līlās* that are accepted by our ācāryas:
*   The first is *nitya-rāsa*: It’s the *rāsa* dance which takes place every night.
*   Then there’s the *vasantika-rāsa*, which takes place on Vasant Pūrṇimā, the full moon in the month of spring.
*   There’s *kārtika-rāsa*, which takes place on the full moon in Kārtika.
*   There’s *śāradīya-rāsa*, which takes place on Śarat Pūrṇimā.

Now, as an interesting note here, the ācāryas say that in *vasanta* (meaning spring) *rāsa*, Rādhā disappears from Kṛṣṇa’s vision, and in *śāradīya-rāsa*, Kṛṣṇa disappears from the Gopīs as well as Rādhā’s vision—just for those of you who are trying to be connoisseurs in this subject matter. So, the springtime *rāsa* dance took place, or you could say it takes place eternally in *aprakata-līlā*, at a very sacred *tīrtha* called Candra Sarovara. Now, the word *sarovara* means lake, but previous to Kṛṣṇa’s dancing with the Gopīs, there was no lake; it was just a forest. So then, how did that place become known as Candra Sarovara, the lake of the moon god? I’ll explain.

To begin with, the springtime *rāsa-līlā* lasted an entire night of Brahmā, and it was attended by all the demigods—all the demigods stationed from the higher planets who watched this topmost of Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes from above in their flower airplanes. Now, the ācāryas write that the demigods filled the sky with showers of flowers as the sound of their kettledrums reverberated through greater Vṛndāvana. From their perspective in the sky, to them, the *rāsa* dance appeared like a brilliant sapphire set in the middle of a gold band, and at other times like sapphire and gold bands intertwined—of course, referring to Kṛṣṇa as bluish-black like a monsoon cloud and the Gopīs as golden. At other times, the ācāryas say the *rāsa* dance resembled an exquisite sapphire paired with a flashing diamond, both fixed at the center of a band, appearing sometimes like solid gold and at other times like both sapphire and gold.

Kṛṣṇa and the ācāryas also write that the demigods who were fortunate enough to see this *rāsa* dance stared in wonder at the incredible ways the Gopīs moved their feet and their hands while flashing their eyebrows and making playful smiles. So many nice details! One ācārya writes that just as Kṛṣṇa appeared more beautiful in the Gopīs' presence, the Gopīs also appeared more beautiful in His presence. And our beloved Rūpa Gosvāmī has said that as the dancing reached the climax, Kṛṣṇa enjoyed the pleasure of the Gopīs' company by embracing them, by caressing them, and glancing lovingly at them, all the while displaying His playful smiles.

Now, amongst these demigods watching from above, the one who was most affected by being able to see all this was the demigod of the moon, Candra-deva. He was especially happy because not only did he get to watch this *rāsa-līlā* performance, he got to serve the *rāsa* dance—and how? By hovering over as the moon planet while giving light and illumination during the entire performance. Remember, this is the night of Brahmā. So, witnessing the *rāsa-līlā* for such a long time, Candra-deva, the demigod of the moon, experienced the topmost ecstatic symptoms of love of God. 

Now, Śrīla Prabhupāda mentions these eight ecstatic symptoms in *Nectar of Devotion*, Chapter 28. He writes:
"There are eight symptoms of existential ecstatic love: becoming stunned, perspiring, standing of the hairs on the body, faltering of the voice, trembling of the body, changing bodily colors, shedding tears, and finally, devastation."

One symptom mentioned there is the shedding of tears, so that’s what happened to Candra-deva. He was experiencing this particular ecstatic symptom. Not only was he shedding tears, but Śāstra describes that he was shedding torrents of tears which all came down and made a *kuṇḍa*, a lake, or *sarovara*, at that spot where *rāsa-līlā* was being performed. And so, from that time on, the area became known as Candra Sarovara. So, we answered that question: how did it become Candra Sarovara, the lake of the moon god in the area of the *rāsa* dance?

Now, our beloved Rūpa Gosvāmī gives special significance to Candra Sarovara by saying that each day, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa and Their friends would visit Candra Sarovara after Their pastimes at Rādhā-kuṇḍa. So, that would mean the afternoon, right? Because Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa meet at Rādhā-kuṇḍa at 12 noon, and apparently, they move on to Candra Sarovara. So you have to visit there next time you come to Vṛndāvana; a very important place. And as such, this *līlā-sthāna* should be given the highest respect and devotion by the devotees. Śrī Raghunātha dāsa Gosvāmī shows us actually how much reverence and love he has for places like Candra Sarovara. He describes in his *Vraja-vilāsa-stava*, Text number 68, he writes:

"Their hearts pleased by the rising waves of the conjugal mellow (*mādhurya-rasa*), the youthful divine couple enjoy pastimes in the beautiful groves of Vṛndāvana, aromatic with the many pleasant flowers and filled with many humming bees. Let us sweep these forest groves with our long hair. Let us sweep these forest groves with our long hair."

So there, he mentions sweeping the groves of Vṛndāvana. So again, this is called *kuñja-bhaṅga-līlā*, specifically sweeping the *rāsa* dance arena after the previous night’s *rāsa*. The ācāryas explain that there’s a need to sweep the *rāsa* dance arena because, while Kṛṣṇa and Rādhārāṇī and the Gopīs are dancing, very often flowers from their garlands, or whole garlands, fall to the ground; or sometimes ornaments they’re wearing fall to the ground; or sometimes even pieces of their clothing would fall to the ground. So each morning after a *rāsa* dance, the Mañjarīs, the younger Gopī girls, they would very eagerly come and sweep the *rāsa* dance arena and collect these transcendental items. Something that’s used in the service of the Lord is called *tadīya*, and it has deep spiritual significance and a very purifying effect.

So, the ācāryas explained that one reason they did so was to hide these items from the watchful eyes of Jaṭilā and Kuṭilā from Yāvaṭ, who, as we know, are always searching for evidence that Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa are meeting somewhere. If they were to come across all this transcendental paraphernalia at Seva Kuñja, they would say, "Ah, just see! Now we have proof Rādhārāṇī is secretly meeting with Kṛṣṇa." But another reason the Mañjarīs collect such items is that they’re allowed to wear them as a stimulus of love for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. In the Vrajavāsī language, this is called *prasādī*. Literally, it means "what the divine couple have left behind," "what the divine couple have left behind."

And in *Nectar of Devotion*, we hear actually that a practicing devotee, a *sādhaka* like ourselves, we can also—if we’re fortunate enough—wear the old clothes of the Deity, or we’re encouraged to wear the garlands of the Deities, or even if you get some ornaments and some pūjārī gives you an ornament, we can also wear them. But for purification—the Gopīs don’t need any purification, this is a stimulus of love for them—but Rūpa Gosvāmī mentions in *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu*, which Prabhupāda translates as *Nectar of Devotion*, we can also wear these things for purification and for protection. I found another verse in *Nectar of Devotion* in this regard. *Nectar of Devotion* is quoting the Eleventh Canto, sixth chapter, verse 46 of *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*:
