"Hari Kṛṣṇa, very dear devotees. Welcome back once again. *Vāñchā-kalpatarubhyaś ca kṛpā-sindhubhya eva ca patitānāṁ pāvanebhyo vaiṣṇavebhyo namo namaḥ.*

We are continuing with our series on Śrīla Prabhupāda’s beloved deity of Govindajī and His eternal consort, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. In our last lecture, we discussed how Mahārāja Jai Singh II successfully defended our Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava tradition in Jaipur and also ensured that Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī would remain on the altar there forever with Her beloved Govindajī.

Now, in our series, we have discussed a number of personalities who you could say gave their all in service to Govindajī, beginning, of course, with Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī, who discovered Govindajī in the Vṛndāvana forest at Yogapīṭha and established worship there. Then Kaśīśvara Prabhu, who, on the request of Lord Caitanya, left Mahāprabhu’s service in Purī to serve Govindajī in Braj. Then Emperor Akbar and his general Mān Singh, who were both instrumental in building Govindajī’s big temple. 

And then there was the unknown soldier who rode on his horse, called Pāvana, as fast as the wind through the night to warn the Brajavāsīs of Aurangzeb’s army who were coming to destroy Govindajī and other deities in Braj. Then there was our hero, Śivārāma Gosvāmī, who arranged to hide Govindajī before the soldiers arrived and who oversaw the late-night bullock cart ride of the deities to Rādhā-kuṇḍa and beyond for many decades. And let us not forget those brave Brajavāsīs who gave their very lives trying to protect Govindajī’s temple as the Mughal soldiers destroyed it. 

Then we have other heroes—Rājā Jai Singh II, Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura, and Baladeva Vidyābhūṣaṇa, who teamed up to facilitate the Lord when He arrived in Jaipur. There are many others as well, known and unknown, too numerous to mention and describe, who served Govindajī. But I think we can properly acknowledge them in the following prayer, which I have used many times as you know, by that famous medieval poet Devakī-nandana:

*Vaiṣṇava-pāde namaskāra, itti kichu aparādha nahuka āmāra.*
"I offer my respectful obeisances unto the lotus feet of all Vaiṣṇavas, praying I will not commit offenses in my attempts to please them. To all Vaiṣṇavas who have ever been and to all Vaiṣṇavas who will ever be, I offer my obeisances to their lotus feet."

But before we move on to what happened in Jaipur after Baladeva’s victory there—which is how we ended our last class—with your permission, I would like to backtrack a little and mention two other amazing devotees, famous for their surrender, their love, and their devotion for Govindajī, that we haven't mentioned yet. These two persons—ladies, actually—were present during the period when Emperor Akbar was offering his support to Govindajī. I only recently found out about them during my research at the nearby Vṛndāvana Research Institute, and I thought, well, we missed these two devotees, but we have to describe their wonderful service to Govindajī at that particular period in time.

### Jodhabai

The first is one of the wives of Emperor Akbar. Her name was Jodhabai, and she was born a Hindu Rājput princess in 1542. Now, how did a Rājput princess become the wife of a Mughal ruler, in particular an emperor? Well, as I mentioned in a previous lecture, at that time in history, the Rājput kings made alliances with the Mughals, who were the more powerful rulers in India at that time. Their alliance meant that instead of perpetually fighting wars against each other, they agreed to an alliance. We defined an alliance in a previous talk: "An alliance is a relationship forged between two or more individuals or groups that works as a positive for both parties involved."

So, part of the alliance between the Rājput kings in Rajasthan and the Mughals who were ruling from Delhi or Agra was that sometimes these Rājput kings would give their daughters to the Mughal emperor or other powerful Mughal rulers in marriage. Jodhabai was given in marriage to Akbar by her father, I think his name was Rājā Bharmal of Amber. When she was married to Akbar, Akbar changed her name to Mariam-uz-Zamani. Historians note that because of her endearing qualities—she was a very qualified woman—and they always note her unique beauty as well, she became Akbar’s principal queen; she became, as we say, Queen Mother. 

I read in several places that Akbar, being very religiously tolerant—we discussed that in several lectures—never forced Jodhabai to change her faith. He never asked her to become a Muslim; rather, he allowed her to practice her Hindu faith, or more specifically, we would say Kṛṣṇa consciousness, because being the daughter of a Rājput king, she was a Kṛṣṇa-bhakta from birth. Such an interesting history! 

So now, like any devotee of Kṛṣṇa, she had heard about and she knew about Vṛndāvana. It is actually described that partly due to her influence, her husband, the Emperor Akbar, was much involved in the construction of the Govindajī temple; she had a big influence on him. Now, in Vṛndāvana at this time, Rūpa and Sanātana Gosvāmīs were writing their books on the science of Kṛṣṇa consciousness, and with the other Gosvāmīs, they were locating the lost places of Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes. At that time, Jodhabai was just 20 years of age when she decided to make a pilgrimage to Vṛndāvana. A 20-year-old young girl, but Queen Mother, she wanted to go to Braj. 

She arrived there with a huge group of servants, cooks, guards, physicians, many tents, and everything. So naturally, all of Vṛndāvana was abuzz with the news that the Emperor’s wife, the Queen Mother, who was a devotee of Kṛṣṇa, had come to Vṛndāvana on pilgrimage. What’s more, everyone had heard of her beauty—again, she was a very beautiful woman—so all the villagers in the region were eager to see her and behold her beauty. I read in the institute that it was written she was more beautiful than all the goddesses of Svargaloka combined, and in the institute, I saw several manuscripts of poets who wrote about the beauty of Jodhabai.

But she herself was very modest and very shy, and when in Vṛndāvana on *parikramā*, she mainly stayed in her tent and wasn’t visible to the public eye, aside from when she did *parikramā*, and even then, she was very closely surrounded by her maidservants and guards. So, not everyone got to see her, the beautiful Queen. 

Now, at that time, there was a disciple of Śrīla Raghunātha Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī in Vṛndāvana—Raghunātha Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī, one of our six Gosvāmīs. One of his disciples was there, and his name was Gadādhara Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī. He was young at the time; I think he was just married, and he couldn’t understand how people were saying that Jodhabai was so beautiful, because as far as he had been taught, Rādhārāṇī and Kṛṣṇa, and in particular Rādhārāṇī, were the epitome and the personification of all beauty. So he was saying, "Why is everyone so interested in the Queen Mother?" But he heard she was so beautiful, so he decided to try and see what everyone was talking about—if this was true, that she was the most beautiful.

To begin with, his friends tried to convince him not to go or not to try and see her, because they said she is always in her tent attended by her maidservants, and men are not allowed even in the vicinity of the Queen’s tent, and when she goes on *parikramā*, you can’t even get a glance of her. So, this young man, Gadādhara Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī, came up with a very unusual plan. He took the clothes of his wife, and he disguised himself as a woman. Impersonating one of the Queen’s personal attendants, he went to where she was encamped, and somehow he made it into her tent. 

When he saw her, it’s described he was impressed because she was so beautiful. But moments later, he was seen running from her tent through the Vṛndāvana forest towards his residence, through the town of Vṛndāvana to his residence, which I believe was near Rādhā-vallabha’s temple. All along the way, he was screaming like a madman, "It’s not true! It’s not so! It’s a lie! Do not believe it!" And when he entered his home, he was in a frenzy. When his wife came out of her room, she was shocked to see her husband, her new husband, and she shouted, "My dear husband, what happened to you? Why are you dressed in my clothes? What’s going on?"

By this time, the whole village had come—all the villagers in Braj and the surrounding areas, I guess, because he was shouting very loud—and they had followed him. They were outside his residence listening as this Gadādhara Bhaṭṭa Gosvāmī replied to his wife. He said very famously, "I had heard that Jodhabai was the most beautiful woman in creation, and that everyone appreciates her beauty, which no doubt is God’s gift to her. But when I saw her with my own eyes, I could understand that her beauty did not compare in any way to our Queen, the Queen of Vṛndāvana, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. Let the world know that Śrī Rādhā’s beauty defeats the beauty of all divine goddesses combined."

Hari Kṛṣṇa. So from that day on in Vṛndāvana and the surrounding areas, the villagers only spoke of the beauty of Śrī Rādhā, Kṛṣṇa’s most beloved. Śrīla Prabhupāda, in his *Rādhā-rasa-sudhā-nidhi* (verse 52), prays, and I quote: "Glory, glory to the delicate personality who bears the name of Rādhā, who all the Vedas cannot fittingly describe, who is the sweetest essence of the ocean of nectar, whose mercy is limitless, who is exceedingly graceful and beautiful, and whose love for Kṛṣṇa has captured Her heart—Vṛndāvaneśvarī Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī." Nice pastimes, isn't it?

### Tāj Bībī

Now, I mentioned two personalities today. There was another wife, the queen of Akbar, a queen named Tāj Bībī, and her story is just as incredible and beneficial for us to hear. Nowadays, you know, people like to hear about mundane persons' activities; it’s all over the news. I mean, who else do you hear about in mundane news? But Śrīla Prabhupāda encourages us to just hear about the divine persons. In his *Padyāvalī* (verse 55), it’s one of my favorites:

"O earth, for as long as the sun and moon do shine, please bear that best of men, that pious person whose mind is motionless with joy as he remembers Hari, whose body bristles, whose eyes overflow with tears of bliss. Why bear the burden of those others who have resolved to come and go in this abode of death?"

So, this following story, this pastime of this Queen Tāj Bībī, it is still very popular here in Śrī Vṛndāvana, and you’ll see why. Unlike Jodhabai, who was born Hindu, Tāj Bībī was born a Muslim. Actually, she was the daughter of Akbar’s spiritual master. When Akbar’s spiritual master passed away, Akbar was thinking, "Who will take care of her? Who will take care of my spiritual master’s daughter?" But not finding anyone he felt qualified, he decided to marry her. So she became Queen—you know, the Emperor has many queens.

One time, Tāj Bībī was going from Delhi to Emperor Akbar’s fort in Agra; actually, he was waiting for her there. She was passing through Mathurā and Vṛndāvana, which of course are on the way, riding on a palanquin, when she heard the sound of a big bell coming from Govindajī’s temple. You may remember this big bell—we mentioned it in another class—how, years later, Śivārāma Gosvāmī rings that bell to warn that Aurangzeb’s army was approaching Vṛndāvana. So, the same bell, she is hearing earlier here in history. 

When she heard that bell, you know, sounding the glories of Govindajī in His temple, she asked the palanquin bearer, "Sir, from where is this beautiful sound coming?" The leader of the palanquin bearers replied, "My dear Queen, you do not have to worry. It is coming from the temple of a small Hindu god who is living in Vṛndāvana." So she said, "Oh, *Allāh*, *Allāh*—small?" And *Allāh* means God. Then she said, "Actually, I want to see that God."

The chief palanquin bearer replied, "No, your husband will be very unhappy with this, and he’s waiting for you. He will become upset." So she replied, "I do not care. I want to see the Lord of the Hindus. I want to see the Lord of the Hindus." And she ordered the palanquin bearers to head for that temple. When they arrived at Govindajī’s majestic temple, she got off the palanquin, and while walking towards the temple, she said to the main palanquin bearer, "And what is the name of this Hindu God?" And he replied, "Oh, they address Him as Govindajī."

Now, her scribes, those who have written about her, have written that when she entered the temple and saw this beautiful, majestic, threefold-bending form of Govindajī, she became mesmerized and she couldn’t move. It is written she could not take her eyes off the deity. After some time, I was reading, her guards saw tears flowing down her cheeks. But not failing to—no one was feeling qualified to go and tell her, "Let’s go, this is a Hindu temple." No one said anything. Actually, several hours went by, and finally, one of her maidservants approached her and said very softly, "My dear Queen, your husband, the Emperor Akbar, is waiting for you in Agra."

Tāj Bībī turned to her and said famously, "I will not leave. I will stay here in Vṛndāvana with Govindajī for the rest of my life." Wow! Everyone was stunned, and she never left. She fell in love—divine love, true love, real love—she fell in love with the deity of Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī, Govindajī. And this so much reminds me of Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī’s prayer in *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu* (1.2.239), a well-known prayer, but it means so much more to me now:

"My dear friend, if you are indeed attached to your worldly friends, do not look at the smiling face of Lord Govinda as He stands on the bank of the Yamunā at Keśī-ghāṭa, casting sidelong glances. He places His flute to His lips, which seem like newly blossomed twigs. His transcendental body, bending in three places, appears very bright in the moonlight."

Born in an esteemed Islamic family—her father was the guru of Akbar—she stayed in Braj. She never went to Agra to meet her husband, and he allowed this. This was Akbar, very religiously tolerant, and he could understand, it’s described, her faith in God, and he allowed her to stay. Really amazing. She renounced her role as Queen and never left Braj. To devotees, this is *bhakti*; this is devotion to the Supreme Lord.

Now, while living in Vṛndāvana, Tāj Bībī wrote many beautiful poems for Govindajī. Again, she was a very talented woman. She wrote over 400 poems for Govindajī and other deities in Vṛndāvana as well, and her writing is a beautiful mix of Urdu language and Brajabhāṣā. Mainly Brajabhāṣā, but because she was from a Mughal family, one can also see the effect of the Urdu language in her poems. 

It’s really interesting that while living in Vṛndāvana, she eventually took initiation. She took initiation into the Puṣṭi-mārga, which is in the line coming from Vallabhācārya, a contemporary of Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu. She took *dīkṣā*, and here in Vṛndāvana, devotees still sing her poems or her songs. She actually left her body while singing in front of Gopāla, or nowadays Śrīnāthajī, the deity discovered by Mādhavendra Purī at Govardhana Hill. That’s where she left—at the foot of Govardhana Hill in front of Gopāla, or Śrīnāthajī. She was singing to Him when she left, and she was placed in *samādhi* in Gokula, some distance away, near to another famous Muslim person who became a great devotee—his name was Raskhān. We mentioned him in the previous lecture, and her *samādhi* in Gokula is called the *samādhi* of Tāj Bībī.

Now, I would like to conclude today with the song she composed in front of Gopāla, or Śrīnāthajī, which she was singing when she left this world. She was composing the song as she was sitting in front of the deity; she was singing from her heart out of her love and devotion for the deity, and as she concluded the song, she left her body. It’s a famous song, so we will conclude with the words from the heart of this pure Vaiṣṇavī. Her poem, or her song, is called *O Sākhī*:

"The beat of countless drums has started. Let’s go and see the play of the Holi festival, heralding that dark-complexioned enjoyer, Śrī Kṛṣṇa. Our in-laws are opposing us, but what can we do? I dared to hear the beats of countless drums as I went to the Yamunā to fill my water pot during the Holi festival in hopes of meeting Kṛṣṇa, but He came and broke my pot on the bank of the Yamunā. Oh, just see! Holi is taking place nearer to the palace of Nanda Mahārāja. Let’s go and play, sing, and laugh together. After all, it’s a big celebration. Various other instruments are being played by the cowherd boys, and Śrī Kṛṣṇa is playing His flute. He is decorated with a peacock feather crown, and a beautiful cloth is tightly bound around His waist. Tāj says, 'Oh, just see! The Lord of Tāj is coming dancing towards me.'"

And with those final words, she left this world and went to Goloka Kṛṣṇa. Oh, boy, to hear the lives of all these different devotees we’ve been speaking about in this series about Govindajī, it’s just so satisfying and enlivening. We want to follow in their footsteps. So, I’ll leave you here with that, and I have a couple more personalities who are very famous for their service to Govindajī in Jaipur, and I’ll share that with you in the next lecture. Thank you, Prabhus.
