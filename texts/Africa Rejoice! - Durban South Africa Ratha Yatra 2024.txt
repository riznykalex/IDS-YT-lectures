"# Africa Rejoice! - Durban South Africa Ratha-yātrā 2024

All together, one voice.

*Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

And again, one Kṛṣṇa.

*Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

A few days ago, I was sitting on the banks of the sacred Gaṅgā River in R̥ṣikeśa, India, where we had our R̥ṣikeśa Kīrtan Fest for 21 days. And now I’m sitting on the shores of the Indian Ocean at the southernmost tip of the African continent, in the Republic of South Africa, for our 36th annual Ratha-yātrā festival. Sitting here, many emotions are coming from my heart; many memories are coming, especially of the very first one we did in 1988. At that time, if I remember correctly, I was temple president for a while, and we got the idea to do Ratha-yātrā here in South Africa. We understood how dear it was to Śrīla Prabhupāda. We were remembering how my dear Godbrother, Jīva Prabhu, received so much mercy from Śrīla Prabhupāda for organizing Ratha-yātrā in the beginning in America. Knowing how dear it was to Śrīla Prabhupāda—actually, from his youth as a means to spread Kṛṣṇa consciousness all over the world—we decided to do it here in Durban, South Africa.

It was a challenge because at that time there was apartheid here in South Africa; segregation in many terrible ways of the various ethnic groups in this country. South Africa was actually infamous for that, but we wanted to do a festival where we could bring all the ethnic groups together under one banner, under one spiritual banner, because that is our understanding of how there can be peace in the world. Although there are many different nationalities, languages, peoples, traditions, and customs around the world, in the ultimate sense, we are all part of one spiritual family. I have many times said that in the *Bhagavad-gītā*, Kṛṣṇa says that He is the seed-giving father of all living entities, every created being. So, if we all have the same father, we are ultimately brothers and sisters, and that is actually the real platform for having peace. Of course, diversity is also nice; they say variety is the spice of life, so we can appreciate many different cultures and the peoples in those cultures. But ultimately, we have to understand we are all part of the greater family of God; we are all God’s children. 

So, we thought to demonstrate that because there is the philosophy—there is the theory—and then there is the practice. So Kṛṣṇa consciousness is meant to show the practice: how people can live together peacefully and, in a peaceful environment, achieve the real goal of life, which is Kṛṣṇa consciousness, love of God. That was our challenge, to do it here where apartheid was causing so much division in society, almost to the point of civil war. So in our humble way, we were going to make the attempt. It was a bold attempt. I was remembering how Prabhupāda said one time that his only fault, "quote-unquote," was that he was always thinking big and engaging us in big projects to spread the Kṛṣṇa consciousness movement from its inception. So Ratha-yātrā was part of Prabhupāda’s plan. 

So, to make a long story short, we devised a plan that we would approach the city hall, the city administration. I went in myself because I was the only white boy in the team that was planning Ratha-yātrā. There was Śyāmasundara, my dear Godbrother from India; there was Śrī Hari, a local Indian devotee here in South Africa; and Yadu-vara, a wealthy businessman at that time—he was also Indian. So I went in as the white man, and I told the city council I wanted to do a little festival for my friends on the beachfront. We were going to pull a little wagon, we were going to have some children there, some balloons, and we were going to distribute some delicious vegetarian food. They thought it was a little odd. They said, "How big?" I said, "This big." Anyway, we managed to charm them, and somehow they agreed and they gave us a place called the Bay of Plenty, a beautiful garden area just off the beachfront. I am sitting well, 30 meters away, we’re doing it here again.

* **I come to the festival here in Durban every year; it’s just a very enlightening time. People come with good, high spirits, and there’s a lot of good energy in the form of philosophy, in the form of singing, dancing, a kind of partying. The food is great, and I think the interaction amongst all the people, the relationship building, is of paramount importance. So I come every year to participate in that and also to put on drama presentations based on the Vedic reality—morality theater, you can call it. So I’m enjoying it; I love it. Every year gets better.** *

It’s my first time performing at the Festival of Chariots in Durban, and it’s been incredible. I’ve been a part of ISKCON for just over a year, and I’ve been wanting to be of service more and I didn’t know how. I think in my heart I wanted to sing for Kṛṣṇa, and Kṛṣṇa almost made it up immediately. It’s been humbling. I think the best thing has just been the kindness of the devotees; it’s been welcoming. I feel like I’ve made family, so I hope to come back. I’m very excited. I think the biggest gift I’m taking away is the relationships I’ve made, and to those devotees, thank you so much; you made me feel at home and I can’t wait to be back. *Hari!*

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

*Kṛṣṇa.*

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.* I come here because this is one of the first festivals that encouraged me to become a devotee. So much is on offer here; plenty to hear from the spiritual discussions, there is a large community of people who attend this festival, and it is what Śrīla Prabhupāda wanted to distribute on mass: the Holy Name, *prasādam*, the association of the deities. So it’s also an annual get-together; we get to meet our friends who we may not see throughout the year, and we come and gather to worship the Lord in the streets of Durban.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

And again.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

***

From the time I was a little girl, I’ve been a part of this festival on various levels and doing so much, but one memory that stands out for me so authentically is that I used to be seated on Lord Jagannātha’s chariot as a little girl. I clearly remember donning a *sārī*; it used to be bright, hot days like this, and my parents would dress me up all beautifully in a traditional Indian *sārī* with *gopī* dots, the *surmā* bindī, and the entire attire. I’d climb up the ladder of the chariot and be seated with Lord Jagannātha and being pulled through the streets of Durban. So today, all those decades later, I’m back here pulling on the ropes of Lord Jagannātha’s chariot, and that for me is so immensely special. It just shows the love that we have for this festival and that personal connection that I will always carry with me in my heart.

And again.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

***

I’ve been involved with this festival more or less since the inception. Indradyumna Mahārāja was the organizer, but I was there. And since then, you know, like 30-something years, I think I’ve been at every festival. And for me personally, it’s a very deep experience. On one level, seeing the mercy of Jagannātha, Baladeva, and Subhadrā Devī—how kind they are and how they accept kind of uncomfortable and awkward situations out of their kindness to make themselves available to ourselves as devotees and the conditioned souls—so that’s a very important thing to me. And then in a similar, but somewhat different sense, personally I feel very happy when I see so many people coming and really getting something out of the festival, getting an experience they in many cases have never had before. Even some of them come and tell me, actually as I’m moving around the festival or sitting somewhere, that they come and tell me that this is just something new and something very elevated for them as an experience. So when I see that, then I feel very satisfied because I know that the devotees have done all the work putting everything together, hands-on. They’ve really worked hard, extremely hard, to make Kṛṣṇa consciousness accessible to people.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

I feel blessed, I feel honored; it’s a great privilege to be here, you know, associate with so many wonderful devotees and connect. It’s always very nice and it’s very inspiring. This is why I always come, you know, because right now in Mthatha where I am, I basically don’t have *saṅga*, you know? So I always come to recharge my batteries, my *bhakti* battery, you know? So yes, I’m really grateful to be here.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

I can’t even explain it to you; it’s something that you personally would have to feel because I can’t put the feeling of an energy into words. I don’t have the poetic capabilities, so my only advice for someone who truly wants to experience the festival is to volunteer your services in something that you enjoy—whether it’s speaking or serving others in different ways, whether it’s at like the gift tent. My personal belief is everybody should at least once go and serve at the Food for Life tent, and when you experience what it’s like to dish a hot plate of food for somebody who’s been hungry for who knows how long, it really changes something in you as a person.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

***

For me personally, it holds a very special place in my heart because I actually got inspired at this festival itself. Although I was raised in Kṛṣṇa consciousness, it was at this very festival when the final *kīrtana* was happening on the stage and the person who then became my spiritual master was singing, and it touched the core of my heart. So I’m sure others would have similar experiences as well where they feel this connection and that seed of devotion will be awakened in their hearts and their spiritual journeys will begin as well.

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

***

I knew it was going to cause some problems because it was like a white lie on behalf of Kṛṣṇa. And well, what we did is we collected a lot of funds, we rented a lot of massive tents, we built a huge chariot—a typical big Ratha-yātrā chariot—hooked up a lot of *pandal* items. Even the devotees were wondering what we were doing because I didn’t want to tell too many devotees so that the word would get out and the city officials would hear. So a team of about 20 devotees, we came down at midnight. We had the cart pulled by truck from a secret location where we were building it, and then the last two kilometers we pulled it by ropes here. No one could see it; it was 2:00 in the morning. Put it on the festival site, somehow we managed to put up all these massive tents and this big stage and lots of hundreds and hundreds of chairs, etc.

And yeah, so devotees were told that—they woke up in the morning, "We’re going to have a Ratha-yātrā parade." They also thought it would be small-time. They started filtering down, but the best publicity we’ve heard—the best advertising—is by word of mouth. So very quickly, somehow by Kṛṣṇa’s mercy, it spread around town: "There’s a big, nice festival going on on the Durban beachfront." It was on a Saturday morning. Word spread quickly because in those days, word spread quickly—the good news or the bad news—in South Africa. So it spread quickly, and suddenly, at around 10:00 in the morning when we were meant to have the festival, it started getting out on the radio, newspaper; early morning radio caught wind of it. It wasn’t there, so because the word got out that the parade was going to start around 10:00, the municipality sent six police officers down on motorcycles to check it out with the idea that it was too big, "You have to stop it." 

So the problem was for them that it was getting so big that it was hard to manage all these peoples—different ethnic groups, the white population, the black Africans, whatever the different ethnic groups were, were spread out. It’s an open festival. So by the time the police got here, it was like a big crowd and really anxious, and they—the police—didn’t know what we were going to do. "You're going to pull that cart?" I said, "Yes." And so rather than report back that, "We have to stop it," it was too big to stop. So what the police did, the six police officers and their motorcycles, they went to the front of the parade, and we gathered the ropes—all the peoples—and started pulling, and the balloons were there, and the *kīrtana* was there, and the amplification was there. And Kṛṣṇa tricked them because the police officers, they headed up the parade just to make sure it didn’t get out of control. That’s the best they could manage, to maintain it, that it didn’t get out of control. They were supposed to stop it, but it was too big, but they ended up leading the parade. 

This was one of the miracles of Kṛṣṇa consciousness. Devotees know that by the mercy of the Lord, Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu, many miracles are happening because of the *saṅkīrtana* movement. Miracle means something which contradicts the laws of nature. So in so many situations, people become interested in our movement; we’re given permissions for this and permissions for that, and so much positive change is going on in the world, spiritual change, because of Lord Caitanya’s *saṅkīrtana*. We could talk about that for hours. Anyway, it was a huge success—so successful that next year, because you know, apartheid was something very sinister and there were those who were for it and there were many South Africans who were against it, of course there’s a whole history about that, but somehow that festival, because it brought together these different ethnic groups—the Zulu nation came, the white people came, the coloreds came—somehow it was a festival everyone was enjoying. The word got out, so next year when I humbly went to the same municipality, they said, "Sure, go ahead."

This is, one time Prabhupāda said, "One day historians will note how this *saṅkīrtana* movement saved the world in its darkest hour." That was a dark period, but we added much light and joy and happiness, and we gave hope to the people of South Africa. And of course, this is not a problem which is just here in South Africa; it’s all over the world. People are fighting over so many things: this country, that country, these peoples, that language, etc., etc. So we showed in a minuscule way how it’s possible to bring people together to enjoy on the spiritual platform. And it was interesting because it was put on by, you know, a crew of devotees—an ethnic crew of devotees—but it’s really not an Indian movement. Some people initially perceived it as an Indian movement, but this is not an Indian movement. It may have appeared in India, but this is a movement from the spiritual sky, as Narottama dāsa Ṭhākura says. This movement has come to the material world; it remains a spiritual movement. It’s meant to take us back home, back to Godhead. 

So we spread the message, and therefore for the last 36 years, the city council has very eagerly given us permission to do this festival. So here we are again on the Durban beachfront, and you’ll see in this video how much people of this city and people of this country are enjoying this Ratha-yātrā festival. So it should continue; it should continue here, it should continue all over the world, because Prabhupāda saw it as a very wonderful way of spreading the message of Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu and Lord Kṛṣṇa’s teachings. All glories to Śrīla Prabhupāda, who had the vision to save the world in its darkest hour. Jagat-guru Śrīla Prabhupāda, Lord Jagannātha, Baladeva, Subhadrā Devī, Ratha-yātrā, Durban beachfront 2024.

Let’s all sing together one voice. *Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

One more time from the heart. *Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*

*Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa.*""
