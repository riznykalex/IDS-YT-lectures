"### 00419_I Must Go To Vrindavan! - Part 4

Jaya Jaya Śrī Caitanya, Jaya Nityānanda, Jaya Jaya Śrī Caitanya, Jaya Nityānanda, Jaya Jait Chandra Jaya Govinda, Jaya Jaya Śrī Caitanya, Jaya Nityānanda.

Gurave Namaḥ, Śrī Caitanya, Jaya Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Hare Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa Kṛṣṇa, Hare Hare, Hare Rāma, Hare Rāma. We continue this short series about the journey of the Lord with great joy and enthusiasm. This is the fourth time I have been here in Vṛndāvana. This morning we reached Jharikhanda, which is a province in India. It is somewhere between Purī and Māyāpur. Mahāprabhu had to go first north and then west to reach Vṛndāvana. This is a forest with a lot of thorny bushes. We heard a beautiful description of how the Lord liberated the animals there with His kīrtana. One living being is another's food, and these animals always fight among themselves in the jungle, but the influence of the Lord cleansed their hearts of jealousy. Afterwards, the tigers and the lions danced together and perhaps even embraced, giving up this jealous nature of theirs, and joy awoke in their hearts.

Kṛṣṇa says this in the Bhagavad-gītā, that the seed of prema is eternally in the heart. This is not something you have to go somewhere and get from somewhere else. It is like sleep; they say the soul sleeps. Jīva Gosvāmī says, "How long will you sleep in front of the witch called Māyā?" And how can you wake up a sleeping person? With a loud noise. The classic example of this is the alarm clock. You have to set the clock to 3:30 to come to your senses. Then it is a different question how many times we press the snooze button so that it rings again. The soul also needs a spiritual sound to wake up, and this sound in Kali-yuga is the holy name of the Lord.

When Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu had this wonderful kīrtana, the stones on the riverbank became liquid; they just melted. All the pilgrims look at the footprints there—the elephants, the tigers, the deer, the snakes, the chipmunks, and the squirrels—all are very close to each other. It is very wonderful because when these stones melted, the marks of the Lord's feet and the marks of the animals all became fixed in the stone. We were there with the shepherd and his family. It was a long journey, and then we had to walk even more into the jungle, but there we saw with our own eyes the imprint of the animals in the stones, and the imprint of the snakes is there too. Years ago, Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas moved there and built a little sanctuary, a little hut, and they are there to take care of this area. Every morning they circle there and meditate on this kīrtana.

In the case of Mahāprabhu, it was also made of molten stone. It is completely set in stone. I ran over and put it on my head in honor of their imprint. I smoothed it out so that I could continue assisting with the saṅkīrtana. And when I woke up, a pebble was stuck to my forehead, and then I put that in my pocket. There are so many things here. I will list everything for you in the lesson tomorrow; you will faint when you hear it. There was a tiny pebble from Mahāprabhu's lotus feet. This kīrtana went along the entire riverbank, and we also walked there, and we saw the imprint of the lotus feet. The footprint was there, only it was smaller. There are still tigers there, so it was a little dangerous to have such a guide. We followed the footprints for about a kilometer along the riverbank, and then the footprints disappeared into the jungle. I wanted to follow them, but our guide said that we were not allowed to go there anymore because it was a dense jungle and there were still tiger cubs there.

This is a very poor area of India, but if I ever get there, I will definitely roll in the dust, because this is a very special place, where these very lowly living entities have been liberated by the Lord's kīrtana. After successfully transferring from Jharikhanda, Mahāprabhu and Balabhadra Bhaṭṭa continued towards Vṛndāvana. The story goes that there were days when the road was as easy as if they were walking on rose petals. How poetic! They don't say that the road was beautiful, but that it was as if they were walking on rose petals. In China, the devotees create a carpet of flowers in all types of different decorations for 100 meters. I was embarrassed, thinking, "These flowers have been picked for me to walk on them." I have had this experience because in China the bhaktas do it. When I get out of the car and get to the temple, they usually sprinkle the 100 meters with flower petals. This is the proper way to worship the spiritual master.

I go to China every year, and the bhaktas there are really hungry for fellowship because it is a communist country and there is no religion. Now, I can't wear a saffron dress, I have to wear a different dress, and it was Chinese clothes; maybe I will wear the Chinese shirt tomorrow. Their culture is that they respect the elderly, their superiors, their parents, and their bosses. If a spiritually developed person arrives, they respect them very much and are very firm in their belief in the guru. This is not a poor country; it is very rich. The people dress nicely, they drive nice cars, the cities are clean. There is a problem with what they eat—they eat everything that flies in the sky except airplanes, and everything that walks on the ground except cars, and everything that swims in the ocean except submarines. They are very hungry for spirituality. For example, during a kīrtana, they all cry. We grew up in religious cultures, so we are used to it, but they don't even have a word for God. There are lower planetary systems that are very rich, as rich as the upper planets, but the upper planetary systems have religion, while the lower ones do not. It is true that there is no religion there, but people are very hungry for spirituality. When they receive the grace of the Lord, they cry real tears.

When they give a donation, they usually put it in a red gold envelope like this, and they always kneel down, bow down, and give the donation. The ground is strewn with lots of flowers. I always offer this adoration to the Lord, but they scatter so many flowers that I can't even see where we are going. Prabhupāda often said that flowers were the only wealth left in the valley. He said that flowers are like Kṛṣṇa. They say that some days when they traveled it was as if they were walking on such soft things, but other days it was as hard as if they were walking on burning coals. That is how it is when you travel; sometimes you are in a palace, sometimes you can stay in a resort, but sometimes it is just so cheap. But Mahāprabhu ignored these things. Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja says that He is the son of Śacī, who is the Lord's devotee tasting the nectar of Kṛṣṇa. His wonderful eyes always sought Kṛṣṇa's abode in everything. Every night when He lay down to rest, He would wave His lotus hand and fan Himself.

He always started by washing His lotus feet, because He was barefoot all day, and because He always washed them with His tears. He cried so much from ecstasy that He washed His own lotus feet with those tears. Let us think about what kind of movement Śrīla Prabhupāda invited us to: that someone worships their guru in this way, washes the lotus feet with their tears, and holds the lotus feet of the Lord, looks at them, and thinks to themselves, "Just look at these lotus feet." These lotus feet are worshipped by Indra. Brahmā washes them. They are adorned by Lord Śiva. The Yamunā kisses them. Lakṣmī Devī serves them and is defeated by Garuḍa.

They are like jewels. They are the life of the living, the heart of the devotees, and the faith of the faithful. These lotus feet saved Mahārāja Ambarīṣa. These touched the hearts of the Mahārāja Bali. These are the only refuge of the Vedas, by the grace of the Matsya Avatāra. These served as the basis for the churning of the Ocean of Milk, and Govardhana Hill alone is pleased when these feet of Mahāprabhu touch it. He has such a pure heart that He thinks of the lotus feet of the Lord in this way. When we see the imprint, we just look at it.

After many weeks, they arrived in Vārāṇasī, which is about halfway between Vṛndāvana and Bengal. We know that there He met Tapana Miśra, who invited Him to his home, and finally, He could live in a comfortable place. The news reached Prakāśānanda Sarasvatī, who was the leader of the Māyāvādī sannyāsīs, that this happy, dancing sannyāsī was here in Vārāṇasī. He accused the Lord of many things, and then the Lord made this historical statement that Prakāśānanda and his followers should never attack the bhaktas because they are insulting. But there is a saying that curiosity killed the cat. It is a little different here; when someone is curious, they get into trouble because they go everywhere they are not supposed to go.

So we are curious about something, and then we might get into trouble. We bought a hedgehog from some boys. The mother hedgehog died, and the boys took the five little ones home. They asked, "Would you like to see our pets? They are so cute. Can you play with them too? They will say you are their favorite Mahārāja." I went out and looked. I picked up one of the hedgehogs. I told him, "Hey, you are very low on the evolutionary ladder." But this morning I was talking about how the Lord released all kinds of animals into the Jharikhanda forest, even hedgehogs. So stop the fussing and listen to me. Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa. That is how I noticed it. Someone asked if I was giving him initiation now. I said no. And then I put it back in the box. One of the boys was also very curious and he wanted to hold it, and then he took it out of the box, but the snake bit him so he threw it away. This is an example of when curiosity kills the cat.

You have heard of Kumbha-melā, where millions of people gather. We were at a crossroads where the waters meet, and once we went too. This was in the 70s. There was a camp of the ISKCON devotees, and we always sang and danced. There was also a camp of the followers of Śaṅkarācārya. I became curious and wanted to go there. They say, "You are Brahman, you are Kṛṣṇa." There were 5,000 to 6,000 people there, and they were listening to the speaker, and he was just talking about little Kṛṣṇa Dāmodara. He told the story of the time in Vraja when Kṛṣṇa became angry at His mother, broke the butter, and ran away, and Yaśodā came and caught little Kṛṣṇa. He told this Dāmodara-līlā story as if I had only listened to it from beginning to end from my Bhāgavatam. He described how golden Kṛṣṇa was and how beautiful His lotus eyes were. He said Yaśodā serves Him in the mood of affection, and Kṛṣṇa plays with the cowherd boys and kills the demons. He spoke so beautifully. I listened intently, and at the end, he said, "Kṛṣṇa was the one who ended it. And you are all this Kṛṣṇa. I will give you a mantra, and one day when you look in the mirror, you will also realize that you are all God." This is what it means when curiosity kills the cat—just like when the snake bit off half of the boy's finger, which we then fixed with a stick.

Today, he said that the Māyāvādīs are the biggest offenders. And then Mahāprabhu gave this instruction that His followers should never hear from the Māyāvādīs and should not associate with them. Then they continued their journey to Vṛndāvana. We know that after a while, they arrived. They walked for three months and reached Rāmagrāma, which is the present-day village of Aligarh, about 70 km from Vṛndāvana. There was a brāhmaṇa who went to the Lord. These are always very auspicious signs when a cow comes up to you and stops in front of you. These are such transcendental omens when a brāhmaṇa comes to you and speaks kind words to you while walking a cow towards you.

I remember when my student friend left. She told me then that she was a very fearless girl. When I was there with him, he told me, "Gurudeva, I am really looking forward to seeing what is there, what is waiting for me on the other side." He was very deeply in love with Kṛṣṇa in the last few weeks because he only wanted to hear about Kṛṣṇa's pastimes. He really wanted me to be there when he left. I was in Poland on a festival tour, and I got the message that Gurudeva was leaving. Then I packed everything, got on the plane, and got there at the last minute. He was very, very happy when he saw me. He said this sentence: "I am so looking forward to seeing what awaits me on the other side," and that is what faith means. Then he asked me to whisper to him. There was Gāndharvikā, one of my first Russian students, whispering in his ear about the other side. When he took his last breath, he sat up like this, and then he fell back. At that very moment, a very handsome man entered the room, and a cow stopped under the window. And that was just Krishna's arrangement.

As the Lord approached Vṛndāvana, this giant bliss arrived. He greeted them and began to tell them about the cowherd boy's adventures. He told a story that there was a very hot summer day when Kṛṣṇa was eating butter on His mother's lap. Mahāprabhu listened to him with great joy, for that was why He had come. A little further away, the shepherd boys stopped and started whistling and calling Kṛṣṇa, saying, "Come, come, play with us! Leave your mother there." When someone whistled to tell us to go now, the brāhmaṇa started talking about how the cowherds whistled. I read this and thought how wonderful it was that I could now learn how the gopas whistled. What a wonderful movement we have joined! Madhumaṅgala whistled by sticking his index finger in his mouth. And Subala whistled so loudly that he hit both of his index fingers. Arjuna, on the other hand, knew how to do it without her fingers. Śrīdāma, with more than three lips, Kṛṣṇa was the hero of the loud whistling, and He was the one who whistled the loudest, at the highest pitch. When you are young boys, you often give each other nicknames. When I was young, we moved schools, and I said, "My nickname is Tiger." We moved to another city, and I started going to a different school, and one of the guys came up to me on the first day and asked, "Brian, what's your first name?" And I said, "Tiger." You have friends, but very few.

Hearing this whistling, other bhaktas also became eager to call Kṛṣṇa. The demigods like to fly there in their flower planes over Vraja and watch these flirtations. That's good. Kṛṣṇa was sitting on Yaśodā's lap, and the boys were whistling for Him to come. "Hey Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa, Kṛṣṇa!" We also call Kṛṣṇa by His mahā-mantra: "Dear Kṛṣṇa, dear energy of Kṛṣṇa, please engage me in Your service." Prabhupāda said this many times: what is the meaning of this mantra? That we shout like this, "My friend, my friend, my friend!" There were the 33 million demigods, and they also became enthusiastic about calling Kṛṣṇa, but not by whistling, because that is for boys. They were all very elevated bhaktas. They called Kṛṣṇa and started chanting the Vedas. The brāhmaṇas began to chant the Puruṣa-sūkta. The smārtas started showing such mudrās. The bhaktas began to call Him by chanting the holy name, but the shepherd boys were covered in mud. They just think He is the coolest boy in the village. They just kept whistling. And instead of going to the demigods, or the smārtas, or the brāhmaṇas, Kṛṣṇa went there, to the cowherds.

The demigods thought, "Well, we are the demigods. Why does Kṛṣṇa go to these cowherds?" It is very difficult to understand what this means, what the love of the Vrajavāsīs means and how it controls Kṛṣṇa. One of them says that no one can understand His mood and how Kṛṣṇa is dealing with these very dear devotees. This was the image with which Mahāprabhu was received when He arrived at the Vṛndāvana border. After he told me about this, that brāhmaṇa started crying. One of them describes that the Lord is called *dīra-gambhīra*, which means that He is infinitely patient. He hugged Śrī Caitanya, and from the moment He started whistling and picked up this cowherd atmosphere, He also started to play like a boy. It is very nice that you met this brāhmaṇa in this way.

Since the journey of Śrī Caitanya was difficult and it was very cold at that time, this brāhmaṇa convinced Him to stay there in this garden. The next morning the Lord woke up and went so fast for the next few kilometers that it was as if He was floating. They say it is very hard to imagine the Lord as He walks towards Vṛndāvana, because with every step He takes, His love for Him grows stronger on the path to Kṛṣṇa-Vṛndāvana. There, in the Gambhīra temple, He experienced this mahā-bhāva—Rādhā-Kṛṣṇa sentiment—than which there is no higher order. He appeared for two reasons: to perform the yuga-dharma and to experience the love of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. As love increased, this is it.

In the Gambhīra, He experiences this great love, and it is difficult to read this, how He hits His head against the wall and experiences such ecstatic symptoms. As He goes along the road, this great love grew in His heart, completely endless. This is from Kṛṣṇadāsa Kavirāja, in the Caitanya-caritāmṛta, Madhya-līlā, 17th chapter, 226th verse. The mind of the Lord was always immersed in ecstatic love, but when He moved towards Vṛndāvana, then this love increased many times. How wonderful this bhakti is! We are moving towards this, all of us. He is now in Vraja. He finally gets there. There is this poet Lalita Kiśora from Vraja, who describes how, as He heard the sound of the birds on the dusty road towards Vṛndāvana, He met another devotee named Govardhana-dāsa. Govardhana-dāsa celebrated a bhajana. There are such sādhu-bābās who just wander around Vraja and sing about the pastimes of Kṛṣṇa. These are such transcendental maestros that they go from one place to another and sing about the glories of Kṛṣṇa. Last year, we also met such a bābā. He was just absorbed in kīrtana. I told him that we were here at Govardhana, trying to get a feel for Vraja's atmosphere, and with your bhajana, we could do it. I could hire you, and he said yes, but we could barely keep up with him. He was 90 years old, but he went so fast. I'll play it for you tomorrow morning. There was no harmonium, no mṛdaṅga, no drums, no violin, no saxophone, just his voice, but it was perfect bhajana, and I could listen to it forever.

The guru also said that the Hare Kṛṣṇa mantra is best heard from a pure devotee. When Prabhupāda was there, in Tompkins Square, he sang there, and he only had those two double-headed drums, and he didn't even have a loudspeaker, but more than 100 people could listen to him sing. That is why it is important for us to listen to your Prabhupāda. When we joined, these bhajanas were heard from everywhere. And these bhajanas still have the same power today as they did 60-70 years ago. Turn on Śrīla Prabhupāda's bhajanas and listen in your homes. It is a very deep sound vibration that is full of nectar. This bābā was also called Govardhana-dāsa and was recorded singing. Govardhana is very mystical. This Govardhana-dāsa, whom the Lord met, he sang a bhajana about the mūrti of Mādhavendra Purī, about the beauty of the Gopāla-mūrti. Mādhavendra Purī has a small shrine not far away. That is the place where Mādhavendra Purī found the Gopāla-mūrti. He sang about the beauty of this Gopāla, and then he asked the Lord, "I would like to know what the meaning of what I am singing, what the translation of this song is." He was very, very excited to hear it here. They so lovingly call Him "Gopāla-jī." The translation is that Gopāla takes on a wonderful form in which He holds one hand up, and that means He is calling His devotees to Him: "Oh my dear devotees, please come to me." He is looking down a little bit like this, and with this, He shows the bhaktas to take refuge at the feet of the lotus. And He keeps His other hand on His waist, and His fist is closed like this, which symbolizes that "I am the only one who knows the most profound rasa, which very few great souls know." So this is the place where the followers of Puṣṭi-mārga worship.

My dear bhaktas, come! Take shelter at My lotus feet. He holds His hand like this, and it symbolizes that I know this very intimate rasa that very few great souls know. Balabhadra heard this and said, "Sir, with all respect, your Gopāla is surely in possession of this rasa, but as you mentioned, He very rarely shares it with anyone. But look at the shape of my Mahāprabhu here. He holds one hand up, which means that He is taking this inner love from your Gopāla, and He holds the other hand down, which symbolizes that He freely shares this inner love with everyone, on all conditions, whereas your Gopāla keeps this rasa for Himself. My Mahāprabhu gives this highest love to all the fallen conditioned souls."

Then they continued walking and finally reached the Yamunā. We know that if we bathe in the Ganges, we will be freed from sinful reactions, but Yamunā-devī, the extension of the Viṣṇu-śakti, is also such that if we bathe in her water, we will receive the reward. Very soon the news spread that they had arrived in Vṛndāvana, and so many people came and offered their belongings to Śrī Caitanya that He could not proceed any further. The ladies are so beautiful, the Vraja-bhāṣā language is so beautiful, and they started singing to them in that language. This is just Rādhārāṇī being glorified.

Another brāhmaṇa appeared and invited the Lord to his home. Many devotees have read the books many times, and once someone asked what it was that captured his mind the most, and he said, "Well, how often I dream of Vṛndāvana." Mahāprabhu hesitated a little to go to the brāhmaṇa, but he said that in our house we have the original deity, and Mahāprabhu said, "My dear brāhmaṇa, you are very highly respected here. I am very happy to have met you. Please take me to meet this Deity, who is my only refuge in this world." They stopped at this temple and began talking about the beauty of the Deity there. I saw this too. Everyone thought the Muslims destroyed it, but then we found it, and it turned out that it had just been hidden for many, many centuries. They started talking about the Deity's beauty—beautiful ornaments adorn the form, and the demigods worship Him with various tools. There are four marks on His back: the conch, the flag, the stick, and the flower. He rests His lotus feet on a lotus flower offered to Him by the elephant Gajendra. Lakṣmī and Garuḍa join their hands and stand like this. Indra is there in his form offering daṇḍavats. His hair is tied over His shoulders and adorned with a sparkling stone. When He heard this description, Mahāprabhu began to dance in ecstasy. You see how important this dance is! When we dance, the soul takes over.

This brāhmaṇa who told me about the Deity also started to manifest these very deep ecstatic symptoms—symptoms of prema. You too became ecstatic when you saw Mahāprabhu's dance, and Mahāprabhu recognized the signs of ecstasy, which only the great knowers of prema, like Mahāprabhu, Mādhavendra Purī, and Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, can recognize. He asked, "Who is the brāhmaṇa?" Mahāprabhu recognized these signs of ecstasy, as He is an excellent connoisseur of prema, and asked him, "Where did you get this from? I want this too!" The brāhmaṇa very humbly answered, "My dear sir, Mādhavendra Purī once visited the holy place of Mathurā, and when his holiness was there, he visited my house, ate with us, and then accepted me as a disciple." When the Lord heard this, He immediately gave the brāhmaṇa a sign, but Mahāprabhu could not tolerate the humility of this brāhmaṇa. He told this brāhmaṇa, "Please don't bow down before me. You are the disciple of Mādhavendra Purī, which means you are at the level of my spiritual master." Balabhadra explained to him, "He is a disciple of the Lord's father, and we too are part of this family; we were officially accepted into this family through initiation."

How is it possible, how could we stop practicing the spiritual life when this unconditional love just keeps coming at us? The brāhmaṇa was very pleased when he heard this and said to the Lord, "When I saw You dancing in ecstatic love, I knew that You must have some connection with Mādhavendra Purī, because otherwise You could not have manifested such transcendental emotions." That is what ISKCON gives us—to be proud followers of Śrīla Prabhupāda. Sometimes it happens that a spring like this bursts out of the ground, and the water bursts out, and there is a little lake like this. We have suffered here in the desert of the material world, and Śrīla Prabhupāda has brought us this exhilarating nectar of the prema. If we are persistent and determined, then we will know all these emotions. There are so many other problems—my body is causing problems because it is getting older, and here we are in this movement where there are also so many problems—how can I maintain my enthusiasm? We have to tolerate it. Even if it is a trial, that is what the Śikṣāṣṭakam talks about: to be tolerant, and all this will pass. When a guru gives instructions to one of his disciples, he is intending it for the other disciples as well.

I once went back to the airport for Prabhupāda's lost package and missed the temple program. I was very tired, I missed my class, and I asked the bhaktas to help me carry the suitcase, saying it was very difficult, but they couldn't help. Then I carried the suitcase up to Prabhupāda, and Prabhupāda gave me a big slap on the back and said, "You are making such a great effort in this material world, but when I take you back to Kṛṣṇa, everything will always be very, very easy and sublime there."

So it is entirely appropriate that we end today's lesson by praising Śrīla Prabhupāda. We will end with a poem by a Vaiṣṇava poet who lived in the 1700s. He was born in Teṭa, which is a few miles north of Kaṭvā, and he has a lot of beautiful devotional poems. He has a very famous collection of them called *Pada-kalpataru*. All glory, all glory to the Śrī Guru. He is the wish-fulfilling tree of prema and manifests in a miraculous way. The nectar of his wisdom cleanses ignorance and darkness from my heart. He prepares my heart with the moon-like radiance of his wisdom. Kṛṣṇa gives His knowledge to the humble in this world and indiscriminately gives the holy name to the most humble. I am a sinner and I am wasting my life aimlessly, and I am constantly immersed in temporary illusions. I don't have the slightest bit of humility. Despite all this, my spiritual master gave me instructions and teachings about the sweet atmosphere of Vṛndāvana, the richness of the bhajanas, and the glory of the divine couple. My reading master showered everyone with the nectar of pure gaura-prema-rasa and fulfilled everyone's desire. Yet I could not attain attachment to the lotus feet. Tomorrow there will be a new lesson, and we will hear about how the Lord passes through the forests of Vraja, and what He hears there, what they tell Him, and what He says.

Mahāprabhu kī jaya! Śrīla Prabhupāda kī jaya! Everyone is making me laugh.""
