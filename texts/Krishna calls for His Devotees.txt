"# Ter-kadamba: Krishna Calls for His Devotees

As usual, I begin my lectures in a wonderful way. What are your favorite places on this earth? I noticed that in this place, every spot I go to, I think, "This is my favorite place." This is what it is like to be in Vraja; every place is a favorite. Definitely, this place, Ter-kadamba, is a favorite—my favorite place. We are halfway between where we were, where Rādhārāṇī lives with her so-called husband, and Nanda Grām, where Kṛṣṇa lives with His parents. Here, we can get a little idea of what Vṛndāvana was like 5,000 years ago. When we walked, we saw a very simple countryside where people plowed the land with plows pulled by oxen, walked in the forest, swam in lakes and rivers, and milked cows by hand.

As I was talking to the girls who live in Vraja, I asked them, "Do you milk cows?" They said, "Every morning and every evening." And they are only 8 to 10 years old. I asked them what they do with the milk, and they said, "Mom makes *saṅkṣaya* from it." Milk, with a big smile, is the natural environment that the Lord Himself prefers to live in. We can see that all people have this inclination because even those who live in cities and work hard still want to spend their vacation somewhere in nature. In English, there is a proverb: "Man created cities, but God created the countryside." This is a special rural place; this is the Lord’s place for games. Once, when I lived in Paris, I was giving an interview to a very famous newspaper, and the correspondent, who was very proud of being French, closed his eyes, paused for a few seconds, and said, "People go to London for education and come to Paris to enjoy their feelings. But devotees of the Lord go to Vṛndāvana in India to develop God consciousness." This is what they printed in the newspaper. 

If given a natural choice, all people prefer the countryside. But since they are deceived by illusion, they live in cities, which are described as being under the influence of the threat of *karma*—terrible activity. We prefer exactly these kinds of forests with overhanging branches; they are called *kuñjas*. Nearby is a lake, *kuṇḍa*, and here are such overhanging trees with branches. Trees sometimes create such isolated rooms that look like playhouses, like children have. I remember from childhood we had such houses, and in these places, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa play Their games. That is why we sing, *"Jaya rādhe, jaya kṛṣṇa, jaya vṛndāvana, śrī govinda, gopīnātha, madana-mohana."* *Kuñja* means such natural houses made of overhanging trees, and *bihārī* means one who enjoys. That is, Kṛṣṇa enjoys such games in such places. The next time you hear this song, you can close your eyes and remember this place. 

Ter-kadamba has several translations. One translation of the word *ter* is "to call." There used to be a lot of Kadamba trees here, and Kṛṣṇa loved to climb them. He would call from the height of these trees, calling His gopī friends and the cowherd boys. It is here that the large *śālā* (barn) of Nanda Mahārāja is located, where an infinite number of cows and calves are kept. Many games of Kṛṣṇa took place here because Kṛṣṇa is known as a cowherd boy, and He began to graze calves when He was still too small to graze cows—when He was 5 years old. Usually, boys began herding cows at the age of 6, but Kṛṣṇa did not want to wait too long.

Cows are Kṛṣṇa’s favorite animal. Kṛṣṇa is addressed as the one who loves cows and *brāhmaṇas*; these are the two most beloved categories of living beings. *Brāhmaṇas* are naturally Vaiṣṇavas, but Kṛṣṇa grazes 900,000 cows every day—this is how many cows Nanda Mahārāja has in his barn. But it is not described anywhere that he grazes them; he goes out to pasture with 900,000 cows. Therefore, it seems Kṛṣṇa likes cows more than *brāhmaṇas*. One day, Kṛṣṇa approached His mother and said, "Mom, I want to graze a calf just like my dad grazes cows." He himself was no bigger than a calf in height, and He wanted to graze all the calves. Mother Yaśodā began to worry when Kṛṣṇa even went out to play with His cowherd friends. She was worried that He would step on a thorn or a sharp stone and hurt His foot, let alone go and graze. But Kṛṣṇa came every day, pulled Mother, and said, "Maiyā, Maiyā, I want to graze." Finally, she agreed and said, "Okay, tomorrow you can graze a few calves, but not cows." 

The news spread throughout Vraja that Kṛṣṇa was leaving to graze the next day. Since everyone in Vṛndāvana is in Kṛṣṇa consciousness, everyone wanted to know what Kṛṣṇa does every second of the day. All the inhabitants of Vraja gathered at the foot of the mountain to see Kṛṣṇa come out of the house. Since this was such a significant event, and since Kṛṣṇa was the son of the King of Vraja, Nanda Mahārāja, Mother dressed Him like a prince: very beautiful, multi-colored silk clothes with a large, beautiful turban, and she decorated Him with different jewelry, ornaments, feathers, and so on. When she finished, Kṛṣṇa looked at Himself in the mirror and said, "Maiyā, usually cowherd boys don't look like me." And Mother Yaśodā said, "Dear son, this is a big holiday today. You will start grazing, therefore you must look accordingly." And of course, the most inspired residents of Nanda-grām were the cowherd boys.

He was accompanied by other cowherd boys who, upon tasting special mercy, were very inspired; their relationship with Kṛṣṇa is friendship. In the palace of Mahārāja, there were at least a significant number of cowherd boys waiting for Kṛṣṇa's exit. When Mother Yaśodā brought Kṛṣṇa into the hall where all the cowherd boys were waiting, they all started laughing loudly, seeing that Kṛṣṇa was dressed like a prince, because they had never seen Him like that before. Subala said, "Look at Him, how He looks! Ha-ha-ha!" All the other cowherd boys laughed, holding their stomachs; some were rolling on the floor laughing, some even had tears flowing from their eyes from laughter. They said, "Wow, He dressed up like a prince to herd the cows, and He doesn't even have a staff to drive the cows or a horn!"

The *Bhāgavatam* describes that, although Kṛṣṇa is traditionally blue like a sapphire, He blushed with shame and looked like a ruby. He became ruby-colored, and to the indignation of His mother, all the cowherd boys pounced on Kṛṣṇa and tore off all these prince's clothes from Him. They gave Him simple shepherd's clothes. Subala gave Him a staff for the cows and a buffalo horn and said, "Now you're a real cowherd boy! Let's go!" And they ran to the exit of the house. They ran past the gopīs who were serving. One gopī said, "Kṛṣṇa, don't forget your flute!" The *Bhāgavatam* describes that this is the Lord at His best.

They came here because this is where the *śālā* was with all the cows and calves. He decided to take revenge on His friends for making fun of Him. They stopped here, and Kṛṣṇa decided to play a joke on them. The lake is small. There used to be a lot of trees and bushes here. Now they have cleared this area; you can easily see this lake. Kṛṣṇa is the Lord of all mystical powers, and as you can see, the trees here are covered with abundant foliage. Kṛṣṇa, with His mystical ability, covered the entire surface of the lake with a thick layer of leaves and flowers, and this surface of the lake looked like earth. 

On the other side of the lake, He made jasmines grow, and Kṛṣṇa said to the shepherd boys, "Let's stop here and rest a little before we start grazing the calves." They sat down, and Kṛṣṇa pointed to the flowers: "There are beautiful flowers there; maybe someone wants to pick and bring them here?" All the shepherd boys were very inspired. Kṛṣṇa's desire was law for them because they were all devotees of Kṛṣṇa. They happily rushed to fulfill His order—there, under the leaves of water—because Kṛṣṇa did everything so mystically that it seemed to them that this was the earth. They all ran and fell into this lake! Kṛṣṇa laughed louder and longer than all the cowherd boys when they laughed at Him. His clothes were taken away, and after He had eaten, He sat down with a contented smile. *Ṭhākura* describes that the radiance of the Lord's smile spread golden rays throughout Vṛndāvana.

There are many important points associated with this place. For example, this is the first meeting place for Kṛṣṇa in the morning. We know that Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī received the blessing of the Supreme Personality of Godhead: it is a wonderful moment that the food that She cooks will have an amazing taste and will free everyone who tries Her dish from old age, illness, and even death. When Mother Yaśodā found out about Vṛndāvana, she wanted Her to cook for Kṛṣṇa, and so she turned to her mother, Kīrtidā Sundarī, and asked Rādhārāṇī to come to their house and cook breakfast for Kṛṣṇa. Of course, Kṛṣṇa was in complete ecstasy but did not show His face. 

Kṛṣṇa comes here to check on the cows and calves before breakfast. Paurnamāsī arranges it so that Rādhārāṇī goes with Her girlfriends to Nanda-grām at this very moment when Kṛṣṇa comes here. Kṛṣṇa is alone without the cowherd boys, and although Rādhārāṇī is surrounded by gopīs, somehow everything is arranged so that they do not see anything at this moment, and Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa communicate. Later in the day, they meet again at Rādhā-kuṇḍa, and in the evening, they meet again at Gupta-kuṇḍa. This is where Ter-kadamba is; that is very confidential information. 

Rādhārāṇī goes to prepare; Kṛṣṇa is here alone. He remembers His friends; He begins to miss His friends. Kṛṣṇa climbs a tall Kadamba tree—and it is called *ter* because *ter* is translated as a "call"—and He begins to call His cowherd friends from the height of this tree. They come running here. The cowherd boys come running for Kṛṣṇa and take Him to breakfast.

After breakfast, they all go together to graze the calves. They all have bundles with lunch with them, and when they gather here, they hang their bundles on the trees. It is said that under the weight of these bundles, the trees bend. Therefore, the *ācāryas* explain that in Vṛndāvana, many trees are bent because these bundles of food hung on them. When Kṛṣṇa and the cowherd boys graze the calves, at this time the gopīs feel separation from Kṛṣṇa. Mother Yaśodā goes out to the terrace of the Nanda palace and looks into the distance to make sure that Kṛṣṇa is doing well. Sometimes when Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma go to graze the calves, Mother Yaśodā tries to accompany them, but naturally, the cowherd boys do not want her to be with them because they want to play; in her presence, they cannot do this. Balarāma gives this role; he says to Mother Yaśodā, "You don’t have to go with us; it’s better you stay at home and prepare dinner for Kṛṣṇa." 

They graze the calves until noon and then they return here under the trees on which these heavy bundles of food hang and decide to have lunch. It suits them so that right before lunch, at 11:45, a demon is sent by Kaṃsa every day at the same time. This demon, of course, looks very scary; he is determined to kill Kṛṣṇa. Kṛṣṇa fights with him, kills him, and all the cowherd boys are very happy. Since they all take part in the battle with the demon, they feel hungry, and they dine with a great appetite and discuss how Kṛṣṇa killed this demon. The *Veda* describes how they dine—all the cowherd boys round on the ground—and all the contents of their bundles they put there: *sandeśa*, *gulis*, *samosas*, *laḍḍus*. They offer it all to Kṛṣṇa. They wait until Kṛṣṇa eats, and Kṛṣṇa is still not satisfied; He always shouts, *"Aur! Aur! Neir!"* ("More! More!"). Just like it happened on Govardhana. 

Every day, one of the cowherd boys looks at the hungry face of Kṛṣṇa and says, "Kṛṣṇa, we know that you will not eat butter from the house of some gopī, but these gopī houses are very far from Ter-kadamba. Where will we get you all these dairy products?" When Kṛṣṇa hears the word "gopī," He feels acute separation from them and He begins to cry. The cowherd boys think, "Well, He is crying from hunger." Kṛṣṇa feels such separation from the gopīs that at the mere mention of the gopīs, He ascends the Kadamba tree and begins to play, and the sounds of Kṛṣṇa's flute spread throughout Vraja. They reach the hearts of the gopīs, and they begin to think of Kṛṣṇa. Kṛṣṇa begins to scream, "I have friends here! All the gopīs are hungry, run to Ter-kadamba immediately and bring milk, yogurt, and butter!" They come running urgently, and they bring with them all possible dairy products. The cowherd boys are very happy that they will have a big feast, and they think Kṛṣṇa is so concerned about them that they eat well. The real reason is that Kṛṣṇa feels separation from the gopīs; He wants to see them. The gopīs are happy, Kṛṣṇa is happy, and the cowherd boys are happy. No matter what Kṛṣṇa does, all the inhabitants of Vṛndāvana feel completely satisfied. 

This is the second call of Kṛṣṇa. *Ter* is translated as a "call." If you remember, at the beginning I mentioned that Kṛṣṇa calls the inhabitants of Vṛndāvana from these trees three times. The first time He calls the cowherd boys, the second time the gopīs, and there will be a third time. 

And who does He call the third time from this tree? Evening comes and it is time to go home. They wander over a large territory, and in the evening, they all need to be collected in order to take them home. They also have to drink milk from their mothers. At this moment, Kṛṣṇa needs to collect everyone—the calves and the 900,000 cows. Can you imagine how many calves there were? In order to collect everyone, Kṛṣṇa climbs a high Kadamba tree, and with the help of His legs, Kṛṣṇa calls the names of all the cows and calves, collecting them. He has a special counter to count all these cows and calves—more than a million beads on this counter. He wants to see them all gathered. This is Vraja, always expanding, always more and more. 

In the ordinary material world, in the late afternoon, everyone gets tired and energy decreases, but in the spiritual world of Vraja, everything is the opposite. In the evening, joy only intensifies, because while Kṛṣṇa is grazing the calves, all the other residents of Vṛndāvana, the older residents, feel great separation from Kṛṣṇa. When Kṛṣṇa returns, the big festival begins, meeting with Kṛṣṇa. They are just waiting for Him to appear; when they see His peacock feather, a festival begins. This is the meaning that in Vṛndāvana, all are walking and dancing. 

This is a place where festivals are held every day. So there is no point for us to leave here at all because we are already at home in the spiritual world. Of course, the only reason why we have to leave here is to share Kṛṣṇa consciousness with the people around us in order to preach. Rūpa Gosvāmī could stay here forever. Since this is such a special place, one of the places where Rūpa Gosvāmī spent his *bhajana*. All these trees are desire trees—conscious, personal, great souls—and He would write about feelings of separation; the trees would cry in the form of all their leaves falling, the ground becoming bare. When He wrote about the union of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, the trees would bloom with fresh new leaves and flowers. Rūpa Gosvāmī sat under these trees, and he wrote about the pastimes of Kṛṣṇa. When he described separation, because all these trees are desire trees—pure devotees aware of everything that happens—they felt such separation that all the leaves fell from them. When I wrote to you about a meeting with Kṛṣṇa, these trees were covered with greenery and flowers. 

One fine day when Rūpa Gosvāmī was here, he suddenly remembered, "Today is a special day—the appearance day of my senior godbrother, Sanātana Gosvāmī—and I have not prepared anything for him." Rūpa Gosvāmī lived here, and Sanātana Gosvāmī lived not so far from here on Pāvana-sarovara. He sat here and said that, in fact, he cannot even offer anything to his older brother since he is just a *bābā* whose entire property consists of beads and a jug of water. He began to cry towards the forest. He saw that one Vraja-gopī was approaching him, and this Vraja-gopī had a jug in her hands. She was an amazingly beautiful girl with all the wonderful qualities, and she said, "You know, Bābā, there was a wedding recently in Nanda-grām, and I brought sweet rice especially for you." Here, sweet rice appeared. Rūpa Gosvāmī took this sweet rice and went to Nanda-grām and gave this sweet rice as a gift to Sanātana Gosvāmī. When they tasted this sweet rice, all eight signs of ecstasy appeared in their bodies simply because they tasted this sweet rice. Tears of love flowed from their eyes, the hair on their bodies stood up, their voices were interrupted—they could not talk, they felt the madness of love—and they fell unconscious on the ground. When Sanātana Gosvāmī came to himself, he asked, "Where did you get this sweet rice?" When Rūpa Gosvāmī began to describe this gopī, it became clear that she had all 25 qualities. When they understood this, they again fell unconscious. 

A lot is happening in transcendental pastimes. You can see that here in the temple is the main altar and the main deity whom they worship. Rūpa Gosvāmī wrote his work here, *Upadeśāmṛta* (The Nectar of Instruction), which contains the whole essence of the instructions in Kṛṣṇa consciousness. When the British government decided to analyze what the Kṛṣṇa consciousness movement is, they studied all our books and came to the conclusion that it best describes the whole process of Kṛṣṇa consciousness. This work was written here. We can remember all of this in order to fill our consciousness with this. We can spend some time rolling in the dust of Ter-kadamba, praying to Sanātana Gosvāmī so that they will grant us a drop of their mercy, so that one day we will not just hear about these things but realize this. So that one fine day, by the mercy of your spiritual masters, you will enter into these pastimes. If you have the opportunity, you will come here, because this is a wonderful place for chanting *japa* and meditation.

### Summary of Ter-kadamba Significance
*   **The Three Calls:** 
    1.  Calling the cowherd boys.
    2.  Calling the gopīs for lunch/milk.
    3.  Calling the herd home in the evening.
*   **Historical Significance:** Rūpa Gosvāmī performed his *bhajana* here and wrote the *Upadeśāmṛta*.
*   **The Legend:** The story of the sweet rice (kheer) brought by a gopī to Rūpa Gosvāmī as an offering for Sanātana Gosvāmī.
*   **Atmosphere:** A place of transcendental festival where every step is a dance and every word is a song.""
