""### The Glories of the Mṛdaṅga

Hi Kṛṣṇa, everyone, and welcome back once again to our ongoing series on the glories of our most beloved *śrī kṛṣṇa nāma*. All glories to Śrīla Prabhupāda. We are continuing with our miniseries on "Stimulation for Ecstatic Love," and this will be part 85. This lecture will be the start of several classes on musical instruments used in devotional service, which can stimulate our divine love for Kṛṣṇa. So, today we will begin with the mṛdaṅga. 

In the *Vāstu-śāstra*, it is said: *vādya-hīnaṁ gītaṁ na śobhate*. This means: "Without instrumental music, there is no beauty in song and rhyme." The great Vaiṣṇava ācārya, Śrīla Narahari Cakravartī Ṭhākura, describes in his *Bhakti-ratnākara*, Chapter 5, texts 319 to 333, that musical instruments are of four kinds: 
*   *Tata*: Stringed instruments.
*   *Anaddha*: Drums.
*   *Suṣira*: Wind instruments.
*   *Ghana*: Percussion instruments.

Regarding *anaddha* (drums), which is the theme today, there are different types of drums in Vedic culture used in kīrtana to glorify the Lord. There is the *pakhāvaja*, an elongated wooden drum with a small side and a bigger side, played with both hands. There is the *ḍholaka*, which is similar to the *pakhāvaja* although smaller in size, making a louder sound. Then there is the *tablā*; devotees are familiar with the *tablā*, which is actually two drums: one is called *ḍayyā*, which is smaller and played with the right hand, and the other is called *bāyā*, which is bigger in size and is played with the left hand. Another type of drum is the *nagara*, a fairly large drum played with two big wooden sticks.

Then comes our favorite as Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas: the mṛdaṅga. As we know, the mṛdaṅga is made out of terracotta clay and the skin of cows who have died a natural death. I was reading that in Jagannātha Purī, the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava *bhajana* singers say that the mṛdaṅga’s larger side is considered Lord Nityānanda Prabhu and the smaller side is considered Gadādhara Prabhu. They also say the beats of the mṛdaṅga drum manifested from the lotus mouth of Lord Caitanya Mahāprabhu. 

Regarding a number of drums, the *Śabda-darpaṇa* says: *anaddhe mṛdaṅgaḥ śreṣṭhaḥ*. It means the *mṛdaṅgala*—which is really just another name for the mṛdaṅga—is the best of drums. It goes on: "The sound of the mṛdaṅga is most auspicious and charming; it enhances the beauty of all other instruments." 

I found in another śāstra, the *Śrī Saṅgīta-dāmodara*, it is said the *mṛdaṅgala* is the best of all musical instruments. When it is made of clay, the *mṛdaṅgala* is called the mṛdaṅga. It goes on: "Lord Brahmā is always situated in the mṛdaṅga's middle part. The demigods who reside in Brahmā's planet are also situated there. Because all the demigods reside in it, the mṛdaṅga is all auspicious." 

The *Saṅgīta-dāmodara* concludes with a description of a player: A mṛdaṅga player should be sober, well-versed in instrumental techniques, elegant, and expert in the beats (*tālas*) given in the sequence of syllables (*bolas*). He should be able to play all the sounds (*gamakas*), be expert in accompanying dance performances and songs, be cheerful, easily proficient, and quick-fingered. Sometimes you see a mṛdaṅga player; he has to be quick-fingered—a nice definition of a mṛdaṅga player.

I was actually able to find the praṇāma mantra of the mṛdaṅga: 
*mṛdaṅgaṁ brahma-rūpāya lāvaṇya-rasa-mādhurī | sahasra-guṇa-saṁyuktaṁ praṇamāmi punaḥ punaḥ ||*

"The mṛdaṅga is the very form of transcendental gracefulness and sweet enchantment. It has a thousand different qualities. I bow down again and again to the mṛdaṅga drum." Haribol.

Now, the ancient Vedic text *Bhāva-tāraṇī-bindu-tāraṇī* says: "Some of the ingredients of *saṅkīrtana*—which means, of course, glorification of the Lord by singing, dancing, and playing musical instruments—are the *khol* or mṛdaṅga, the *karatālas* (hand symbols), and the *śṛṅga* or *viṣāṇa* (the horn)." You may have seen in old paintings of the Pañca-tattva that when Mahāprabhu and the devotees are dancing, sometimes you see these long horns. We don't have them in our movement today, but 500 years ago they had these long horns called *śṛṅga* or *viṣāṇa*. They are part of the assembly. Then *karatālī* means clapping of hands; *nṛtya* is dancing; and finally, *luṭana* means literally rolling on the ground.

It goes on to say that in this assembly which makes up *saṅkīrtana*, the mṛdaṅga speaks. When it is played, it says: *tik-taṁ tik-taṁ dig-dhaṁ dig-dhaṁ dhiṁ-dhiṁ dig-dhaṁ*. Pundits of ancient times have ascertained that this message the mṛdaṅga is conveying is as follows: "To those people who have no devotion for the lotus feet of Śrīmat-īśvara Kṛṣṇa, to those whose tongues are not lovingly attached to speaking of the transcendental qualities of the beloved of the cowherd girls, to those whose ears are not fond of hearing the *kathā* of the charming qualities of Śrī Kṛṣṇa's divine pastimes—I say, 'mṛdaṅga'—I say, 'fie unto them, fie unto them, fie unto them.'" Thus speaks the mṛdaṅga during kīrtana.

Regarding *karatālas* or hand symbols, the *Bindu-tāraṇī* says: "During *saṅkīrtana*, the *karatālas* ring out their famous 1-2-3 rhythm, which conveys the following three messages to the devotees: 'I will be victorious over death; I will be victorious over Yamarāja; and I will very happily be victorious over all his servants.' By hearing this sound of the *karatāla* from afar, these three—death, Yamarāja, and his servants—certainly do not come anywhere near the performers of kīrtana."

Regarding *viṣāṇa* or the horn, *Bindu-tāraṇī* says: "When devotion arises during *nāma-saṅkīrtana*, it acts like a fire to burn to ashes the impurities within the mind. Therefore, the auspicious horn blasts away this residue by the sound issuing forth from the path of its tube." 

As for *karatālī* (clapping of hands), the *Bhāva-sindhu-tāraṇī* says: "Alas, residing in the tree of the body are certainly many birds of sin, but they are undoubtedly chased away by the clapping of the hands during kīrtana." 

And as for *nṛtya* or *luṭana* (dancing, which also includes rolling on the ground), the *Bhāva-sindhu-tāraṇī* says: "Alas, I have passed so many days engaged in fruitive activities that are bound by karma, according to my own intelligence. By that, I have fallen far away from the Father of the Universe, and I have not been able to put an end to my misery. Therefore, today I shall behave as a child and dance with abandon as I raise my arms aloft. Then I will cry and roll about in the dust. Just see, will He not be merciful and take me onto His own lap?" 

Small children are prone to beg attention from their fathers. A child puts his arms up in the air, dances around, and calls out to the father, who responds by taking the child into his lap and shows him love. But if the father does not respond right away, the child cries and rolls on the ground. Seeing this is intolerable for the kind father, who picks the child up. In this mood, dancing and rolling on the ground are an integral part of the performance of *saṅkīrtana* and are meant to petition the favor of the all-merciful Lord, the Supreme Father. Therefore, there is no doubt that by these practices, one attains special benefit. Of course, we dance, but we don't roll on the ground; that's not part of our *saṅkīrtana*. But I have seen in India many times, when people come for kīrtana before the Deity, they pay obeisances and they roll a little bit to the left and a little bit to the right, very respectfully, just trying to get the Deity's attention. So we can do that, but we don't roll over on the ground. Prabhupāda didn't encourage us, but it appears in the old days devotees were doing that to get the attention of the Supreme Father. Hare Kṛṣṇa.

We can see why in the *nāma-saṅkīrtana* movement, this one instrument, the mṛdaṅga, is played and will play for the next 10,000 years of the golden age of Caitanya Mahāprabhu's movement. It will play such an essential role. Lord Caitanya is quoted in *Caitanya-caritāmṛta* (Antya 3.19) as follows: *yuga-dharma nāma-saṅkīrtana-praveśana / nāciyā-nāciyā gāya kṛṣṇa-saṅkīrtana*. "I shall personally inaugurate the religion of the age, *nāma-saṅkīrtana*, the congregational chanting of the holy name. I shall make the world dance in ecstasy, realizing the four mellows of loving devotional service." 

So Mahāprabhu came to introduce the *saṅkīrtana* movement. And just how did He inaugurate this singing and dancing? *Caitanya-caritāmṛta*, Ādi-līlā 17.135 says: "During Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu's time, one party was composed of 21 men: with four people playing mṛdaṅgas, one leading the chanting, and 16 others striking *karatālas* while responding to the leading chanter. He introduced the *yuga-dharma*, which is chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa in this fashion." This is the authorized process; this is how the *saṅkīrtana* was begun. All glories to Lord Caitanya Mahāprabhu.

The following mantra I found has come down through our Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sampradāya and is chanted with love by many kīrtaniyas in Māyāpura and Oḍiśā: 

*jagannātha-miśra-suta-nāmā-kīrtanāya namaḥ*
*lāvaṇya-rasa-mādhurī-mṛdaṅgāya namaḥ*
*sahasra-guṇa-saṁyukta-mṛdaṅgāya namaḥ*
*balarāma-mṛdaṅga-rūpāya namaḥ*

A beautiful translation: "I offer my respectful obeisances unto the son of Jagannātha Miśra, Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu. I offer my obeisances to the mṛdaṅga from which sweet and nectarean sounds emanate. I offer my obeisances again and again unto the mṛdaṅga who has thousands of good qualities, and I offer my obeisances unto Lord Balarāma, who assumes the form of the mṛdaṅga to serve Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu."

Because this amazing drum, the mṛdaṅga, is also so prominent and so important in the modern-day pastimes of Lord Caitanya's movement, including our International Society for Kṛṣṇa Consciousness, Prabhupāda wrote to his disciple, my godbrother Bahulāśva, on November 10th, 1975: 

> "My dear Bahulāśva, please accept my blessings. I am in due receipt of your letter dated September 23rd, and I have noted the contents. I am glad to note that you are taking out the traveling party, traveling *saṅkīrtana*. I hope you have good success. Just do everything very soberly. Regarding the instruments, stringed instruments are Vedic, but the real Vedic instrument is mṛdaṅga and *karatāla*."

I was so happy to read that. We can also appreciate how important the mṛdaṅga drum was to our beloved Śrīla Prabhupāda by his arrival lecture on March 3rd, 1975, in Dallas, Texas. In that lecture, he was speaking affectionately about his father, Gour Mohan De, when he said: 

> "Whenever some saintly person would come, my father would ask him, 'Please bless my son that he can become a servant of Rādhārāṇī.' That was his prayer; he never prayed for anything. And he gave me education—how to play the mṛdaṅga. My mother was against—there were two teachers: one for teaching me A-B-C-D, and one for teaching the mṛdaṅga. So the one teacher was waiting and the other teacher was teaching me how to play the mṛdaṅga. So my mother would be angry, 'What is this nonsense you are teaching mṛdaṅga? What will he do with this mṛdaṅga?' Little did she know! But perhaps my father wanted that I should be a great mṛdaṅga player in the future." 

Therefore, I am very much indebted to my father, and I have dedicated my book, *Kṛṣṇa* book, to him. He wanted me to be a preacher of *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* and a player of mṛdaṅga, and to become a servant of Rādhārāṇī. Every parent should think like that; otherwise, one should not become a father and mother. That is the injunction of śāstra. All glories to Śrīla Prabhupāda.

Actually, we can consider that it was Prabhupāda’s plan to utilize the mṛdaṅga in his mission here in the West from the very beginning. I remember reading that as early as possible, he ordered a drum to come from India. In some of the earlier videos, Prabhupāda was just playing the bongo—the two-Western drum—in Tompkins Square Park. In San Francisco, he wanted a mṛdaṅga. That's very clear. On January 25th, 1968, he wrote to his disciple, Hamsadūta dāsa: 

> "Another proposal is I want to form a *saṅkīrtana* party in which two members will play mṛdaṅga, eight will play the symbols, two will play on tambura, and one harmonium; besides that, there will be the leader of the party. This party will be so trained that exhibitions of our chanting and dancing, along with the distribution of prasādam, will be performed on a stage, and for this performance we will sell tickets to the public. It will be known as a spiritual movement, including the mṛdaṅga."

Furthermore, on January 30th, 1969, he wrote to my beloved godbrother Brahmananda Prabhu: 

> "My Guru Mahārāja indicated that the mṛdaṅga and the press are the mediums of our missionary propaganda activities, and we shall follow his path in the same way. We must have large quantities of mṛdaṅgas for vibrating in different parts of the world, and we must distribute our literature also."

In the early days of the movement, when devotees would visit India for festivals, they would bring back the traditional original clay mṛdaṅgas, but there simply weren't enough for the number of kīrtana parties chanting in America and around the world. There was the Hare Kṛṣṇa explosion, but unfortunately, being made of clay, they would often break. So eventually, wonderful devotees in Los Angeles started a small business making fiberglass mṛdaṅgas that were strong and unbreakable. They called them, very appropriately, "Balarāma mṛdaṅgas." They are famous now. 

And Śrīla Prabhupāda was greatly encouraged by this. On November 9th, 1975, he wrote a letter to Badarī-nārāyaṇa dāsa, one of the original musicians in ISKCON. Prabhupāda wrote to him regarding the mṛdaṅga making: "This is a very, very important item. It will open up a great scope of our activities, so it should be done very carefully. I want to see large production." 

Then, at the next Gaura-pūrṇimā festival in Māyāpura, when some devotees came from Los Angeles, they came with several of the new brand-new Balarāma mṛdaṅgas. They were colorful and lightweight, and everyone was excited—a new drum for ISKCON. While entering Śrīla Prabhupāda's room one evening, Prabhupāda was giving darśana. These devotees from Los Angeles carefully placed one of these newly made Balarāma mṛdaṅgas on Prabhupāda's desk where he was sitting. Everyone was holding their breath, waiting to see if Śrīla Prabhupāda liked it. Placing it on his lap, Śrīla Prabhupāda very deftly and expertly played several beats, and then, sitting back, he said, very matter-of-factly, "It's dead. It is dead." The sound was dead. Mṛdaṅga players are very particular; you see, before the kīrtanas, they put a little water on the drum and move it, getting the tone of the drum properly. This one was dead. So the devotees were devastated. But back in the USA after the festival, these devotees made big improvements on the drum, and later Śrīla Prabhupāda expressed his pleasure with the drums. 

On September 26th, 1976, Prabhupāda wrote to Kandarpa dāsa: "My dear Kandarpa, please accept my blessings. I am in due receipt of your letter and have noted the contents." Then Prabhupāda writes: "If you make 2,500 mṛdaṅgas per year, that will be very good. Then distribute them to the whole of the American people." I'm quoting verbatim here: "If you make 2,500 mṛdaṅgas per year, that is very good; then distribute them to the whole of the American people." Prabhupāda thought big. Hoping this meets you in good health, your ever well-wisher, A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami.

The rest is history. Now the whole world is resounding with the sound of the sacred mṛdaṅga drum, giving great pleasure to the devotees of the Lord and purifying the hearts of anyone who hears it. Devotees love the mṛdaṅga; it represents the *saṅkīrtana* movement. In fact, Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, the pioneer of the Kṛṣṇa conscious movement in the modern era, wrote in his song *Bhajana-aṅga-anubhaba*:

*mṛdaṅga-vādana śuni’ hṛdaya-kāmpana*
*gora-bāṇī śuni’ āra nā rati*
*ānanda-hṛdaye rāya, nāciba āra gāya*

"I love this part: When I hear the sound of the mṛdaṅga in my heart, I always desire to join in the kīrtana, and when I hear the bonafide songs describing Caitanya's pastimes, my heart dances in ecstasy." Hare Kṛṣṇa.

I'd like to finish today with a beautiful quote from the *Kṛṣṇa-bhāvanāmṛta*, Chapter 19, by Śrīla Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura. It describes how the mṛdaṅga also plays a very special role in Kṛṣṇa's pastimes in Goloka Vṛndāvana, in the spiritual world. I was thinking: as we play it here, we also hope one day to play it there. So this is what Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura wrote: 

> "Kṛṣṇa cleverly broke out of the gopī circle in the rāsa dance and said, 'Dear girls, now please dance wonderfully one after the other.' Śrīmatī Lalitā agreed and began to dance most beautifully while the mṛdaṅga drum played, *dhi-dhi-dhig-dhaṁ, dhi-dhi-dhig-dhaṁ, kut-to-dhaṁ*. Then Viśākhā and the other sakhīs showed their skill in dancing one after the other. Kṛṣṇa relished this while swinging His head to and fro constantly, along with the sakhīs. He was very satisfied with these performances. Then all the sakhīs gathered and encouraged Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa to dance while they accompanied them with sweet songs; they played on the mṛdaṅgas. These sounds came to Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa like sweet nectar for their ears." 

The sound of the drum is the music to the ears of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa in the spiritual world, and to ours now here in this material world. So thank you, prabhus. It was nectarean; it was so blissful researching and finding all this wonderful information and pastimes of the mṛdaṅga drum. 

We'll be back next week with another musical instrument. At the moment, I'm on the Baltic Sea coast in Poland; we're having our 34th annual Festival of India tour here. I have about 300 devotees with me, and we're relishing going out every day playing the mṛdaṅga and the *karatālas*, dancing and singing, inviting people to our evening festival programs. We have about 40 festivals to do this summer; it's practically a festival every day. But we're practicing, right? Because in the spiritual world, we hear there's a festival every day. So this is *śakti*; we're practicing having a festival every day. But for the people, that's a special nature of our festivals: that we're doing it for the people so they get a little taste of what is this wonderful world of Kṛṣṇa consciousness. So please give us your blessings. See you next Friday. *Jaya Śrī Caitanya!* Hare Kṛṣṇa."
