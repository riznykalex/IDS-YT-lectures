So today we continue with our mini-series on stimulation for ecstatic love. This is Part 164, and we will carry on with our present theme—the glories of Vṛndāvaneśvarī, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. This will actually be the fourth lecture on her transcendental eyes.

But before we begin, I would like to share a very beautiful prayer by a Vrajavāsī poet. His name was Dāmodaradāsa. He was born in 1687 and was a disciple of Hita Harivaṁśa Gosvāmī, who established the Rādhā-vallabha sect in Vṛndāvana.

Actually, he wrote many scriptures, but unfortunately most have been lost in time. This is from his *Dāmodaradāsa Granthāvalī*, which is actually a compilation of at least some of his poems.

So he writes:

“Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, the Queen of Vraja, governs the three worlds and enchants Śrī Kṛṣṇa Himself with the playful glances of her restless eyes, whose brilliance surpasses even the moon.

When she casts those same loving glances upon her devotees, she awakens pure love of Kṛṣṇa within their hearts.

May those exquisitely beautiful eyes of Śrī Rādhā protect you all.”

We thank that poet.

So today we'll share a pastime involving Śrī Rādhā’s eyes, described by another Vrajavāsī poet. His name was Mādhavadāsa.

I did some research. Mādhavadāsa was a mid-16th-century Vaiṣṇava poet from the line of Gadādhara Paṇḍita. This is from his book *Līlā-vilāsa*.

He writes:

One early morning Kṛṣṇa was sitting near a lake in the Vṛndāvana forest when suddenly He saw Viśākhā-sakhī filling water pots nearby.

Approaching her, He said:

“O Viśākhā, your darśana has purified My vision. With the first rays of the sun, I have seen your beautiful form today.”

And Viśākhā replied:

“O Mohana, please do not withdraw Yourself from us. Your presence alone is our life and breath. This day has become blessed simply by seeing You. How much greater is our good fortune when we can remain with You.”

So true.

Kṛṣṇa said:

“O Viśākhā, where are you heading, and why are you filling these silver water pots?”

Viśākhā replied:

“I am filling these water pots to bathe the lotus feet of Śrī Rādhā. Walking barefoot in the forest in search of You, her feet have been hurt. Today we want to massage her feet and wash them with the fresh water of this lake. Mother Paurṇamāsī said that the water of this lake has special medicinal qualities that remove fatigue.”

Kṛṣṇa said:

“Indeed, Mother Paurṇamāsī is right. Just by looking at this water, My burning desire to meet Śrī Rādhā seems fulfilled.”

So Viśākhā said:

“But I cannot take You to her because Jaṭilā has sent her daughter Kuṭilā to guard Śrī Rādhā.”

Kṛṣṇa said:

“I see. Therefore, can you take a parcel of love from Me and give it to her?”

“A parcel of love?” Viśākhā asked. “And what is that?”

Smiling, Kṛṣṇa removed His garland of fresh, white, fragrant jasmine flowers and said:

“My dear Viśākhā, please place this garland around her neck and tell her that I miss her.”

Accepting the garland, Viśākhā walked to Rādhā’s palace at Varṣāṇā, where she very gently placed the garland around Śrī Rādhā’s neck.

It is said that Śrī Rādhā accepted the garland by humbly bowing her head in great respect.

Now Lalitā, who was fanning Śrī Rādhā, said:

“O Priyā, amongst those bound by love, a day is deemed auspicious when, at the break of dawn, a message or a gift arrives from one’s beloved.”

Wow.

So, with a smile, and touching the jasmine garland to her lotus eyes, she said:

“Lalitā, you understand the depth of my heart. Touching these fresh, tender, and fragrant jasmine blossoms to my eyes is like applying cool camphor to eyes burning in separation from the sight of Kṛṣṇa.”

Eyes burning in separation from the sight of Kṛṣṇa.

So then Śrī Rādhā sang a poem:

“My heart desires to meet only Kṛṣṇa. My ears wish only to hear His names. My tongue knows how to glorify only Him. And my eyes, my restless eyes, long only to see Kṛṣṇa. Without the beauty of His vision, these eyes cannot find peace.”

Now, sobbing in tears, the poet writes, Lalitā sympathized with Śrī Rādhā and said:

“O Priyā, do not be anxious. Moments of separation always feel long, yet they carry within them the hope of meeting again. The dream of meeting is no less sweet than the meeting itself.

O Queen of the forests of Vraja, be patient and let your heart dream of your reunion with Kṛṣṇa. Let your heart dream of your reunion with Kṛṣṇa.”

So, smiling at Lalitā, Śrī Rādhā began to smell Kṛṣṇa’s jasmine garland around her neck, when suddenly her body began trembling. Her lips started shivering, her eyes turned angry red, and her face faded.

Seeing this condition, Viśākhā asked:

“O Priyā, what’s happening?”

And Rādhā replied:

“It is another gopī dear to Kṛṣṇa whom He meets when I am not present.”

So Viśākhā said:

“What are you talking about?”

Śrī Rādhā replied:

“O Viśākhā, you have brought me a gift from that clever Kṛṣṇa, but I clearly detect the scent of sandalwood oil blended with aguru upon this garland. And in all of Vraja, it is only Candrāvalī, my rival, who adorns herself with such a fragrance.

Surely Kṛṣṇa embraced her at dawn while wearing this garland. Or perhaps she herself prepared it as an offering to Him.

O Lalitā, please remove this garland from my neck. Its fragrance carries the mark of rivalry and the jealousy that burns in Candrāvalī’s heart.”

Now, hearing the name Candrāvalī, all of Rādhārāṇī’s sakhīs, her faithful girlfriends, closed their ears with their hands, unwilling even to hear the name of Rādhā’s rival.

And Śrī Rādhā continued:

“Here I am, confined and isolated as punishment for meeting Kṛṣṇa, spending my days and nights meditating on Him, while He enjoys the company of my rival. This is not fair.

I swear I will never see Kṛṣṇa again. I swear I will never see Kṛṣṇa again.”

So hearing all that—it’s very cute—all the forest birds, rabbits, deer, and peacocks began lamenting her decision. They began lamenting her decision.

And when this news reached Kṛṣṇa’s ears, He became devastated and immediately started making plans how to meet Śrī Rādhā once again and appease her māna.

But this time, the poet writes, it would be truly difficult. It would be truly difficult.

Now, a few days later, seeing Kṛṣṇa’s condition, Lalitā approached Him and said very firmly:

“Kṛṣṇa, what spell have You cast upon Śrī Rādhā? Without Your darśana, she no longer chews betel leaves. Without You, she has lost interest in singing, dancing, and even laughing. Without Your darśana, she doesn’t even look in the mirror anymore.

Without Your darśana, her eyes are like moonless nights or oceans without currents.

O ornament of her eyes, come with me. I will take You to the shelter of her lotus feet. There, beg her forgiveness and reunite, like the bee meeting the lotus and the raindrop meeting the cātaka bird.”

Like the bee meeting the lotus and the raindrop meeting the cātaka bird.

So Kṛṣṇa said:

“O Lalitā, this dear favorite of yours, Śrī Rādhā, will weigh heavily upon My heart for a whole lifetime. O savior of My life, please lead Me to the shelter of her mercy.”

So Lalitā then brought Kṛṣṇa into Śrī Rādhā’s courtyard, but like a criminal bound with ropes and chains, restrained and forced to kneel.

Presenting Him before Śrī Rādhā, she declared:

“O Queen of Vraja, O supreme arbitrator of justice, please judge His case. He has committed an offense at the feet of love itself.”

I love that.

“At the feet of love itself.

He admits His wrongdoing and now begs for forgiveness. Will Your Highness, who embodies mercy, grant Him pardon?”

Now Śrī Rādhā, without even looking at Kṛṣṇa, replied:

“His offense is not forgivable. His offense is not forgivable.”

Then Kṛṣṇa spoke up, saying:

“O bud of the lotus forest, your tongue speaks one thing, yet your eyes confess another. Your eyes confess another.

Your words carry the harsh language of separation, but your eyes reveal the hidden truth.

O My Queen, let your eyes prevail. Those eyes that mirror the realm of love and devotion.

Let your gaze meet Mine and grant My eyes the shelter they long for.

Your eyes and My eyes.

My eyes are the darkness of night. Your eyes are a flash of lightning.

My eyes are the dryness of the desert. Your eyes are life-giving rain.

My eyes are orphaned and all alone. Your eyes are their only refuge.”

Wow.

Now, hearing this, Kṛṣṇa’s words had touched Śrī Rādhā’s heart.

So hearing this, Rādhā gazed into Kṛṣṇa’s eyes, and as their eyes met, all the gopīs finally celebrated a blissful festival.

What type of festival?

It is called *nayana-utsava*—the festival of meeting eyes. The festival of meeting eyes.

The poet Mādhavadāsa concludes by offering himself, wishing to ward off any evil influence from the beautiful moment when the eyes of the Divine Couple meet.

Hare Kṛṣṇa.

Wow.

Now, moving on—it just gets better and better.

The poet Paramānandadāsa, whom we often quote, of the Rādhā-vallabha sect in Vṛndāvana, describes how one time during a game of hide-and-seek, Rādhārāṇī was assigned the role of finding who had hidden in the grove where they were all playing.

Now, while the sakhīs were running here and there trying to hide, Rādhā sat on a stone, and Kṛṣṇa covered her eyes with His hands.

He said:

“O Rādhe, when I close your eyes, the entire world becomes a dark abode of ignorance. The forest of Vṛndāvana loses its flavor and its hues.

Your beautiful eyes alone bestow color upon Vṛndāvana. Without them, the beauty of the world remains incomplete.

From your glance flow the sweet musical notes of Mother Sarasvatī’s vīṇā.

Alas, O My hands, how cruel you are to have closed Śrī Rādhā’s eyes.”

So Rādhā replied:

“O Gopāla, Your eyes are no different from Mine.”

And after some time, Kṛṣṇa very gently opened her eyes. He took His hands away and allowed her to find all her sakhīs.

She searched through every grove and hidden corner of the forest until she finally found them all.

And having won the game, her sakhīs placed her upon a boat where Rādhā and Mādhava sat facing each other, gazing into each other’s beautiful faces.

Gazing into each other’s beautiful faces.

Lots of nectar.

So, dear devotees, I would like to end the class today with one last poem by the 16th-century poet Kṛṣṇadāsa. He writes so beautifully. A perfect way to end.

“The forest of Vṛndāvana is glorious, where the Divine Couple roam and enjoy blissful days and nights.

The vines and creepers are Their girlfriends, and the trees are Their servants.

The River Yamunā flows along banks of soft sandy forest paths.

Moon rays reflecting on the surface of the Yamunā brighten the entire forest.

In one small grove, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa sit gazing into each other’s eyes as They play the game of love.

Kṛṣṇa glorifies the veil of groves that hide Their playful pastimes from unqualified eyes.”

“Kṛṣṇa glorifies the veil of groves that hide Their playful pastimes from unqualified eyes.”
