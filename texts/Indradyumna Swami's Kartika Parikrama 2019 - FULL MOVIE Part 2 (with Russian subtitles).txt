### Part 2: The Significance of Jaipur

Hari Kṛṣṇa. It is our good fortune that by the grace of our spiritual master, Śrīla Prabhupāda, we have come to a very historic place in our Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava tradition: the city of Jaipur. Of course, Jaipur, with all its charm and all its beauty, is very popular with tourists who come from all over the world. It is sometimes called the "Pink City." There is—or there used to be, partially there still is—a wall around the city, and it has been painted a pinkish or saffron color. But for Vaiṣṇavas, it has special significance. Jaipur—well, its very name indicates it. *Jaipur* means the "city of victory." *Pura* indicates a village or a town, and *jaya* means victory, like we say *Jaya* Rāma or *Jaya* Kṛṣṇa. *Saṅkīrtana*—all glories to the victorious *saṅkīrtana* movement of Śrī Caitanya Mahāprabhu. 

So, "city of victory"—in what sense? Actually, this city was constructed wholly and solely, to begin with, to protect the deities of Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī: Śrī Śrī Rādhā-Govinda, and other Vṛndāvana deities as well, like Rādhā-Gopīnātha and Rādhā-Dāmodara. These deities are also very popular here. But as we have discussed and mentioned before in these videos, there were foreign invasions into this country beginning a thousand years ago. Actually, for hundreds of years, India was conquered by Muslims, the British ruled here, etc. In these invasions, particularly the Muslim invasions, the deities were in danger; they would destroy the temples. So, in one particular invasion in the 1700s, the deities of Vṛndāvana were attacked and temples were destroyed. The devotees received notice of this imminent attack just in time. So, many—for most of the deities of Vṛndāvana—were removed very quickly overnight. 

There are seven principal temples in Vṛndāvana, but in our Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava tradition, we first approach Madana-mohana to become free from desire; we approach Govinda-deva to engage in his service and purify our senses so we can serve him in unalloyed devotion; and then we approach Gopīnātha and we serve him in the mood of the Vraja-vāsīs—pure, unalloyed love. So Madana-mohana, Govinda-deva, and Gopīnātha—the focus was on those deities. They were removed from Vṛndāvana, and they went first to Rādhā-kuṇḍa, where they were served for seven years. A rumor got out that they were there, so the invaders wanted to come and harm them. They were taken to Kāmavana, and from Kāmavana, well, again, they were in peril. So the devotees were thinking, "Where can we take the deities to protect them?" 

One King, one Rajput King here in Rajasthan—this part of India, the Kings were known as being very good *rājarṣis*, devotees of the Lord, and very powerful personalities. One King, Jai Singh—I believe it was the first or second—offered to give protection to these Vṛndāvana deities: to Madana-mohana, to Govinda-deva, and to Gopīnātha. It took some time, months or perhaps a year or more. The deities were removed secretly from Kāmavana down here to this desert-like region, you could say, Rajasthan, and they were received by the Rājā Jai Singh in Amber. Yesterday, our *parikrama* party visited Amber, beautiful palaces, beautiful forts, and the deities were safely brought to that place. 

Over time, different deities arrived at different times, but particularly Govinda-deva is given shelter there. And the King, after some time, he said, "Lord Govinda-deva, you deserve a proper accommodation and protection, so I will build a city for you," and he would be protected in that city from any invasions. So it took some time, but the King built Jaipur, the "city of victory," meaning that the victory was that the deities were protected. Jai Singh constructed the city in such a way that the temple, at that time—and to some degree even today—that temple is right in the center of Jaipur. The King built his residence some maybe a hundred meters from the temple, and he built his residence in such a way that when he woke up in the morning and took his bath and did his various rituals, he would walk out onto the porch and he could look directly into the temple and see Rādhā-Govinda-deva. This is a real King who rules in such a way as to make sure people prosper materially, because that's required so one can be peaceful to worship Kṛṣṇa. But he also was concerned about people's spiritual welfare. In Vedic times, the Kings would tax the people to perform *yajñas* because *yajña* costs a lot of money to perform properly. He would perform *yajñas* for the prosperity of society. Kṛṣṇa says in the *Bhagavad-gītā*: "In the beginning, I sent forth generations of men and demigods and blessed them that by the performance of *yajña*, all desirable things will be achieved." So what is a *yajña* really? You could say *yajña* means *yajña*—it's a way of acknowledging the mercy of the Supreme Personality of Godhead. 

So Kings like Jai Singh, the first or second, and so forth, in their succession—these were *rājarṣis*, and they were Kṛṣṇa-conscious. Jai Singh built the city; he built a big wall around the city, and the deities have been protected ever since. This is some 300-odd years ago, and no one has been able to harm them. And of course, no one can harm Govinda, Kṛṣṇa, but he gives us the service. So many different types of services, and one service is to protect him. As a result, these deities—eventually Gopīnātha came as well; he has a beautiful temple—Madan-mohana came, but eventually went to Karauli. That's another story, another pastime. Karauli is a city some distance away from here. But if you know, the deities of Lokanātha Gosvāmī are here. Little Dāmodara—he had to leave Vṛndāvana as well. Dāmodara is the deity now of Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī, and that deity was carved by Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī. Rūpa Gosvāmī carved the deity and gave it as a present to Jīva Gosvāmī. So very significant, especially this month; this is the month of Dāmodara, this is Kārttika. So our *parikrama* party of almost 350 devotees, we are relishing going to these different temples and taking shelter of these deities. 

And if you think about it, it's quite amazing. These deities, who for the most part were established by Vajranābha, the great-grandson of Kṛṣṇa, 5,000 years ago, and rediscovered by the Six Gosvāmīs of Vṛndāvana 500 years ago—one would naturally think that they should stay in Vṛndāvana, which is a good four-hour drive from here, another state, UP. So the history is, they were moved here for a particular reason. So, why not take them back? You would say they are Vṛndāvana deities and they were worshipped with great devotion by the Vrajavāsīs. So how is it that after several hundred years, they remain here? Well, all you have to do is go to the *maṅgala-ārātrika* in the Rādhā-Govinda temple, the main temple in the city, to see how much devotion the people of this city have for Rādhā-Govinda. 

When I first came here in the early 70s, I was just—I mean, I was just amazed. The first time I walked into that temple long ago, I came a little early and I was ready to see that the doors open. Thousands and thousands and thousands of people, suddenly streaming in, singing so many devotional songs from the heart. They have a group of men in the back, you know, who have been singing, the *kīrtana* tradition of singing for hundreds of years. So much devotion. And you just feel the atmosphere is electric with *bhāva*, with devotion to the deity. And they keep streaming in. Actually, they have built—made the temple even bigger, but even now it can hardly accommodate the thousands of people who come. Literally thousands and thousands of people come every morning before they go to work, before they go to school, before whatever their activities at home are. Everyone—not everyone, but a good amount of the people of Jaipur—they come to see the Lord. 

That's our tradition; it's called *saṁskāra*. *Saṁskāra* means something that makes a deep impression in one's heart. So if you begin your day by seeing the Lord in his deity form, by chanting his holy names, by hearing his glories in the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, and finally tasting the foodstuffs that have been offered to him, loving devotional *prasādam*, it creates a *saṁskāra*, a deep impression in your heart. So when you go out in the world to work—as we have to do—it's difficult to forget Kṛṣṇa. Of course, you're working, but in the back of your mind, in your heart of hearts, you're offering all that work to the Lord because we've had that deep impression in the morning. And perhaps because of the nature of that hard work during the day, being in contact with the material energy, we need a boost. We see the people here in Jaipur, they come back—the same thousands and thousands and thousands, I could say tens of thousands—they come back to see Govinda, Rādhā-Govinda, Rādhā-Gopīnātha, what have you, late in the evening. So impressive to see a whole city Kṛṣṇa-conscious. We want to make our home Kṛṣṇa-conscious; we want to make our congregation Kṛṣṇa-conscious. When you see a whole city Kṛṣṇa-conscious, they're proud. 

Therefore, our spiritual master, Prabhupāda, came here. He brought his disciples here in the 70s to see, for their spiritual awakening, to see the devotion of the people of this city, and to see the deities Rādhā-Govinda. And oh my God, whether it's Tamal Kṛṣṇa Goswami—he took *sannyāsa* from Prabhupāda in front of Rādhā-Govinda-deva—and Prabhupāda met some of the royalty when he was here. So it's very dear to the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas. If you have an opportunity, you should come here just to see the beauty, the architecture. The whole city was constructed hundreds of years ago for the purpose of glorification of the deity. It's just so beautiful, the architecture, the way the town is laid out. You can see here where I'm sitting; it's unique, somewhat maintained by the city authorities. So even for the purpose of tourism, for the beauty of the eye and the atmosphere here, you can come. But mostly to experience the deep devotion that comes from surrendering to these deities that were Radha and Govinda, the personal deities of Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī. Their history is another talk, how Rūpa Gosvāmī found them, actually how they came here—I could give a half-hour class—but they're here. If you have the opportunity, Vṛndāvana is only four hours away. Come and drink the beauty of this town, drink the beauty of the devotion of the people of this town, and see these beautiful, historic deities of Govinda-deva, who is 5,000 years old, and all their glory being worshipped here in the City of Victory, Jaipur. Hare Kṛṣṇa.

### Part 3: Tāpavana and Austerities

Hare Kṛṣṇa. By the mercy of our spiritual master, Śrīla Prabhupāda, we find ourselves in a very wonderful forest here in Vṛndāvana. Previously, all of Vṛndāvana was forest, but through the years, those forests have been cut down for agricultural land. But we come to a forest like this, Tāpavana, which is still very much like it was many years ago, and we get an idea of what Vṛndāvana was like. We visited many forests during our *parikrama* in Vṛndāvana this year, Kārttika *parikrama*. We visited Madhuvana, which was the forest of children, the forest of kids; Kamyavana, the forest of jasmine flowers; Bhandirvana, the forest of enjoyment; Kāmavana, the forest of desire, where all of one's spiritual desires can be fulfilled. And now we are in Tāpavana. *Tāpa* means "austerity," and again, *vana* means "forest." So, this is the forest of austerity, and this is the place where the Vraja-Gopīs came to perform austerities to get the mercy from Kātyāyanī to have Kṛṣṇa as their husband. 

A few weeks ago, we were at Cher Gaṇṭha, and Cher Gaṇṭha is the actual place where the Gopīs were bathing in the... they got there, and Kṛṣṇa climbed the big Kadamaba tree and stole their clothes. So that's a very, very famous place, the pastime where he stole the clothes of the Gopīs. But before that, for a month, the Gopīs came here to Tāpavana and they performed austerities. It's mentioned by Śukadeva Gosvāmī in the Tenth Canto of *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* that one big part of their austerity was that they just ate something similar to *kicari* every day, without salt, without spices. And they would bathe early in the morning in Gopī-kuṇḍa, which was part of Tāpavana—actually the Yamunā River used to run right along here—so they would bathe in Gopī-kuṇḍa, and they would bathe in the early morning. 

Their *vrata* took place during the first month of winter. Kṛṣṇa mentions in *Bhagavad-gītā* that he is of seasons; he is that November/December season. So the *ācāryas* say that because Kṛṣṇa says he is that particular month, the Gopīs decided to do their *vrata* in that month. Everything is connected to Kṛṣṇa. Everything the Gopīs did was connected to Kṛṣṇa for his pleasure. So for one month, they would come here and they would bathe very early in the morning, and they would just eat that, like *kicari*, once a day. And they would take the soft sand of the Yamunā River and they would make a beautiful *mūrti* of Kātyāyanī, and they would worship it with sandalwood pulp and flowers and so many different items, and offer *ārati* like that. So that went on for quite some time. And when they had fulfilled that *vrata* for one month, they went to Cher Gaṇṭha, and there they took their bath. And at that time, Kṛṣṇa appeared—he stole the clothes and went in the tree—and they came out fully surrendered souls, *ātma-nivedanam*, in such a way that Kṛṣṇa accepted them, accepted their desire. 

Like that, it's all very deep. It's all in the *mādhurya-rasa*, the conjugal *rasa*. *Ācāryas* say actually everything, all the *rasas*—all the relationships with Kṛṣṇa, as a friend, or when we have Kṛṣṇa as one's child—all the relationships here in Vṛndāvana facilitate the amorous pastimes of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, the *mādhurya-rasa*, the *kānta-bhāva*. It's all-pervading. It's beyond aspiring devotees like myself, but the mercy of Śrīla Prabhupāda and the Six Gosvāmīs is that we're aware of it. We know who is the part of the team, we know who we are, we know who is Kṛṣṇa, and we know that we have an eternal loving relationship with him. 

And Vṛndāvana is the perfect place for awakening that relationship. We have a long way to go, we're "works in progress." *Ādau śraddhā tataḥ sādhu-saṅgo 'tha bhajana-kriyā...* as the *śāstra* says, real love begins when one is free from material desire. So we have a way to go, but we're very content that we know what is the goal. And we know that by coming here to Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma, visiting these different forests, visiting Tāpavana where the Gopīs performed their *tapasya*, we're getting extra mercy. It's stated that any devotional service done in Vṛndāvana, the result is magnified a thousand times. So we have that song we sing every morning: *Jaya Rādhā-Mādhava Kuñja-bihārī Gopī-jana-vallabha Giri-vara-dhārī*. Prabhupāda said that's the perfect description of Vṛndāvana. But actually, previously, for example in Gauḍīya Maṭha, that wasn't sung because the authorities felt it was too intimate. Yes, but again, it helps us remember the goal, and our goal is to go back home, back to Śrī Vṛndāvana. Of all holy places, Vṛndāvana is the highest, and the deepest love for Kṛṣṇa is exhibited by the Gopīs of Vṛndāvana. 

We worship from a distance. We come in here, we roll in the dust, we take the water of sacred lakes and rivers and we put it on our head, and we pray one day that we can become qualified to serve our spiritual master, Śrīla Prabhupāda, in his relationship with the residents of Vṛndāvana. We're servants of the servants of the servants. Coming to a conclusion now, our Kārttika *parikrama*—we've been here almost one month. We have many memories to take back with us. I pray that these memories don't fade. In the material world, everything deteriorates in time, so memories do fade, but we're all praying that these memories will stay in our heart. And if we can remember just one of them when we leave this mortal frame, then our deliverance is guaranteed. So thank you, Śrīla Prabhupāda, for inviting us here and telling us we can wander and visit all these amazing holy places in the forests of Vṛndāvana. Hare Kṛṣṇa.

### Part 4: The Transcendental Nature of Rādhā-kuṇḍa

*Oṁ namo bhagavate vāsudevāya.* By the causeless mercy of our spiritual master, Founder-Ācārya of the International Society for Krishna Consciousness, Śrīla Prabhupāda, our *parikrama* party for this Kārttika has come to the banks of Rādhā-kuṇḍa and Śyāma-kuṇḍa. I have no qualification for speaking about this sacred place, and no realizations, but I have been asked to give this interview. In *Upadeśāmṛta*, Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī gives a hint about the transcendental nature of this pond, this lake behind me, which is the personal bathing place of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. He says, *prīti-mānam-lilam*. He describes this water as "liquid love," you could say, the *kunda* of love. And again, it's very confidential. This is the bathing tank of Kṛṣṇa's dearest devotee, dearest Gopī, eternal consort, Rādhārāṇī—she who never leaves the association of the Lord. 

So we've come here with a blade of grass between our teeth, rolling in the dust of the sacred place, begging for her mercy that we can serve the divine couple. Rādhārāṇī is in charge of devotional service because she has *mahābhāva*. *Mahābhāva* means the greatest, purest love. Only three personalities have that love: herself, Caitanya Mahāprabhu, and Mādhavendra Purī. It's described in *Nectar of Devotion* that even great saintly persons, even demigods, even our spiritual ancestors, they rarely—sometimes if ever—can come to such a sacred place. So how have we come? We've come on the invitation of Śrīla Prabhupāda, and we are doing here what we do in every sacred place in Vṛndāvana: *śravaṇaṁ kīrtanaṁ viṣṇoḥ smaraṇam*. Specifically here at Rādhā-kuṇḍa, it's the most sacred place in the three worlds. 

One time, one of my god-sisters asked Śrīla Prabhupāda—this is 1974, 1975—if they, meaning my god-brothers and god-sisters, could come here and open a preaching center and do *harināma* around the sacred lakes every day, and distribute books, and distribute *prasādam*, and preach, and make devotees. And interestingly enough, unique occasion, Prabhupāda said no. He said, "Rādhā-kuṇḍa is *pavitra*." Like he said about Vṛndāvana in general, he said that Vṛndāvana is for inspiration. This is the playground of God. Even now, the Lord's pastimes were going on; they're going on at this very moment for those who have the eyes to see. *Premāñjana-cchurita-bhakti-vilocanena*—when all the dirty things are gone from the heart, and that love of God, which rests as a seed in our heart, is awoken by the chanting of the *mahā-mantra* and service to guru and Gaurāṅga, then one can see those pastimes taking place even now. 

So these are the places of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa's most confidential pastimes. Actually, Rādhārāṇī takes her bath and Kṛṣṇa takes his bath in Śyāma-kuṇḍa every day. They meet here at 12 o'clock every day. Lalitā-sakhī, she arranges that pastime. Kṛṣṇa leaves the cowherd boys and the cows, and he comes here to meet his most beloved. Such a sacred place. It's said that one who bathes in Rādhā-kuṇḍa achieves the same love that Rādhā has for Kṛṣṇa-candra, and one who bathes in Śyāma-kuṇḍa attains the same love that Śyāma has for Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī. So these are very deep subject matters. 

We come here, we don't stay very long. All the most qualified, elevated devotees come here to refine their last steps in their pure devotional service. It's a process. We are progressing on the path of Kṛṣṇa consciousness; we are "works in progress." So we understand our position. *Ādau śraddhā tataḥ sādhu-saṅgo 'tha bhajana-kriyā...* as Śrīla Prabhupāda said, real *bhakti* begins when one becomes free from material desire, specifically from lust, because lust means for me, and love means for Kṛṣṇa. So although we are just getting an inkling of that love, we are getting a drop of that love by the mercy of Śrīla Prabhupāda and our *ācāryas*. We're still allowed to come here. Prabhupāda allowed us to bathe here. He didn't want us to bathe in a frivolous way; he wanted us to bathe with great awe and reverence, because at this stage of devotional service, that's how we're approaching the Lord. We dress our Rādhā-Kṛṣṇa deities like Lakṣmī-Nārāyaṇa, that's stated by Prabhupāda. We develop that mood of awe and reverence, and then through the process of Kṛṣṇa consciousness, we become purified. 

But still, we're allowed to come here to this place with the highest love and worship Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, and we pray that we can get a drop of that *prema* and in our lifetimes become pure devotees of the Lord. In our own movement, this is—we often say this is a "preaching movement." This was the mood of Mahāprabhu when he came. Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa came: *Śrī Kṛṣṇa Caitanya Rādhā-Kṛṣṇa*—the one became two, and the personalities of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa again, they became one in the personality of Caitanya Mahāprabhu. He is the *yuga-avatāra*. Although he experienced the highest sentiments of love of God, he was an active preacher, and so were all the *ācāryas* who came after him, and so was our beloved leader, Prabhupāda. A resident of Goloka Vṛndāvana, we're not meant to speculate on his relationship with Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa in the spiritual world, but when all these divine personalities were here on this earth planet, they were in the mood of preaching, delivering the fallen conditioned souls. 

Especially Caitanya Mahāprabhu, he gave the special message that the topmost planet is Goloka Vṛndāvana, the topmost love is the love of the residents of Vṛndāvana for Kṛṣṇa, especially the Gopīs. So we know these things, and we know who we are, we know where we're going, we know what is the goal. But it's a preaching movement, and we're meant to spend every ounce of our energy assisting Śrīla Prabhupāda to establish Kṛṣṇa consciousness, not only here, but in the 14 worlds. At the same time, Prabhupāda wanted his followers to become pure devotees of the Lord. He wanted that we do that by a combination of our *sādhana*, our *bhajana*, and our preaching. 

So it is appropriate that we come here, although these are the topmost pastimes still going on—Rādhārāṇī's bathing tank, Śyāma's bathing tank, their meeting place is Lalitā-kuṇḍa. There are so many transcendental pastimes you can read about in Gosvāmī literature. We're to come here for inspiration for preaching, but to come here for inspiration to become pure, unalloyed devotees of the Lord so that we can exemplify that wonderful verse in the *Śikṣāṣṭaka*: "O Govinda, feeling your separation, I am considering a moment to be like twelve years or more, and tears are flowing from my eyes like torrents of rain." Prabhupāda wanted us to come to that stage because that is the qualification for going back home, back to Godhead. The ticket is pure, unadulterated love for the Lord.

### Part 5: Kāmavana

My Kṛṣṇa, during this auspicious month of Kārttika, our Kārttika *parikrama* has come to Kāmavana, one of the 12 main forests in Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma. Actually, the scriptures say that of all the 12 forests of Vṛndāvana, Kāmavana is actually the largest. There is a perimeter of 20 kilometers, and from time immemorial it's been a holy place. We could go on for hours about all the transcendental activities and pastimes that have taken place here. Of course, this is a place where Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa had many of their transcendental pastimes. Devotees come here because Kāmavana is that forest which fulfills all of one's spiritual desires. *Kāma*—*kāma* can be translated as lust, but it can also be taken as transcendental love. Just like Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī says, "Young boys are attracted to young girls and young girls are attracted to young boys with the same intensity, but in the transcendental way, may I be attracted to your lotus feet, My dear Lord." So lust is supposed to turn into transcendental love. So then we say Kāmavana—it means that forest where one can come and fulfill all of one's spiritual desires. 

At this point in our lives as aspiring devotees, we're still conquering our material desires, but by the mercy of Śrīla Prabhupāda and our *ācāryas*, we know what these spiritual desires are deep in our heart. First of all, to become a good disciple of our spiritual master, to serve the mission of Caitanya Mahāprabhu, and one day to awaken that *prema*, that love of God that is deep within our hearts—that love for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. So devotees come here and in a variety of ways, they pray to fulfill those spiritual desires that we hear about in *śāstra*, from *Guru-śāstra-sādhu*. 

It's a very nice place to come because this is a very historical place for Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas, particularly because when there were foreign invasions in India—well, for a millennium, actually, but specifically 500 years ago—the deities in Vṛndāvana were in peril. So the Gosvāmīs and their followers removed those deities during one particular attack, a very vicious attack where the invaders wanted to break the temples and destroy the deities. The beloved deities of the *ācāryas*, specifically Govinda-deva, Madana-mohana, and Gopīnātha, they were brought here to Kāmavana. Actually, first they went to Rādhā-kuṇḍa and they stayed seven years hidden there, and after some time, they came here to Kāmavana. 

Along with Govinda-deva came Vṛndā Devī. As we know, Vṛndā Devī is one of the five personalities who grants entrance into and residence in Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma. She is very important to all the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas. She also is an assistant at Paurnamasi—Yoga-māyā—she organizes all the pastimes of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa in great detail. Vṛndā Devī helps her, and we know that we pray to Vṛndā Devī regularly, or daily, in her form of Tulasī-devī in this material world to become an eternal servant of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. So that original deity, you could say the original deity of Vṛndāvana, was established by Vajranābha, the great-great-grandson of Kṛṣṇa. When Kṛṣṇa left and devotees were feeling separation, he installed a number of deities of Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma, different deities around Vṛndāvana, and he established the deity of Vṛndā Devī. Again, the invasions came, so the deity was placed in a *kuṇḍa*, in Pāvana-kuṇḍa, and 500 years ago, Rūpa Gosvāmī, when he was in Vṛndāvana, he found Vṛndā Devī. And again, the invaders came, so she was moved out to Kāmavana. 

At some point, Govinda-deva, Madana-mohana, and Gopīnātha—they were to be taken to Jaipur for further protection. The city of Jaipur was actually built for Govinda-deva and the other deities as well. But when the devotees tried to move Vṛndā Devī from Kāmavana, she wouldn't go. She wouldn't cross the frontier. She so much wanted to stay here. As it's said in *Bhakti-ratnākara*, it quotes her to always increase the devotees' faith in Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma: "I will never leave," she said. "Kṛṣṇa may come, Kṛṣṇa may go, but I will always remain here for the devotees to help them increase their love and devotion for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa." So that's a high point in visiting Kāmavana, among many other pastimes as well. This was where Yudhiṣṭhira and his brothers came, and the brothers drank the poisonous water from one lake and... Dharmarāja, Yamarāja, he asked Yudhiṣṭhira to answer a number of questions; if he answered them correctly, his brothers would be revived. He answered them correctly, and the brothers were revived. That pastime, Dharma-līlā, also happened here. Again, we could go on and on, but yes, we come here to fulfill our spiritual desires and to take shelter, particularly of Vṛndā Devī. We pray that by now, we're on our way out, that our hearts have been somewhat purified, our material desires have been kept at bay, and our spiritual desires are more important to us, and we will try to fulfill them and fulfill the desires of Śrīla Prabhupāda and our previous *ācāryas* to share the glories of Kāmavana all over the world.""
