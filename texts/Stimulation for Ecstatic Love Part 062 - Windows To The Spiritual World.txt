# Stimulation for Ecstatic Love Part 062 - Windows To The Spiritual World

Hare Kṛṣṇa, dear devotees, welcome back once again to our ever-ongoing series on the glories of our most beloved Śrī Śrī Nām. So today, we are continuing with our miniseries on "Stimulation for Ecstatic Love," and this will be part 62. For this lecture, I would like to explore the influence of paintings in Vraja-līlā which stimulate the vrajavāsīs' love for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, and in turn, our love for them as well.

To begin with, we as Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas accept that a painting of Kṛṣṇa is non-different than the Lord Himself. We actually go so far as to say that a proper painting of Kṛṣṇa can be considered a deity of the Lord. That is a bold statement, but it is actually confirmed in śāstra. Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam 11.27.12 says that one of the eight types of deities is a deity painted out of color—a painting: *śilā daru-mayī lauhī lekhyā saikatī tathā manomayī maṇimayī pratimāṣṭa-vidhā smṛtā*. It is the verse: the deity form of the Lord is said to appear in eight varieties—stone, wood, metal, earth, paint, sand, the mind, or jewels. 

By "proper paintings," I mean to say paintings that conform to the descriptions of Kṛṣṇa given by bona fide scriptures and pure devotees of the Lord; and as such, such paintings of Kṛṣṇa are not imaginary. Śrīla Prabhupāda writes in his purport to Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam 4.8.46: "It is really nice. Sometimes we give instructions to our students about the bodily features of the Lord, and they paint Him. Their paintings are not imaginary. The description is given through disciplic succession, just like that given by Nārada Muni, who sees the Lord and describes His bodily features. Therefore, such descriptions should be accepted, and if they are painted, that is not imaginative painting."

Now, one of my Godbrothers, Viṣṇudāsa, who painted for the BBT in the early years, asked the best way to paint Kṛṣṇa so that people would realize that He is the Supreme Personality of Godhead and a real person. Śrīla Prabhupāda replied, "First understand that He, Kṛṣṇa, is most beautiful, the strongest, and never alone. He is always with His associates." And then Viṣṇudāsa continued asking Śrīla Prabhupāda if Kṛṣṇa should be shown with some animals and some trees, as these were mentioned as also being His associates. Śrīla Prabhupāda replied, "Yes, even the trees, flowers, birds, and cows are all His devotees. You can paint them." 

Then Viṣṇudāsa asked if he should try to create high-quality paintings, and Prabhupāda replied, "You know the art I have seen, but your service has no limit, so flood the world with these paintings everywhere." Kṛṣṇa and Śrīla Prabhupāda wrote to Jadurāṇī dāsī that such paintings—this is a famous quote, of course—are "windows to the spiritual world." Windows to the spiritual world! He actually instructed her in many of the details of painting. In a letter dated May 26, 1970, Prabhupāda wrote to her: "The art department is doing very nicely, and surely the production will improve even more by the grace of Kṛṣṇa. You are all being inspired by Kṛṣṇa how to portray the Lord and His associates for the devotees' eyes, so everyone who sees these transcendental pictures will turn to become a devotee. And that is our aim, preaching through paintings—windows to the spiritual world."

Now, Śrīla Prabhupāda called his favorite painting "Wonderful Kṛṣṇa." In this painting—it is a famous painting—Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī is kneeling and offering flowers at Kṛṣṇa’s lotus feet as He stands in His classic threefold-bending pose. In that form is an eternal youth. Kṛṣṇa’s dress, His actions, His features, His excellences surpass those of His childhood or boyhood or any one of the other phases of His youth. It is described that His original eternal form is that of an adolescent in the last stage of youth, and that transcendental form is the source of all other forms and ages. So, Prabhupāda liked that painting.

Now, Śrīla Prabhupāda is of course not the only one who appreciates Kṛṣṇa in paintings; so does Kṛṣṇa’s dearmost, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, particularly when she is in the mood of *pūrva-rāga*. We discussed *pūrva-rāga* I think two years ago, but *pūrva-rāga* is the young gopīs' mood of loving Kṛṣṇa even before they meet Him for the first time. It is described as the stage where the lover and the beloved are unable to meet due to social restrictions. As we have discussed that before, in Vṛndāvana, like Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa when they were young adolescents, the social restrictions were that boys and girls couldn't meet. So, during that stage of their lives, whenever Rādhārāṇī would see a painting of Kṛṣṇa, it would greatly intensify her love for the Lord. That specific mood of *pūrva-rāga* is called *citra-darśa*. *Citra* literally means seeing a painting of one's beloved.

Now, there are actually several types of *pūrva-rāga*. I can actually list them:
* **Sākṣāt-darśa** : A direct vision of the beloved.
* **Svapna-darśa** : Seeing a vision of one's beloved in a dream.
* **Sabhya-mukha-śravaṇa** : Hearing about the beloved from professional reciters.
* **Dūta-mukha-śravaṇa** : Hearing about the beloved from a messenger.
* **Sakhī-mukha-śravaṇa** : Hearing about one's beloved from a girlfriend.
* **Gīta-hītra-śravaṇa** : Hearing a song about the beloved.
* **Vaṃśī-śravaṇa** : Hearing the sound of the beloved’s flute.

This is *pūrva-rāga*. Even before meeting Kṛṣṇa, if the young gopīs experienced any of those things, it stimulates their love for the Lord. But for now, we are speaking about Rādhārāṇī’s love for Kṛṣṇa increasing by *citra-darśa*, seeing a painting of Him. And I was able to find an example of this in Rūpa Gosvāmī’s *Vidagdha-mādhava* 2.23. Here, referring to her friends Lalitā and Viśākhā, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī says to Kṛṣṇa: "My near and dear ones said to me that I should cool my eyes by seeing your beautiful form painted on a slate. O Śiva, Śiva, we simple-minded village girls do not know that we are gazing at a person who constantly increases the flames of the greatest forest fire of separation just by seeing a painting! A win-on-win would that day be ours."

Another example I found is given by Śrīla Kavikarṇapūra in his *Alaṅkāra-kaustubha* 5.35. There, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī is speaking to a friend: "Oh sakhī, while wandering in Vraja, what delightful person did you see? You painted Him and brought His image near me. You’ve painted Him and brought His image near to me, and simply by seeing this image, the glory of my lineage as well as my entire life is slipping away."

And there is also a beautiful verse in Rūpa Gosvāmī’s *Padyāvalī*, verse 366, wherein we hear Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī herself, a painting of Kṛṣṇa. Rādhārāṇī in this verse—a young gopī is speaking to Kṛṣṇa: "Kṛṣṇa, when Rādhārāṇī meditated on You in order to draw Your picture, Kāmadeva drew his bow. When her two fingers moved to grasp the paintbrush, Kāmadeva placed an arrow on the bowstring. When she began to draw, Kāmadeva shot an arrow. She was severely wounded; oh, she fainted, and for a long time she was motionless as a painted picture." Wow!

Now, it is also interesting that in *Kṛṣṇa* book, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes that Kṛṣṇa Himself—not only Rādhārāṇī paints, but Kṛṣṇa Himself learned the art of painting from Sāndīpani Muni when He was in Gurukula. Writing about Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma in chapter 45 of *Kṛṣṇa* book, Prabhupāda writes: "They (meaning Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma) learned the various types of painting, from simple village arts up to the perfectional stage. They also learned how to paint tilaka on the face by making different kinds of dots on the foreheads and cheeks, and they learned the art of making paintings on the floor with the liquid paste of rice and flour. Such paintings are very popular at auspicious ceremonies, performed at household affairs or in the temple. Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma learned how to make a resting place with flowers and how to describe or rather how to decorate clothing and limbs with colorful paintings." So, Kṛṣṇa is an expert painter.

Now, one of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī’s names is Kalāvatī, which means that she is skilled in all the 64 traditional arts and crafts of devotional service to Kṛṣṇa. These famous 64 arts are described, as I researched, in a number of our scriptures including Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam (10th Canto, 45th chapter), *Brahma-saṃhitā*, *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu* 2.1.84, *Vṛndāvana-mahimāmṛta*, and also I found in *Govinda-līlāmṛta* the 64 arts. But specifically in *Bhakti-rasāmṛta-sindhu*, the art of painting is included as number five of the 64 qualities listed; it is called *alekhya*. *Alekhya* is defined as drawing, calligraphy, and painting pictures to the highest perfectional stage of masterful painting. And interestingly enough, all the Vraja-gopīs, they learn all of these 64 arts from Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī; she teaches them.

In the *Haridāsa-sādhyā* in Vṛndāvana, it is said—it is really beautiful:
* The art of dance is manifested by the walk of the gopīs.
* The art of singing is manifested by their voices.
* The art of acting is manifested through their eyes.
* The art of playing is manifested by their laughing.
* The art of decorating is manifested by their apparel.
* The art of painting is manifested by moving their fingers.
* And the art of love is manifested by their very lives.

Hare Kṛṣṇa. Now, getting a little deeper here, getting into more nectarian detail: Love is in the details. The main gopī who serves the Divine Couple through this art of painting is Citrā-sakhī, or just Citrā-devī as she is sometimes called. Citrā-devī is the most famous for her painting. Actually, she is famous for many things in Rādhā-Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes. Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī describes her as follows: Citrā can detect hidden intentions in a love letter. She is a master cook; she knows tastes just by glancing. She strikes water pots to make amazing music. Citrā-devī knows astronomy, astrology, raising domestic animals, gardening, collecting herbs, and how to make tasty, tangy nectar drinks.

Now, Rasik-bihārī, he is a medieval Vrajavāsī poet of Vṛndāvana—I discovered him in the Vṛndāvana Research Institute—wrote in his famous *Rasa-priyā* that Citrā-devī’s village, called Citrauli, it is near Varuṇagrāma. We went there on our last Kārttika Parikramā. It is known as the village of the master of all art forms, where Citrā-devī is the connoisseur of painting, and her village is surrounded by trees of *kuśuma* which ooze lac or resin that the girls use for painting. And Citrā-devī herself lives in a palace in the village called Citra-mandir, or sometimes it is just referred to as the Palace of Paintings. And in that palace are 1,008 pillars made of red ruby stones, and the path from the main courtyard there takes one to Citraśālā, which is, how would you say, the classroom of arts, where young gopīs expertly paint outfits, jewelry, crowns, umbrellas, canopies, chandeliers, and carpets for all of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes.

I read that they use conch shells as cups that hold bright, effulgent, and very deep colors, and the colors themselves smell of fragrant flowers and look very satisfying. I thought that was interesting; they look very satisfying. Now, it is written about those gopīs: "Gold rings are like ankle bells on their fingers which dance on their work that expand the glories of Citrā-devī." It is said that by their sevā, they are filling the world of love with art and colors of awe. 

It is also written that every corner of Citraśālā, the classroom of arts, is decorated with paintings of pastimes of Rādhā and Śyāma. Also, all these gopīs, they know the art of making natural paints and ingredients used for the painting. They actually collect them from—this is interesting, it is so detailed—they collect them from 12 forests in Vṛndāvana. And it even gives the names of the forests: Kamyavana, Tālavana, Lohavana, Bahulāvana, Vṛndāvana, Bhadravana, Śāntikavana, Khadiravana, Bhāṇḍīravana, Śeṣavana, Citravana, and Vṛndāvana. That is 12; those are the 12 forests where the best of flowers grow. And those 12 forests were actually gifted to Citrā-devī by Vṛṣabhānu Mahārāja, the father of Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, specifically for making 12 different emotions appear in Rādhārāṇī’s eyes. That is like poetry: for making 12 different emotions appear in Rādhārāṇī’s eyes.

And this Rasik-bhakta, Rasik-bihārī, writes that whenever Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa want to disguise themselves, they take shelter of Citrā-devī, who paints their faces and limbs to be perfectly disguised. In doing so, she paints red grape nectar on their cheeks, red rose nectar with a red pigment on their lips, and paints their eyes with a gold stick dipped in collyrium. Who wants to hear anything else? The poet concludes: "These are the cosmetics which fade the joy of Cupid and make him feel ashamed by his own beauty. These are the cosmetics which fade the joy of Cupid and make him feel ashamed of his own beauty." Hare Kṛṣṇa.

Now, we always like to include pastimes in the lectures. So, there is a wonderful pastime described by Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī in his illustrious drama *Vidagdha-mādhava*, wherein another gopī, sakhī Viśākhā, her expertise in painting is glorified. It is really nice; it is one of my favorites, actually. And Raghunātha dāsa Gosvāmī heard this pastime in an assembly of senior devotees, including Rāmānanda Rāya in Jagannātha Purī 500 years ago, and very much appreciated it, as we will also. 

It begins with Lalitā and Viśākhā finding Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī hiding and lamenting in separation from Kṛṣṇa in a grove of vaṃśī trees somewhere in the Vṛndāvana forest. And seeing her like that, Lalitā and Viśākhā were heartbroken. And when Viśākhā-devī inquired from Rādhārāṇī as to the cause of her sorrow, Rādhārāṇī just sighed, and lowering her head, she said, "Let me disclose the truth of the matter, and then you will understand my dilemma." And then Rādhārāṇī said, "Since I first heard the name of a person called Kṛṣṇa, I have lost all good sense. Sometime later, I heard a flute song played by some unknown cowherd boy, and an intense madness arose in my heart. Then, Viśākhā, you drew a picture of a most handsome boy. When I was alone gazing at your drawing, that beautiful boy came out of the portrait and ignited an inferno in my heart with a smile and touch. Now, now my mind has become attached to him as well. I am condemned, for I have become simultaneously attached to three persons! Since such wantonness is unpardonable for a chaste woman, it would be better for me to die and be freed from my sins." Wow.

Now, Lalitā, who had been listening very carefully, she asked, "Rādhā, did this happen in a dream?" So Rādhārāṇī replied, "Whether I was dreaming or awake, I don't know. All I know is that my burning heart now longs for that handsome boy’s touch." And overcome, Rādhārāṇī said softly, "Wicked heart, are you not ashamed to love three persons simultaneously? You love some boy whose name is Kṛṣṇa. You also love a cowherd who plays a flute, and now you love this boy in the picture! Oh heart, when I kill my body, only then will I be free from you." 

Hearing Rādhārāṇī speaking this way, Lalitā said with compassion, "My dear friend, it is springtime; none of us are immune from the attacks of Kāmadeva." But unmoved by Lalitā’s attempt to appease her, Rādhārāṇī, turning towards Lalitā and pointing to a nearby flower vine, she said, "If you truly want to cure me, then wrap this flowering vine around my neck like a noose and hang me!" So Lalitā and Viśākhā quickly covered their ears in horror of Rādhārāṇī’s words, and tears burst from their eyes as they said, "If you continue to say such horrible things, we will be the ones to die."

Suddenly—Lalitā Mañjarī is arranging all this—suddenly Lalitā had a revelation, and as her eyes opened wide, she smiled brightly and she said, "My dear friend Rādhārāṇī, I understand what has happened! How could you, the most chaste of all Vraja-gopīs, be in love with three different men? It is simply not possible. Listen to me: the three persons—the one named Kṛṣṇa, the flute player, and the boy in the picture—are all the same person. Quite simply, your heart is drawn to three features of Kṛṣṇa: His name, His pastimes, and His form. Your love is supremely chaste, and you may now give up your shame as well as your plans to end your life. Dear one, even the least reputable of the beautiful girls of Gokula could never love any other man than Kṛṣṇa."

Rūpa Gosvāmī then writes that hope then dawned in Rādhārāṇī’s eyes as she breathed a sigh of relief. Then she thought to herself, "My dear mind, be peaceful. The man you seek to attain is none other than the refuge of all living beings, the controller of all worlds, and the soul of all souls." Śrīla Rūpa Gosvāmī—such a beautiful pastime. 

So I believe we may have more about painting in Vṛndāvana-līlā and painting pastimes as a means of stimulation for ecstatic love, which we will continue with next Friday. I’d like to conclude today with a beautiful verse, and it is by one of our favorite Vrajavāsī poets, Sūradāsa, in his poem *Aṣṭachāp*. "I bow to the bow of His beauty. He hit the arrow of a glance and pierced my heart into two different parts. One part of my heart was beating with hope and the other part with the desire to see Him, Kṛṣṇa, again. My eyes were staring at His beautiful face like a madman continuously stares at a painting without ever blinking. I was admiring His picture-like beauty, which was painted in my memory by all mediums—my heart, my soul, and my mind." Sūradāsa says, "Govindadeva is the master of capturing the hearts of His devotees."
