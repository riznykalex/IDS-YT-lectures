"# The Binding Rope of Spotless Love - Part 2

Everyone, welcome back again to our ever-ongoing series on the glories of our beloved Śrī Vṛndāvana. We are continuing with our new series for the month of Kārtika entitled, appropriately, "The Binding Rope of Spotless Love," and this will be part two. I would like to begin today with a quote from Śrīla Prabhupāda. It is from a lecture he gave in New Vṛndāvana, America, on August 31st, 1972. He said:

> "We should simply hear about Kṛṣṇa. We have forgotten Kṛṣṇa, our Supreme Father, on account of material contamination, but if we hear about Him attentively, we will begin to remember Him. You have kindly come here to listen to talks about Kṛṣṇa, so this is a very good opportunity for you. You are all young boys and girls and you are very fortunate. Your fathers and grandfathers have not come here, but you have come. So just continue hearing about Kṛṣṇa and your life will be successful."

Thank you, Śrīla Prabhupāda. 

Now, before we move ahead, I would like to again mention the devotee-scholar and poet, Śrī Hari Sūri, whom we quoted a number of times in the first lecture. I was introduced to this devotional scholar through listening to the lectures of my very dear godbrother, Rādha Govinda Mahārāja, one of ISKCON’s most loved initiating spiritual masters, as well as lectures by Haripāśa Dāsa, my friend whom I mentioned in the last lecture, who sits on our GBC Śāstric Advisory Committee and with whom I often consult to make sure the pastimes, the verses, and the philosophy that I share in my lectures are all authentic. 

I thought it would be appropriate to share some details here about Śrī Hari Sūri’s life and work. Of course, he is not mentioned in the works of the Six Gosvāmīs, but surely there are many known and unknown Vaiṣṇavas in the annals of time who we can take shelter of if their words and deeds are perfectly—and I say perfectly—in line with ours. 

In this mood, Śrīla Prabhupāda writes in the purport to *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 1.1.1: 

> "Within the past 500 years, many erudite scholars and ācāryas like Jīva Gosvāmī, Sanātana Gosvāmī, Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura, Vallabhācārya, and many other distinguished scholars even after the time of Lord Caitanya made elaborate commentaries on the *Bhāgavatam*, and the serious student would do well to attempt to go through them to better relish the transcendental messages." 

Hari Sūri appeared in the 18th century in Nashik, India, in a brāhmaṇa family belonging to the Kaśyapa-gotra. His family members were all initiated in the *Vārkarī-kīrtana-sampradāya* of the famous saint Tukārām. This is a sub-branch in the movement of Lord Caitanya Mahāprabhu, and the *sampradāya* is still existing in the state of Mahārāstra in the western part of India. Śrīla Prabhupāda says in his introduction to *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* that Tukārām, whom we mentioned earlier, was initiated by a merchant. In his youth, Hari Sūri mastered many branches of the Vedas such as grammar, *nyāya*, *purāṇa*, *itihāsa*, and especially poetry—Vedic poetry. But he was wholeheartedly attracted to and attached to the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, the king of all scriptures. In fact, I was reading that he devoted most of his life to studying and commenting on the Tenth Canto of the *Bhāgavatam*, where the special emphasis is, as we know, on *Rāsa-līlā*. 

Later in his life, scholars have written that Kṛṣṇa appeared to him in a dream and said—and these are Kṛṣṇa’s words—"I will manifest the deep meaning of My pastimes in your heart. You please write a commentary on them and express them to the world." So, with that instruction, he wrote a commentary on the first 49 chapters of the Tenth Canto of *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, and he entitled it *Bhakti-rasāyanam*. *Bhakti-rasāyanam* means "The Elixir of Bhakti." Now, interestingly enough, it is the only commentary on the *Bhāgavatam* in the form of Sanskrit verses, most of which, I was reading, are extremely cryptic and difficult to decode, but he did this on purpose so that only a serious reader would take up the task of exploring Kṛṣṇa-līlā in such detail. Serious students! As he is an accepted authority by my illustrious godbrother, whom I hold in the highest esteem, as well as an authority for our GBC Śāstric Advisory Committee, I will proceed with our discussions on Dāmodara-līlā, quoting Śrī Hari Sūri from time to time.

So, in our previous lecture, we had reached the point in Dāmodara-līlā where Kṛṣṇa, being frustrated, had broken several pots of yogurt and run away. And upon returning to where she had been feeding Kṛṣṇa, our dear Mother Yaśodā was surprised at what she saw. How do I know this? Because I was reading *Gopāla-campū* by Jīva Gosvāmī, and Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī gives us an insight in *Gopāla-campū* as to exactly what Mother Yaśodā saw. It is short but sweet: he writes that all the yogurt spilled in the courtyard of Nanda Mahārāja made it look like a "festival of yogurt." I thought that was nice. All the yogurt spilled everywhere; it looked like a festival of yogurt. 

Wanting to know who did that, Mother Yaśodā followed the small yogurt marks of the lotus feet of Kṛṣṇa to wherever they were leading. And Hari Sūri writes that Kṛṣṇa wanted to give an important lesson through this pastime. He says that Kṛṣṇa displayed transcendental *kāma*—we know that word, it means lust—but transcendental *kāma* with a strong desire to drink the breast milk of His mother. And then He exhibited transcendental anger (*krodha*) when His mother walked away, and Mother Yaśodā demonstrated transcendental greed (*lobha*) in her desire to save the boiling milk for her son. Hari Sūri writes that all this together created so much chaos and confusion in the house of Mother Yaśodā, as we previously discussed. 

So, Hari Sūri concludes, it is interesting that if transcendental *kāma*, *krodha*, and *lobha* can create such chaos—of course, of a transcendental nature—then what to speak of the degraded, perverted reflection of *kāma*, *krodha*, and *lobha* in the material world? So he advises us that although we may worship the *kāma*, *krodha*, and *lobha* of the spiritual world, which ultimately enhances our attraction to Kṛṣṇa, we should strictly avoid the perverted reflection of *kāma*, *krodha*, and *lobha* in the material world. And that we know all too well from Lord Kṛṣṇa’s instruction in *Bhagavad-gītā* Chapter 16, verse 21: 

> "There are three gates leading to this hell—lust, anger, and greed. Every sane man should give these up, for they lead to the degradation of the soul."

Now, Hari Sūri mentions another interesting thing. He says that although there was so much milk and yogurt and so many other—he describes *go-rasa* (cow products)—in the home of Mother Yaśodā, Kṛṣṇa was actually only interested in daily consuming butter. He just loved butter. He writes that butter is the *sāra*—the complete essence—of yogurt. Therefore, in Vedic cuisine, butter is often referred to as *sāra*, the essence of all milk products. And he says there is another important lesson to be learned here, and the lesson is that he says although there may be many *rasas*, or let us say pleasures, in this world, an intelligent person should only search after *sāra*, the complete essence of all these *rasas*, all these pleasures. And he says, "What is that?" *Bhakti-rasa*, loving devotion to Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. Actually, there is no higher taste to be found anywhere in creation or beyond.

Now, *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* Kanto 10, Chapter 9 describes that as Mother Yaśodā was searching for Kṛṣṇa, He eventually sat down on a turned-over wooden mortar. These mortars are used in Indian culture—Vedic culture—for grinding spices. And as He did so, He was distributing milk products like yogurt and butter to the monkeys. Now, because He had stolen these milk products, He was looking all around in great anxiety, thinking that very soon He was going to be chastised by His mother. And sure enough, when Mother Yaśodā saw Him from a distance, she very cautiously approached Him from behind. 

Now, here Hari Sūri points out that Kṛṣṇa was sitting on a *ulūkhala*. *Ulūkhala* means a mortar. However, Hari Sūri plays creatively with this term because in Sanskrit, it can also mean a "great ruffian." *Ulu*—great; *khala*—ruffian. And he writes that in the same way that a *sādhu* associating with a *khala* (a ruffian) never loses his sādhu-like nature, so Kṛṣṇa, who is the Lord of all *sādhus*, although associated directly with a *ulūkhala* (you can take it as a mortar or associating with a big ruffian somewhere), He still exhibits His charitable nature, in this case by distributing the essence of life to the monkeys, which is butter, which He gave to them. He may be in the material world fighting the demons, but He is always charitably disposed to the devotees, to everyone actually. He never loses His charitable nature. 

Kṛṣṇa, especially as Caitanya Mahāprabhu, association continues to describe this pastime in Chapter 9 of *Kṛṣṇa Book*. He writes, with great pleasure I repeat the words of my spiritual master:

> "Looking this way and that way in fear of her, because He was conscious of His naughty behavior, and seeing her son so engaged, she very silently approached Him from behind. Kṛṣṇa, however, saw her coming toward Him with a stick in her hand."

Now, at this point, Jīva Gosvāmī writes again in *Gopāla-campū* that a person who wants to catch a thief has only two eyes, like the policeman looking for the thief; he only has two eyes. But he writes, "A nervous thief has thousands of eyes," in the sense that he is constantly looking in every direction to see if someone is coming to catch him. And this is literally true in the case of baby Kṛṣṇa. Sometimes Prabhupāda would say, "Our Kṛṣṇa," he would say like that, "Our Kṛṣṇa." How is that literally true? Because Kṛṣṇa is described in Vedic literature: *sahasra-puruṣa*, *sahasrākṣa*. It means He is thousand-headed and thousand-eyed, as the *Puruṣa-sūkta* describes. There’s thousands of ears and thousands of eyes; that’s our Kṛṣṇa. 

So, sensing that His mother was coming, Prabhupāda describes Kṛṣṇa immediately got down from the grinding mortar and began to flee in fear. He is actually running in fear. Kṛṣṇa is fearful! But wait a minute—how can Kṛṣṇa experience fear? The *Bhāgavatam* 1.1.4 says: 

> *bībheti āyur iti*—fear personified is afraid of Him. 

And in the *Nṛsiṁha-tāpanī Upaniṣad*, we hear how Kṛṣṇa as the Lord, and Nṛsiṁhadeva, they are non-different:

> *ugraṁ vīraṁ mahā-viṣṇuṁ*

The fierce, brave, all-pervasive Viṣṇu who has His face in all directions. So how can such a Lord experience fear? But Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇavas, they have a very nice explanation, a very simple but *rasika* answer to this question, which is given by the ācāryas. And that is that all of Kṛṣṇa’s grandeur—you know, His awe-inspiring personality—exists only on the outside of the borders of Vraja. But within Vṛndāvana, a single display of Mother Yaśodā’s stick is enough to drive away all those grand qualities. They only exist outside of Vraja. Nevertheless, within Vṛndāvana, a single display of Mother Yaśodā’s stick is enough to drive away all those grand qualities. This is Vraja! 

Your child has gone on to describe that outside Vṛndāvana, Kṛṣṇa may be *ugram*, fierce, but in this pastime, Dāmodara-līlā, He has become *abīram*. It means feeble. Elsewhere He may be very brave, but in Dāmodara-līlā and the sweetness of Vṛndāvana, He becomes *abhīram*, running afraid. Elsewhere He may be Mahā-Viṣṇu, the all-pervasive Lord, but in Vṛndāvana He is *aviṣṇu*; He is localized as the form of a small boy. As the *Nṛsiṁha*, He may be *sarvato-mukham*, having His faces in all directions, but in Vṛndāvana the child is afraid—He is even afraid to show His face to Mother Yaśodā in this pastime in this month of Dāmodara. 

So I was reading further, and Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī explains in *Bhāgavat-sandarbha* that the Supreme Lord is complete when His *viruddha-guṇa*—Kṛṣṇa is complete when the harmonization of opposite qualities exists within Himself. Now, that is a tongue-twister—the harmonization of opposite qualities exists within Himself. In other words, Kṛṣṇa cannot simply be all-pervasive like Viṣṇu; He also has to be localized like Kṛṣṇa. He has to be both of those; He has to harmonize these opposites. This is what Kṛṣṇa does; He harmonizes the opposites. He can’t simply be brave all the time; He also has to show some transcendental human weakness. In other words, He has to show a complete range of auspicious qualities, a complete range, in order to be known as *Bhagavān* or God. He must have *viruddha-guṇa*, the harmonization of opposite qualities, within Himself. 

Now, the same *Nṛsiṁha-tāpanī Upaniṣad* where we quoted the mantra *ugraṁ vīraṁ mahā-viṣṇuṁ*—this is really interesting—later says in this context: 

> *māṁ mahāntam aviṣṇum*

He is fierce, the Lord is fierce, yet feeble; brave, yet afraid; all-pervasive, yet localized; having His face in all directions, yet not having His face in all directions. These are the contradictory qualities that are harmonized only in Kṛṣṇa, and therefore our ācāryas see that Kṛṣṇa is the harmonizer of all opposites. We give so many opposites, but they are all in Kṛṣṇa, harmonized in His transcendental personality. *Bhagavān Śrī Kṛṣṇa kī jaya!*

So, Śrīla Prabhupāda goes on to explain in *Kṛṣṇa Book*: 

> "Mother Yaśodā chased Kṛṣṇa to all corners, trying to capture the Supreme Personality of Godhead, who is never approached even by the expert meditations of great yogis." 

In other words, the Supreme Personality of Godhead, Kṛṣṇa, who was never caught by the yogis and speculators, was playing just like a child for such a great devotee as Mother Yaśodā. So, here, Hari Sūri points out that Kṛṣṇa was looking in all directions, but very carefully *not* in the direction of Mother Yaśodā. And what is the reason for that? He writes: 

> "Kṛṣṇa wanted to show that as long as even a little anger towards Me is there in someone’s mind, I will never look in that person’s direction." 

This is what Kṛṣṇa’s... the lesson to be learned here: as long as even a little anger towards Me is there in anyone’s mind, I, Kṛṣṇa, will never look in that person’s direction. So it is important for devotees to know that we should never keep any grudges or misgivings towards Kṛṣṇa in our hearts—like, you know, "Why did this happen to me? How can Kṛṣṇa be so cruel? Why is God...?" No, we should never think like that, otherwise He won’t look in our direction, just as He was not looking in the direction of Mother Yaśodā. Very interesting perspective here by the ācāryas. In other words, we can conclude our *bhakti*, our devotion for Kṛṣṇa, should always be, as Rūpa Gosvāmī describes it, *ānukūlyena*—favorably disposed. 

So Śrīla Prabhupāda continues: Mother Yaśodā, however, cannot easily catch the fast-running child because of her thin waist and her heavy body. Now, Jīva Gosvāmī says in *Gopāla-campū* that Mother Yaśodā was not able to catch Kṛṣṇa easily. It is very beautiful: just as a heavy cloud driven by the wind is unable to catch a small cloud driven by the same wind. Transcendental poetry! Just as a heavy cloud driven by the wind is unable to catch a small cloud driven by the same wind, in this way, Mother couldn’t catch Kṛṣṇa. 

And he goes on to say that Kṛṣṇa could not be caught by Mother Yaśodā as long as He was looking away. However, as children are, one time He looked around, and He saw Mother Yaśodā rushing towards Him, and what happened at that moment? He froze. And this was the moment that He finally got caught. We are making some progress here! This was the moment that He finally got caught. 

So, Śrīla Jīva Gosvāmī goes on to describe so sweetly—and this is my favorite part of the lecture—he describes that as soon as He was caught, all the tears in the world came to His rescue. Kṛṣṇa! All the tears in the world came to His rescue. I just love that, "all the tears in the world came to His rescue," because He started crying loudly. And at this point, Hari Sūri helps us understand this in a very clear way. He quotes a verse: 

> "A religious king is the strength of those who are weak; speaking lies is the strength of thieves; keeping quiet is the strength of fools; and crying loudly is the strength of small children." 

How so? Well, because when they cry, you know, your heart melts and you come to them for whatever reason. A religious king is the strength of those who are weak; speaking lies is the strength of thieves; keeping quiet is the strength of fools; and crying loudly is the strength of small children. So Kṛṣṇa started crying in order to save Himself from any possible chastisement of Mother Yaśodā. 

Wow, I’m hanging on the edge of my seat; probably you are, too. I’m going to stop here. We’re going to stop here, oh Kṛṣṇa, and we’ll leave you today with an impression of the Lord in the courtyard of Nanda Mahārāja’s palace, because He didn’t run so far; He’s a little toddler. He’s still in the courtyard of Nanda Mahārāja’s palace. And there is a favorite verse of mine from one of my favorite ācāryas, Rūpa Gosvāmī, from his *Padyāvalī*, and I found it’s also quoted in *Caitanya-caritāmṛta, Madhya-līlā* 19.96. So beautiful: 

> "Those who are afraid of material existence worship the Vedic literature; some worship *smṛti*, the corollaries to the Vedic literature; and others worship the *Mahābhārata*. As far as I am concerned, I worship Kṛṣṇa’s father, Mahārāja Nanda, in whose courtyard the Supreme Personality of Godhead, the Absolute Truth, is playing." 

All glories to the Lord Dāmodara! All glories to Mother Yaśodā! All glories to Śrīla Prabhupāda, the revealer of the Dhāma! So thank you forever. This is lecture number two—I don’t know, maybe three, four, five, six—I just hope it goes on forever. And surely we’ll be back next Friday with more nectar about this month of Kārtika, Dāmodara-līlā. Thank you so much, see you soon.""
