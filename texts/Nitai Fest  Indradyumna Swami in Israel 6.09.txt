"Knowledge is the most valuable thing we have. It is the main difference between our movement and all the others—that we have very deep questions, and as long as the soul is in the body, the matter grows until dawn. The goal of childhood, adolescence, the next level—as long as the soul is in the body, and while you are in the house, the repair continues. You might think of replacing, cleaning, or painting; as long as the soul is here, we grow old. If your car is in use for too long, of course, it brings more anxiety.

Once I met an elderly woman who repeated the *gāyatrī* mantras regularly for eighty-six years. Despite her age, her face shone with the knowledge of wisdom. It turned out to be a great blessing to visit her. She said she wanted to leave this world to enter her eternal service. I suggested that we need to prepare to leave this world; we must not live like cats and dogs, and we certainly should not die at such a low level of consciousness. You will have to die anyway, so you shouldn't ignore this fact. You need to prepare for that moment.

There is a story about a man who, having grown old, was very afraid of death. He was worried about the god of death, Yamarāja, coming to take his soul. He went to an astrologer and asked him to calculate the exact time of his death. Astrology is an exact science that can sometimes predict the future. This man decided to bribe the astrologer, and the astrologer told him, "You will die in a year, on December 14th, at such and such a minute."

Fearing this, the man made marble sculptures of himself in different poses. These sculptures were so life-like that they were indistinguishable from a living person. On the day of his predicted death, he arranged all these statues around the room and stood among them, freezing in place. When Yamarāja appeared in the room to take the sculptor, he saw all these figures and could not determine which one was the real person. Yamarāja, being a very busy person, did not have time to figure out which was the original.

However, Yamarāja thought, "I am a direct manager in this universe; it is impossible to deceive me." He asked, "Why are these sculptures so ugly and their proportions so off?" The sculptor, indignant, shouted, "What do you mean, they are perfect!" By speaking, he revealed himself, and Yamarāja took him. This story illustrates that it is impossible to cheat death. We shouldn't be afraid, however; Vedic scriptures compare ignorance to darkness and knowledge to light. Where there is light, there cannot be fear.

The Vedic philosophy teaches that changing the body is as simple as changing clothes. If we understand that we are not the body, we will not be afraid of death. We are constantly changing bodies. I remember the words of Śrīla Prabhupāda: we change bodies, but the soul remains. Therefore, the body is mortal, but the soul is eternal.

We must realize that this life is not just for material activity. According to the scriptures, we have already gone through countless cycles of birth and death. I was once asked, "What do you recommend?" The most important thing is to be thoughtful and ask the right questions about the meaning of life.

Consider the story of Buddha. Born as a prince in a kingdom filled with suffering, his father tried to shield him from all inconvenience. He was raised in an idyllic atmosphere where he never saw sickness, old age, or death. One day, he went beyond the walls of the palace and saw the realities of life: birth, illness, and old age. He realized that the most important thing in life is to ask, "What is the meaning of my life?"

He eventually left his royal life, found a sacred tree, and meditated for years to realize spiritual truth. Of many thousands of people, only a few strive for self-awareness, and of those, only one can truly comprehend the Supreme Lord. This is a great rarity.

When I was younger, I heard this story and thought about achieving enlightenment under a tree. I even tried to live in a cave for a time. But eventually, I realized that I needed to find a group of people who worked with this knowledge. I returned to America, and one day I saw an exhibition dedicated to Kṛṣṇa. I looked at the image of Kṛṣṇa surrounded by His devotees and felt that I needed to go there. I heard the sounds of bells and *kīrtana*, and I discovered a group of devotees chanting "Hare Kṛṣṇa."

A devotee spoke to me about Kṛṣṇa. He explained that one cannot imitate the Lord, but one can follow in His footsteps by developing the highest taste through chanting the Hare Kṛṣṇa *mahā-mantra*:

$$Hare\ Kṛṣṇa,\ Hare\ Kṛṣṇa,\ Kṛṣṇa\ Kṛṣṇa,\ Hare\ Hare$$
$$Hare\ Rāma,\ Hare\ Rāma,\ Rāma\ Rāma,\ Hare\ Hare$$

Now, we have a special opportunity to engage in this chanting. We have wonderful musicians here who will decorate the chanting with their instruments. As we chant, we must be completely focused and repeat the words from the heart. God is a natural state of mind. We are not impersonalists; we turn to the Lord as a person. We have no qualifications of our own, so we simply pray for His mercy.""
