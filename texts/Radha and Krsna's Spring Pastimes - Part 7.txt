"Hari bol, everyone! Welcome back again to our ongoing series on the glories of our beloved *śrī-vṛndāvana-vāsī*. *śrī-kṛṣṇa-preṣṭhāya bhūtale śrīmatī bhaktivedānta-svāmin iti nāmine namaste*. So, we are continuing with the spring pastimes of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa in Vṛndāvana, and today I would like to begin—actually, finish—the series with a description of the various ways in which the Vrajavāsīs celebrate Holi to this very day.

In Vṛndāvana, it is said that: "If someone plays Holi in Vṛndāvana, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa will fill their life with the joy of love and the colors of devotion." In our last few lectures, we discussed the traditional way of observing Holi, which is throwing colored powders and squirting liquid dyes. In olden times—meaning going back several hundred years—the Vrajavāsīs used various pure substances to make the dyes for the powders and the liquids. I found the ingredients in the Vṛndāvana Research Institute: they used saffron called *keśar*, a white scented powder known as *coba*, and a black powder called *argajā*. It is said that the *argajā* powder can make one blind with love. Of course, they used sandalwood paste (*candana*), vermilion (*kuṅkuma*), and a pink-colored powder called *gulāla*. They also used colored water made out of boiling the spring flower called *tāsu*; this flower only grows in spring, so they would boil that in water and use the essence of that water in the substances. I read that the *tāsu* flower is a flower very dear to the *gopīs*. Because these items are expensive these days, nowadays the Vrajavāsīs play Holi just with the pink-colored substance called *gulāla*—which is made out of dry roses mixed with rice, wheat, and various perfumes. So, those are the ingredients in the past and in the present.

Aside from the method of throwing colored powders and dyes in syringes during Holi, there are four other prominent ways the Vrajavāsīs in Vṛndāvana observe this festival of colors. "Holi" translates into a "festival of colors," and each of these four particular ways happens just once during Vṛndāvana spring in separate places. These methods of observing Holi are all part of the Vṛndāvana culture, but I couldn't find any śāstric reference for how they all started or evolved through time, or even if Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa played these kinds of Holi. However, they are certainly an integral part of Vṛndāvana today, and when you are in Vṛndāvana during Holi, you see the Vrajavāsīs play these types of Holi. I will mention them so you can understand them if you are ever in Vṛndāvana in springtime:

*   **Lathmar Holi:** The ladies playfully strike their elder brother-in-laws with wooden sticks. I have seen this many times; it takes place in Varṣāṇā.
*   **Laddu-mar Holi:** The cowherd boys and the *gopīs* throw *laddus* at each other. I have also seen that; the first time, I thought, "Wait, what about all those *laddus*?" They throw *laddus* at each other, and this also takes place in Varṣāṇā.
*   **Chadi-mar Holi:** Played in Gokula, where mothers playfully touch their sons with small, thin sticks.
*   **Huranga:** Played in the village of Dāujī. There is a beautiful, big deity of Balarāma in that village, and the village is named after Him—Dāujī. In this celebration, sisters-in-law playfully attack their younger brother-in-laws with wet *cādaras*. It is a riot! I have seen it; they have these wet *cādaras* and they chase after their younger brother-in-laws.

Officially, Holi starts 31 days after Vasant Pañcamī, which we remember is the first day of spring. The whole Holi celebration lasts eight days; altogether it is called *Holī Aṣṭaka* (the eight days of playing Holi). For our information:
1.  The first day is Lāḍū-mār Holi in Varṣāṇā.
2.  The second day is Lāṭh-mār Holi in Varṣāṇā.
3.  The third day is a smaller version of Lāṭh-mār Holi in Nandagrām.
4.  The fourth day is the color and flower Holi in general Vṛndāvana town.
5.  The fifth day is the Holi of playing with small sticks in Gokula.
6.  The sixth day is the day of burning Holikā; it is actually on the day of Gaura Pūrṇimā, a pastime we discussed in a previous lecture.
7.  The seventh day is played in all the other villages and forests of Vṛndāvana.
8.  The eighth day is played with the wet *cādaras* in the village of Dāujī.

Keep that in mind, and perhaps one day spend some time in Vṛndāvana during spring and attend all these eight festivals. The Holi festival fits well into Vṛndāvana spring because both spring and the celebration of Holi are bursting with a kaleidoscope of colors—spring with its newly blossomed flowers and Holi with its colored powders and dyes. They are both very colorful.

There is a wonderful pastime I came across connected to Vṛndāvana’s spring flowers. It is described that one spring morning, the *gopīs* set out to explore the fragrant and colorful Vṛndāvana. They were joyfully wandering from one forest garden to another. It is said that spring—the king of all seasons—decorates every creeper, tree, garden, and meadow in Vṛndāvana with its flowers, but until it can offer its floral beauty to its queen, it feels melancholy and incomplete. That is so beautiful. So, when the *gopīs* arrived at a special grove called *Mahāraṇyā*, they were inspired to offer all of its wealth—the flowers—to their worshipable deity, Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī, because, as we know, Rādhārāṇī is the queen of Vṛndāvana (*Vṛndāvaneśvarī*). The *gopīs* sat Rādhārāṇī on a golden throne and went about collecting all kinds of beautiful flowers in that grove. They collected flowers that were budding, blossoming, or in full bloom. They set about making anklets, garlands, a crown, and other ornaments with flowers. It is an art; the *gopīs* are expert at everything with flowers. Sometimes in our ISKCON temples, for Ratha-yātrā, we make a full flower outfit for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa—so like that, we are getting trained up. Lalitā and Viśākhā directed the older girls, who in turn told their own assistants what kind of specific flower decorations to make. All the while, the girls happily chatted about Śrīmatī Rādhārāṇī’s natural beauty. As the ornaments were handed to them one by one, a few especially clever girls like Rūpa and Rati placed them on Śrī-Rādhā while others danced around the throne, singing and playing musical instruments. When all the ornaments were in place on Rādhārāṇī’s limbs, she appeared like the queen of spring. Therefore, at that time, the *gopīs* started calling her *Mahārānī*, and they named that place where she was adorned in such a beautiful way *Mahāraṇyā*, the queen of spring. That pastime takes place in Mahāraṇyā. I was reading that in Vraja, the title *Mahārānī* signifies more than just royal lineage; it is also awarded to *gopīs* who rule over pure devotion. Thus, Vaiṣṇavas also honor Yamunā-devī and Tulasī-devī with the title *Mahārānī*.

Garga-saṁhitā mentions that another time when spring arrived in Vṛndāvana, all the *gopīs* were overcome with the desire to celebrate by throwing colors. However, Rādhārāṇī was not in a festive mood at that time, and no one dared make any preparations for Holi without her. The whole life of the *gopīs* is centered around Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. One morning, all of Varṣāṇā’s friends gathered together and pleaded with her: "Please listen, now is the time for the Holi festival and Kṛṣṇa will come soon to your palace for celebrations. His friends are already at Pīlīpokhara, that famous lake, planning a big celebration." 

Then everyone looked out the window, and the *gopīs* said, "Oh, just look! Śrī-Rādhā, here He comes, the son of Mahārāja Nanda. Wow! Rādhā, just see His enchanting eyes; they are dancing amidst His dark, curly tresses which are dangling over His lovely, round cheeks. He is magnificently dressed, wearing a flowing yellow garment and golden inlaid anklets which are tinkling so sweetly. Yaśodā-māyā has decorated Him with a glittering crown. Just see, Rādhā, His earrings are shining through His garland like lightning in dark clouds. Holding a spraying syringe in His hands, He is fully drenched in red color! O Rādhā, He is waiting for you."

It is written that as Kṛṣṇa approached the palace, He stopped and called out, "Hey Rādhe! Hey Rādhe! It’s a lovely moonlit night. How can you be sitting inside? Groups of *gopīs* and *gopas* from all over Vraja have gathered here for Holi." She was not in a transcendentally good mood, but by hearing these words of Kṛṣṇa, her heart melted, and surrounded by her lovely friends, she came out of the palace. It is described: "As if a moon traversed through a pond of lilies," this is how Rādhā came out of the palace. The Holi festivities began by throwing *gulāla*—the red powder made from rose petals—between the two teams: the *gopīs* and the *gopas*. But when Kṛṣṇa employed His *picārī*—that is the spring syringe—it failed to drench Śrī-Rādhā with color. She was just so expert at moving that He couldn't hit her, so instead, He sent her His love.

After some time, it became apparent that Kṛṣṇa’s team was losing in this battle of Holi. To save His team from being defeated, Kṛṣṇa desperately sprayed color over Rādhārāṇī’s closed eyes. However, in the intricacies of *rasa* and performing these pastimes, through the *ācāryas’* eyes, we are blessed to see these things: this was not acceptable according to the rules of Holi. You cannot spray someone in the eyes, whether open or closed. The *ācāryas* conclude that Kṛṣṇa was cheating, and as a result, all the excitement and cheers vanished in an instant. Everything came to a stop. They put it so poetically; it just melts the heart. 

To resolve this critical issue, the case was taken to Lalitā’s court. Lalitā said, "Kṛṣṇa has certainly cheated; as such, He should be appropriately punished. He always tries to save Himself by cheating, and our Rādhārāṇī is well aware of this devious trait. Our Rādhārāṇī is ever-vigilant." Hearing all this, Kṛṣṇa became embarrassed; He became extremely displeased with His behavior and became submissive to Śrī-Rādhā. She has the *mahā-bhāva* to control Kṛṣṇa. Kṛṣṇa came before Śrī-Rādhā and asked for her pardon, but she was not in a mood to forgive. Lalitā loudly declared an appropriate punishment: "Color Kṛṣṇa’s eyes with *kājala*." Śrī-Rādhā took some *kājala* and, with two of her fingers, she tried to apply it around Kṛṣṇa’s eyes. But the moment she tried, Kṛṣṇa turned His head away. So, Śrī-Rādhā quickly put her arm around His neck, pulled Him in real close, and applied the *kājala*. She won! Afterwards, Kṛṣṇa presented His *paṭaka*—a waistband—to Śrī-Rādhā and then returned home. It was a rule in competition that the loser in any event had to surrender their waistband to the person who defeated them.

There is also a Holi pastime where Śrī-Rādhā and the *gopīs* go from Varṣāṇā to Nandagrām. As they traversed through the forest to Nandagrām, the *gopīs* sang a particular song which is quite famous in Vṛndāvana: "Kṛṣṇa, please give us Your *darśana*. You wear a crown with a peacock feather. The moon, sun, and millions of stars also meditate on Your unique personality. The earrings in Your ears and the garland around Your neck made of fragrant flowers look so wonderful." 

When in Varṣāṇā or Nandagrām for Holi, there is always a public gathering; all the Vrajavāsīs want to come and see. During that time, the Vrajavāsīs sing many songs to facilitate the spiritual emotions in the hearts of those who are participating. One of these songs is captured by our illustrious *ācārya* Prabodhananda Sarasvatī in his book, *Rādhā-rasa-sudhā-nidhi*. Text 96 captures this mood: 

"May the two syllables 'Rādhā', with Kṛṣṇa sitting like the king of yogīs in a cottage near the bank of the Yamunā in Mānasarovara, meditating on her lotus feet, being overcome with the most splendid, blissful, and nectarine love, appear in my heart and in my eyes filled with tears as a result of chanting His name at all times."

Here is one last spring pastime before ending our series on springtime in Vṛndāvana. It takes place in Phalenā, a small forest. It is mentioned that on one special occasion, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa became mere playthings of their girlfriends. At the center of Phalenā, there is a very large banyan tree, beneath which sits a throne fit for the King and Queen of Vraja, and the ground around the throne is paved with gems that outshine those in Indra’s court. In the heart of spring, a group of *gopīs* once asked Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa to sit upon that throne and allow them to show their unrestrained love. We worship the *gopīs* because they have that pure *amala-bhakti*. The divine couple agreed. As they sat down, some *gopīs* began playing musical instruments, others sang songs about the divine couple's mutual love, while others covered Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa with red vermilion and blue dye. 

As the music sped up, the *gopīs* sprinkled dye all over the divine couple's bodies. The exchange reached a climax when the *gopīs* used "flower bombs" of colored powder to cover Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa so much so that they became indistinguishable from their throne and from each other. The entire area was engulfed in a cloud of *gulāla* rising higher and higher into the air until it could be seen by amused Vrajavāsīs from as far away as Nandagrām! Kṛṣṇa sat hand in hand, apparently unaffected but subtly responding with submissive smiles to the laughter, gaiety, and affection of their friends. Love is reciprocal. This place is called Phalenā, where the *gopīs* showcased their loving devotion. It is said that by going there, one can understand these truths and thereby gain inspiration for realizing them in one's own life.

"During the festival of relishing the nectar from the lotus feet of the Supreme Lord, drops of nectar fall from the mouth of the bees as they hum the glories of the Lord in an intoxicated mood. For my own supreme benefit, I have carefully gathered some of those drops of nectar herein. In this way, I serve the dust of the feet of those great devotees again and again."
