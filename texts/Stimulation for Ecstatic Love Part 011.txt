""# Stimulation for Ecstatic Love - Part 11

Welcome back once again to our ongoing series on the glories of our most beloved Śrī Vṛndāvana, *viṣṇu-pādāya kṛṣṇa-preṣṭhāya śrīmatī-bhaktivedānta-svāmin iti nāmine namaste sārasvate deve*. So, we are continuing with our mini-series on "Stimulation for Ecstatic Love," and this will be Part 11. We have been discussing different items and articles that are part of Kṛṣṇa’s Vṛndāvana pastimes which invoke love for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa. Last week, we discussed in some detail the role of flowers, and today we’ll continue with that same theme.

Flowers have always played an integral role in Vedic culture. For example, there’s the ceremony of *puṣpāñjali*. *Puṣpāñjali* is made up of two words: *puṣpa*, meaning flowers, and *añjali*, which means an offering made with folded hands—or hands cupped like this—to show respect to a distinguished personality like the Supreme Lord, the spiritual master, or a saintly person. Actually, I was reading that in ancient times, wealthy persons would offer not flowers, but palmfuls of jewels in such ceremonies; it was called *ratnāñjali*. But in this age, that’s obviously not possible, so we offer nice flowers, and that’s appropriate. We mentioned last week that Śrīla Prabhupāda once said that flowers are the only opulence left in Kali-yuga.

The Vedas say that, first and foremost amongst deserving personalities to offer such worship and gratitude to, is the Supreme Lord, Śrī Kṛṣṇa. Somebody might ask, "Why should we be so grateful to the Supreme Lord?" Our answer, amongst many, is because He is providing us with the basic necessities of life, like the Earth we walk on, the air we breathe, the water we drink. So often, we take these things for granted. We should say that such things are not accidents of material nature; rather, they are gifts from the Lord, given to us so that we can perfectly execute the real mission of life, which, as we know, is self-realization culminating in love for the Divine Couple. We can express our gratitude for such amazing gifts by offering something so simple as a flower. But more important—or maybe it's better to say what’s most important—is the attitude in which we offer that flower. In other words, it’s the devotion, the love, the *bhakti* in which we offer the flower.

Kṛṣṇa Himself mentions this in *Bhagavad-gītā*, famous verse 9.26:
> "If one offers to Me with devotion a leaf, a flower, a fruit or even water, I will accept them."

It’s incredible, actually, that although He provides so much, we need only offer back a simple flower, or in some cases, a handful of water. Just like here in India, when people worship the sacred Ganges River, they simply offer a palmful of water back to the Ganges; Mother Ganges accepts. Or those who worship Sūrya-bhagavān, the Sun, they simply offer Him a candle, and He accepts if there’s devotion there. And again, it’s more the consciousness in which we offer the item than the item itself. In other words, a big offering made without devotion doesn’t match a small offering made with love.

I was thinking there’s a very nice example in the *Rāmāyaṇa*. We’ve all heard how Lord Rāmacandra was trying to build a bridge from India over to Laṅkā in order to rescue His dear wife, the Goddess of Fortune, Sītā-devī, who had been kidnapped by the evil Asura, Rāvaṇa. Now, Lord Rāma had an army of powerful monkeys and bears who were actually throwing huge boulders into the ocean to make the bridge. Now, seeing this, a small squirrel who was also a devotee of Rāma decided to help by kicking small little particles of sand into the ocean, but with great love and devotion. However, when Rāma’s devotee Hanuman noticed what she was doing, he approached her and asked, "Little squirrel, why are you putting such tiny pieces of sand into the water when we powerful monkeys and bears are throwing big boulders into the sea to construct the bridge? Your sand is so insignificant."

But just at that moment, Lord Rāma appeared, and He told His very dear devotee Hanuman that He actually appreciated the squirrel’s service as much as He did all the monkeys and bears' service, because she was doing it with *bhakti*, with devotion. That’s what counts. So then, it’s really amazing—Lord Rāma patted that little squirrel’s back like this with His fingers—His three fingers—patted the back like this, praising her efforts. And it’s said that’s why we see here in India that all the squirrels have three white lines going over their back like this; it’s the blessing of Lord Rāma on the little squirrel who had shown her love for Rāma by her tiny efforts imbued with *prema*. It’s all about love.

So, similarly in our lives, we may perform services to the Lord—some big and some small, like offering flowers to the Lord—but we must do it with as much devotion as we can muster. Then, it’s accepted.

Now, I was reading that the many varieties of flowers that grow in Vṛndāvana appear on all sorts of bushes and trees. Last week, we spoke extensively about the *kadamba* tree, which has an especially fragrant flower that Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa enjoy very much. Now, when we speak about these *kalpa-vṛkṣa* trees, these flowering trees, we understand that actually, they are all eternally liberated souls who have a very unique *rasa* with Kṛṣṇa. And what is that? A *rasa* of neutrality. But don’t think for a minute that such a *rasa* is less than the *rasas* of servitorship, friendship, parenthood, or conjugal love. No, the trees' *rasa* with Kṛṣṇa is just as pleasing to Him as any other *rasa*.

The *ācāryas* say that just as devotees in *dāsya*, *vātsalya*, *sakhya*, and *mādhurya-rasa* experience the eight symptoms of ecstatic love for Kṛṣṇa, so do those in the mood of neutrality, such as trees. And what is the stimulus for the ecstatic symptoms these trees sometimes exhibit? We could say, what is the cause? It’s very nice. The *ācāryas* say: number one, it’s just the simple fact that these trees are growing in the *cintāmaṇi* soil of Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma, in the *braja-reṇu*. That’s how their ecstasy comes: just from the fact that they’re connected to the soil, to the *cintāmaṇi* ground, the dust of Vraja. And we’re so fortunate that when we come to Vṛndāvana, we walk in that same dust. That’s why generally you see people walk barefoot in Vṛndāvana.

Now, that fact alone, that they’re growing in the *cintāmaṇi*-dhāma, causes ecstatic feelings to manifest within these trees' hearts. What’s more, such trees—actually, their good fortune is that they get to witness the very confidential loving pastimes of Kṛṣṇa with Rādhā and the *gopīs*, because these pastimes are often performed more or less hidden from everyone else in the *kuñjas*, or the groves of Vṛndāvana. The *kuñja* means with those bushes and trees. So there they are as trees, but they’re witnessing these very sweet pastimes of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, which not all devotees get to see.

Now, another proof that such trees know well of the confidential pastimes of Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa is that in the *Gaura-gaṇoddeśa-dīpikā* by Śrīla Kavi-karṇapūra, he writes that Mādhavendra Purī, who introduced the concept of *mādhurya-rasa* a little before the appearance of Lord Caitanya, is a *kalpa-vṛkṣa*, a desire tree in the spiritual world. Now, Śrīla Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura confirms the same thing in his *Gaura-gaṇa-svarūpa-tattva-candrikā*. I found this verse, 26; he writes:

> "Mādhavendra Purī was previously Lord Hari’s desire tree in Vṛndāvana."

There you go. Mādhavendra Purī was previously Lord Hari’s desire tree in Vṛndāvana. Now, a side note here: I know that—well, I read that—that desire tree, Mādhavendra Purī, is situated in Nidhivana in Vraja. And anybody will tell you that’s a very special place; Nidhivana, Kṛṣṇa has pastimes and *rāsa*, etc., desire trees.

Now, *Govinda-līlāmṛta* describes that Kṛṣṇa feels very endeared to these flowering trees, and sometimes He even speaks to them. He says, "Oh, my dear friends, the trees! Oh, wise ones, is everything all right with you today? Did last night’s gentle rain quench your thirst and nourish your flowers and fruits?" And just so that we can understand how exalted these tree personalities are, the *ācāryas* say that in the darkest night, the trees often faint because they’re unable to see Kṛṣṇa anywhere. Wow! But later on, when they hear the soothing sound of Kṛṣṇa’s voice or the celestial music that flows from His flute, they come back to life and instantly blossom into so many flowers and leaves. Then they shake their leaves in ecstasy, or they shed honey tears and honor Kṛṣṇa with the smile of their blossoms and their swaying in happy breezes. And they’re swaying in happy breezes—again, this is the only poetry we want to hear, this type of poetry. *Hari-kṛṣṇa*.

And it’s described these trees are so fortunate—why, what’s the great fortune, or special fortune? They get to touch Kṛṣṇa. These devotees get to touch Kṛṣṇa. How so? Because as He passes by them in the Vṛndāvana forest, they bend down and touch His feet with the tips of their branches and their blossoming flowers. Kṛṣṇa Prabhupāda actually describes this in *Kṛṣṇa Book*; he describes that when Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma are walking through the forest, the trees bow down to offer obeisances, offer their fruits and their flowers to Kṛṣṇa and Balarāma, and they get the touch of the Supreme Lord. It’s described that by touching the lotus feet of Kṛṣṇa, they experienced a heightened—how’s it put?—a heightened state of ecstasy, which causes a sap from within their trunks to drip from their pores, which muddies the ground everywhere. However, after Kṛṣṇa passes by and leads them behind, they lament the fact that they’re not able to follow Him because they’re trees, and they’re rooted to the ground. One devotee puts it very poetically; he writes:

> "Thus the trees decry their lamentable state with inspired sentiments born of separation."

And in a mood of extreme humility, which is always demonstrated by advanced devotees of the Lord, the trees discuss amongst themselves as follows: "Oh, we’re simply rooted in the mode of ignorance. We must have previously committed some offenses because we can’t follow Kṛṣṇa." Of course, they’re all liberated souls, or rather *nitya-siddhas*, eternally liberated souls, so there’s no question of any offense or sin on their part, but this is their humility. However, everything has a good ending in Kṛṣṇa consciousness. It’s described that before Kṛṣṇa gets too far away, one of their great friends comes to their rescue. And who’s that great friend? Who’s the friend of the trees? It’s the wind. For the wind blows very strongly at that time and helps them to bow down and stretch out a great distance, and again touch Kṛṣṇa before He gets too far away.

In order to remove any doubts that someone may have about these trees, I found a beautiful verse in *Vraja-rīti-cintāmaṇi* by Viśvanātha Cakravartī Ṭhākura, it’s 2.23, where he nicely glorifies these trees. He writes:

> "These splendid trees of Vrajabhūmi are perfectly spiritual in nature; they’re full of all potencies, and they are just like incarnations of the Lord."

Wow, amazing feature. Another amazing feature of these flowering trees is that they yield ripe, juicy fruits. It’s also said the wives of these trees appear as vines wrapped around the trees and moisten the ground with their joyful tears of honey, and they perfume the air with pollen as they embrace their husbands, the trees. That these vines are the wives of the trees is actually confirmed by Śrī Prabodhānanda Sarasvatī in his *Vṛndāvana-mahimāmṛta*. I found this in *Śataka* 2, text 11; he writes:

> "With their soft flower-petal hands, Śrī Śrī Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa carefully watered the trees and vines from the time of their first sprouting. They nourished them and made them grow, and at the proper time, married each vine, or every vine, to an appropriate tree. And when they saw new flowers beginning to blossom, they became delighted and spoke many playful, joking words. Let us bow down and offer obeisances to these trees and their wives, the vines in the Vṛndāvana forests."

*Hari-kṛṣṇa*.

What, he also describes other kinds of flowering trees in Vṛndāvana, like, really special—he said these other flowering trees, their trunks are made of gold. This is all in Vṛndāvana, actually. He writes a lot about trees. He writes: their branches are made of blue opals, their twigs are made of sapphires, and their leaves are made of emeralds and rubies, and their flowers—their flowers are all crystals. And when these trees produce fruits, their fruits are all valuable gems. All glories to the flowering trees of Vraja.

Now, the ultimate proof that these flowering trees are super, super special in their *rasa* of neutrality is Kṛṣṇa’s own praise of them. In *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 10.22.31, we hear how Kṛṣṇa once famously said to His cowherd friends: "O Stoka-kṛṣṇa, O Śrīdāmā, Subala, Arjuna, O Viśāla, Vṛṣabhadeva, Ojasvī, and Devaprastha, just see these greatly fortunate trees whose lives are completely dedicated to the benefit of others. Even while tolerating the wind, rain, heat, and snow, they protect us from these elements." He’s specifically talking about Vṛndāvana trees, but trees on this Earth also, they render nice service to mankind. And then, in *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam* 10.22.33, Kṛṣṇa says: "Just see how they are maintaining every living entity. Their birth is successful; the behavior is just like that of great personalities, for anyone who asks anything from a tree never goes away disappointed." And that’s especially true with *kalpa-vṛkṣa* trees, because anything that you need in the service of Kṛṣṇa, a *kalpa-vṛkṣa* tree will provide. Not just fruits or flowers, but if you need utensils for cooking, the *kalpa-vṛkṣa* tree can give; you need a gift for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, it will give; you need vegetables, it’ll give. It’s *kalpa-vṛkṣa*—they’re there to serve, like all devotees.

Now, in his song *Vibhāvarī Śeṣa*, from his work *Kalyāṇa-kalpataru*, Śrīla Bhaktivinoda Ṭhākura, he glorifies the *kadamba* tree and its flowers in verse 6. Many devotees know that that song. In verse 6: *yośodā-nandana, kaṁsa-niṣūdana, nikuñja-rāsa-vilāsī, kadamba-kānana, rāsa-parāyaṇa, vṛndāvana-vipina-nivāsī*. Now, Śrīla Prabhupāda has given a very beautiful translation and commentary of that particular verse from that song. Śrīla Prabhupāda translates it, and he gives a commentary; it’s very nice. He writes: "*Yośodā-nandana*—He is very much celebrated as the son of Mother Yaśodā; *kaṁsa-niṣūdana*—the killer of Kaṁsa; and *nikuñja-rāsa-vilāsī*—He used to dance the *rāsa* dance in the *nikuñja*. There are many *kadamba* trees—*kadamba* is a kind of flower that is especially grown in Vṛndāvana, very fragrant and beautiful," Prabhupāda writes, "solid flower, round; He used to enjoy the *rāsa* dance underneath the tree of this *kadamba*."

Now, for some *līlās*, or *līlā*, there’s a very nice pastime by one of my favorite poets, Lalita Kiśora, whom we often quote. As we’ve mentioned before, he was a contemporary poet of Śrīla Narottama dāsa Ṭhākura; he was from the village of Uṭī-gāon near Varṣāṇā, Lalita’s village, and he was initiated in the line of Śrī Gadādhara of Pañca-tattva. One time, we mentioned his beautiful poetry, still sung today in both Rādhā-Ramaṇa Temple in Vraja and Rādhā-Govindajī’s temple in Jaipur. Now, in one of his poems, it’s entitled—in Braja-bhāṣā, *Rāja Karo*. The translation is, "May you rule age after age." He writes about a beautiful pastime where—how can I put it?—a flowering *kadamba* tree takes center stage.

He writes: once in a playful mood—you know, playful mood—Kṛṣṇa and His cowherd friends, they had—they played a game where Kṛṣṇa became the King of Vraja, and He assigned Madhumaṅgala to become His chief minister, and Śrīdāmā, he pretended to be the royal advisor, and the cowherd boy Viśāla, he became the holder of the whisk tail fan and the royal umbrella. Kṛṣṇa’s government! In their game, they brought Stoka-kṛṣṇa, one of the cowherd boys, in front of Kṛṣṇa and said, "My dear Lord, this farmer stole the cows of Subala and painted them like his own cows." So Kṛṣṇa is like the king now; He said, "Then arrest him and give all the cows back to Subala."

Now, the plot—by Madhumaṅgala’s arrangement—just at that moment, Śrī Rādhikā and a large procession of Her *gopīs*—I mean, you could say thousands—came by, and they were accompanied by many musical instruments, and the *gopīs*, all these *gopīs*, were singing very loudly. So seeing this, Madhumaṅgala became very upset, and he ran up to them and he said, "Stop this nonsense, please, girls! Our King is attending His royal court duties; now do not disturb Him." So hearing this threat, Lalitā-sakhī, she came forward and she pushed Madu back—she’s very fiery—she pushed him back, and she said, "No king rules this land! Śrī Rādhā is the Queen of Vraja, and Her authority is absolute. Therefore, you cannot stop us from praising Her; you cannot stop us from praising Her!"

Now, noticing this disturbance, Kṛṣṇa came forward and He said, "What is happening, my dear Minister Madhumaṅgala?" Madu said, "Kṛṣṇa, these ignorant cowherd girls are saying that You are not the King of this land, and that their friend, Śrī Rādhā—this girl—is the only ruling authority; that’s Queen!" [They] started laughing. So Kṛṣṇa said very seriously, "Then call for My advisor, Śrīdāmā; he will give us the final verdict on this." So Madhumaṅgala called for Śrīdāmā, who came forward and declared that the one who wins the game of *bāgh-bakrī* will be the ruling authority of Vraja.

*Bāgh-bakrī*—I looked it up, actually; it’s an ancient game still played today all over India, actually. It’s played on a wooden board, and in the game, one player controls four tigers, and the other player controls 20 goats. They have little figurines like that. And in the game, the tigers, you know, they hunt the goats, and the goats attempt to block the tigers' movements. This game ends only when the tigers have captured all the goats, or if the goats are successful at entrapping all the tigers; you know, one side wins, one side loses. So as they started preparing the game, Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa and all the *gopīs* and cowherd boys, they sat under the shadow of a beautifully flowering *kadamba* tree, where many flowers had actually fallen, and the girls, they made a very comfortable *āsana* for the Divine Couple. The poet—our poet—describes the *āsana* as "soft as a moving rain cloud." They’re just in their mind’s eye, there’s this cushion of *kadamba* flowers, and it’s as soft as a moving rain cloud.

So then, Śrīdāmā, he placed the play board on a wooden table, and at that point, Madhumaṅgala said, "Oh, you poor *gopīs*, don’t you see? There’s only one tiger, which is Kṛṣṇa, and all the goats are you poor girls who are fighting uselessly to trap our undefeated King Kṛṣṇa!" So then, Lalitā became fiery again; she retorted, "Don’t you know, Madu, that in this area of Vraja-maṇḍala, tigers hide in caves in fear of foxes like me? Tigers hiding in caves in fear of foxes like me!" So Madu kind of backed off.

So at that point, the game began in earnest, as Lalitā, representing the side of Śrī Rādhikā and the *gopīs*, used all kinds of tricks to defeat Kṛṣṇa—He was playing on the other side. So like this, to make a long story short, for some time the game went back and forth, back and forth, but eventually, it became obvious that the cowherd boys were winning, the boys were winning. Now, of course, we know that with Her pure love, She controls Kṛṣṇa, and in that sense, She’s undefeatable. However, the nature of loving relationships is that lovers often yield to the desires and wishes of their beloveds. Now, at that point, the *gopīs*—all the *gopīs*, you know, there’s thousands—they called out to the Divine Couple, "You, *rāsa karo*, may you rule age after age."

Now, one *ācārya*, he places the question why the cowherd girls were calling out *karo*—that Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa may rule together—in Śrī Vṛndāvana. So, he answers the question by saying the *gopīs* never like to accept that Śrī Rādhā is ever defeated by Kṛṣṇa. Therefore, in calling out *karo*, they meant, "May you both rule age after age." They included Rādhārāṇī and Kṛṣṇa.
