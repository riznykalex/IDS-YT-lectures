Hare Krishna, devotees.

Today we've come to Sri Khanda, a simple Bengali village that became one of the holiest places in Gaudiya Vaishnava history. It's not famous for huge temples or palaces; it's famous for devotees. Here live Mukunda Das, his son Raghunandan Thakur, Narahari Sarkar Thakur, Chiranjeev, Sulochana, and others who filled this village with *kirtan* and prayer.

Chaitanya Mahaprabhu calls them great branches of Mahaprabhu's mercy tree, spreading the fruits and flowers of love of God everywhere. When we visit these places, our purpose is not sightseeing but purification, to understand the qualities that made Mahaprabhu personally come here.

Sri Khanda shows us what a Vaishnava community truly is: friendship, humility, and *kirtan* centered on love for Krishna.

Among the residents, one stands out: Narahari Sarkar Thakur. Long before the great biographies were written, Narahari composed songs describing Lord Chaitanya's pastimes. Mahaprabhu was shy when devotees called Him Krishna, but at the same time, He would happily listen to the songs of Narahari Thakur.

One famous song, said to have been written here after the first meeting between Narahari and Chaitanya Mahaprabhu, reveals the mystery of Goranga. The translation is that Narahari was saying, "Where is your peacock feather?

Where is your yellow garment?

Where is your flute?

Your eyes are the same, your beauty is the same. Yet you're now wrapped in Radharani's love." When Narahari saw this, he became mad in divine love, unable to distinguish whether he was seeing Krishna or Goranga.

That is Sri Khanda's gift: to see Vrindavan inside Navadvip, to realize that the Lord appears again out of His compassion for His devotees. This place also teaches how devotees relate to one another. Once Mahaprabhu asked Mukunda, "Who was your father?

You or your son Raghunandan?" Mukunda immediately answered, "Raghunandan is the father, and I'm the son, because through him I've learned devotion to Krishna." In that moment, Mahaprabhu smiled.

This is the spirit of Sri Khanda. Seniority is measured not by age but by *bhakti*. The most famous pastime here is that of young Raghunandan and Gopinath. One day, Mukunda had to go out and told his little boy, "Offer lunch to the Deity." Raghunandan stood before Gopinath and prayed, "My Lord, my father told me to offer this food.

Please accept it." But when he came back, the plate was still full. Again, he pleaded, crying, "Please eat, my Lord." Finally, Gopinath ate everything. Later, Mukunda hid behind the altar and watched the Deity take a *ladu* from the plate.

Overwhelmed, he embraced his son and said, "Now I see you are the father; I am the son." That half-eaten *ladu* still rests in Gopinath's hand, proof that the Lord responds to simple faith. Sri Khanda is also the place where Abhiram Thakur met Raghunandan.

Abhiram was so pure that false deities would crack if he bowed before them. Hearing of Raghunandan's devotion, he came here longing to see him. They met at the gate, embraced, and their *kirtan* became so ecstatic that one of Raghunandan's ankle bells flew off and landed several kilometers away in a pond the devotees still call new pond.

Such is the power of meeting between pure devotees; their affection shakes the universe. Also nearby is a place called Mati Kund, the honey pond. When Nityananda Prabhu came here with Mahaprabhu, He told Narahari, "Hey, I'm thirsty. Bring me some honey." Narahari prayed, drew water from the pond, and by the Lord's will, that water turned to honey.

Even the elements here respond to devotional love. Sri Khanda is also home to Lochandas, the disciple of Narahari, who wrote the Chaitanya Mangal while sitting beneath a banyan tree here. Through his poetry, Mahaprabhu's mercy continues to reach us today.

But these stories are not just meant to inspire; they are meant to instruct. From Mukunda, we learn humility, to keep sacred experiences private and never seek recognition. From Raghunandan, we learn simplicity and faith that a child's prayer can move the Lord.

From Narahari, we learn spiritual vision to recognize Krishna in the form of Goranga.

From Abhiram, we learn purity and courage. And from Lochan, we learn dedication to glorify the Lord through writing and *kirtan*. Together, they show that Sri Khanda is not just a place; it's a living example of how devotees live together in love and respect.

The Bhagavata Purana states that ordinary friendship begins with a few steps, but friendship among saintly people deepens at every step. That's the essence of this *parikrama*: to walk together, serve together, and let friendship become devotion. Shrila Bhakti Swami wrote that when you go on *parikrama*, don't try to see the dawn with your eyes; see through the eyes of a *sadhu* and Guru.

If we walk with that mood, the Dharma will reveal itself. So, as we leave Sri Khanda, let's carry its mood: the humility of Mukunda, the faith of Raghunandan, the insight of Narahari, and the joy of *kirtan* that binds the devotees together.

With such love, appears Mahaprabhu Himself. Sri Khanda Dhama ki Jay!

Chaitanya Mahaprabhu ki Jay!

Goranga!

Hari Hari!
