"Yes, please sit back and calm down a little from the lobby inspection. Today we will have Krishna-katha about the main street, and Prahlad will share the stories you were looking for. As you know, this area is called Varshānā; it has its own very interesting history. It is described that at one time, Lord Brahmā decided to perform austerities with the goal of becoming a hill on which Rādhā-Kṛṣṇa and the gopīs would walk while performing their divine pastimes. This is an esoteric topic because the desire to become a hill in itself is unusual, but this was his desire. His whole idea was to become a hill and get the dust from the divine feet of Rādhārāṇī for his head. As we know from the beginning, the guru of our sampradāya, Lord Brahmā, has four heads, and in this regard, Lord Brahmā appeared in this area as a mountain with four peaks.

The top of this range is known as the great peaks. In due course, Vṛṣabhānu Mahārāja, Rādhārāṇī’s father, decided to build a palace for his family, which, of course, included Rādhārāṇī. A panorama of this area is now opening for you. You see two palaces: the palace on the left is the Jaipur Palace, but this is not the main attraction. It is more important to look to the right, forward—there is Rādhārāṇī’s palace. This area was named Varshānā in honor of Rādhārāṇī, since Varshānā is one of Rādhārāṇī’s names, and it signifies rainfall or bliss. Rādhārāṇī’s childhood games took place here with her father and mother. As many of you know, Rādhārāṇī has a brother, Śrīdāmā, and a younger sister named Anaṅga-mañjarī. Kṛṣṇa spent His childhood playing games here; Rādhārāṇī’s childhood games took place in the valley, and Her teenage games took place here at the top.

According to the ācāryas, Rādhārāṇī’s palace included 30 separate rooms on each floor. The walls were marble, all the doors and gates were golden, and all the curtains were embroidered with beautiful white pearls. This palace is distinguished not only by its beauty but also by its special spirit. Unlike Yāvaṭ, in which the mood is somewhat sad because Rādhārāṇī was limited in her freedom there, being married, here the atmosphere is very joyful and bright. According to Rūpa Gosvāmī, Rādhārāṇī’s laughter here at the top inspires and makes even the oldest of Vṛṣabhānu Mahārāja’s guests smile. The palace servants are inspired by a special spirit of bliss in their service. Rūpa Gosvāmī provides a poetic description: the sound of Rādhārāṇī’s laughter is mixed with the ringing of the ankle bells of Her sakhīs, as well as the sounds of the vīṇā. This combined sound surpasses in beauty even the sound of Kṛṣṇa’s flute.

Therefore, one can tune in to the fact that this area is the place of Rādhārāṇī’s childhood and youth games, in which She, along with Her cowherd friends, walked around the neighborhood. Please be aware that this particular hill on which we are now sitting is one of the heads of Lord Brahmā. This place is the first of four hills, and this particular one is called Vilāsa-giri. The word *vilāsa* means "pastimes," so this is the place for the pastimes of Rādhārāṇī.

In the end, I would like to tell you that once, in the midst of the heat in the month of July, Rādhārāṇī and Kṛṣṇa got into a very sharp argument. As a result, Rādhārāṇī was angry at Kṛṣṇa. When Rādhārāṇī gets angry, Kṛṣṇa has to calm Her down by giving various gifts and saying sweet words. However, in this particular case, She wanted neither gifts nor sweet words; She just came here, having secluded Herself with Her girlfriends. Kṛṣṇa tried to climb this slope to reach Rādhārāṇī to calm Her down, but the gopīs interfered and did not allow Him to ascend, specifically Lalitā-devī, who is very protective. She said to Kṛṣṇa, "Rādhārāṇī does not want to see You, hear You, or speak to You at all." 

Kṛṣṇa then sat down surrounded by His cowherd friends and began to think out loud about what to do. He came up with a plan: "I will dress as one of the girls, and I will try to disguise Myself so that none of these girls will guess who I really am." The boys were skeptical, but Kṛṣṇa was very persistent. He said, "Dress Me like a girl," and they had to do it. They collected kajal, decorations, and various sarīs. When He was dressed, He was beautiful. Kṛṣṇa then approached the shepherdesses guarding the hill and said in a very gentle, feminine voice, "I am a local girl who came from a nearby village." In this way, He managed to get to Rādhārāṇī, and He began to sing songs of praise—after all, who can better glorify Rādhārāṇī than Kṛṣṇa Himself?

The singing reached the ears of Rādhārāṇī, and She was enchanted. She asked, "Who is this new girl?" However, Lalitā, who is always on the alert, said, "The singing is actually nice, but something is not right here. If you listen, the voice is not very feminine, and look at how this girl is dressed." As Kṛṣṇa continued His singing, Lalitā began to look at Him point-blank. Kṛṣṇa began to sweat from excitement. Lalitā whispered in Rādhārāṇī’s ear, "Let’s investigate." The sakhīs withdrew the fabrics, and after some discussion, they worked out a plan. Rādhārāṇī stopped Kṛṣṇa’s singing and told Him, "My dear mañjarī, I really like your singing, so I would like to reward you. I will hug you."

Kṛṣṇa interrupted His singing and said, "Yes, yes, by the way, with what would you like to reward me?" The gopīs then surrounded Him, and Rādhārāṇī commanded, "Girls, let's change her clothes; these clothes look a bit careless." They began taking off the headscarf and hairpins, and when they reached the hair, two huge peacock feathers fell out with a crash. The gopīs began to laugh, and Kṛṣṇa’s complexion turned purple despite His inherent blueness. Finally, Rādhārāṇī, seeing this state of Kṛṣṇa, took pity on Him and decided to forgive Him. She asked the sakhīs to offer Him normal clothes. They dressed Him properly, and together with the sakhīs, He went onto the swing that is here. The gopīs helped swing the swing, and thus began the famous Jhūlan-yātrā festival, which takes place in different places in Vṛndāvana, but the original festival was held here.

Such is the history of this place, which is one of the heads of Brahmā that appeared to fulfill his wish to receive the dust of Rādhārāṇī’s feet. That is the hill before us, and the beautiful palace in which Rādhārāṇī lived is at the top. Another palace is located a little lower; it is called the Jaipur Palace. We often hear about the strength and courage of the nobility of the Indian kings, and one of these kings came here and did not really like the architectural style of Rādhārāṇī’s palace, so he decided to build an alternative palace. But in the end, most refused to change its location, and the Jaipur Palace remained empty. 

Here is my short introduction to get you in the mood for your stay in Varshānā. Let’s head to these wonderful places and absorb their mood. We will head along local paths to Rādhārāṇī’s palace, we will bow to the Deities who are there, and then we will go down to the lake where Rādhārāṇī once washed Her hands. We will have lunch there and enjoy every moment of your stay here on Varshānā.""
