"### An Iron Pot Full of Holes – Kārtika Parikramā 2025

If we gave the *nāma-pala* more time, which we will definitely do later, it would be possible to describe many important and significant points in this part of Navadvīpa. This morning, she said that she could talk for days, weeks, and months about the *rāsavatī-purāṇa* of this holy place. It is the same here, especially in this holy place. The fame of this place is limitless, but the organizers—and I myself am not an organizer, I only help behind the scenes—were busy. Rasika-śiromaṇi, she is the main organizer, and she asked me to tell about the wonderful devotee, Kolavecha Śrīdhara.

It is said that a person who goes to a holy place and hears there about a holy person, or hears from a holy person, can receive the complete benefit. Therefore, I feel very honored to have the opportunity to speak about Kolavecha Śrīdhara today. Nearby, not far from here—actually, if you go through the path—unfortunately, the ground there has been washed away now, so you cannot drive there, especially for our group. That is why we are here. There is a famous place nearby called Śrīdhara-aṅgana. When you think that a dwelling or a monastery implies a certain building with everything necessary for living, you will be surprised to see that, for example, 500 years ago, walking along this path, you would have seen just an abandoned hut.

Śrīdhara was famous then, and he is still famous today. With the prosperity of the golden age that is predicted, the next 10,000 years will be glorious for Śrīdhara. There is a deep meaning to the fact that he lived in such an abandoned hut and led such a renounced lifestyle. In order to understand and go directly to the discussion of this exalted personality, we will take shelter of Kavi-karṇapūra, directly in his *Gaura-gaṇoddeśa-dīpikā*, text 140. Today, this is very important for us.

He writes: "He who was Kusumāsuva in Vraja, who caused Kṛṣṇa to laugh, became a learned brāhmaṇa, known as Kolavecha Śrīdhara." This is a transcendental "who is who." When I was 11 years old, I studied something similar. My father was quite a literary, well-read person, and he brought me a book that described "who is who," featuring American politicians, presidents, and generals from the past 300 years. Now, who cares about them? But for us, this "who is who" is expressed directly in the beautiful book of Kavi-karṇapūra, where he describes those personalities who were originally in the spiritual world with Kṛṣṇa. Five hundred years ago, in the Lord's pastimes, Kolavecha Śrīdhara is described as a shepherd named Kusumāsuva. A small detail is mentioned about him, but as they say, love is revealed in nuances. He was the one who made Kṛṣṇa laugh.

We are lucky that we also have information from the *Śrī Caitanya-bhāgavata*, Ādi-khaṇḍa 10.67, which says that Śrīdhara, one of the trees of the Lord, traded in banana tree bark. He was a very close servant of the Lord. Many times, the Lord played a joke on him. You can see how this great soul, this mahātmā, came to the pastimes of Caitanya Mahāprabhu to participate in them. He is *nitya-siddha*, eternally liberated. We are all *ātmās*, but not yet *mahātmās*. This exalted personality, a *nitya-siddha*, came by the Lord’s wish as Kolavecha Śrīdhara, a poor brāhmaṇa who lived here in Māyāpura. He was so poor that he earned his living by selling bowls and plates made of banana leaves, as well as bananas, banana flowers, and the plant's pulp.

It is a very interesting story that this exalted personality not only sold cups and plates but also bananas and flowers. It is interesting to note that he did not have much income, but nevertheless, he spent 50% of his daily earnings on the worship of Mother Gaṅgā to collect the necessary paraphernalia for *pūjā*, and whatever was left, he somehow lived on. He is such an important personality that he is mentioned in the *Śrī Caitanya-bhāgavata*. Would you like to hear how great the fortune of the devotee Kolavecha is? Lord Brahmā and Lord Śiva shed tears upon seeing his greatness. Lord Kṛṣṇa cannot be attained by any amount of wealth, followers, or scholarship. The *Caitanya-bhāgavata* explains that He is subject only to pure devotion, and Śrīla Prabhupāda, in his commentary to *Caitanya-bhāgavata* 1.5.17, makes this generalization. For us, the nature of this pastime becomes very clear. This knowledge is initially hidden, but by the grace of the teachers and the sacred scriptures, this glory is revealed to us, giving us the peace of *śānti*.

I remember when I was 14 years old, a friend of mine was into photography, and we went to a botanical garden to capture the opening of a beautiful lotus flower. We sat there for hours, waiting for it to bloom. At every stage, he recorded this unfolding. It is like the manifestation of a beautiful personality when we listen. Prabhupāda writes: "Kolavecha Śrīdhara is an exemplary example of how a poor person, lacking material wealth, can become the most exalted devotee." For most of the world, that is good news. We are all, in some way, like little Śrīdhara, who barely managed to get enough money for a ticket to India. We all have to suffer to earn money, and all this gloss, wealth, and fame are available to only 1% of the population. But here he is—poor, yet pure and devoted. 

Someone may ask: "Why did Kṛṣṇa place him in such an extraordinary position?" We have an answer: different devotees took birth to help the Lord in different ways in different conditions. Those pure devotees who came from the spiritual world to accompany Caitanya Mahāprabhu—some were poor, some were rich, some simple, some educated, some great kings, some simple servants—were all exalted devotees because they gave their life and soul to the service of Caitanya Mahāprabhu. They took birth in these different positions to serve as examples for people who could relate to them.

As we heard today from Mahārāja about what a scholar Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura was, while his spiritual teacher (Gaura-kiśora dāsa Bābājī) could not even read properly. You can either follow if, like me, you are not so learned, or be part of the Bhaktivedanta Book Trust—whoever you are, you can be a devotee. The true indicator is the tears that Bābājī shed while chanting the holy names. If you are simple, you can take an example from the uneducated Gaura-kiśora dāsa Bābājī; if you are more learned, you can take an example directly from Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura. But even if someone is not a scholar, it is very important that he is sincere. Śrīla Prabhupāda built a house in which everyone can live.

How poor was Śrīdhara? He lived in a hut with gaps in the roof. When it rained, everything inside flooded. Imagine, I am sitting here with a silver cup, a gift, while Kolavecha Śrīdhara had a metal glass full of holes, so when he tried to drink, he had to fill it and drink quickly. That is how poor he was. Despite spending the entire day selling banana leaf utensils, he stayed up almost all night loudly chanting Hare Kṛṣṇa with great enthusiasm. It was the best, most important moment of his day. 

For example, Jayapatākā Mahārāja used to chant very loudly in Montreal. The devotees sitting there could not hear themselves chanting, so they complained to Śrīla Prabhupāda. Prabhupāda sent him to the mountains—there was a hill just nearby—so he could repeat the holy names there. The neighbors who heard Śrīdhara chanting all night assumed that because he was so poor, the loud noise from his hut was a painful cry of hunger. They thought he could not sleep because of hunger.

In the morning, Śrīdhara performed his ablutions and rituals, went to his banana plantation, collected leaves and flowers, and opened his small stall to sell them. Do not feel sorry for Kolavecha Śrīdhara, because one of his regular customers was Nimāi Paṇḍita—Caitanya Mahāprabhu. Mahāprabhu regularly bought bananas from him, but the process was very unusual; He argued with him every time about the price and ended up paying only half. They would pull the bunch of bananas back and forth, arguing. At some point, Mahāprabhu would say, "Are you kidding? You should give them to Me for free!"

One might wonder how Mahāprabhu had the audacity to ask a poor fellow to give things away for free. But reading the *Caitanya-bhāgavata* helps. Mahāprabhu would say, "Kolavecha Śrīdhara, every day you buy things to offer to Mother Gaṅgā. Why don't you just give something to Me without taking payment? Don't you know that I am the Father of that Gaṅgā you worship?" Poor Śrīdhara had to endure these disputes every day. Yet, the scripture states that the Lord ate with great satisfaction from the banana leaves given to Him by Śrīdhara. The Lord accepts everything a devotee offers with *bhakti*, but rejects even the most sumptuous dishes given by a non-devotee.

One day, the Lord approached the counter and said, "Kolavecha Śrīdhara, why are you so mischievous? You worship the goddess of fortune, Lakṣmīdevī, yet you are always short of money for food and clothing. Your hut is falling apart, while those who worship the bloodthirsty Kālī seem to have an abundance of material wealth. How can you be satisfied with such worship?" 

Śrīdhara answered beautifully: "Sir, a bird makes its nest on a tree and spends time wandering in search of food. A king lives in a palace and spends his day making plans for sensual pleasure. But in reality, there is no difference in the amount of pleasure experienced by both. Therefore, I have no desire for sensual pleasures. My pleasure comes from serving the Supreme Personality of Godhead." 

The Lord smiled and said, "Yes, although you appear poor on the outside, you are actually a rich man. True wealth is love for the Supreme Lord." And He added, "I will reveal to the whole world that the devotee is worthy of all this wealth."

The important ingredient is *bhakti*. In the Seventh Canto of the *Śrīmad-Bhāgavatam*, it is said: "One may possess wealth, aristocratic family, beauty, austerity, education, expertise, luster, influence, physical strength, or diligence, but I think that even with all these qualifications, one cannot satisfy the Supreme Personality of Godhead. The Lord can be pleased only by devotional service."

This is also expressed in the *Gajendra-stuti* prayers, which are my favorites: "Where was the piety of the hunter Dhruva? Where was the birthplace of Dhruva? Where was Gajendra's knowledge? Where was the beauty of Kubjā? Where was Sudāmā's wealth? Where was Vidura's noble birth? They had none of that, but the Lord accepted them because Lord Mādhava is satisfied only with devotional service."

Later, when the Lord revealed His form as the Supreme Personality of Godhead at the house of Śrīvāsa Ṭhākura, He called for Kolavecha Śrīdhara. When Śrīdhara saw the divine form of the Lord, he fell unconscious. When the Lord "woke him" (as this was a transcendental manifestation), He wanted to offer him a blessing. He offered to end Śrīdhara's poverty and grant him eight mystical powers. But Śrīdhara said, "No, it is not necessary." 

Mahāprabhu insisted, but Śrīdhara answered: "My dear Lord, if You must give me something, let it be this: let that young brāhmaṇa who stole my banana leaves and bark be my Lord life after life. Let that brāhmaṇa who argued with me be my master, and may I forever serve His lotus feet." 

Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura comments: "No one can recognize the truths and qualities of a devotee by external analysis. No one should think that the ability to collect large sums of money is a sign of devotion, or that one who can gather a large number of followers is a better Vaiṣṇava. A servant of the Lord may have very little money, may not show signs of a large following, and may not have the talent for argument, but at heart, he is a great Vaiṣṇava."

Finally, when Mahāprabhu liberated Cānd Kazi, He was thirsty. He came to Kolavecha Śrīdhara's hut and drank water from his old, battered iron jug. Mahāprabhu wanted to show that water from the pitcher of a pure devotee, even if it is old and rusty, is like nectar to the Supreme Lord, whereas He ignores the water of a proud non-devotee.

The *Caitanya-bhāgavata* describes this poetry: "When the Lord saw Śrīdhara's dilapidated hut from afar, He laughed softly. He led a group of devotees along the path to Śrīdhara's hut, where He noticed an iron water jug full of holes... and the Lord drank the water greedily." 

Always loving His devotees, this act filled the Lord with feelings of love, and tears flowed from His eyes like the streams of the Ganges. At the court of Śrīdhara, a wonderful *kīrtana* began, in which all the devotees cried, headed by Lord Nityānanda and Advaita Prabhu. So, we have a lot to learn from the pastimes of Śrīdhara. By the end of our *parikramā* in Māyāpura, we hope to acquire some of these qualities and thus become dear to Mahāprabhu. 

I conclude with a composition by Bhaktisiddhānta Sarasvatī Ṭhākura from his *Śrī Śrī Gauḍīya-stotra-śatakam*: "He gives a song with the names Hare, Kṛṣṇa, and Rāma. He destroys all obstacles like sorrow, delusion, greed, and suffering. He bestows devotional service to Lord Kṛṣṇa upon the many devotees who seek shelter of His lotus feet." 

Thank you, Śrīla Prabhupāda, for bringing us here and revealing this *dhāma*. Hare Kṛṣṇa.""
