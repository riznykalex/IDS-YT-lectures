"# Historical Vṛndāvana - Part 3

Dear devotees, welcome back once again to our ongoing series on the glories of our beloved Śrī Vṛndāvana. Today, I am addressing you from Spanish Fork, Utah, here in the United States, where we are having a big kīrtana-mela sadhu-sanga retreat. There are about 1,500 devotees with us; it is really ecstatic, Śrīmatī Bhaktivedānta Svāminī. 

So, we are continuing with our series on historical Vṛndāvana; this will be part three. In our first two lectures, we focused on two ladies from royal families. You may remember Surangi Mahārāṇī from Kashmir and Karmaiti Bai from Rajasthan, both of whom renounced this world at a young age to live in Vṛndāvana as sādhvīs—renunciates in devotion to Kṛṣṇa. Both of them inspired their followers to build very beautiful temples in glorification of Kṛṣṇa, thus adding to what I have been calling the charm of Vṛndāvana. 

Just yesterday, I read the following in a travel brochure encouraging people to visit Vṛndāvana, in part because of this architecture, and I quote: "Vṛndāvana is a wonderland of architectural design and artistic prowess. Amazing panel work and arches mark the decorations that adorn the entrances to its many temples, giving them an aura like palaces. The designs, colored walls, arched galleries, and carvings on the walls of the temples tell us much about the thoughts and emotions that are part of Vṛndāvana’s ancient history." Wow, come to Vṛndāvana and see the artwork, see the palaces, see the temples. 

So today, I would like to speak about a king; his name was Mahārāja Sāvant Singh, who also renounced his kingdom to live in Vṛndāvana. At the tender age of 20 years, he left the kingdom to go to Vṛndāvana; he was only 20 years old. He subsequently took initiation into the Vallabha-sampradāya, receiving the name Nāgarīdāsa, which means a servant of Rādhārāṇī. Actually, he became one of Vṛndāvana’s most famous poets. 

To begin with, we shouldn't take it lightly that a young king with a promising future of riches, glory, and fame could renounce it all to live in a forest—well, not just any forest, but to live in a forest as a humble pilgrim for the rest of his life. It is quite amazing. But Mahārāja Sāvant Singh did just that, and he explains why he did so in one of the poems he wrote later on, in his old age, after living in Vṛndāvana most of his life. It is very beautiful; he wrote:

"My true identity is that I am a resident of Braja. All obstacles associated with my birth cannot restrict me. This land is a place where we are surrounded with cows, and we live with them. We do not seek liberation at all. All acts of pilgrimage to Vṛndāvana are rewarded by the presence of the divine Yamunā River. The saints' association is our livelihood and means to achieve perfection, and the divine love of Śrī Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa in the forest groves gives us a reason to exist."

And he concludes at least that part of that famous book: "My kula-dev is Govardhana, my mantra is Śrī Kṛṣṇa’s name, and my worshipable deity is Śrī Rādhā."

As his name indicates, Mahārāja Sāvant Singh wasn't always a sādhu. He was born into a family of Kṣatriyas. Actually, I was reading that he was the eldest son of a king called Rāj Singh, and that king was the king of Kishangarh, and our hero was born on the twelfth day of the month of Pauṣa, which means December–January, in the year 1699. 

Kṛṣṇa mentions the qualities of a Kṣatriya in the *Bhagavad-gītā*, I think it is 18.43: heroism, power, determination, resourcefulness, courage in battle, generosity, and leadership are the natural qualities of work for the Kṣatriyas. He proved his worth as such a warrior at a very young age. I was actually really amazed, for one day in the year 1712, in New Delhi, I was reading that an elephant belonging to the army of the emperor went berserk in the city and began attacking everyone and everything around him. But as everyone fled, young Sāvant Singh, who was only 13 years old at the time, rushed forward with his sword raised high and confronted the elephant, so much shocking the wild elephant that it caused him to retreat, finally turn around, and run away in fear. Thirteen years old. 

And the same year, Sāvant Singh single-handedly killed a famous warrior king named Hada Singh of Bundi in a battle, a full-on battle, while defending his father's kingdom. Fighting in those days, it appears that men as young as 13 or 14 were engaged in massive battles. 

In 1714, while attending the royal court in Delhi, Sāvant Singh was attacked by a large poisonous snake as he was delivering a lecture to an audience of royalty, and he shocked everyone present by defending himself and eventually killing the snake while not missing a single word in his presentation. These are Kṣatriyas. 

Now, sometime later, a lion attacked the gośālā, the barn of his royal family. And when Sāvant Singh was informed of this, that the royal cows were being attacked by a lion, he ran to the gośālā, this time only armed with a knife, and he confronted the lion. A fierce fight ensued in which he eventually killed the lion, although he had many wounds. I was reading when people would notice him because of the wounds he had as a result of fighting that lion. But he killed the lion, and recently I saw an old painting of Sāvant Singh in the museum of the Vṛndāvana Research Institute where he is blissfully riding on a live tiger. 

Sometime later, Prince Sāvant Singh—because he is a prince still in his teens—was married to a beautiful princess from a nearby kingdom named Bani Thani. He is a teenager, but that's how things were done in those days. In due course of time, they had two sons together. Before he was 19, his father died; the king died. So, Sāvant Singh was crowned king in a very lavish coronation ceremony, and he became the ruler of two vast states in Rajasthan: Kishangarh, which I mentioned earlier, and Rupa Nagar. He became the king at 19. 

However, during that period, he became disillusioned with the many intrigues and politics of palace life. As a result, I read he began attending discourses given by visiting sādhus on Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa’s pastimes 5,000 years ago in the groves of Vṛndāvana. He found solace in that, and slowly the illusion of material life and the positive reality of Kṛṣṇa consciousness became fully apparent to him as a young man. The sentiments he felt at that time he also reminisced about in the book he wrote in his elder years, which I mentioned earlier, *Manorath-mañjarī*. He wrote, reminiscing when he was younger:

"Oh, when will I get to set foot on the sacred soil of Śrī Vṛndāvana? I will roll in the dust and place some upon my head and also into my mouth. When will Śrī Rādhā-Kṛṣṇa, who are splendidly strolling from one kuñja to another with their arms around each other, and whose swaying gait resembles that of an intoxicated elephant, come to me? Come to me!"

As the days, the weeks, and the months went by, our young king lost more and more interest in his royal duties. Finally, one day when his brother, his name was Bahādur Singh, started a rebellion in the kingdom with the purpose of overthrowing Mahārāja Sāvant Singh—rebellion—he didn't fight. Our young hero just left everything and went to Vṛndāvana. He said, "I've had enough of this politics," and one night he just walked out of the palace with only a stick and the clothes on his back. It took some time, you know, from Rajasthan to walk to Braja. 

But upon reaching Vṛndāvana, he lamented that much of his life had passed in administration, warfare, and family duties. He wrote again in *Manorath-mañjarī* years later:

"Shame on me! Despite being endowed with all the facilities—because he was king—I did not come to Vṛndāvana earlier. Even the monkeys, dogs, crows, animals, and birds of Vṛndāvana are better than me." 

He also wrote: "Kuñja-vihārī Śrī Rādhā and Kuñja-vihārī Śrī Kṛṣṇa have now bestowed Their grace upon me and rectified everything in my life. There is not a single inconvenience here in this forest, where one finds the highest level of bliss. This is why this place is unique. This nectar is not to be found anywhere except in my beloved Śrī Vṛndāvana. Wow. How many days did I live without Vṛndāvana? In my royal duties and enjoyment, I spent my time in vain, ignorant of the flowery beds of the river Yamunā. I wore thorns on my head in the form of a crown. Not being able to see the pure rasika devotees of the Lord, I was content with the association of materialistic people. That kind of happiness makes me laugh now and sometimes cry. For so long, I didn't engage myself in spiritual activities, which are meant for achieving the goal of life; rather, I loaded myself with the burden of mundane activities. I went here, I went there, and found no happiness in this world. When Nāgarīdāsa will settle in the groves of Vṛndāvana, only then he will obtain all happiness."

Speaking about himself, "When Nāgarīdāsa will settle in the groves of Braja, only then he will obtain all happiness." 

We see he was a powerful warrior and Kṣatriya. When he went to Braja, all these good qualities came about and he shared them with us in the form of poetry. Later, his wife, the queen, Bani Thani, she also renounced the kingdom and palace life and very happily joined him in Vṛndāvana. I was reading that she also started to write poems under the name of Rasika Bihari. She also became a famous poet. 

It was while in Vṛndāvana that Mahārāja Sāvant Singh took initiation into the Vallabhācārya line and received the name that we mentioned before, Nāgarīdāsa. The place where he and his wife stayed in Vṛndāvana is known even today as Nāgarīdāsa-kā-gherā. I went there the other day, Nāgarīdāsa-kā-gherā. It was there that Nāgarīdāsa inspired several well-wishers to construct a beautiful temple for Rādhā and Kṛṣṇa, thus adding to the charm of Vṛndāvana, which we keep speaking about all the time in this series. 

It was there that Nāgarīdāsa wrote over 75 books of poetry, all about Vṛndāvana, that are relished by Braja-vāsīs even today. In fact, his works have been translated into Hindi and Urdu languages and are studied in several Indian universities—I know in Benares and Delhi—studied in universities. You can get a Ph.D., I heard, in just his works. So famous. 

We can understand and appreciate that writing 75 books of poetry while living in a forest like Vṛndāvana in the 1720s was no easy thing. We can hardly imagine the austerities that our own Six Gosvāmīs underwent while writing *śāstras* in their Vṛndāvana forest 500 years ago, especially in their old age. The Vaiṣṇava poet Gopāl Kavi, he writes in his *Vṛndāvana-dharma*, one book we got out of the institute, he writes about the rigors of writing in old age in the forest of Vṛndāvana. It is very revealing:

"The mind becomes frustrated, words and names become difficult to recall, the body is in pain after sitting in one posture for so many hours, food is not digested properly, people do not look entertaining, and the legs become rigid without walking. Only those who spend their lives in such conditions for writing scriptures can understand the austerities undergone in writing them."

We thank all those authors for the nectar they gave us and the austerities that they underwent. I also found a manuscript in the Vṛndāvana Research Institute wherein another author expressed similar sentiments. He wrote in Sanskrit: "Due to constantly writing scriptures, our necks, backs, and chests feel broken. By always looking downward for writing, our eyes no longer function well. Therefore, we pray to all readers: please take care of our manuscripts, as we write them with great effort." I thought that was actually cute. 

Now, I was just thinking of the austerities they underwent, so I did more research—seek and ye shall find. One problem in particular that all authors living in old Vṛndāvana, including of course all of our previous ācāryas, encountered when writing was the lack of light. There was no problem writing in the day, of course, but at night the only source of light were candles or at some point oil lamps. And bear in mind also that in those days there were no eyeglasses; as one grows older, that is a factor. Neither was there a place for those authors, for our Gosvāmīs, to store books. There was no climate-controlled room for the manuscripts; there was only the dense forest of Vṛndāvana. 

What's more, in order to write, many materials were needed. You couldn't go to a shop or a mall and buy the materials; you had to make them yourself. Take paper, for example. Paper for writing scriptures and manuscripts was made by hand by the sādhus from a mixture of tree bark, cotton, and cow dung. After mixing appropriate amounts of these elements, the sādhus used to beat the mixture for many hours with big wooden hammers, flattening it out into the shape of paper. Then they would dry that for many days in sunlight, and when it was dry, they would make the paper shine with the help of rolling a round-shaped conch with sticky paste on the paper. They would take sticky rice paste, and with a conch, they would roll that onto the paper, probably very thin, and it would give it a little shine so it looked attractive. 

I found out that the black ink they used for writing was also made from natural ingredients. This is really interesting: they would take the sap of a banyan tree, mix it with water, and boil that substance for many hours in a clay pot. Later, after that substance cooled, it was mixed with red pigment and collyrium, black kajjala, the peel of almonds, and cow urine. Cow urine was one of the elements that they would mix with water and put it in a small wooden box, and after a few days, when that substance was almost ready, they would make it bright by crushing it with the burned tusk of an elephant. 

They also used to make red and blue ink for writing the first and last letter of a manuscript. They would use these colors for writing the first and the last letter. That red and blue ink was also used for the *puṣpikā*—*puṣpikā* meaning the mark of the day, week, month, year, and time in which the manuscript was finished. They would put that mark when they finished on the last and the first page; they gave the time when it was finished with the red and blue ink. 

For removing or covering mistakes—because you couldn't just delete it like we do on a computer these days—to cover those mistakes while writing, they would use a yellow paste known as *hartala*, which would cover a word or a sentence written by mistake. Whenever I visit the Vṛndāvana Research Institute and we go through the different scriptures, I see sometimes this yellow paste on a mistake that they may have made. 

I also read that some of the ink that the Gosvāmīs made—because they also made their ink—was made in such a way that the ink would never, under any circumstance, smudge. There was a particular reason for that: because when our Six Gosvāmīs would write, they would sometimes cry so profusely with ecstatic emotions that their tears would fall on the paper and wipe away what they had written. Therefore, they developed a special ink that could withstand their tears of ecstasy.

But despite all the inconvenience involved in gathering these materials—the paper, the ink, the writing instruments, etc.—the authors of such prestigious works loved that service. I found a relevant verse in this regard, and it is famous among scholars in Vṛndāvana:

"With strong collyrium ink, equivalent to the Himalayas; with the amount of water contained in an ocean; with a pen made from a *kalpa*-vṛkṣa; and the entire earth as paper; the goddess of knowledge, Sarasvatī, can sit and write *śāstras* forever. So too can we, Śrī Hari, Lord of the universe."

They loved to write despite the inconveniences. Another poet of Braja writes: "If I will make the whole earth my paper, the wood of every forest my pen, the water of the seven oceans my ink, then still I would not be able to write the full glories of my spiritual master in my lifetime."

As for making straight lines on paper in order to write nicely, in the past, sādhus would draw a line with a special ink that would disappear after a few minutes. When I first went to the Vṛndāvana Research Institute, I saw that the writings—everything was very straight. I wondered, "How do they keep it so straight?" I read the other day they would make lines on the parchment or the paper with an ink that disappeared. Then they would write, and what they wrote remained, but the lines disappeared. 

I also read something very interesting: that in the olden days in Braja, sādhus wrote books in different shapes, including *kacchapī* (a manuscript in the form of a turtle, meaning oval), a manuscript on square-shaped paper, *dīrgha* (long rectangular manuscripts), and *muṣṭi* (fist-sized manuscripts meant for distribution, for going out and preaching). If you give them a small book like the size of a fist, you could distribute it. So book distribution was going on for hundreds of years in Vṛndāvana. 

In making the manuscripts, these authors, these poets, these sādhus had a special instrument for cutting the papers into that particular shape; it was called a *dhārī*. And any paper that was left over from cutting the *śāstras* was put in the ground as fertilizer. Talk about an organic society. The paper, because we heard what the paper was made from, is all organic material, so they put back into mother earth, *bhūmi*, what they didn't use. Such an organic Kṛṣṇa consciousness is the perfect way of life. 

Another very interesting thing is that often those who wrote or copied sacred manuscripts would write inside them the following: "Do not ever give this precious manuscript to one who doesn't know how to handle it or care for it." These books are great treasures, and we have those same books that our Gosvāmīs wrote on our shelves. The next time you look at them, you should think how they were originally produced, the first copies, and just yearn to take advantage and read them, not just have them sit on the shelf. 

They would sometimes write *sada rakṣitā*—"always protect the following manuscript from oil"—and sometimes those sādhus would write *na atīva bandhanam*—"do not bind it very tightly." I guess there was a problem in binding it too tightly. 

Now, as far as copying those *śāstras*, there was a caste in Vṛndāvana called *lekhiyā*, and they were very respected persons because their work was not only very important, but it was also very difficult. They had to be very careful not to add or subtract anything when they were copying. Prabhupāda would often say like that: "I have not added anything, I have not subtracted anything; I am just giving the message as I heard it from the Sarasvatī, who also gave it as it is." One such *lekhiyā* wrote, *chāyā-granthāni likhi*: "I write the shadow of the original scripture." He is writing the exact same thing; he is not adding or subtracting. He compared it with the shadow of the original manuscript. 

In those olden days, there was also a trend in some cases to write the year, month, day, and time of the completion of a scripture in secret form. Those who wrote did that simply out of humility. For example, at the end of *Vidagdha-mādhava* by Śrī Rūpa Gosvāmī, he hints at the date of completion by writing *nanda-sindhur-vāṇī-indu*. He gives four hints of the year:
* **Nanda** : There are nine *nandas* in Braja.
* **Sindhur** : This refers to eight different types of elephants.
* **Vāṇī** : People understood that meant the arrows of Kāmadeva, which are always five in number.
* **Indu** : In those days, people knew that meant there is only one moon.

This was meant to be read backwards: *indu-vāṇī-sindhu-nanda*, which indicated that the book was completed in the year 1589. 

In general, in those days, a manuscript consisting of 30 pages took almost 460 days to complete. How grateful we have to be to all those who wrote transcendental literature, the *bhakti-śāstras* in particular. Our Six Gosvāmīs—just think, 460 days to complete 30 pages! We see how much work Śrīla Prabhupāda did also in translating and commenting on those *bhakti-śāstras*. We hear how he would rise at 1:00 or 1:30 in the morning and write the rest of the night. 

Let us conclude today with a few more verses written by our king Sāvant Singh, who became the sādhu Nāgarīdāsa:

"All glory to Varṣāṇā-dhāma! Even Mukti (liberation) stands with folded hands at the door, eager for any service, but the servants of the servants of Varṣāṇā do not even glance at her. I neither desire liberation nor anything else the heart may long for; I only wish to reside in Braja, the holy land of cows. Without the dust of Braja, all kinds of pleasure appear insignificant in this world. I no longer wish to even look at the opulent wealth of a king. The kingdom of all the worlds can easily be renounced upon taking residence in Braja-maṇḍala. The divine bliss of love which the residents of Vṛndāvana always drink cannot be attained even in a dream for one who resides in Vaikuṇṭha-dhāma. The Supreme Personality of Godhead, who is known as imperishable by all, stays like a toy in every house of Braja. Śrī Nāgarīdāsa says this abode of Braja is unique in the whole world. By the causeless grace of my guru, by his grace only, we have attained this treasure of divine bliss."

Haribol! So much nectar and such an interesting history. It is all connected, all connected. Śrī Vṛndāvana-dhāma kī jaya! Śrīla Prabhupāda, the revealer of the Dhāma, kī jaya! 

We will be back in a couple of days—a little busy here at the kīrtana retreat—but we will find time to research for all of you, out of love for all of you. We will be back in a couple of days with another lecture on historical Vṛndāvana, part four.
